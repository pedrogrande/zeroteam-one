## Diátaxis: What It Gets Right and What It Misses

**What Diátaxis gets right:**
- Four distinct needs (learn, do, understand, look up) mapped to four forms
- Systematic relationship between needs and forms
- Clear architecture — documentation organised by need, not by feature

**What Diátaxis misses for your audience and method:**

| Diátaxis assumes | Your reality |
|---|---|
| Reader is comfortable and motivated | Non-technical users face identity threat, shame, and overwhelm (your 14 annoyances insight) |
| Documentation is consumed passively | Co-design sessions *produce* artefacts — the session IS the output |
| Four needs are sufficient | Non-technical users need a **fifth need: "Convince me this is worth my time"** before they'll engage with any other form |
| Needs are independent | Your method is progressive — understanding deepens through doing, and doing produces understanding |
| Documentation is the end product | The *session* is the end product — documentation describes how to have productive sessions |
| Plain reference is enough | Non-technical users need **contextual reference** — not "here's what X does" but "here's what X does *for your situation*" |
| One depth per document | Your progressive disclosure principle means each document type needs **entry, middle, and deep** levels |

The key addition: **a "Convince" layer before tutorials**. Your 14 annoyances research shows that non-technical business owners don't search for "AI methodology" — they search for solutions to named problems. ## Proposed Framework: Co-Design Diátaxis

I'd suggest expanding Diátaxis from 4 to 5 forms, with a progression dimension within each:

```
                    ACTION-ORIENTED
                        │
         ┌──────────────┼──────────────┐
         │              │              │
    CONVINCE         HOW-TO         TUTORIAL
   (Why bother?)   (Step-by-step)  (Learning by doing)
         │              │              │
         └──────────────┼──────────────┘
                        │
                 UNDERSTANDING-ORIENTED
                        │
         ┌──────────────┼──────────────┐
         │              │              │
    EXPLANATION      REFERENCE      PATTERN
   (Why it works)   (Look-up)     (Reusable examples)
         │              │              │
         └──────────────┼──────────────┘
```

The additions to Diátaxis:

1. **CONVINCE** (new quadrant) — "Why should I invest time in this?" Before any tutorial, non-technical users need to see the problem named and the outcome demonstrated. This is your 14 annoyances insight — people don't search for solutions they can't name.

2. **PATTERN** (expanded from reference) — Not just "what does X do" but "here's a reusable pattern for your situation." This is your Pattern Library concept — Signal Detector, Review Partner, etc. Non-technical users need *situational* reference, not abstract reference.

Each form has **three depth levels** (your progressive disclosure principle):

- **Entry** — Plain English, one-sentence concepts, analogies to familiar things
- **Practitioner** — Structured processes, templates, real examples
- **Architect** — Deep principles, trade-offs, when to break the rules

## Key Design Decisions for Both Documents

**Plain English first.** Your own rule — practical language, academic terms in brackets. A 16-year-old should understand the Convince and Tutorial sections.

**One clear next step always.** Your ADHD design principle applied to documentation itself. Every section ends with one action the reader can take.

**The session IS the artefact.** Both documents should emphasise that co-designing produces something — a boundary map, a specification, a decision tree — not just a conversation.

**Progressive disclosure.** Entry level in each section first. Practitioner and Architect levels clearly marked and skippable.

**Named in the reader's language.** "Where should AI stop?" not "complementarity allocation." "What would make this wrong?" not "failure mode identification." The Plain English Taxonomy principle applied to documentation.

**Real examples, not hypotheticals.** Every concept should have at least one concrete example. Ideally from your own sessions — the most compelling proof is "here's what this method produced."
