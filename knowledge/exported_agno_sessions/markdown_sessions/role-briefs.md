## The Hard Arithmetic First

Before any milestone planning, the capacity reality must be named plainly:

- **Melbourne university semesters:** Semester 1 runs roughly March–June; Semester 2 runs July–November; breaks fall in late June/early July (~3 weeks) and November–February (~14 weeks)[^2_1]
- **30 students × 5 hrs/wk** during ~38 semester weeks = **5,700 student-hours** across the year in semester
- **30 students × 10 hrs/wk** during ~14 break weeks = **4,200 student-hours** in breaks
- **Total annual capacity: ~9,900 student-hours** — roughly 5 full-time equivalents across the whole year
- The Christmas/New Year window (late November 2026 to mid-February 2027) is the **single largest capacity window** — 10 hrs/wk from 30 students = ~3,600 hours in 12 weeks. This is where the heaviest delivery work must land

The vision is achievable, but only if every role is designed around *leverage* — student effort that compounds, rather than effort that resets each week. Agentic workflows become the architecture that makes this possible: students configure systems that continue to produce output even when they're offline studying for exams.[^2_1]

***

## The Non-Negotiable Constraint: Roles Must Be Re-Scoped

The 30 roles in the vision are not 30 full-time jobs — they are **30 domains of ownership**, each executed at 5–10 hrs/week through AI-amplified workflows. The students who succeed are those who quickly learn to build agentic systems that do the *execution* while they do the *thinking and directing*. This is itself a live demonstration of the Structural Trust Framework's core thesis: trustworthy outcomes don't require proportional human effort — they require the right architecture.[^2_2]

The roles divide into three real capacity tiers:


| Tier | Examples | Why They Work at 5–10 hrs/wk |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Agent-amplified output roles** | Content Strategist, Research Analyst, Growth \& SEO, Data Analyst | A well-configured agentic workflow can draft, schedule, and publish; student owns curation and quality gate |
| **High-leverage coordination roles** | Chief of Staff, Head of Community, Events Coordinator, Student Programs Coordinator | Most value comes from structure and relationships; agentic tools handle tracking and comms |

## Student Programs Coordinator

### The Short Version

The Open Research Lab's long-run value depends on a pipeline of talented students cycling through it — bringing fresh perspectives, contributing to research and product work, and either joining Zero Team or carrying Zero Team's thinking into the organisations they move into. You build and run that pipeline: the recruitment, the onboarding, the rotation structure, the co-supervision arrangements, and the pathway from lab contributor to team member or Future's Edge member.[^9_4][^9_3]

### What This Role Actually Involves

- **Designing the student rotation program** — structured 3–6 month rotations where students contribute to specific research streams, product workstreams, or consulting support tasks; each rotation has a defined contribution, a supervision arrangement, and a clear output the student can point to when it's done[^9_4]
- **Building the university recruitment pipeline** — relationships with faculty at RMIT, the University of Melbourne, and other target universities who can identify and refer strong students; information sessions, application processes, and the selection criteria that find students who will genuinely contribute rather than just accumulate a CV line[^9_4]
- **Running the co-supervision arrangements** — when a student contributor is working on a research-adjacent task, they need both a Zero Team supervisor and an academic co-supervisor; you manage those relationships, ensure both parties are meeting their commitments, and resolve the coordination problems that inevitably arise
- **Onboarding student contributors effectively** — the biggest failure mode in student programs is throwing people into work before they have the context to contribute; you design and run an onboarding experience that gets contributors to useful capability quickly, covers Zero Team's frameworks at the level needed for their specific contribution, and establishes the working norms that make distributed collaboration function[^9_4]
- **Tracking and reporting on the 20+ active contributors target** — who is in the lab, what they're working on, what they've contributed, and what their pathway forward looks like; you maintain the visibility that lets the Research Lab Director and Pete understand the program's health at a glance
- **Creating the pathway to employment or Future's Edge membership** — the best student contributors should have a clear line of sight to ongoing involvement with Zero Team; you design and manage the process by which exceptional contributors are identified and offered that pathway, and you make sure students know from day one that it exists[^9_4][^9_3]


### The Honest Realities

This role is operationally demanding in a way that is easy to underestimate. Managing 20+ student contributors simultaneously — each with different rotation lengths, different supervision needs, different contribution types, and different levels of engagement — is a genuine coordination challenge.[^9_4]

The quality risk is significant. A student program that attracts 20 contributors who produce low-quality or superficial work is worse than a program with 10 contributors who produce excellent work — because the low-quality program damages Zero Team's reputation with its university partners and produces no real intellectual or commercial value. Your selection criteria and onboarding design are the quality controls; they need to be thoughtful from the start.

This role has a paradox built into it: you are a student running a program for students. That creates a specific credibility challenge — you need to be the person who takes the program seriously, holds contributors to genuine standards, and manages the relationships with academic supervisors as a professional, not a peer. Pete and the two full-time leaders provide institutional backing; you provide the operational rigour.[^9_4]

At 5 hrs/wk during semester, the program needs to be largely self-running through the systems you build — onboarding documents, rotation templates, supervision check-in cadences, and the contributor tracking dashboard that gives you visibility without requiring a meeting for every update. The heavy design work happens in the June break window, before the first cohort of contributors arrives.[^9_4]

### What You'll Walk Away With

- Demonstrated experience designing and running a structured student talent pipeline for a research and consulting organisation — a capability that is directly applicable to roles in talent, learning and development, program management, and academic administration
- Working relationships with faculty and research offices at RMIT and the University of Melbourne — institutional relationships that are genuinely hard to build and that open doors across academic and professional settings
- Evidence of having built and managed a community of 20+ contributors to a measurable standard — the kind of scale and accountability that most graduate roles don't give you access to for years
- A clear professional identity at the intersection of research, talent development, and organisational operations — a positioning that is unusual and valuable across universities, consultancies, think tanks, and mission-driven organisations
- The career story: *I built and ran Zero Team Open Lab's student program from scratch — I designed the rotation structure, built the university partnerships, and had 20+ active student contributors working in the lab within 12 months, several of whom joined Zero Team or Future's Edge as ongoing members*


### Who This Role Suits

People who are energised by other people's development — who find genuine satisfaction in designing an experience that makes someone else more capable, more confident, and more connected. Human resources, education, management, or organisational behaviour students who want to work on talent and learning in a context that is intellectually serious rather than administratively routine.
