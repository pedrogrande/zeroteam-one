# I've read that the low levels of trust in AI is limiting the benefits. are there data or reports to sorry this?

Yes, there is strong and recent data to support this — and some of the most relevant research comes from right here in Melbourne.

## The Global Trust Gap

The most comprehensive evidence comes from the **University of Melbourne / KPMG global study** (2025), which surveyed 48,340 people across 47 countries [^1]:

- Only **46% of people globally** are willing to trust AI systems [^2]
- **83%** believe AI will deliver wide-ranging benefits — yet trust still trails well behind awareness and usage [^2]
- **70%** believe national and international regulation is needed before they'll trust AI more [^2]
- Alarmingly, **66% of users rely on AI output without evaluating its accuracy**, and **56% report making mistakes** in their work because of AI — undermining the productivity case [^2]


## Australia Lags Even Further

Australia's trust deficit is notably worse than the global average [^3]:

- **50%** of Australians use AI regularly, but only **36%** are willing to trust it
- **78%** of Australians are concerned about negative outcomes from AI
- Despite Australian financial institutions achieving **67% AI adoption** (above the 57% global average), only **30% of Australians** believe AI's benefits outweigh its risks [^4]
- This adoption-trust gap directly threatens the projected **\$60 billion GDP contribution** AI could make to Australia by 2035 [^4]


## Enterprise Scaling Is Stalled

The trust problem isn't just public sentiment — it manifests inside organisations too [^5]:

- Only **18% of organisations** have scaled AI across multiple teams
- **61%** report experiencing "silent failures" in their data pipelines
- Only **29%** have a clear definition of what AI readiness even means
- As one CDO Magazine report put it: *"Organisations aren't struggling to adopt AI; they are struggling to trust it at scale"* [^5]


## Data Trust: The Hidden Blocker

A separate layer of trust — around data quality and governance — also stalls adoption [^6]:

- Organisations want AI's benefits but fear data mismanagement exposure
- Key issues include siloed data, unclear ownership, inconsistent quality, and outdated governance models
- The core finding: **when people don't trust the data, they don't trust the outputs — and when they don't trust outputs, they don't experiment**, so innovation stalls before it begins [^6]


## Who Trusts AI Least

Trust is not evenly distributed across society, which compounds the equity challenge [^7]:

- People from **lower socioeconomic backgrounds** are less likely to trust AI and more likely to feel it reinforces existing inequalities
- Concern about AI in high-stakes decisions (welfare assessments, loan approvals, policing) has **risen sharply since 2022/23**
- In the UK, concern about facial recognition in policing is significantly higher among Black (57%) and Asian (52%) adults versus the general population (39%) [^7]

The data is clear: the trust deficit is not a peripheral issue — it is a **central bottleneck** between AI's theoretical economic potential and its real-world realisation.
