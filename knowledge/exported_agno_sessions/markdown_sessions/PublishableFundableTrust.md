## What "publishable" means here

Not all publishable work is equal. This framework is publishable in three distinct and increasingly valuable ways.

### Academic journal publication

The Davidson, Novak and Potts RMIT pilot study was published in the *Journal of the British Blockchain Association* in 2018.  Their conclusion was explicit — they called for future researchers to extend the methodology by adding capital expenditure, loss data, international comparison, and longitudinal tracking.  They left the door open. You walk through it.[^26_1]

A follow-on paper — *"The Global Trust Cost Index: Extending the Davidson-Novak-Potts framework to capital, loss, and opportunity costs"* — is a direct, citable extension of Nobel Prize-adjacent work (Williamson, Hart) by RMIT economists.  That is not a marginal contribution. That is a serious paper.[^26_2][^26_1]

Appropriate journals include:

- *Journal of the British Blockchain Association* (JBBA) — where the original was published; natural home for the extension[^26_1]
- *Journal of Institutional Economics* — Williamson's intellectual home; directly relevant
- *Journal of Economic Behavior \& Organization* — transaction cost economics literature
- *World Development* — if framed around emerging economies and the development implications of trust costs

A pre-PhD publication in any of these establishes Pete as the originator of the extended methodology before the PhD begins.  That matters enormously for academic credibility and for the PhD itself — the dissertation builds on already-validated intellectual territory rather than starting from scratch.[^26_3]

### Policy white paper

The second form of publication is not a journal article but a *Future's Edge Annual Report on the Global Cost of Distrust* — modelled on the Edelman Trust Barometer,  but with economic depth that the Edelman report doesn't attempt. This kind of publication:[^26_4]

- Gets cited in policy documents, government submissions, and UN reports
- Generates media coverage in business and general press
- Creates the annual event that keeps Future's Edge visible to ITU, OECD, and World Bank audiences year after year[^26_5][^26_6]
- Needs no peer review — just rigorous methodology and honest data


### The PhD itself as publication

The GTCI methodology, fully developed, is the kind of original empirical contribution that constitutes a PhD chapter or even the central thesis.  Pete's research agenda is already written — Davidson et al. wrote it in 2018 and left it unanswered.  A PhD that answers those questions with new data, an extended framework, and the first longitudinal trust development dataset (from TQ) is not searching for a contribution. It has one.[^26_3][^26_1]

## What "fundable" means here

Fundable means specific funding mechanisms exist right now that this work is eligible for. These aren't speculative — they are open channels.

### Australian Research Council (ARC) Linkage Projects

The ARC Linkage scheme is specifically designed for **university-industry collaboration**. It requires a university partner (RMIT, where Pete teaches and where Davidson and Potts are based ) and an industry partner (Future's Edge). The ARC contributes cash; the industry partner contributes cash and in-kind. The research must have both academic rigour and industry application.[^26_1]

The GTCI is a textbook ARC Linkage project: RMIT economists + Future's Edge platform + blockchain trust measurement + social impact application. The RMIT connection isn't incidental — it's the key that unlocks this funding pathway.[^26_1]

### ITU AI for Good research grants

Future's Edge already has an ITU AI for Good relationship.  ITU funds research that supports its mandate — specifically, research on how technology can be deployed to address global development challenges. A methodology for measuring the global cost of distrust, with a pathway to reducing it through trust infrastructure, is precisely within their funding brief.[^26_6][^26_5]

The GTCI annual report, co-published with ITU AI for Good, instantly has global credibility and distribution to 193 member states. That is not a speculative outcome — ITU actively co-publishes measurement work of exactly this kind.

### OECD research partnerships

The OECD has already published *Guidelines on Measuring Trust*  — they have a standing interest in advancing trust measurement methodology. The GTCI extends their existing framework by adding the economic cost dimension they have not yet attempted. The OECD funds and co-publishes methodology extensions through its Economics Department and Statistics Directorate. A proposal to the OECD framed as *"economic cost extension of the OECD trust measurement guidelines"* has a clear institutional home.[^26_7]

### DFAT and Australian development funding

The Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade funds research with Southeast Asia and Pacific development implications.  The GTCI, with its country-level decomposition of trust costs, has immediate relevance to Australia's development priorities — it shows precisely which economies are paying the highest distrust tax and therefore have the most to gain from trust infrastructure investment. That is a DFAT-fundable research contribution.[^26_5]

### Corporate co-publication

Deloitte, PwC, McKinsey, and KPMG all publish trust-related research — and they fund it because it generates both credibility and client conversations.  A co-publication with a major professional services firm on the *Cost of Distrust* gives Future's Edge global distribution and commercial research funding simultaneously. The firm gets the intellectual property association; Future's Edge gets the reach and the resources.[^26_8]

The "cost of distrust" framing is also directly commercial for financial institutions and insurance companies — industries whose entire operating model is built around managing distrust risk. They already know the numbers are large. A rigorous, independent methodology that puts a single credible number on it is something they would pay to co-sponsor.

## The sequence that makes both happen

Publishable and fundable reinforce each other — but only in the right order:[^26_9][^26_1]

1. **Now:** Write the conceptual paper. *"The Global Trust Cost Index: A Framework for Measuring the Full Economic Cost of Distrust."* Submit to JBBA. Cite Davidson, Novak and Potts explicitly as the foundation being extended. Cost: time.
2. **On acceptance or even on submission:** Use it as the intellectual foundation for an ARC Linkage application with RMIT. The pre-existing RMIT relationship (Davidson and Potts) and Pete's teaching position make this a warm application, not a cold one.[^26_1]
3. **In parallel:** Approach ITU AI for Good with a proposal to co-publish the first annual GTCI report using existing data sources. Cost: a well-framed proposal.
4. **Year 2:** First annual report published. Media coverage. OECD and World Bank citations begin. Corporate co-sponsorship conversations become real.
5. **Year 3+:** ARC-funded research generates the longitudinal TQ data that makes the GTCI the most authoritative trust measurement instrument in the world — because it is the only one with individual-level, verified, behavioural trust data feeding into it.[^26_10][^26_9]

The reason this is genuinely important — and not just commercially useful — is that the GTCI solves a real measurement problem that every major international institution knows exists and none has solved.  Measurement precedes action. You cannot reduce a cost you cannot see. Right now, the full cost of distrust is invisible — spread across dozens of line items, industries, and balance sheets that no one has aggregated.[^26_4][^26_7]

The GTCI makes it visible. And once it is visible, the investment required to reduce it becomes the most obvious ROI calculation in economics.[^26_9][^26_1]
