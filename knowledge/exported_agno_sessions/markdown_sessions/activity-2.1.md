# Module 2

## Activity 2.1 — Transferability: Applying AI Across Your Work

***

### The trap this activity addresses

By the time you reach this activity, you have a solid mental model of what AI is, how it works, and how to construct and refine effective prompts. Those are foundational skills. But there's a pattern worth naming before you go further.

Many people who develop early AI competence do so in relation to one tool, one workflow, or one set of tasks. They get good at using ChatGPT for a specific kind of writing, or Copilot for meeting summaries, or a particular prompt structure that works reliably in their current role. Then the tool updates, or their organisation switches platforms, or they move to a new job, and the competence they built feels much less portable than they expected.

This is the difference between tool familiarity and genuine AI literacy. Tool familiarity is knowing how to do something in a specific interface. AI literacy is understanding why effective AI use works the way it does, well enough that you can apply the same thinking in a new context.

This activity builds the second kind of competence. It gives you frameworks for decomposing tasks, mapping AI to your own work, transferring principles across platforms, and building habits that remain useful regardless of how the tools change.

***

## Task 2.1.1 — Task Decomposition for AI Use

***

### Why whole-task thinking leads to poor AI use

One of the most common mistakes in workplace AI use is treating a complex task as a single AI job. You have a report to write, so you ask AI to write the report. You have a problem to analyse, so you ask AI to analyse the problem. The output is usually disappointing, and the temptation is to conclude that AI isn't useful for serious work.

The issue is almost never the tool. It's the approach.

Most meaningful work tasks are not single actions. They're sequences of smaller actions, each of which has different characteristics. Some involve generating text or structure. Some require access to your specific context, relationships, or professional judgement. Some carry accountability that can't be delegated. When you treat a complex task as a whole and hand it to AI, you lose the ability to apply it where it genuinely helps and retain human control where it genuinely matters.

Task decomposition solves this. It's the practice of breaking a work task into its component parts before deciding how AI fits in.

***

### The three component types

Once you've broken a task into components, each component can be classified into one of three categories:

**AI-led components**
These are parts of the task where AI can do most of the work effectively and efficiently, with a human reviewing the output. They typically involve generating, formatting, structuring, or transforming text or data according to clear specifications.

Characteristics:

- The output can be objectively evaluated for quality
- Errors in the output are detectable and correctable before use
- The stakes of an imperfect first draft are low
- The task is primarily about producing a form rather than exercising judgement

Examples: drafting a first version of a document, reformatting data into a table, translating content into plain language, generating a list of options for human evaluation.

***

**AI-assisted components**
These are parts of the task where AI plays a supporting role but human input, judgement, or context is essential at each step. AI accelerates or augments the work; it doesn't lead it.

Characteristics:

- The output requires meaningful human input to be accurate or appropriate
- The learner's professional context, relationships, or expertise are necessary to evaluate or complete the output
- Errors in the output could have consequences if not caught

Examples: analysing options where the criteria involve organisational priorities only a human understands, preparing talking points for a meeting where tone and relationship context matter, researching a topic where accuracy will be relied upon.

***

**Human-only components**
These are parts of the task that AI cannot perform appropriately, either because they require genuine judgement, carry accountability, involve sensitive relationships, or fall within a regulated professional domain.

Characteristics:

- The outcome of getting it wrong is serious
- The task requires knowledge, relationship, or ethical reasoning that AI cannot replicate
- Professional standards, regulatory obligations, or organisational policy require human responsibility at this point

Examples: making a decision with financial or legal consequences, delivering feedback to an individual employee, providing professional advice in a regulated context, final approval of any external-facing communication.

***

### A worked decomposition

**Task:** Prepare a business case for upgrading the organisation's project management software.

| Component | Type | AI role |
|---|---|---|
| Research current tool limitations and common pain points | AI-assisted | AI can help structure the research and summarise findings, but the input data must come from the human (interviews, internal feedback, usage data) |
| Draft an executive summary of the problem | AI-led | Human provides the key facts; AI drafts the summary for review and editing |
| Identify and compare three alternative software options | AI-assisted | AI can generate initial comparison; human must validate against organisational requirements and procurement constraints |
| Calculate cost-benefit analysis | Human-only | Requires access to internal financial data and professional judgement about assumptions |
| Write the recommendation section | AI-assisted | AI drafts based on human-provided conclusions; human reviews and confirms the reasoning reflects actual organisational context |
| Final approval and sign-off | Human-only | Accountability cannot be delegated |

The decomposition reveals something important: AI is genuinely useful in this task, but only for specific components. Treating the entire task as an AI job would produce a business case that looks plausible but lacks the internal data, financial rigour, and contextual judgement the organisation needs to act on it.

***

### What decomposition reveals

Applying this framework consistently tends to reveal two things that surprise most learners.

First, AI is useful for more tasks than they expected, because many tasks contain AI-led components that are currently done manually out of habit rather than necessity.

Second, AI is appropriate for fewer complete tasks than first appears, because most meaningful work involves components where human judgement, context, or accountability is not optional.

Both insights are productive. The goal isn't to maximise AI use. It's to use AI where it genuinely helps and be clear-eyed about where it doesn't.

## Task 2.1.2 — Mapping AI to Your Work Day

***

### From framework to practice

Task decomposition gives you a way of thinking about individual tasks. This task applies that thinking across your role as a whole.

The goal is a personal AI use map: a practical picture of where AI fits into your actual working week, based on your specific responsibilities rather than generic examples. This is more useful than any list of "top AI use cases" you might find online, because it reflects the tasks you actually do, the context you actually work in, and the constraints you actually face.

The mapping process uses both frameworks from this module together. The three-zone framework from Activity 1.2.5 works at the task level: it tells you whether AI use is appropriate for a given piece of work at all. The three component types from Task 2.1.1 work at the sub-task level: they tell you, for tasks where AI can assist, exactly which parts AI can handle and which parts require human judgement. Steps 2 and 3 below apply each in turn.

***

### The mapping process

Work through the following steps, using the role-based worksheet provided.

**Step 1: List your most frequent tasks**

Write down the 10 to 15 tasks you do most regularly. Focus on tasks that involve producing something: a document, a communication, a summary, a recommendation, a report, a plan. Include routine tasks that might feel too small to analyse, because these are often where AI saves the most cumulative time.

Examples of what to list:

- Weekly status update for the management team
- Responding to routine client or stakeholder enquiries
- Preparing agenda and notes for recurring meetings
- Summarising research, reports, or data for a non-specialist audience
- Drafting internal communications
- Reviewing and providing feedback on documents from others
- Preparing presentations or briefing materials

***

**Step 2: Classify each task**

For each task on your list, apply the three-zone framework from Activity 1.2.5:

- **Green:** AI is well-suited and the task is appropriate for AI use with standard review
- **Amber:** AI can assist but meaningful human input is required at key points
- **Red:** AI use is inappropriate, requires organisational approval, or carries significant risk

Most tasks will land in the green or amber zone. That's expected. The purpose of identifying red-zone tasks is not to discourage AI use generally, but to be explicit about where it should not be the default.

***

**Step 3: Identify component-level opportunities**

For tasks in the amber zone, apply the decomposition thinking from Task 2.1.1. Which components of this task could be AI-led? Which need to remain human-led? Writing this down makes the distinction concrete rather than theoretical.

***

**Step 4: Identify your highest-value opportunities**

Look across your map and identify the two or three tasks where AI would save you the most time or produce the most meaningful improvement in quality. These are your priority use cases, the starting points for building productive AI habits in your role.

***

### High-value AI use: what to look for

High-value AI use cases in most professional roles share common characteristics:

- The task is done frequently, so time savings compound
- The task involves producing a first draft or structure that a human then refines
- The task is time-consuming but not analytically complex
- The current quality of the output is limited primarily by the time available rather than by expertise

If you can identify two or three tasks that fit this pattern in your role, you have a clear starting point for building productive AI habits.

***

### Limited-value AI use: what it looks like

Not every task that AI can assist with is worth the effort. Limited-value AI use typically looks like:

- Tasks that are faster to do directly than to prompt, review, and correct
- Tasks where the AI output requires so much revision that the efficiency gain disappears
- Tasks so short and routine that the overhead of constructing a prompt outweighs the benefit
- Tasks where the primary value is in the thinking process itself, and outsourcing the output means losing that value

Taken together, these patterns describe what you might call the efficiency illusion: the feeling of productivity that comes from using AI on a task, without the actual time or quality gain. Recognising these limited-value use cases protects you from using AI habitually rather than deliberately.

## Task 2.1.3 — Transferability Across Tools

***

### Tool familiarity is not AI competence

If you've developed strong habits with one AI tool, it can feel disorienting when your organisation introduces a different one, or when the tool you've been using changes significantly. Users who built their AI skills around specific features, workarounds, or interface patterns often find their confidence drops when the surface changes.

This is the cost of tool-dependent learning. It's worth understanding why it happens and how to build skills that don't have this vulnerability.

***

### What stays the same across tools

The principles that make AI use effective are not specific to any platform. They are properties of how large language models work, and that architecture is shared across every major generative AI tool.

Across ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini, Anthropic's Claude, and any future tools built on similar foundations, the following remain consistent:

**Prompt quality determines output quality.** The four-element structure from Task 1.2.2 (instruction, context, constraints, desired format) works in every tool because it addresses the structural need every model has for clear, well-specified input.

**Iteration improves results.** The conversational, multi-turn approach from Task 1.2.4 applies in any tool that maintains conversation context, which is all major tools.

**The model predicts plausible output, not verified truth.** The mental model from Task 1.1.2 applies regardless of which tool you use. Overconfident, hallucinated, or biased outputs are possible in every tool.

**The evaluation framework transfers.**  A structured set of red flags for reviewing AI output (which you'll build in Activity 2.2) applies regardless of which tool produced the output. The habit of critical evaluation is not platform-specific.

**Human oversight remains your responsibility.** The accountability principle from Task 1.2.6 is not platform-specific. It is a professional principle.

***

### What genuinely differs across tools

Differences between tools are real and worth knowing, but they are mostly at the surface level:

| Dimension | What varies | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Data handling and privacy** | Where your inputs go, how long they're retained, whether they're used for training | Critical for decisions about what data to enter |
| **Knowledge cutoff and internet access** | Whether the tool has current information or is limited to training data | Important for tasks involving recent events or live data |
| **Integrations** | Which workplace systems the tool connects to (email, calendar, documents) | Affects what the tool can access and act on |
| **Default behaviour** | Tone, verbosity, formatting preferences, how the model handles ambiguity | Affects what you need to specify explicitly vs. rely on defaults |
| **Organisational controls** | Whether your employer's version has additional restrictions or data protections | Affects what use cases are permitted |

None of these differences require you to rebuild your AI skills from scratch. They require you to ask a few additional questions when you encounter a new tool.

***

### Orienting yourself in a new tool

When your organisation introduces a new AI tool, or you begin using one you haven't used before, apply this orientation sequence before using it for substantive work:

1. **What is it trained on, and does it have internet access?** This tells you how current its knowledge is and whether it can retrieve information you haven't provided.

2. **What data am I sending to it, and where does it go?** Check your organisation's guidance on data handling for this tool before entering anything confidential or commercially sensitive.

3. **What are the default behaviours I need to override?** Spend five minutes testing a familiar task to see how the tool handles it, so you know what constraints to add to compensate for unhelpful defaults.

4. **What does my organisation's AI policy say about this tool?** Is it on the approved list? Are there specific use cases it should or shouldn't be used for?

This sequence takes 15 to 20 minutes and significantly reduces the risk of missteps in early use.

***

### The underlying principle

Becoming competent with a new AI tool is not a matter of relearning how to use AI. It's a matter of learning how this particular tool implements the principles you already understand.

A professional who has built genuine AI literacy treats a new tool the same way a skilled driver treats an unfamiliar car: the fundamentals are the same, the controls are in slightly different places, and a short orientation is all that's needed before productive use.

## Task 2.1.4 — AI in Business Functions

***

### AI is not just for technical teams

One of the persistent misconceptions about AI in the workplace is that it's primarily relevant to people in technology, data, or specialist roles. In practice, the use cases with the broadest reach and the most immediate workplace impact are in the functions that every organisation relies on: communication, administration, analysis, and coordination.

This task presents AI use cases across a range of common business functions. The purpose is not to provide a comprehensive directory, but to give you enough concrete examples across different contexts that you can more readily see where AI applies in your own work, even if your function isn't listed here.

***

### Drafting and communication

AI is well-suited to the text-production work that forms a significant part of most professional roles.

**Where AI adds value:**

- First drafts of emails, memos, briefing notes, and internal updates
- Adapting the same message for different audiences (technical vs. non-technical, internal vs. external)
- Reformatting or restructuring documents for clarity
- Responding to routine enquiries from a structured template
- Translating jargon-heavy content into plain language

**Where humans remain essential:**

- Tone and relationship judgement in sensitive communications
- Final approval of anything external-facing or consequential
- Content that requires access to confidential or real-time organisational information

**Example prompt pattern:**
> "Rewrite the following internal update for an audience of frontline staff who are not familiar with the project background. Use plain language, keep it under 200 words, and focus only on what directly affects their day-to-day work."

***

### Summarising and research

AI is particularly effective at condensing large volumes of text into usable summaries, a task that is time-consuming but often not analytically complex when done manually.

**Where AI adds value:**

- Summarising long reports, policy documents, or research papers
- Extracting key points from meeting transcripts
- Synthesising multiple documents into a combined overview
- Identifying relevant sections within a large document

**Where humans remain essential:**

- Assessing whether the source material is credible and current
- Interpreting findings in the context of organisational strategy or priorities
- Deciding what to act on, not just what the document says

**Example prompt pattern:**
> "Summarise the following report in five bullet points. Focus on findings that are most relevant to a team responsible for [function]. Exclude methodological detail. Flag any findings that appear contradictory or that you are uncertain about."

***

### Analysis and reporting

AI can support analytical work, but this is one of the areas where the distinction between AI-led and AI-assisted components matters most.

**Where AI adds value:**

- Structuring an analytical framework before the analysis begins
- Generating a first draft of a report narrative around data a human has prepared
- Identifying patterns or trends in structured text data
- Drafting the interpretation and "so what" section once the human has drawn the key conclusions

**Where humans remain essential:**

- Interpreting data in context (AI doesn't know your organisation's history, strategy, or constraints)
- Verifying numerical outputs (AI can make calculation and data errors that look plausible)
- Making recommendations that carry professional accountability

**Example prompt pattern:**
> "Based on the following summary of quarterly data, draft a three-paragraph narrative for a management report. The first paragraph should describe what happened, the second should identify the two most significant drivers, and the third should outline the implications for the next quarter. I will verify the figures and adjust the conclusions before the report is submitted."

***

### Workflow support

AI is effective at the structural and formatting work that supports productive workflows but doesn't require deep expertise.

**Where AI adds value:**

- Creating templates for recurring documents (agendas, status reports, project updates)
- Converting unstructured notes into structured formats
- Generating checklists or step-by-step process guides
- Reformatting content for a different medium (e.g. converting a report into slide bullets)

**Where humans remain essential:**

- Ensuring templates and processes reflect actual organisational requirements
- Reviewing outputs before they enter any formal system or workflow
- Decisions about process design and approval

**Example prompt pattern:**
> "Convert the following meeting notes into a structured action register. For each action, identify the owner, the deadline, and a one-sentence description of what needs to be done. Format as a table with three columns: Action, Owner, Deadline."

***

### A cross-function use case gallery

The following examples illustrate AI application across common business functions. Each includes a brief description of the use case and a note on where human oversight is most important.

| Function | Use case | Key oversight point |
|---|---|---|
| **HR** | Drafting job descriptions from a role brief | Verify that language is inclusive and compliant with relevant employment standards |
| **Finance** | Summarising budget variance reports for a non-finance audience | Verify all figures before the summary is distributed |
| **Marketing** | Generating first-draft copy variations for review | Confirm brand voice, factual accuracy, and compliance with any advertising standards |
| **Operations** | Drafting standard operating procedures from process notes | Verify against actual process and have subject matter expert review before publishing |
| **Customer service** | Drafting templated responses to common enquiry types | Review each response before sending; templates require human judgement in application |
| **Legal / Compliance** | Summarising regulatory updates for non-specialist staff | All legal interpretation must be reviewed by a qualified professional before distribution |
| **IT** | Drafting user-facing communications about system changes | Review for technical accuracy and accessibility of language |
| **Executive / leadership** | Preparing briefing notes and talking points | Human input on strategic context and relationship sensitivity is essential |

***

### The transferable thread

Across all of these functions, the same pattern holds: AI is most useful when it handles the structural, generative, or formatting components of a task, freeing the human to focus on the components that require context, judgement, and accountability.

The function changes. The principles don't.

## Task 2.1.5 — Building Transferable AI Habits

***

### Habits outlast tools

Individual prompt techniques and platform-specific features are useful in the short term. Habits are useful indefinitely.

A habit, in this context, is a consistent practice that you apply across AI use generally, not just when you remember to or when it's convenient. The habits worth building are the ones that remain valuable regardless of which tools your organisation uses, how those tools evolve, or what role you find yourself in next.

This task identifies five such habits and gives you a structure for making two of them concrete before you finish Module 2.

***

### Five habits that transfer across any tool or context

**Habit 1: Document prompt patterns that work**

When you find a prompt structure that produces reliably good outputs for a recurring task, write it down. Capture it in the format from Task 1.2.7: a generalised template with placeholders, notes on what to adjust for different audiences, and a note on what constraints to always include.

This habit ensures that the quality of your AI use doesn't depend on whether you happen to write a good prompt on any given day. It also makes your AI skills portable: when you move to a new role or tool, your library of patterns moves with you.

***

**Habit 2: Check fit-for-purpose before reaching for AI**

Before starting any task with AI, spend 15 seconds asking: Is this a task where AI genuinely helps, or am I reaching for it out of habit? Does this task involve data or context I shouldn't be entering into this tool? Is the time I'll spend reviewing and correcting the output likely to be less than the time it would take to do this directly?

This habit protects against the efficiency illusion from Task 2.1.2 and keeps your AI use deliberate rather than reflexive.

***

**Habit 3: Review outputs with genuine critical attention**

Every AI output you use for work should pass through a real review, not a scan. This means reading for accuracy, checking specific claims that could cause problems if wrong, confirming the tone is appropriate for the audience and context, and asking the three questions from Task 1.1.5: Is this accurate? Is this appropriate? Am I accountable for this?

This habit is the one most frequently abandoned under time pressure, and the one with the most significant professional consequences when it lapses.

***

**Habit 4: Use AI deliberately rather than habitually**

Deliberate AI use means choosing to use AI for a specific task because it's the right tool for that component of the work. Habitual AI use means reaching for AI by default because it's available and familiar.

The distinction matters because habitual use tends to expand into tasks where AI is low-value or inappropriate, and can subtly erode the thinking and writing skills that professional work requires. A useful check: ask yourself, at the end of each week, whether the tasks you used AI for were genuinely better or faster as a result. If the answer is consistently yes, your use is deliberate. If it's mixed, revisit your use map.

***

**Habit 5: Stay current without chasing every new tool**

The AI landscape changes quickly. New tools, new features, new model releases, and new use cases appear constantly. Staying current is a genuine professional responsibility, but it doesn't mean evaluating every new tool the moment it appears.

A sustainable approach:

- Follow one or two trusted sources for AI in your industry or professional field
- When your organisation introduces a new tool, apply the orientation sequence from Task 2.1.3 before using it for substantive work
- Review your prompt library and personal use map once or twice a year to check whether your practices still reflect the best available options

***

### Deliberate vs. habitual use: a practical illustration

| Deliberate use | Habitual use |
|---|---|
| Chooses AI after assessing the task | Reaches for AI before assessing the task |
| Applies clear prompts with appropriate context | Submits vague prompts and edits around the result |
| Reviews output critically before use | Scans output and passes it on |
| Documents what works for future use | Starts from scratch each time |
| Recognises when AI is not the right tool | Uses AI regardless of fit |

The goal is not perfection. It's direction of travel. Each time you make a deliberate choice about AI use, you're building the habit of professional judgment that will serve you regardless of how the tools evolve.

***

### Reflection and action planning

Before moving to Activity 2.2, take 10 minutes to complete the following:

**Part 1: Habit identification**
From the five habits above, identify the two that would make the most practical difference to your AI use right now. Write a brief explanation of why you chose each one.

**Part 2: Workflow change**
For each habit, describe one specific change to how you currently work that would help you build it. Be concrete: what would you do differently, and when?

**Part 3: Progress check**
For each habit, describe one observable sign that it's becoming established. How will you know, in four weeks, that this is working?
