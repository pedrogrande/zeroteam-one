# What are potential story arcs for the series? Let's start with a plan for 6 articles.

This is an excellent foundation for a series — and you have significant original IP in your own documents that most Substack writers on this topic simply won't have. Here's a 6-article series plan that builds a compelling, coherent narrative arc from problem to solution.

***

## Series Title \& Framing

**"The Trust Gap: Why AI's Economic Promise Is Stalling — And What It Will Take to Unlock It"**

The series follows a classic narrative arc: *establish the stakes → diagnose the problem → deepen the diagnosis → examine the evidence → explore solutions → issue a call to action.* Each article stands alone but rewards readers who follow the whole series.

***

## Article 1: The \$15 Trillion Problem Nobody Is Talking About

**Hook:** AI promises to add \$15 trillion to the global economy. So why are 74% of the benefits flowing to just 20% of organisations — and why is adoption *flatlined* at 11% of workers for over a year?

**Core argument:** The AI productivity gap is real and growing, but the conventional explanations (regulation, skills, infrastructure) miss the root cause. The missing variable is trust — and nobody in the mainstream AI debate is quantifying what it's actually costing us.

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- PwC's 74%/20% concentration finding[^1]
- The Economist / Windfall Trust data on adoption flatlining at 11%[^2]
- £29 billion/year lost to AI verification burden in the UK alone[^3]
- \$56 billion annual productivity loss from the generational AI trust gap[^4]
- Van Geelen's "Ghost GDP" framing as the mainstream take you're going beyond[^5]

**What you add:** The question Van Geelen raises but doesn't answer — *why* doesn't AI GDP circulate? Because trust hasn't been built at the human level. Frame this as the series' central thesis.

***

## Article 2: Trust All the Way Down — The Stack Nobody Is Drawing

**Hook:** The AI trust crisis isn't just about whether you trust a chatbot. It's about whether you trust the company that built it, the government overseeing it, and the institutions deploying it. And all three are broken simultaneously.

**Core argument:** Introduce the "trust stack" as your original organising framework — AI trust sits on institutional trust, which sits on social trust. Each layer is degraded. This is why technical fixes alone won't work.

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- Edelman Trust Barometer's long-run institutional trust decline, deployed into AI context[^6]
- Only 23% of Australians trust tech companies to manage AI safely[^7]
- Only 42% trust government agencies to use AI responsibly[^8]
- Robodebt as Australia's defining case study: governance policies existed, ministerial accountability existed, none of it prevented systematic harm — because the problem was architectural, not documentary[^9]
- Your Structural Trust Framework's core insight: "organisations are trying to solve an architecture problem with a documentation solution"[^9]

**What you add:** The trust stack diagram. The Robodebt framing. The distinction between *declarative* ethics (we say we'll be responsible) and *structural* ethics (the architecture makes irresponsibility impossible). This is your most original intellectual contribution to the series.[^9]

***

## Article 3: Australia's Sceptical Adopter Problem — A Case Study in the Trust Gap

**Hook:** Australia is the world's leading nation of "sceptical adopters" — people using AI every day while remaining fundamentally distrustful of the systems behind it. That paradox is costing us \$60 billion.

**Core argument:** Use Australia as a granular, local case study that makes the global trust argument concrete and personal for your audience. Australia's trust deficit isn't just cultural — it's structurally produced by specific, fixable failures.

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- KPMG/University of Melbourne data: 50% use AI, only 36% trust it; 30% believe benefits outweigh risks — lowest globally[^10]
- Only 24% of Australians have undertaken AI training vs. 39% globally[^11]
- Only 9% have heard of the AI Safety Institute or National AI Plan[^12]
- 70% with high AI knowledge trust government to use AI responsibly vs. only 12% with no knowledge — your literacy = trust proof point[^8]
- Brooke Wright's "MLM vibes" framing as the cultural diagnosis[^13]

**What you add:** Connect the literacy gap directly to the trust gap with the government's own data. Argue that a significant chunk of Australia's trust deficit is simply a *communication and education* deficit — solvable before any major policy changes.

***

## Article 4: Trust as an Economic Multiplier — The Research Most People Haven't Read

**Hook:** Trust isn't a soft, feel-good concept. It is a hard economic variable with a measurable multiplier effect — and the research proves it.

**Core argument:** This is your most intellectually rigorous article. Draw on your own "Trust as a Multiplier" research  combined with the AI-specific economic evidence to establish a formal framework: trust reduces transaction costs *and* amplifies value creation — simultaneously.[^14]

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- Your trust multiplier framework: velocity of transactions, volume of transactions, quality of outcomes[^14]
- Deloitte: 2.5x higher AI adoption rate in high-trust organisations; 149% trust *decline* when AI is introduced into low-trust organisations[^15]
- PwC: organisations with Responsible AI frameworks are 1.7x more likely to capture AI's economic value[^1]
- The economic multiplier analogy — just as government infrastructure spending circulates through the economy creating compounding returns, trust creates an accelerated positive feedback loop[^14]
- Counter-argument engagement: Acemoglu's 1.1–1.8% GDP estimate vs. the McKinsey/PwC trillion-dollar projections — and why the gap between them is *exactly* the size of the trust deficit[^16]

**What you add:** The explicit framing of trust as the *constraint variable* in the AI productivity equation — the factor with the lowest multiplier that limits total output. This hasn't been published this way before.

***

## Article 5: What Actually Builds Trust — And What Doesn't

**Hook:** Every organisation now has an AI ethics policy. Almost none have AI trust. There's a reason for that — and it's architectural, not aspirational.

**Core argument:** Distinguish between performative trust-building (ethics statements, responsible AI principles, governance committees) and structural trust-building (explainability by design, human-in-the-loop architecture, co-design with affected communities). Only the second kind works at scale.

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- Your Structural Trust Framework: declarative vs. structural ethics; "principles alone cannot prevent anything"[^9]
- McKinsey 2026: organisations that openly report AI errors build more durable trust than those projecting infallibility[^17]
- Government transparency statements: mandatory public AI transparency statements now required of APS agencies[^18]
- The literacy finding: simply *telling* Australians what the AI Safety Institute does shifts their trust — awareness is itself a trust intervention[^12]
- JP Morgan's institutional trust-as-moat framing: organisations already trusted by customers extract far more AI value[^6]
- Engage with the counter-argument: can trust be *built*, or does it have to be *earned*? (Your answer: structurally enforced trustworthiness earns trust automatically over time)

**What you add:** The Structural Trust Framework's four conformance profiles as a practical ladder organisations can climb. This gives readers a concrete takeaway and positions your framework as a reference point.[^9]

***

## Article 6: The Trust Dividend — What a High-Trust AI Economy Could Look Like

**Hook:** What if we closed the trust gap? Not just for AI companies, but for the whole economy?

**Core argument:** This is your aspirational, forward-looking closer. Model what the economic, social, and democratic benefits would look like if Australia — and the world — moved from the current low-trust AI equilibrium to a high-trust one. Position trust-building as the highest-leverage economic policy intervention available right now.

**Key evidence to deploy:**

- The \$60 billion Australia stands to gain by 2035 if the trust gap is closed[^19]
- WEF: 78 million net new jobs created — but only if adoption is broad, which requires trust[^20]
- Your trust multiplier's compounding effect: early trust-building investments pay increasingly large dividends[^14]
- The collective intelligence dividend: high-trust AI systems enable knowledge sharing that produces exponentially more value than individual contributions[^21][^14]
- Australia's structural advantages: strong research institutions, KPMG/University of Melbourne as a global trust research hub, National AI Plan as a foundation to build on

**What you add:** A concrete "trust dividend" estimate for Australia. A call to action directed at three audiences simultaneously — policymakers (regulate structurally, not declaratively), organisations (adopt the Structural Trust Framework), and individuals (invest in AI literacy as a civic and economic act). End with the series thesis restated: trust isn't the soft layer on top of the AI economy — it *is* the AI economy.

***

## Publishing \& Sequencing Notes

- **Cadence:** Fortnightly gives you time to promote each piece and gather reader responses before publishing the next
- **Cross-promotion:** Articles 2 and 4 are your strongest LinkedIn extract candidates — the trust stack diagram and the multiplier framework are both highly shareable as standalone visuals
- **Audience hook:** Article 3 (the Australia piece) is your best lead-generation article for an Australian readership — local, data-rich, and directly relevant
- **Paywall strategy:** Articles 1–3 free (audience building), Articles 4–6 paid (depth reward for subscribers)
