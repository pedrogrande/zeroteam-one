# Problem space — Future's Edge AI products \& services

The problem is not simply that NFPs lack AI tools. That gap is where Future's Edge operates.[^3_1][^3_2]

AI compliance policies exist — governance does not. Adoption is accelerating while readiness is not [^3_3]. The result is AI that processes communities rather than serves them, and erodes the very relationship that makes the organisation's mission possible [^3_4][^3_2]. |
| **Who experiences it?** | Executive directors, heads of impact, program managers, and IT leads in NFPs, social enterprises, foundations, and mission-focused institutions globally — plus, downstream, the communities, beneficiaries, and funders who depend on those organisations to act with integrity [^3_4][^3_5]. |

## The problem statement

> **An Executive Director of a mission-driven organisation wants to harness AI to extend the reach and impact of their team, BUT when board and funder pressure triggers an AI adoption push, she finds herself navigating a landscape of abstract ethics principles, generic compliance frameworks, and commercial tools not designed for her context — with no practical way to know whether her community actually trusts how she's using AI. This problem arises because Trust Intelligence — the capacity to design AI deployments that structurally produce trust — has never been developed as a practical, accessible capability for the sector. The impacts are significant: community relationships are strained, staff raise concerns in private rather than publicly, funders ask harder questions about governance, and the organisation's mission is quietly being substituted by the efficiency it was trying to protect. - **The capability problem** — even organisations that understand the need don't have trained people to act on it → answered by the AI for Good Leadership Certificate[^3_3][^3_4]
- **The tools problem** — even well-intentioned organisations deploy off-the-shelf AI tools not designed with community trust in mind → answered by the open-source Trust-Intelligent tool suite[^3_4]

Each problem is real independently. Together they describe why the sector is stuck — and why solving all three through a coherent Trust Intelligence framework is genuinely new.[^3_7][^3_2]

# Business strategy — Future's Edge AI products \& services

*Written in Roger Martin's five-choice cascade. This is not a plan — it is an integrative set of choices with a coherent theory of winning. The angst is appropriate*.[^10_1]

***

## Winning aspiration

Martin's first question is not "what do we want to do?" but "what does winning look like for us and for the customer we serve?"[^10_2]

**For Future's Edge:** Winning means becoming the globally recognised standard for Trust Intelligence in mission-driven organisations — the name that Priya cites when her board asks how her organisation is deploying AI responsibly, the credential her funders look for, and the framework that ITU and UNESCO point to when the sector needs a benchmark.[^10_3][^10_4]

**For Priya:** Winning means she can deploy AI with genuine confidence — not because she's ticked a compliance box, but because she has designed deployments her communities can actually feel safe with. Her OrgTQ has improved measurably. Her staff raise concerns openly. Her funders see real governance, not theatre. And she's become someone her peers call when they're trying to figure this out.[^10_5][^10_3]

Winning is not "a successful consulting business." It is **Trust Intelligence becoming the lens through which the entire mission sector evaluates AI adoption — and Future's Edge having built that lens**.[^10_4][^10_3]

***

## Where to play

Martin is explicit: choosing where *not* to play is as important as choosing where to play. A strategy that tries to serve everyone serves no one.[^10_1]

**We are choosing to play here:**

- **Customer segment:** Executive directors, heads of impact, and program leaders in global NFPs, social enterprises, and foundations — organisations whose primary asset is the trust of the communities they serve, for whom an AI misstep is not just a reputational risk but a mission-critical failure[^10_3][^10_5]
- **Geography:** Global from the start — digital-first delivery makes geography a distribution choice, not a constraint. Priority markets: Asia-Pacific (home market, sophisticated NFP sector), East Africa and South Asia (large, globally-connected NFP sectors with significant AI adoption and minimal governance infrastructure), Western Europe (regulatory urgency from the EU AI Act), and North America (large sector, US regulatory vacuum creating independent framework demand)[^10_6][^10_3]
- **The problem space:** The gap between abstract AI ethics principles and practical, felt community trust — not AI implementation generally, not data privacy compliance specifically, but the specific Trust Intelligence layer that no other offering addresses[^10_4][^10_3]
- **The moment:** The category-definition window — right now, before ISO/IEC 42001 and sector-specific frameworks consolidate around a standard that doesn't centre community trust[^10_7][^10_6]

**We are explicitly choosing not to play here:**

- General AI consulting for commercial organisations (different trust dynamics, crowded market, no mission alignment)
- AI implementation and technical build (different capability set, not our moat)
- Government and public sector as a primary market (long sales cycles, procurement constraints — adjacent market for later, not now)
- Organisations whose primary AI governance motivation is compliance rather than genuine community trust (Slovic's asymmetry risk; the wrong clients damage the brand)[^10_5][^10_4]

***

## How to win

This is the theory. Martin says it must be coherent, specific, and focused on being better than anyone else at serving the chosen customer — not just different.[^10_1]

**The theory of winning:**

Mission-driven organisations live or die by the trust of their communities. AI deployed without Trust Intelligence erodes that trust — often invisibly, until a community relationship breaks and the damage is done. No existing offering addresses this at the right layer: general consultancies have generic frameworks; tech platforms have compliance tools; academics have research but no practical delivery. The gap between "abstract principles" and "what a food bank needs to do on Tuesday morning" is entirely unoccupied.[^10_3]

Future's Edge wins by occupying that gap with a proprietary, research-grounded Trust Intelligence framework that:

1. **Starts where Priya actually is** — the free OrgTQ Diagnostic gives her language for a problem she's already felt, before any commercial relationship begins. Trust is extended first; that is itself the Trust Intelligence methodology in action[^10_4][^10_3]
2. **Operates at the right level of depth** — not compliance documentation but structural trust architecture: the capacity to design AI deployments that communities can genuinely feel safe with[^10_4]
3. **Builds capability, not dependency** — every product in the portfolio makes Priya more capable, not more reliant on Future's Edge. This is the inverse of the consulting model that Priya already distrusts[^10_5][^10_3]
4. **Is verifiably non-generic** — the blockchain-verified TQ credential and the OrgTQ benchmark data are proprietary, earned, and impossible to fake. No large consultancy can replicate this without replicating the underlying research, methodology, and mission-driven culture[^10_4]
5. **Compounds through the peer network** — Priya's referral is the most powerful distribution mechanism in this sector. The strategy wins not through advertising but through the quality of outcomes for early clients[^10_3][^10_5]

**The competitive advantage is not lower cost, not broader reach, and not more products. It is the depth and specificity of Trust Intelligence applied to a sector that no competitor has genuinely served.** That is differentiation that large consultancies cannot buy and technology platforms cannot replicate.[^10_1][^10_3]

## Capabilities required

Martin's third choice: what must we be genuinely excellent at to execute the where-to-play and how-to-win choices? Not everything — only what the strategy requires.[^10_2]

**The non-negotiable capabilities:**

- **Trust Intelligence framework development and maintenance** — the ongoing research, iteration, and publication of the TI framework as a living, credible, sector-current body of knowledge. This is the intellectual engine. It must stay ahead of AI capability advancement and ahead of the compliance floor[^10_4]
- **Design thinking-led client practice** — the ability to enter a mission-sector organisation, empathise with its specific community trust context, and design a Trust Intelligence pathway that is genuinely applicable on Tuesday morning. This is a human capability, not a templatable process[^10_8][^10_4]
- **The OrgTQ Assessment instrument** — a psychometrically credible, sector-validated diagnostic that produces honest, specific, actionable output. The quality of this instrument is the quality of the first impression with every Priya who takes it[^10_3]
- **TI practitioner development** — the capacity to develop, certify, and maintain a global network of practitioners with genuine sector understanding and verified TQ. This is both the delivery capability and the distribution network[^10_3][^10_4]
- **Open-source tool development and community governance** — the ability to build, release, and maintain trust-intelligent AI tools through an open contributor community. The community governance model is itself a Trust Intelligence demonstration[^10_9][^10_3]
- **Sector credibility infrastructure** — published research, OrgTQ benchmark reports, peer-reviewed academic partnerships, and the ITU/UNESCO co-endorsement pathway. This is what makes Priya trust Future's Edge before she knows it[^10_5][^10_3]

**What we do not need to be excellent at:**
Large-scale direct sales, proprietary technology platform development, or broad-based marketing. The strategy wins through framework authority, peer referral, and the freemium diagnostic funnel — not through commercial infrastructure.[^10_1][^10_3]

## Management systems

Martin's fourth choice: what systems must be in place to build and maintain the capabilities, and to measure whether the strategy is working?[^10_2]

**The systems that matter:**

- **Client intake and quality gate** — a formal process using OrgTQ results and a values-alignment conversation as prerequisites before any paid engagement. This is not a sales filter; it is a reputation protection mechanism and a brand integrity system[^10_5][^10_4]
- **OrgTQ data governance** — the system for collecting, anonymising, aggregating, and publishing OrgTQ benchmark data. This converts every diagnostic taken into a sector intelligence asset while respecting the privacy of participating organisations[^10_3]
- **TI practitioner certification and quality standards** — the development pathway (AI for Good Leadership Certificate Levels 1–4), the TQ threshold for client-facing roles, and the ongoing CPD system for maintaining practitioner quality as AI capability evolves[^10_4][^10_3]
- **Framework iteration cadence** — a structured annual review of the Trust Intelligence framework against new AI developments, regulatory changes, and OrgTQ benchmark findings. Strategy is a journey; the framework must travel with it[^10_1][^10_4]
- **Revenue and impact measurement** — tracking not just commercial metrics (revenue, clients, certificates issued) but the Trust Intelligence outcomes that the strategy exists to produce: measurable OrgTQ improvement in client organisations, community trust scores, staff psychological safety indicators, and funder satisfaction[^10_2][^10_3]

# We'll come back to the go-to-market strategy later. Are there any other tools or frameworks we could apply to give ourselves the best chance of success?

Good context on where Future's Edge has been. Now I have enough to give a well-considered, curated answer — not a list dump, but a genuine recommendation of the most valuable frameworks, chosen specifically for where Future's Edge is right now and what the strategy needs next.

***

Given everything we've built across this session — the PESTEL, SWOT, TOWS, Martin's five-choice strategy, Five Forces, and brand strategy — here is an honest assessment of the frameworks that would add the most value now, and why.

***

## The most useful frameworks from here

The key question isn't "what other frameworks exist?" It's "what are the remaining gaps in our strategic thinking, and what's the best tool for each one?"[^13_1][^13_2]

Five gaps remain:

1. **We haven't validated our assumptions with real people yet** — the strategy is internally coherent but still a hypothesis
2. **We don't have a clear picture of what Priya experiences along her journey with us** — our touchpoints aren't mapped
3. **We haven't made our business model concrete** — revenue streams, costs, and unit economics are still abstract
4. **We don't have a way to measure whether we're winning** — success is defined but not operationalised
5. **We haven't thought carefully about how we build and sequence what we need to build** — capability development has no roadmap

Here is the right tool for each gap:[^13_3][^13_4]

***

## 1. Jobs to be done (for assumption validation)

Clayton Christensen's Jobs to be Done framework reframes the question from "what does Priya want?" to "what job is Priya hiring this product to do?" The distinction matters because it surfaces the *real* motivation — which is often not what either the customer or the provider initially articulates.[^13_5][^13_6]

For Future's Edge, the risk is that the OrgTQ Diagnostic and TI framework are solving the job we *think* Priya has, rather than the job she *actually* has. The JTBD interview protocol — asking Priya about the moment she first felt the problem, what she tried before, what she wishes existed — produces qualitative insight that no amount of desk research can replicate.[^13_7][^13_5]

**What to do:** Run five to eight JTBD interviews with real Priyas before finalising the OrgTQ instrument and the product packaging. The interviews will almost certainly surface at least one assumption in the current strategy that needs adjusting. This is the design thinking empathy phase in practice — not as a principle, but as a scheduled activity.[^13_8][^13_5]

***

## 2. Customer journey mapping (for experience design)

The brand strategy defined how Future's Edge should feel at every touchpoint — but we haven't yet mapped what those touchpoints actually are, in sequence, from Priya's perspective. A customer journey map traces Priya's experience from first awareness through to becoming a practitioner and peer advocate, surfacing the emotional highs, lows, and friction points at each stage.[^13_4][^13_5]

This matters specifically because the strategy depends on peer referral as the primary distribution mechanism — and peer referral only happens if the experience is consistently excellent at every stage, not just at delivery. A journey map will identify where the experience currently has gaps between the brand promise and the actual touchpoint reality.[^13_9][^13_5]

**What to do:** Map the journey across five stages — *unaware, aware, evaluating, engaged, advocating* — and for each stage ask: what is Priya doing, thinking, and feeling? What is Future's Edge providing? Where is the gap? This becomes the brief for every piece of content, tool, and process in the go-to-market strategy.[^13_4][^13_5]

***

## 3. Business model canvas (for making the model concrete)

The business model canvas is already in the toolkit. It's time to fill it in — not as a planning exercise, but as a rigorous test of internal coherence. The canvas forces the question: do our revenue streams actually cover our cost structure? Are the key resources and activities we've identified truly the ones the value proposition requires?[^13_10][^13_3]

The specific tension to resolve is the freemium OrgTQ entry point versus the revenue model. Giving the diagnostic away for free is strategically correct — but it requires a clear, credible path from free to paid, and a cost structure that can sustain the free tier long enough for that conversion to happen.[^13_10][^13_9]

**What to do:** Complete the BMC with numbers attached — estimated cost per free OrgTQ completion, estimated conversion rate to paid engagement, average revenue per paid client, and the grant/foundation revenue required to fund the gap in the early stages. This is where strategy meets financial reality.[^13_3][^13_10]

***

## 4. OKRs (objectives and key results — for measuring whether we're winning)

Roger Martin is clear: strategy is a theory, and you need a mechanism to watch it unfold and tweak it as you go. OKRs — the goal-setting system developed at Intel and refined at Google — are the right tool for this. They translate the strategy's winning aspiration and logic into a small number of measurable, time-bound outcomes, with a clear distinction between the *objective* (the qualitative direction) and the *key results* (the specific, measurable evidence that you're moving in that direction).[^13_2][^13_1]

For Future's Edge, the OKRs should be structured around the assumptions in Martin's logic table — because those are the things you most need to watch. If the OrgTQ-to-paid conversion rate is a critical assumption, it should be a key result. If peer referral is the primary distribution mechanism, referral source tracking should be a key result.[^13_1][^13_2]

**A starting set of OKRs for Year 1:**


| Objective | Key results |
| :-- | :-- |
| Establish the Trust Intelligence category | 1. TI framework published as open citeable document; 2. 3+ sector publications reference TI framework; 3. ITU/UNESCO partnership conversation initiated |
| Prove the OrgTQ Diagnostic creates genuine value | 1. 100 diagnostics completed; 2. ≥70% of completers describe the experience as "honest and specific"; 3. ≥20% conversion from diagnostic to paid engagement |
| Build a referenceable client base | 1. 10 paying clients in Year 1; 2. ≥8/10 average Net Promoter Score; 3. ≥5 clients agree to be public case studies |
| Validate the peer referral distribution model | 1. ≥50% of new clients referred by existing clients or OrgTQ completers; 2. 3+ sector conference speaking invitations generated through peer network |

[^13_2][^13_9][^13_1]

***

## 5. A lean startup build-measure-learn cycle (for sequencing what to build)

Eric Ries' lean startup methodology is particularly relevant here because of the specific risk Future's Edge faces: building too much before validating the assumptions. The TI framework, OrgTQ Diagnostic, training pathway, credential infrastructure, and open-source tools are a large build — and the strategy depends on several assumptions that haven't been tested yet.[^13_11][^13_9][^13_1]

The lean approach says: identify the riskiest assumption, design the smallest possible experiment that could prove or disprove it, measure the result, and learn before building the next thing. For Future's Edge, the riskiest assumption is probably this one: *Priya will find the OrgTQ Diagnostic honest and specific enough to trust Future's Edge with a paid engagement*. Everything else depends on this being true.[^13_5][^13_9]

**The minimum viable sequence:**

1. **Build:** A lightweight version of the OrgTQ Diagnostic (even a structured interview protocol, before the full instrument is built)
2. **Measure:** Run it with 10 real Priyas; track honesty, specificity, and relevance ratings
3. **Learn:** What dimensions of TQ resonate most? What language does Priya use that Future's Edge doesn't? What's missing?
4. **Then:** Build the full instrument incorporating those findings — before building the training pathway or credential infrastructure[^13_9][^13_1][^13_5]

***

## What to save for later

Two well-known frameworks are *not* the right priority right now:

- **Balanced Scorecard** — powerful for managing a mature organisation across multiple strategic perspectives, but premature at this stage. OKRs are lighter, faster, and more appropriate for a startup still testing assumptions[^13_1]
- **Ansoff Matrix** (market penetration / development / product development / diversification) — useful for growth strategy decisions once the core offering is validated, but applying it now would be premature scaling before product-market fit is confirmed[^13_2][^13_1]

The honest answer is that the most valuable thing Future's Edge can do right now is less framework and more fieldwork — getting the OrgTQ in front of real Priyas, running JTBD interviews, and letting the evidence shape the assumptions before the strategy locks in.[^13_5][^13_1]

# I love this.

What are the top pains and gains that we should really focus on as we build our products?

Great question — and a discipline worth applying before building anything. Not all pains and gains carry equal weight. The ones worth focusing on are those that are **felt most intensely**, **occur most frequently**, and where Future's Edge has a **genuine, defensible advantage** in addressing them.[^15_1][^15_2]

Here's the honest prioritisation:

***

## Top pains to focus on

**1. The fear of invisible community harm** *(Pain \#1)*

This is the deepest, most emotionally resonant pain — and the one most unique to Priya's context. A commercial business that deploys AI badly loses customers. Priya loses the trust of communities who are often already marginalised and who trusted her organisation specifically because of its mission. This is existential, not operational. No other offering addresses it at this depth, which makes it Future's Edge's single most important pain to design against.[^15_3][^15_4][^15_5]

**2. "This wasn't built for me"** *(Pain \#2)*

Priya has already tried the generic alternatives and found them wanting. This pain is felt every time she opens a framework that talks about shareholder value, or sits in a webinar pitched at tech companies. It creates immediate disengagement. The moment Future's Edge's language, examples, and framing feel unmistakably sector-specific, this pain dissolves — and trust begins.[^15_5][^15_3]

**3. Staff using AI without governance** *(Pain \#5)*

This is the most urgent operational pain — it's happening right now in Priya's organisation, whether she knows it or not. It creates real risk today, not hypothetically. Products that give staff a practical framework to evaluate and use AI tools themselves solve a live problem, not a future one. It also gives Priya something to deploy immediately, which accelerates time-to-value.[^15_4][^15_3][^15_5]

**4. Not being able to answer the board / funder governance question confidently** *(Pain \#3 \& \#6)*

These two pains share the same root: Priya is being asked to demonstrate something she doesn't yet have the language or evidence for. This is the pain the OrgTQ Diagnostic solves most directly — it gives her a specific, honest, communicable answer. It also has a compounding quality: every board meeting or funder conversation where she can't answer well erodes her confidence and her credibility.[^15_3][^15_5]

***

## Top gains to focus on

**1. Language for the problem she's already feeling** *(Gain \#6)*

This is the highest-leverage gain at the very beginning of the customer journey — before any product is purchased. The moment Priya encounters the term "Trust Intelligence" and the five-dimension TQ model and thinks *"that's exactly what I've been trying to say"* — the brand has won. This gain costs almost nothing to deliver (it's the framework, the diagnostic language, the website copy) and creates the strongest possible first impression.[^15_4][^15_5]

**2. Measurable evidence of genuine progress** *(Gain \#5)*

Priya works in a sector where impact is notoriously hard to measure. A trackable OrgTQ score that moves over time is genuinely rare — and genuinely valuable. It gives her something to show her board, her funders, and herself that the work is real and compounding. Build the measurement infrastructure well and this gain becomes one of the strongest retention and referral drivers in the whole product suite.[^15_5][^15_4]

**3. Being the person who brought Trust Intelligence to their network** *(Gain \#4)*

Priya's social and professional identity is deeply tied to being a thoughtful, forward-thinking leader. The open TI framework — something she can share freely with peers — gives her a way to express that identity actively. This is not just a nice-to-have; it is the primary distribution mechanism for the business. Design specifically for shareability.[^15_3][^15_5]

**4. Honest board / funder conversations** *(Gain \#1)*

The positive version of Pain \#3 and \#6 above. When Priya can sit in front of her board and say *"we've designed this with our communities' trust at the centre"* — and mean it, and prove it — that's a profound professional and personal win. It validates why she came to this work in the first place. Products that deliver this gain earn deep loyalty.[^15_5][^15_3]

## The right sequence matters

Yes — **do the customer journey map first**, then build the product strategy from it. Here's why the order matters: a product strategy built before the journey is mapped tends to organise around what you *have* rather than around what Priya *experiences*. The journey map forces the product strategy to answer a different, better question: not "what should we build?" but "where in Priya's experience is the most important gap, and what's the minimum product that fills it?"[^17_1][^17_2][^17_3]

The assumptions analysis we just completed reinforces this — our highest-priority untested assumptions (C1, C2, C4) are all about what Priya does and feels *before* she becomes a paying client. The journey map is precisely where those assumptions get surfaced and challenged.[^17_3][^17_4]

***

## The product strategy frameworks worth knowing

There are several strong frameworks. Here's an honest assessment of which ones are most useful for where Future's Edge is right now:

### 1. Jobs to be done + opportunity solution tree (highest value right now)

Teresa Torres' **Opportunity Solution Tree** is the most practical product strategy framework for an early-stage business with a strong mission but untested product-market fit. It starts with the desired outcome (what does winning look like for Priya?), maps the opportunity space (what jobs, pains, and gains create the opening?), then branches into solutions and experiments. It prevents the most common early-stage product mistake — jumping to solutions before the opportunity space is properly understood. Given that C1, C3, and C4 are our highest-risk assumptions, this is exactly the discipline we need.[^17_4][^17_5][^17_1][^17_3]

### 2. The Kano model (for prioritising features)

The **Kano model** distinguishes between three types of product features: *basic expectations* (must-haves that cause dissatisfaction if absent but don't delight if present), *performance features* (more = better), and *delight features* (unexpected, create disproportionate satisfaction). For Future's Edge, this framework will be particularly valuable when designing the OrgTQ instrument and the training pathway — identifying which elements Priya takes for granted, which she'll pay more for, and which will generate the peer referrals the distribution strategy depends on.[^17_2][^17_6]

### 3. Product-market fit canvas (for validating before building)

Rahul Vohra's **Product-Market Fit canvas** (built on Sean Ellis' "very disappointed" test) is a lightweight validation tool that asks: how would Priya feel if Future's Edge's product no longer existed? If less than 40% say "very disappointed," product-market fit hasn't been achieved and building more is the wrong move. Given our lean startup recommendation and the urgency in the assumptions analysis, this is the right validation test to run after the first 20 OrgTQ diagnostics.[^17_7][^17_3]

### 4. The Strategyzer value proposition canvas → product portfolio map (already started)

We've already completed the VPC — the natural next move is to map each pain, gain, and job directly to a specific product or feature, then ask: which products create the *most* fit for the *highest-priority* pains and gains? This becomes the product portfolio prioritisation tool.[^17_5][^17_6][^17_1]

### 5. Now / next / later roadmap (for communicating product sequence)

Once the portfolio is prioritised, the **Now / Next / Later roadmap** (preferred over a date-based Gantt) communicates what's being built and why, without locking the team into a plan that the first customer conversation will probably change. It's honest about uncertainty in a way that date-driven roadmaps aren't — which is itself a Trust Intelligence behaviour.[^17_3][^17_7]

***

## The recommended sequence

Given everything we've built, here is the order that will give the product strategy the best possible foundation:

1. **Customer journey map** — first, always. Maps Priya's full experience from unaware to advocate; surfaces the emotional high and low points; identifies where the biggest gaps are between her experience and the brand promise[^17_8][^17_2]
2. **Opportunity solution tree** — takes the journey map's gap moments and turns them into a structured opportunity space before any solution is proposed[^17_1][^17_3]
3. **VPC → product portfolio map** — connects each pain and gain from the VPC to a specific product or feature, and scores each product on pain/gain fit[^17_5][^17_1]
4. **Kano analysis** — for each prioritised product, distinguishes must-haves from delighters to guide feature decisions[^17_6][^17_2]
5. **Now / next / later roadmap** — the output: a sequenced, assumption-prioritised build plan that everyone on the team can navigate by[^17_7][^17_3]

This doesn't make "Multiply your impact" wrong. It means the **sequencing matters enormously**:

- Fear-based messaging is what gets Priya's attention and brings her to the door
- Aspiration-based messaging is what makes her *stay*, *invest*, and *tell her peers*

The risk of leading with "Multiply your impact" is that it doesn't resonate with the unaware or evaluating Priya — she's not yet thinking about multiplying impact, she's thinking about not making a catastrophic mistake. The risk of *not* using it is that the brand feels heavy and anxiety-driven, which won't generate the pride and advocacy you need at Stage 6.[^19_7][^19_2]

***

## The recommendation: use both, intentionally sequenced

Rather than choosing between fear and aspiration, design the messaging architecture to mirror Priya's actual emotional journey:[^19_2][^19_7]


| Journey stage | Messaging register | Example |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Unaware → Aware | Name the fear with precision | *"AI deployed without Trust Intelligence erodes the one thing your organisation lives or dies by."* |
| Evaluating → First value | Offer relief and clarity | *"Find out exactly where your organisation's Trust Intelligence stands — honestly, specifically, free."* |
| Engaged | Build confidence | *"Every step builds the capability your communities deserve."* |
| Advocate | Amplify the aspiration | *"**Multiply your impact.** Trust is the one investment that compounds."* |

"Multiply your impact" earns its place as the **brand's aspirational north star** — the emotional destination of the journey — rather than the acquisition hook. It's what Priya feels when she looks back at the journey from Stage 6, not what gets her to take the OrgTQ at Stage 3.[^19_3][^19_1][^19_2]

Think of it the way Brené Brown uses vulnerability research — she leads with the pain of disconnection and shame to get attention, then the aspiration of wholehearted living is what people carry away and share with others. The fear opens the door; the aspiration is what people quote to their friends.[^19_7][^19_2]

***

## Does earlier work need refactoring?

**Mostly no — and here's why.** The strategic architecture we've built is actually already structured around the positive frame. The Trust Intelligence framework leads with capacity and growth, not with risk and compliance. The brand archetype (Sage + Caregiver) is forward-looking and empowering, not fear-mongering. The value proposition canvas is balanced between pains and gains.[^19_5][^19_6][^19_8][^19_1]

**Two things are worth updating:**

1. **The brand strategy** — add "Multiply your impact" explicitly as the brand's aspirational headline, positioned as the emotional destination of the customer journey rather than the acquisition message. It belongs in the positioning and growth sections[^19_7]
2. **The customer journey map** — the Stage 6 (Advocate) emotional state should carry "Multiply your impact" as its resonant phrase. It's the feeling Priya has when she's referring peers and seeing her OrgTQ improve — she is, quite literally, multiplying her organisation's impact through Trust Intelligence[^19_3][^19_2]

**One thing to keep as-is:**

The pain-led language in the awareness and evaluation stages. "Multiply your impact" won't get Priya to take the OrgTQ Diagnostic — *"find out whether your communities actually trust your AI use"* will. Keep the honest tension visible at the front of the journey.[^19_4][^19_2]

***

## The refined brand message architecture

> **Acquisition:** *"Does your community actually trust how you use AI?"*
> **Value delivery:** *"Practical, honest Trust Intelligence — built for organisations like yours."*
> **Aspiration / north star:** **"Multiply your impact. Trust is the one investment that compounds."**

This is a stronger position than either message alone — because it honours both where Priya is when she first encounters Future's Edge, and where she arrives after the journey has done its work.

## Opportunity 1: Priya recognises "Trust Intelligence" as her problem, named precisely

*The category recognition moment — Stage 2→3 in the journey map*

This opportunity is upstream of everything. If the language doesn't land as recognition, Priya never reaches the diagnostic.[^21_4][^21_2]

### Why this opportunity exists

Priya has been feeling an unformed, nameless anxiety about AI and community trust. She has encountered generic "AI ethics" content and found it either irrelevant or corporate. | Solution | What it is | Experiment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **S1.1 — Open TI framework publication** | Publish the five-dimension Trust Intelligence framework as a free, citable, sector-specific document — written entirely in NFP language, with NFP examples | Share with 10 Priyas cold; measure whether they use the term "Trust Intelligence" unprompted in follow-up conversation within 2 weeks |
| **S1.2 — Category-defining content** | A short series of sector-specific pieces (articles, LinkedIn posts, one webinar) that use real NFP AI scenarios to introduce TI language — not thought leadership, but *recognition content* | Publish 3 pieces; track whether readers share them with peers using their own words, not Future's Edge's |

## Opportunity 2: Priya trusts Future's Edge enough to take the free OrgTQ diagnostic

*The diagnostic decision moment — Stage 3→4 in the journey map. This is assumption C2, our second-highest priority*

### Why this opportunity exists

Priya is sceptical of new frameworks and consultants. Her scepticism is protective — she has been disappointed before. The barrier to taking the OrgTQ is not effort (it's free and takes 15–20 minutes); it's trust that the output will be worth the vulnerability of honest self-assessment, and that she won't immediately be sold to.[^21_5][^21_2][^21_1]


| Solution | What it is | Experiment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **S2.1 — Radical transparency on the landing page** | A diagnostic landing page that explicitly states: what the diagnostic covers, what the output looks like (show a real sample), what happens after she completes it, and that there is no automatic sales follow-up | A/B test: landing page with sample output shown vs. without — measure completion rate |
| **S2.2 — Peer-referred entry path** | Design the diagnostic to be easily shared peer-to-peer, with a specific referral mechanism (*"[Peer's name] thought you'd find this useful"*) that pre-loads trust via the referring relationship | Track completion rates: peer-referred vs. organic. Hypothesis: peer-referred completions will be 2–3× higher |
| **S2.3 — No-gate entry** | Remove all data gates before the diagnostic — no email required to start, only at the end to receive the report. ## Opportunity 3: The OrgTQ output feels honest, specific, and worth acting on

*The diagnostic output moment — Stage 4. This is assumption C3, and the gateway to assumption C4 (conversion to paid)*

### Why this opportunity exists

This is the most consequential product design challenge in the entire portfolio. A generic, vague, or over-positive output destroys trust immediately and irreversibly. | Solution | What it is | Experiment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **S3.1 — Dimension-specific scoring, not a single aggregate** | The output shows Priya's organisation's score across all five TQ dimensions separately (community trust, staff trust, data trust, governance trust, mission trust) — with a specific plain-language interpretation for each gap | Prototype with 5 real Priyas; ask them to rate each dimension output on "honesty" and "specificity" out of 10. Target: ≥8/10 on both |
| **S3.2 — Sector-specific consequence language** | Each gap is described in terms of its real-world consequence for an NFP — not generic risk language (*"your community may be complying rather than genuinely trusting — which means they're less likely to share the information you actually need to serve them well"*) | Test two versions of gap language — generic vs. sector-specific — with separate cohorts of 5 Priyas; compare "this describes my organisation" ratings |

## On interviewing your Priyas — this is the most valuable thing you can do right now

Your instinct to run the JTBD interviews first, *then* go through the assessment with them, is exactly the right sequence — and here's why the order matters:[^22_6][^22_3]

**If you show them the assessment first**, their answers to your problem questions will be contaminated by the framework you've just shown them. They'll use your language rather than their own.

**If you ask the problem questions first**, you capture their unfiltered language — which is the most valuable raw material in the whole project. The words Priya uses to describe her problem, unprompted, are the words that should appear on the landing page, in the report, and in the recognition content.[^22_7][^22_3]

Here is a suggested interview structure, designed to get the most useful signal from each conversation:

***

### Suggested JTBD interview guide

**Part 1 — The problem in their words** *(20 minutes, before showing anything)*

These questions follow the JTBD protocol — ask about real past experiences, not hypothetical opinions:[^22_6][^22_3]

1. *"Tell me about a moment in the last 12 months when you felt genuinely uncertain about how your organisation was using AI — what was happening?"*
2. *"What did you do about it? What did you try?"*
3. *"What did you wish existed that you couldn't find?"*
4. *"When you think about your communities and AI — what's the thing that keeps you up at night, if anything?"*
5. *"How would you describe this challenge to a peer — what words do you actually use?"*
6. *"Who else in your organisation feels this? Is it just you, or is it a shared concern?"*
7. *"If this problem were completely solved — what would that look like? How would you know?"*

**Part 2 — The assessment walkthrough** *(20–30 minutes)*

Go through the OrgTQ together — but observe as much as you listen:[^22_3]

- Watch where she hesitates or asks a clarifying question — those are instrument design problems
- Watch where she nods or says *"yes, exactly"* — those are the dimensions that resonate most
- After each dimension, ask: *"Does this feel like it's describing something real for your organisation?"*
- After the full report: *"What surprised you? What did you already know? What's missing?"*
- The critical question: *"If you received this report on your own, without me here — what would you do next?"*

**Part 3 — The sharing question** *(5 minutes)*

- *"Is there anyone in your network you'd want to share this with? What would you say to them?"*
- *"If you were going to recommend this to a peer, how would you describe it?"*

The answer to that last question is your word-of-mouth marketing, verbatim.[^22_2][^22_7][^22_3]
