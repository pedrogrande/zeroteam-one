

# What steps can build trust in AI among Australians

Research and government policy converge on four core pathways to building trust in AI among Australians, grounded in what Australians themselves say they need.

## What Australians Say They Need

The KPMG/University of Melbourne study is direct on this: **83% of Australians say they would be more willing to trust AI if specific assurances were in place**, including:[^1]

- Adherence to international AI standards
- Responsible AI governance practices
- Visible monitoring of system accuracy
- Government and regulator oversight (supported by 80%)[^1]


## 1. Regulation \& Clear Rules

This is the single most cited trust-builder:[^2][^1]

- **77% of Australians** believe regulation is necessary, and 76% want international laws specifically
- Only 30% believe current laws are adequate — so action, not rhetoric, is what's needed
- Policy recommendations include transparency frameworks for automated decisions, electoral integrity safeguards, and expanding Australia's AI Safety Institute[^2]
- The Government's updated **Policy for the Responsible Use of AI in Government** (effective December 2025) introduces mandatory agency transparency statements, accountability measures, and a new AI Review Committee[^3][^4]


## 2. Transparency \& Visible Accountability

Australians distrust what they can't see — so making AI use visible is critical:[^5][^6]

- Government agencies are now required to publish **public AI transparency statements** explaining *why* they use AI, *how* it is governed, and *how* citizens can challenge decisions[^5]
- Making AI-generated content labelled and identifiable addresses deepfake-driven fear directly
- Businesses should visibly explain where AI is used in customer interactions and provide a clear human-in-the-loop option and appeals pathway[^6]


## 3. AI Literacy \& Education

Low AI knowledge is a root cause, and Australians are significantly under-educated compared to global peers:[^1]

- Only 24% of Australians have undertaken any AI training (vs. 39% globally) — closing this gap is foundational[^1]
- The **National AI Plan** (released December 2025) commits to workforce AI literacy programs and APS-wide capability uplift[^7]
- Organisations should implement role-based AI training and short internal AI policies — not just top-level briefings, but practical, task-specific guidance[^6]
- Critically, Minderoo Foundation research found that **when Australians are simply informed** about the AI Safety Institute and National AI Plan's intentions, most shift to believing they could be effective — meaning *awareness itself* moves the needle[^8]


## 4. Demonstrated, People-First Benefits

The motivational pathway matters too — trust grows when people see AI working for them, not just for productivity metrics:[^9]

- The APS AI Plan frames its approach explicitly as **"people-first"** — ensuring AI improves public service delivery in ways Australians can directly experience[^3]
- Highlighting genuine community benefits (faster healthcare, better accessibility, smarter services) rather than abstract economic projections shifts public perception
- Avoiding the "MLM vibes" problem means co-designing AI tools *with* communities rather than deploying them *at* them[^10]


## The Communication Gap

Perhaps the most actionable near-term finding: only **9% of Australians** have heard of the AI Safety Institute or the National AI Plan — yet most respond positively once they learn what these initiatives do. A large part of Australia's trust deficit is simply an **awareness and communication deficit**, meaning consistent, plain-language public messaging could shift sentiment meaningfully before any major policy changes take full effect.[^8]
