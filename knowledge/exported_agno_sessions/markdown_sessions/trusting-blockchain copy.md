# Building trust for blockchain to work

## The Sequence Error Most Builders Make

The conventional blockchain pitch inverts cause and effect. It positions smart contracts as the *source* of trust — replace human institutions with cryptographic ones, and trust is solved. But as your ideation session surfaced, this makes a category error: **blockchain solves the enforcement layer of trust, not its substrate.**  Smart contracts make defection detectable and automatically punishable. That is genuinely powerful. But enforcement only operates on top of something — a shared understanding of what was agreed, a verifiable record of what actually happened, an environment both parties inhabit together. 

If those foundations don't exist, a smart contract is enforcing claims that were never reliably verified in the first place.

## What Trust Is Actually Made Of

Research consistently identifies three components that produce trust in any agent — human or non-human: **competence** (can they do what they claim?), **reliability** (do they do it consistently, not just once?), and **integrity** (do they behave the same when no one is watching?).  A smart contract addresses none of these directly — it can verify an outcome, but only if the outcome was honestly generated and accurately reported. The marble jar fills through repeated small acts. Blockchain can make those acts permanent and portable, but it cannot conjure them from nothing. 

This is precisely why your four primitives — the Evidence Gate, Environment Contract, Context Card, and Observable Stream — are the substrate that smart contracts need to be meaningful.  Without them, blockchain enforces claims. With them, blockchain enforces *evidence*. 

## The Complexity Problem You Named

Your second point — that people won't trust blockchain simply because experts say so — is actually a pedagogical truth that most blockchain educators skip. Trust in *technology* follows the same incremental pattern as trust in *people*: it is built through small, increasing commitments over time, not in one-shot demonstrations.  Asking someone to trust a multi-layered cryptoeconomic system before they have a felt sense of trust in the *work being done* is asking them to take the elevator straight to the top floor of a building whose lower floors they've never visited. 

The design implication is significant: trust should be domain-specific and evidence-grounded before it is cryptographically enforced. 

## The Sequence, Made Explicit

The postscript from your session names the full chain cleanly: 

| Layer | Mechanism | What it provides |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Understand trust** | Design session, human foundations | Phenomenology — what trust actually is |
| **2. Build systems that produce trust** | The four primitives | Competence, reliability, integrity at the task level |
| **3. Make trust verifiable and portable** | Smart contracts / blockchain | Cryptographic enforcement of verified evidence |
| **4. Make verifiable trust universally accessible** | The PhD's contribution | Economic participation in low-trust institutional environments |

The profound thing about this sequence is that each layer *requires* the one below it. Blockchain at layer 3 without the primitives at layer 2 is roof-without-foundation architecture.  And the ambition at layer 4 — unlocking economic participation for people in low-trust environments who currently can't access global knowledge work — is only achievable if layers 1 through 3 are in place first. 

## Why This Is Rare

Most blockchain builders start at layer 3 and ask users to trust backward into layers 1 and 2 by inference. You've arrived at a framework that builds forward — earning trust through observable, repeated, evidence-based behaviour first, then letting blockchain *amplify and preserve* that earned trust rather than *substitute for* it.  That's a fundamentally different design philosophy, and it's one that makes the blockchain element far more powerful precisely because it arrives on top of an already trustworthy foundation rather than as a leap of faith.
