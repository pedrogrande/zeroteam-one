# What are popular or common methods to examine, design or optimise workflows?

Here is a comprehensive overview of the most popular methods for examining, designing, and optimising workflows, grouped by category.

***

## Visualisation \& Mapping Techniques

These methods help you understand *how* a workflow currently operates before making changes.

- **Flowcharts** — Simple diagrams using standardised symbols to represent tasks, decisions, and flow direction; ideal for a quick overview of process structure[^1_1]
- **Business Process Mapping** — A more detailed form of flowcharting that documents relationships between tasks, subprocesses, resources, and information; highlights redundancies and inefficiencies[^1_1]
- **Value Stream Mapping (VSM)** — A lean management technique that distinguishes value-adding from non-value-adding steps, helping you eliminate waste across the whole process[^1_1]
- **Swimlane Diagrams** — Organise tasks into "lanes" by role, team, or department, making handoffs, dependencies, and coordination gaps visible[^1_1]
- **BPMN (Business Process Model and Notation)** — A standardised, universally readable notation used by both business analysts and IT teams for detailed process documentation[^1_2]
- **SIPOC Diagrams** — A high-level map of Supplier → Input → Process → Output → Customer; great for quickly scoping a process end-to-end[^1_2]

***

## Data-Driven Analysis Methods

Used to measure and diagnose problems objectively.

- **Process Mining** — Analyses event logs from ERP, CRM, or BPM systems to automatically discover actual workflows, detect bottlenecks, and identify deviations from expected behaviour[^1_1]
- **Time and Motion Studies** — Observers measure the time taken for each task, pinpointing inefficiencies in labour-intensive or manual processes[^1_1]
- **Work Sampling** — A statistical technique collecting random observations over time to estimate how time is distributed across activities[^1_1]
- **KPI Monitoring** — Tracks metrics like cycle time, error rate, and throughput to quantify performance and flag areas needing improvement[^1_3]

***

## Improvement Frameworks \& Methodologies

Structured approaches for implementing lasting change.


| Methodology | Best For | Core Focus |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Lean** | Eliminating waste | Value stream optimisation [^1_4] |
| **Six Sigma / DMAIC** | Reducing defects | Data-driven quality control [^1_4] |
| **Kaizen** | Ongoing improvement | Continuous small incremental changes [^1_4] |
| **PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act)** | Testing solutions | Iterative problem-solving cycles [^1_4] |
| **5 Whys** | Root cause analysis | Tracing errors to their source [^1_4] |
| **TQM (Total Quality Management)** | Customer satisfaction | Organisation-wide quality culture [^1_4] |
| **BPM (Business Process Management)** | Scaling operations | End-to-end process governance [^1_4] |
| **Theory of Constraints (TOC)** | Bottleneck removal | Identifying and relieving system constraints [^1_5] |
| **Agile** | Iterative delivery | Flexible, adaptive workflow design [^1_5] |


***

## Root Cause \& Problem-Solving Tools

Used once an issue is identified to find its origin.

- **Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagrams** — Visually map all potential causes of a problem by category (people, process, environment, etc.)[^1_6]
- **5 Whys** — Repeatedly asking "why" (typically five times) to drill down to the root cause of a workflow failure[^1_6]
- **DMAIC** (Define, Measure, Analyse, Improve, Control) — A Six Sigma problem-solving cycle for structured, data-backed improvements[^1_6]

***

## Design \& Ongoing Optimisation Practices

Principles applied when building or iterating on workflows.

- **Standardisation** — Define consistent steps so the same process always runs the same way, reducing ambiguity and errors[^1_7]
- **Automation** — Offload repetitive, rules-based tasks to software to reduce human error and free up capacity[^1_8]
- **Conditional branching** — Build flexible workflows with logic-based paths so processes adapt to different scenarios without manual intervention[^1_3]
- **Feedback mechanisms** — Continuously collect input from team members and stakeholders to surface hidden inefficiencies[^1_3]
- **Simulation modelling** — Test changes to a workflow virtually before rolling them out, assessing impact on performance without disrupting live operations[^1_1]

The choice of method typically depends on whether you're in a *diagnostic* phase (mapping, mining, sampling), a *design* phase (BPMN, flowcharts, simulation), or an *improvement* phase (Lean, Six Sigma, Kaizen, PDCA).

# Do any of these methods include imagining the best possible outcomes?

Yes — several dedicated methods centre specifically on imagining the best possible outcomes, rather than just fixing what's broken. These are distinct from the diagnostic/problem-solving methods listed earlier.

***

## Appreciative Inquiry (AI)

This is the most prominent "best possible outcome" method for organisations and workflows.  Developed by David Cooperrider and Suresh Srivastva, it is a strengths-based approach that focuses on what *already works well* and uses that as a springboard to envision an ideal future — rather than analysing past failures.[^2_1]

It follows the **4-D Cycle**:[^2_2]

1. **Discover** — Identify processes, strengths, and moments that already work well
2. **Dream** — Co-create a vivid, shared image of the best possible future ("what might be?")
3. **Design** — Develop concrete plans aligned with that shared vision
4. **Destiny/Deliver** — Execute and sustain the change collaboratively

The "Dream" phase is explicitly about imagining the ideal future — stakeholders describe what the process would *look, sound, and feel like* at its very best.[^2_2]

***

## Future State Mapping

A close companion to Value Stream Mapping, this technique asks teams to deliberately design the *optimal* version of a workflow — unconstrained by current inefficiencies or legacy habits.  After mapping the current state, the team reimagines the process as it *should* be, then builds a roadmap to get there.  It bridges imagination and execution by pairing the ideal vision with concrete action plans, timelines, and responsibilities.[^2_3][^2_4]

***

## Backcasting

Backcasting starts at the ideal end point — a vivid, desirable future — and *works backward* to the present to identify what steps would need to happen to get there.  It forces genuine creativity by asking people to set aside current constraints and first agree on *what success looks like*, then reverse-engineer the path.  It is particularly powerful for longer horizons (often 5–25+ years) and strategic workflow redesign.[^2_5][^2_6]

***

## How These Compare

| Method | Starting Point | Time Horizon | Core Focus |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Appreciative Inquiry** | What's working now | Near–medium term | Strengths → shared dream → design |
| **Future State Mapping** | Current process pain points | Near term | Optimal process design |
| **Backcasting** | Ideal future outcome | Long term (5–25+ years) | Reverse-engineer from the ideal |

All three sit in contrast to problem-first methods (like Root Cause Analysis or Six Sigma), which start with what's *broken*. These methods start with what's *possible*.[^2_1][^2_5]

# What about any methods that ask what could go wrong?

Stakeholder interests?
Positive and negative impacts?
All possible outcomes?

Absolutely — there is a rich set of methods specifically designed around each of those angles. Here's a breakdown:

***

## "What Could Go Wrong?" Methods

- **Pre-Mortem Analysis** — The team *assumes* the workflow or project has already failed, then works backward to identify every possible cause. Popularised by psychologist Gary Klein, it counters optimism bias by making failure feel real and discussable *before* it happens. Participants generate a list of risks, then prioritise and assign mitigations[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3]
- **FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)** — A systematic method that identifies every possible way a process step could fail, assesses the likelihood and severity of each failure, and ranks them by risk priority[^3_4]
- **Risk Registers \& Risk Matrices** — Structured tools for logging potential failures, rating them by probability × impact, and tracking mitigation actions throughout a workflow's lifecycle[^3_2]

***

## Stakeholder Interests

- **Stakeholder Analysis** — Identifies everyone affected by a workflow and maps their level of *influence*, *interest*, *impact*, and *position* (supportive vs. opposed). Common outputs are a **Power/Interest Grid** (plotting stakeholders on two axes) or a **Salience Model** (mapping by power, legitimacy, and urgency)[^3_5][^3_6]
- **RACI Matrix** — Maps every workflow task to who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed — clarifying ownership and stakeholder involvement at each step[^3_7]
- **Stakeholder Mapping** — Visual tools (grids, circles, networks) that show relationships, dependencies, and potential conflicts between parties who have a stake in how the workflow runs[^3_5]

***

## Positive \& Negative Impacts

- **Impact Assessment** — Systematically evaluates both the positive and negative effects a workflow or process change may have on people, systems, and the environment. Widely used in policy and project governance, it assesses baseline conditions, identifies potential impact events, and recommends mitigations[^3_8][^3_9]
- **Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)** — Quantifies all expected positive outcomes (benefits) against all costs and negative impacts of a process, including indirect and long-term effects[^3_10]
- **Social Return on Investment (SROI)** — An extension of CBA that also values social and environmental impacts, assigning monetary proxies to intangible outcomes[^3_11]

***

## "All Possible Outcomes" Methods

- **Scenario Planning** — Teams identify the key uncertainties driving a process, then construct multiple plausible futures (typically 3–4 scenarios) spanning best case to worst case. Rather than predicting one future, it prepares decision-makers to *navigate any* future[^3_12][^3_13]
- **Decision Trees** — Visual diagrams that map out all possible decisions and their downstream consequences, including probabilities and values at each branch
- **Monte Carlo Simulation** — Uses statistical modelling to run thousands of randomised outcome simulations, producing a probability distribution of all possible results — useful for complex, variable workflows[^3_10]
- **SWOT Analysis** — A simpler structured tool that surfaces Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats, covering both positive/negative and internal/external dimensions[^3_14]

***

## How These Methods Cluster

| Focus | Key Methods |
| :-- | :-- |
| What could go wrong | Pre-Mortem, FMEA, Risk Matrix |
| Stakeholder interests | Stakeholder Analysis, RACI, Power/Interest Grid |
| Positive \& negative impacts | Impact Assessment, CBA, SROI |
| All possible outcomes | Scenario Planning, Decision Trees, Monte Carlo |
| Best possible outcome | Appreciative Inquiry, Future State Mapping, Backcasting |

Together with the previous methods (Lean, Six Sigma, Process Mapping, etc.), these cover virtually the full spectrum of how to rigorously examine and design a workflow.

***

## What Is a Workflow? (Human-Centred Definition)

Most definitions are system-centric: *"a sequence of tasks performed to complete a process."* But if we start from humans, a more accurate definition emerges:

> **A workflow is a structured series of coordinated actions taken by one or more actors — human or agent — in response to a human need, designed to transform an initial state into a desired outcome that creates value for stakeholders.**

Several key things this definition centres:

- **Origin in human need** — a workflow only exists because someone has an unmet desire, problem, or goal. As your own prior work captures: *"A task with no human desire behind it — however many levels of abstraction removed — is, by definition, waste."*[^4_1]
- **Actors, not just roles** — the people and agents doing the work are first-class participants with capabilities, constraints, and experiences, not just execution nodes
- **A transformation** — a workflow takes something from one state to another (information, material, a relationship, a decision)
- **Stakeholders, not just users** — multiple parties are affected by both the process *and* the outcome, directly and indirectly
- **Value creation as the purpose** — the workflow's legitimacy is derived from whether it actually delivers what stakeholders need

## What a Human-Centred CAWDP Foundation Could Include

Building from first principles, the *foundation* of any workflow examination should answer these questions before touching process steps:

**1. Who exists in relation to this workflow?**

- Direct actors: those who *perform* tasks within it
- Direct beneficiaries: those who *receive* the output
- Indirect stakeholders: those affected by side effects or outcomes
- Governing parties: those who set constraints (compliance, safety, policy)

**2. What human need or desire does this workflow serve?**

- What is the gap between current reality and desired reality for each stakeholder group?[^4_1]
- What problems does a *successful* outcome actually solve for each?
- What does *failure* cost each stakeholder group?

**3. What is the desired transformation?**

- What state exists *before* the workflow begins?
- What state *must* exist for the workflow to be considered successful?
- How would each stakeholder group recognise that the transformation occurred?

**4. What does the best possible outcome look like?**

- For each stakeholder group, what would "exceeds expectations" mean?

**5. What could go wrong, and for whom?**

- Risks to the actors performing the work
- Risks to the beneficiaries receiving it
- Risks to indirect stakeholders
- Risks to the organisation running it

**6. What tensions or conflicts exist between stakeholder interests?**

- Where do the needs and desired outcomes of different groups diverge?

Only *after* this foundation is established would CAWDP move into examining the workflow's structure, steps, actors, tools, handoffs, and logic.

This would make CAWDP genuinely distinct — not a process improvement methodology, but a **workflow understanding methodology** that starts where work actually originates: in human needs, desires, and relationships.

Would you like to begin drafting the formal framing of this foundation layer for CAWDP — what it's called, how it's structured, and what questions it asks?

Great set of foundations. Here are additional statements that could be true about a workflow:

**Structure \& Flow**

- A workflow may contain loops — steps that repeat until a condition is met
- Steps may run in parallel, not just in sequence
- A workflow may call or invoke another workflow as a sub-process
- Some steps may be optional depending on context or path taken

**Actors \& Responsibility**

- A workflow involves one or more actors, each with defined or assumed responsibilities
- Different steps may require different actors, skills, or capabilities
- A step may require sign-off or approval from an actor who is not performing it
- Accountability for the workflow as a whole may belong to someone other than those performing the steps

**Time \& Scheduling**

- A workflow has an expected duration, and may have a deadline
- Steps may have waiting periods between them — including waiting for external events
- A workflow may be time-triggered rather than event-triggered
- The same workflow may recur on a schedule or ad hoc

**Quality \& Governance**

- A workflow may have quality standards that each step or the output must meet
- A workflow may require an audit trail — a record of what happened, when, and by whom
- A workflow may have constraints — rules about what is or isn't permitted within it
- Some steps may be mandatory; others discretionary

**Context \& Environment**

- A workflow exists within an organisational, social, or regulatory context that shapes what is permissible
- A workflow may depend on external systems, tools, or information sources
- The same workflow may behave differently depending on the context in which it is triggered
- A workflow may have a version — it can evolve over time

**Failure \& Exception**

- A workflow may have defined exception-handling paths for known failure modes
- A step may be retried before the workflow is considered failed
- Partial completion may have value — or may leave the system in a worse state than before it began

# Can you find even more statements that can be true about workflows?

**Visibility \& Observability**

- A workflow may be fully visible to all actors, or partially hidden depending on role
- The current state or progress of a workflow may or may not be observable in real time
- A workflow may produce logs, notifications, or signals as it progresses

**Inputs \& Resources**

- A workflow may require resources — time, money, materials, access, information — before it can begin or continue
- Some inputs may be consumed by a step; others are referenced but not consumed
- A workflow may compete with other workflows for shared or limited resources

**Relationships Between Workflows**

- Workflows may be interdependent — one cannot proceed until another reaches a certain state
- A workflow may be a component of a larger workflow or process
- Multiple instances of the same workflow may run simultaneously
- Workflows may conflict — competing for the same resource, actor, or output

**Triggers \& Conditions**

- A step may only become available when a prior condition is satisfied
- A workflow may have multiple valid starting points depending on context
- A workflow may be paused and resumed — not all workflows run to completion in one continuous session

**Value \& Purpose**

- A workflow may serve multiple goals simultaneously, not just one
- The value produced by a workflow may be distributed unevenly across stakeholders
- A workflow may produce unintended outputs — positive or negative — beyond its stated purpose
- The cost of a workflow may outweigh its value — making it a candidate for elimination

**People \& Experience**

- Actors performing a workflow have an experience of doing so — it may be effortful, stressful, satisfying, or dull
- A workflow may require emotional labour from its actors, not just cognitive or physical effort
- A workflow may erode trust between stakeholders if it is opaque, inconsistent, or unfair
- The actors in a workflow may have unequal power — affecting how steps are performed or decisions are made

**Knowledge \& Learning**

- Completing a workflow generates knowledge — about what worked, what didn't, how long things take
- A workflow may be documented or undocumented — existing only in the heads of those who perform it
- A workflow may encode assumptions that were once valid but are no longer true
- Performing a workflow may build or degrade the capability of the actor doing it

**Risk \& Consequence**

Here is the relationship as it stands:

**What the task contract covers well that a workflow contract inherits in aggregate form:**

- Origin \& intent (the workflow contract holds the root version; task contracts trace back to it)
- Value declaration (tasks declare micro-value; the workflow declares the total value transformation)
- Risk \& safety (tasks declare step-level risk; the workflow declares the cumulative and systemic risk)
- Definition of done (tasks have acceptance criteria; the workflow has a completion condition across all tasks)
- Audit \& immutable record (task trails roll up into a workflow-level trail)

**What a workflow contract needs that no task contract can provide:**

- The full stakeholder map — who is affected by the workflow as a whole, including those untouched by any single task
- Harm potential to indirect stakeholders — as you've already noted as a gap
- The trigger and preconditions for the workflow itself — above and before any task begins
- The workflow-level blast radius — which may be greater than the sum of its tasks
- Failure modes at the workflow level — including partial completion states and what they leave behind
- The intended transformation — the delta between start state and end state of the world
- Tensions between stakeholder interests — which only become visible at workflow scope
- Abortion conditions — triggers that end the workflow regardless of task status
- The recurrence and scheduling logic — which governs when the whole workflow runs, not individual tasks
- External constraints — regulatory, contractual, or legal obligations on the workflow as a unit
- The workflow's relationship to other workflows — dependencies and conflicts at a level above task dependencies

**The key structural insight:**

The task contract is a *commitment between a requester and a performer*.  The workflow contract is a *commitment between an organisation and its stakeholders* — it declares what transformation will occur, for whom, at what cost and risk, and under what conditions. # A workflow rarely exists in isolation. It has dependencies and its outputs are perhaps dependencies for other workflows.

Help me understand workflows in an organisational context.

Here are different lenses through which workflows exist within an organisation:

***

## Workflows as a Connected System

- Every workflow exists within a **value chain** — a sequence of transformations that collectively deliver something of value to an end beneficiary
- The output of one workflow is frequently the input or trigger of another — creating a **dependency graph**, not a list
- A workflow may be **upstream** (its outputs enable others), **downstream** (it depends on others), or both simultaneously
- Failure or delay in one workflow propagates — a blocked workflow blocks every workflow that depends on it

***

## Levels of Workflow

Workflows exist at multiple levels of granularity within an organisation:

- **Operational workflows** — day-to-day execution: process an invoice, onboard a client, resolve a support ticket
- **Management workflows** — coordinate and govern operational work: review performance, approve budgets, manage exceptions
- **Strategic workflows** — shape direction: evaluate opportunities, make investment decisions, set priorities
- **Governance workflows** — ensure compliance, safety, and accountability: audit, report, escalate, remediate

Each level depends on the one below producing reliable outputs, and shapes the conditions under which the one below operates.

***

## Workflows and Organisational Structure

- Workflows rarely respect organisational boundaries — most cross teams, departments, or even organisations
- **Handoffs** between actors are the most fragile points in any workflow — and most handoffs cross a boundary of some kind
- The same workflow may be **owned** by one function but **performed** across many
- Organisational silos often exist precisely because workflow dependencies between teams are poorly understood or poorly designed

***

## Workflows and Time

- At any moment, an organisation has dozens or hundreds of workflows running simultaneously — in different states, at different stages
- Workflows compete for the same actors, tools, budgets, and attention
- Some workflows are **continuous** — always running in the background
- Some are **episodic** — triggered by events, then dormant
- Some are **seasonal** — tied to reporting cycles, financial years, regulatory calendars
- The timing of one workflow's completion can create pressure or opportunity for another

***

## Workflows and Organisational Health

- The cumulative experience of performing all workflows is what **work feels like** in an organisation
- Poorly designed workflows create friction, stress, rework, and disengagement
- Well-designed workflows create clarity, autonomy, and a sense of meaningful progress
- An organisation's culture is partly an emergent property of how its workflows treat the people inside them
- Waste in one workflow consumes capacity that other workflows need

***

## Workflows as Organisational Memory

- Documented workflows encode how the organisation currently believes work should be done
- Undocumented workflows encode how work is actually done — which often diverges
- Both contain assumptions — some valid, some outdated, some never examined
- When people leave, they take undocumented workflow knowledge with them
- A workflow that has never been written down is a single point of failure

***

## The Workflow as a Unit of Organisational Design

- If you mapped every workflow in an organisation and their dependencies, you would have a more accurate picture of how the organisation actually functions than any org chart provides
- Org charts show reporting lines; the workflow graph shows how value actually flows
- Redesigning a workflow is an act of organisational design — it changes who does what, when, for whom, and with what authority
- Adding or removing a workflow changes the shape of the dependency graph for everything connected to it

## The Workflow Exists Before Its Steps Do

The most important insight is that a workflow has a complete identity — purpose, stakeholders, value, risk, dependencies, constraints — that exists entirely independently of what its steps are. Current design practice almost always starts with the steps. This exploration suggests the steps should come last, not first.

***

## A Workflow Is a Promise

Every workflow is implicitly a commitment to stakeholders — direct and indirect — that a certain transformation will occur, at a certain quality, within a certain time, without causing undue harm. Designing a workflow without first making that promise explicit means you cannot know whether the steps you design actually fulfil it.

***

## Most Workflows Are Invisible

The gap between documented and undocumented workflows is not a minor operational issue — it is where organisational risk, inefficiency, and cultural friction live. Any reimagining process that only examines documented workflows is examining a fraction of reality.

***

## Handoffs Are Where Workflows Break

The most fragile point in any workflow is where responsibility transfers — between people, teams, systems, or organisations. Current design processes tend to focus on tasks within steps, not on the transitions between them. A reimagined process would treat every handoff as a primary design object.

***

## The Org Chart Is the Wrong Map

Workflows cross organisational boundaries constantly. Designing or improving workflows using the org chart as the frame produces workflows shaped by reporting lines rather than value flow. The dependency graph of workflows is the real map of how an organisation functions.

***

## Categories Are Design Levers

The categories we identified — by risk, reversibility, regulatory status, human experience, value type, position in the value chain — are not just descriptive. Each one implies a different design approach. A safety-critical, irreversible, regulated workflow should be designed completely differently from a low-risk, reversible, internal one. A reimagined design process would use categorisation as a routing mechanism — directing each workflow to the appropriate design method and level of rigour.

***

## Human Experience Is a First-Class Design Concern

Cognitive load, emotional labour, autonomy, and the experience of performing the workflow are currently afterthoughts in most design processes. But the cumulative experience of all workflows is what work feels like in an organisation. Designing for human experience is not a nice-to-have — it is the difference between a workflow that is performed well and one that is performed grudgingly, inconsistently, or not at all.

***

## Value and Waste Are Visible at the Workflow Level

By declaring the value type and position in the value stream for every workflow, an organisation can see — possibly for the first time — how much of its collective effort is value-creating versus value-enabling versus waste. This is a strategic view that task-level analysis cannot produce.

***

## The Workflow Contract Changes What Design Means

If a workflow is a promise to stakeholders, then designing a workflow is not an exercise in mapping steps — it is an exercise in negotiating and committing to a set of outcomes, constraints, and responsibilities. The contract precedes the process. This fundamentally reframes what a workflow designer is doing.

***

## The Implication for CAWDP

A reimagined design process would have at least two distinct phases before touching steps:

1. **Understand the workflow** — its identity, purpose, stakeholders, value, risk, dependencies, categories, and the promise it makes
2. **Evaluate whether the workflow should exist** — and in what form, at what level of rigour, for which beneficiaries

Only then would it proceed to examine and redesign the steps within it. This makes the foundation not just useful — it makes it the most important part of the process.

***

## Why Handoffs Fail

**Information failures**

- The output of the upstream workflow is incomplete, ambiguous, or in the wrong format for the downstream workflow to consume
- Assumptions made by the sender are not shared by the receiver
- Context that existed in one actor's head was never externalised into the handoff artefact

**Accountability failures**

- No one is explicitly responsible for the handoff itself — only for the task before or after it
- The gap between workflows is owned by nobody
- Both parties assume the other is watching

**Timing failures**

- The downstream workflow is not ready to receive when the upstream delivers
- The upstream delivers too early (output sits unactioned) or too late (downstream is blocked)
- No notification reaches the right person at the right moment

**Trust failures**

- The receiver doesn't trust the quality of what was handed off and re-does work already done
- The sender doesn't verify the receiver actually received and understood
- No shared definition of what "ready to hand off" means

**Boundary failures**

- The handoff crosses a team, system, or organisational boundary where incentives, tools, or language differ
- Each side optimises for their own workflow completion, not the overall outcome
- Power imbalances mean one party's needs dominate the design of the handoff

**Design failures**

- The handoff was never explicitly designed — it emerged as a default
- No one has ever mapped what the downstream workflow actually needs as its starting condition
- The handoff format serves the sender's convenience, not the receiver's requirements

***

## The Fractal Problem \& Minimum Designable Workflow

Your instinct is right — this is a genuine design economics problem. The question is not *can* we design this workflow, but *should* we, and *to what level of rigour*.

**The core variables that determine design investment:**

- **Run frequency** — how many times will this workflow execute? A workflow that runs once warrants minimal design. One that runs a million times warrants significant investment, because even marginal improvement compounds
- **Cost per run** — time, money, human effort, emotional labour per execution
- **Failure rate** — how often does it currently go wrong, and what does each failure cost
- **Blast radius per failure** — small frequent failures in a high-volume workflow may dwarf a single large failure in a rare one
- **Reversibility** — irreversible workflows warrant more design rigour regardless of frequency
- **Regulatory obligation** — some workflows must be designed to a minimum standard regardless of economics

**The compounding benefits you've identified are real:**

- A 10% reduction in effort per run × 10,000 runs = 1,000 runs worth of capacity recovered
- A reduction in failure rate compounds further — fewer failures means fewer exception workflows triggered downstream
- Better handoff design reduces rework in the *receiving* workflow, creating benefit beyond the workflow being designed
- Knowledge generated from a well-designed workflow improves the design of related workflows — a network effect

**The minimum designable workflow is probably defined by:**

A workflow is worth explicit design when at least one of these is true:

- It will run more than once in identical or near-identical form
- Failure carries consequences beyond the workflow itself
- It crosses a boundary — between people, teams, systems, or organisations
- A human performing it experiences friction, confusion, or inconsistency
- Its output is a dependency for another workflow
- It involves an irreversible action
- It is currently undocumented but is being performed

**Below this threshold** — a truly one-off, low-stakes, self-contained action with no downstream dependencies — the cost of design likely outweighs the benefit, and a simple checklist or even an informal approach is sufficient.

**The fractal insight this creates:**

Rather than asking *"what is the smallest workflow worth designing?"*, a more useful question may be *"at what level of granularity does design investment produce the greatest return?"* — and that level will differ for every organisation, every workflow category, and every point in time. A startup running a workflow ten times a week needs less rigour than an enterprise running it ten thousand times a day. The design depth should scale with the run economics.

Yes — establishing rigour level should be one of the earliest decisions in the process, because it determines how much of the workflow contract to complete, which design methods to apply, and how formally the output needs to be documented and governed.

***

## Data Needed to Determine Rigour Level

**Volume \& Economics**

- Expected run frequency (once / recurring / continuous)
- Estimated cost per run (time, money, human effort)
- Current failure rate, if known
- Cost of each failure (rework, downstream impact, compensation)
- Expected lifespan of the workflow — how long will it run before it changes or is retired

**Risk \& Consequence**

- Blast radius if the workflow fails
- Reversibility of outputs
- Safety risk to actors or affected parties
- Regulatory or compliance obligations attached to this workflow

**Dependencies**

- Is its output a dependency for other workflows?
- Does it depend on upstream workflows whose reliability affects it?
- How many workflows does it block if it fails?

**Human Experience**

- Does it cross a boundary (team, system, organisation)?
- Is it currently causing friction, confusion, or inconsistency for performers?
- Is it currently undocumented?

**Strategic Importance**

- Is this workflow directly in the value-creating stream, or enabling/protecting?
- Is it customer-facing?
- Does its quality directly affect stakeholder trust or satisfaction?

***

## Levels of Rigour

**Level 1 — Informal**
*For: one-off, low-stakes, no downstream dependencies, single actor, fully reversible*

- No formal documentation required
- A simple mental checklist or brief note is sufficient
- No handoff design needed
- No audit trail required

**Level 2 — Lightweight**
*For: recurring but low-risk, single team, low blast radius, reversible outputs*

- Basic documentation of purpose, trigger, steps, and definition of done
- Informal but consistent handoff conventions
- Simple checklist for performers
- Reviewed periodically but not formally governed

**Level 3 — Structured**
*For: cross-functional, moderate blast radius, customer-facing, or moderate run volume*

- Full workflow contract completed for the workflow level
- Explicit handoff specifications between subworkflows
- Defined exception paths for known failure modes
- Assigned owner and reviewer
- Audit trail maintained
- Reviewed on a regular cadence or after significant failures

**Level 4 — Governed**
*For: high run volume, high blast radius, irreversible outputs, regulated, or safety-sensitive*

- Full workflow contract including stakeholder map, risk register, and compliance obligations
- Formal change control — changes require approval before implementation
- Mandatory audit trail
- Quality gates at defined points
- Retrospective required after failures or significant variance
- Version controlled — every change is recorded and dated

**Level 5 — Safety-Critical**
*For: life, legal, or catastrophic financial consequences; externally regulated; irreversible harm possible*

- Everything in Level 4, plus:
- External review or certification required
- Mandatory pre-execution checklists (like aviation or surgical protocols)
- No discretion permitted on mandatory steps
- Independent verification of outputs
- Incident reporting obligations
- Designed to assume human error will occur — failure safeguards are structural, not behavioural

***

## The Decision as a Routing Mechanism

The rigour level determination is essentially a triage step that routes the workflow to the appropriate design depth. It answers: *how much of the workflow contract do we need to complete, and how formally does the output need to be governed?*

It also needs to be **revisited** — a workflow that starts at Level 2 may need to move to Level 4 if run volume grows, regulatory obligations change, or a significant failure occurs. Rigour level is not a permanent classification; it is a current assessment.

# What would be the fewest questions we could ask a user to determine the appropriate rigour level?

Here are the fewest questions that collectively cover all the decisive variables — each one capable of pushing the rigour level up on its own:

***

**1. How many times will this workflow run?**
Once / A handful of times / Regularly / Very frequently / Continuously

**2. What is the worst realistic consequence if this workflow fails or produces a poor output?**
Inconvenience to one person / Rework or delay within a team / Impact on a customer or external party / Significant financial, legal, or reputational harm / Risk of physical or psychological harm to someone

**3. Are any of the outputs of this workflow difficult or impossible to undo?**
Yes / No

**4. Does this workflow cross a boundary — between teams, systems, or organisations?**
Yes / No

**5. Is this workflow subject to any legal, regulatory, or contractual obligation?**
Yes / No / Unsure

**6. Do other workflows depend on the output of this workflow to proceed?**
Yes / No

***

Six questions. Each one is independently sufficient to raise the rigour level — you don't need all six to point the same way. The scoring logic would be something like:

- Any "Yes" on questions 3, 5 → minimum Level 3
- Question 2 at harm to a person → minimum Level 4
- Question 2 at physical/psychological harm → minimum Level 5
- Question 1 at very frequently or continuously → minimum Level 3
- Questions 4 or 6 as "Yes" → minimum Level 3
- All answers pointing low → Level 1 or 2

The highest level triggered by any single answer wins.

# What is the shortest path through these questions with exit points when a design shape has been determined?

Here is the shortest path — a decision tree that exits as soon as enough is known to determine the design shape:

***

**Q1. Does a version of this workflow already exist?**

- **No** → go to Q2
- **Yes** → go to Q3

***

**Q2. Is the desired outcome clearly defined and agreed?**

- **No** → **Exit: Discovery mode** — facilitate stakeholder alignment on the desired outcome before any design work begins
- **Yes** → go to Q4

***

**Q3. Is there a known problem with the existing workflow?**

- **No** → **Exit: Document only** — capture the workflow as-is; no redesign needed
- **Yes** → go to Q4

***

**Q4. Are the steps largely known and understood?**

- **No** → **Exit: Mapping mode** — investigate and document current or intended steps before designing; use process discovery, observation, or stakeholder interviews
- **Yes** → go to Q5

***

**Q5. Does the workflow require human judgement at any point?**

- **No** → **Exit: Automation design** — workflow is a candidate for full automation; design for rules, exceptions, and monitoring rather than human execution
- **Yes** → go to Q6

***

**Q6. Do stakeholders have conflicting interests in how this workflow operates?**

- **Yes** → **Exit: Facilitated design** — run a structured stakeholder alignment process before or alongside design; conflict unresolved at this stage will surface as failure later
- **No** → go to Q7

***

**Q7. Is the context this workflow operates in stable, or likely to change?**

- **Likely to change** → **Exit: Iterative design** — design a minimum viable version now with explicit review triggers; over-engineering a workflow in an unstable context creates waste
- **Stable** → **Exit: Full design** — proceed to complete the workflow contract and design the steps with the rigour level already determined

***

## The Seven Design Shapes

| Exit | Design Shape | What It Means |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Discovery mode | Align before design | Outcome unknown — design cannot begin |
| Document only | Capture as-is | Working well — just make it visible |
| Mapping mode | Investigate before design | Steps unknown — understand before redesigning |
| Automation design | Engineer, don't humanise | No judgement needed — remove humans from execution |
| Facilitated design | Align then design | Conflict present — design without alignment will fail |
| Iterative design | MVP now, evolve later | Context unstable — design for change, not permanence |
| Full design | Complete the contract | Everything known and stable — design it properly |

- Returns a rigour level (1–5)

**Design shape router**

- Accepts answers to the design shape questions
- Applies routing logic
- Returns a design shape and the recommended first action

**Contract populator**

- Accepts structured inputs (from a human, a conversation, or an agent)
- Writes values into the correct fields of the workflow contract
- Tracks which fields are populated, which are empty, which are flagged for review

**Artefact producer**

- Given an artefact type and a production method, runs the appropriate production path
- Shallow: computes or extracts from existing contract fields
- Medium: prepares inputs, invokes an agent or human interaction, validates output
- Deep: runs a structured multi-step workflow, gates on quality criteria

**Dependency resolver**

- Knows which artefacts depend on which other artefacts or contract fields
- Prevents an artefact from being produced before its inputs exist
- Surfaces what is blocking production of a given artefact

**Progress tracker**

- Knows which artefacts are required for the current tier
- Reports what is complete, what is in progress, what is not yet started
- Determines when a phase gate condition is met

## The Minimal Slice

If you wanted to prove the concept with the least possible code:

1. A workflow contract record with its fields
2. Triage questions hard-coded, answers stored, rigour level computed
3. Design shape questions hard-coded, answers stored, shape returned
4. The contract populator writing answers into the right fields
5. One complete artefact production path — just Shallow to start
6. A progress tracker that shows what is done and what remains

That gives you a working end-to-end loop: a user describes a workflow, the system determines rigour and design shape, begins populating the contract, and produces its first artefact. Everything else is built on top of that foundation.

# what insights do you see from performing that activity?

Several things became visible in the act of completing that second contract:

***

## The Workflow Is Self-Referential

The workflow design process is itself a workflow — and completing its own contract is one of the most honest tests of whether the contract template works. If the template cannot describe itself, it cannot describe anything. The fact that it completed cleanly is a meaningful validation.

***

## The Contract Surfaces the Bootstrap Problem

The precondition "at least one person with knowledge of the workflow is available" is trivial for most workflows. For this one, it is the entire problem. The workflow design process requires someone who understands the workflow being designed — but for new workflows, that person doesn't exist yet. The contract makes this visible in a way that a process map never would.

***

## Partial Completion Is Especially Dangerous Here

For most workflows, partial completion is neutral or mildly negative. For a design specification workflow, a partially completed specification is actively misleading — it creates the appearance of understanding where none exists. This is a design implication: the prototype should make incompleteness highly visible, not quietly tolerated.

***

## The Artefact Is Also an Input

The completed workflow contract for the design process is simultaneously the output of this workflow and the template that guides it. This is unusual — most workflows produce artefacts that are consumed elsewhere. This one consumes its own output format as its operating instructions. That recursive relationship is worth making explicit in the prototype.

***

## The Rigour Triage Has a Blind Spot

The second contract revealed that the rigour level of the design process depends entirely on the rigour level of the workflow being designed — not on fixed properties of the design process itself. A Quick Start tier design of a simple workflow is Level 2. An Architect tier design of a safety-critical workflow is Level 4 or 5. The design process inherits its rigour from its subject. The triage questions need to account for this — they are currently asking about the workflow in isolation, not about the design process being applied to it.

***

## Stakeholders of the Designed Workflow Are Invisible in the Design Process

The stakeholders listed in the second contract are the designer, the owner, and the performers. But the stakeholders of the workflow being designed — the people who will be affected by it — are only indirectly present. The design process has no formal mechanism to bring them into the room. This is the human-centred design gap: the people most affected by the workflow have the least voice in how it is specified.

***

## "Could This Workflow Be Eliminated?" Has a Recursive Answer

The contract asks whether the workflow could be eliminated without meaningful loss. The answer given was "no — if a workflow is worth running, it is worth understanding." But that answer contains an assumption: that the workflow being designed is itself worth running. The design process should include a gate that challenges the existence of the workflow before investing in specifying it. The triage already partially does this — but it could be made more explicit.

***

## The Most Important Insight

Completing the contract for the design process itself makes it obvious that **the workflow contract is not just a documentation tool — it is a thinking tool**. The act of answering the questions surfaces things that were not visible before answering them. The value is not in the completed document. The value is in what the questions force you to confront.

This has a direct implication for the prototype: the questions should be presented conversationally, not as a form to fill in. A form invites shallow answers. A conversation invites genuine reflection.

# Is this the process we're looking for, hidden within these principles?

A tool that structuralises these principles in some way? that perhaps limits complexity until simple prototypes are tested and learned from?

Yes. And it already has a name — or rather, several names that each capture part of it.

***

## What You're Describing Already Exists

The closest single methodology is **Double Diamond** combined with **Build-Measure-Learn** — but neither alone is quite right, and you've identified something that sits between them.

What you're actually describing is closer to a **hypothesis-driven design loop** with a structural complexity gate. The principles you've listed are the operating rules of that loop.

***

## The Hidden Process in the Principles

Reading across all the principles, the underlying process is:

**1. Bound** — restrict scope until it is small enough to hold in your head
**2. Hypothesise** — state explicitly what you believe to be true and what success looks like
**3. Expose assumptions** — name everything you're taking for granted
**4. Build the minimum thing that tests the hypothesis** — happy path only, one variable
**5. Run it** — in real or simulated conditions
**6. Observe what surprises you** — surprises are the signal, expected outcomes are just confirmation
**7. Record** — write down what you learned, what you assumed that was wrong, what the next hypothesis is
**8. Decide** — expand scope, change direction, or discard and restart
**9. Repeat**

The loop only expands complexity when the current scope is understood. Complexity is earned, not assumed.

***

## The Structural Tool

What makes this a *tool* rather than just a *process* is the gate between steps 7 and 8. The gate asks:

- What did we learn?
- What assumption was wrong?
- Are we ready to expand scope — or do we need another cycle at this level?
- What is the single next hypothesis?

Without the gate, the loop becomes undisciplined iteration. With the gate, it becomes a structured ratchet — you can only move forward when the current level is understood, and you never lose ground already gained.

***

## What This Means for CAWDP

The workflow design process you're building *is* this tool — applied to workflow design specifically. The workflow contract is the bounding structure. The triage questions are the complexity gate. The design shape is the hypothesis about what kind of work is needed. The artefacts are the evidence.

The insight is that **the design process and the thing being designed share the same underlying loop**. You don't need a separate methodology for designing CAWDP — CAWDP applied to itself, at the smallest possible scope, is the process.

Which means the first prototype is not a tool that helps others design workflows. It is you, using these principles, to design the tool itself — one hypothesis at a time.

# What have the last two exercises helped us understand? What are the learnings? Insights?

Several things became visible that weren't visible before:

***

## The Triage Works — But Has a Sensitivity Problem

Both workflows hit Level 3 or 4 immediately. The triage is good at identifying that something needs rigour — but it may be too sensitive. Almost any cross-functional, recurring workflow with downstream dependencies will score high. This raises a question: **is the triage distinguishing between workflows that need careful design and workflows that need urgent redesign?** Those are different problems requiring different responses.

***

## The Design Shape Is Doing Less Work Than Expected

Both workflows landed on "Full Design" without much deliberation. The design shape questions may need sharper discrimination — particularly the question about whether steps are known and understood. In both cases the answer was "partially" but the routing still sent us to Full Design. A "Partial Mapping" shape — where you document what you know and fill gaps before designing — may be missing.

***

## Every Real Workflow Has a Data Quality Problem Hiding Inside It

The PO workflow assumed form completeness. The data compilation workflow explicitly surfaced data quality as a risk. But in both cases, the quality of inputs is the most common real-world failure point — and the contract currently treats this as a risk rather than a design requirement. **Input quality specification may need to be a first-class section in the contract**, not just a note in the failure modes.

***

## The Stakeholder Tension Pattern Is Consistent

Both workflows showed the same underlying tension: **the person who does the work bears the most burden; the person who benefits bears the least.** The requester fills the PO form. The compiler chases contributors. Neither is the primary beneficiary. This is a recurring human experience problem that workflow design almost never addresses — and it is probably where the most friction lives in most organisations.

***

## Partial Completion Is the Highest Risk in Both

In both workflows, the most dangerous state is not failure — it is **apparent completion that is actually incomplete**. An unapproved PO that looks approved. A compiled dataset with silent gaps. The contract captures this, but the design implication is significant: **every workflow needs an explicit mechanism to make incompleteness visible**, not just a note that partial completion is bad.

***

## The Contract Is Generating Design Requirements, Not Just Documentation

This is the most important insight. Filling in the contract is not describing a workflow — it is **revealing what the workflow needs to be true to function well**. The retrospective questions, the stakeholder tension observations, the failure modes — these are not historical records. They are design specifications in disguise. The contract is doing the work of a design brief, not just a documentation template.

***

## The Rigour Level Shapes the Contract More Than the Workflow Does

A Level 3 structured workflow and a Level 4 governed workflow filled in almost identically — the difference was in degree, not in kind. This suggests the rigour level needs to directly gate which sections are required and how deeply each must be completed. Otherwise the contract becomes a one-size-fits-all form that feels like overhead for simpler workflows.

***

## The Biggest Gap: There Is No "Before" Picture

Both contracts describe the desired workflow — but neither captures what is actually happening now. For any redesign, the gap between current and desired state is where all the design work lives. **A "Current State" section — even just a few fields — would make the contract dramatically more useful as a redesign tool.**

# What other recommendations do you have to improve the contract at this stage?

Based on everything we've built and tested today, here are the recommendations — grouped by type:

***

## Add to the Contract

**Current State section**

- What does the workflow look like today — documented or not?
- What is broken, missing, or causing friction?
- What workarounds exist?
- This is the delta that design must close

**Input Quality specification**

- What does a good input look like for this workflow?
- What makes an input insufficient to proceed?
- Who is responsible for input quality?
- Move this from a risk footnote to a first-class field

**Visibility \& Status Mechanism**

- How will incompleteness be made visible during execution?
- What signals that the workflow is stalled vs progressing?
- This directly addresses the partial completion risk both exercises surfaced

***

## Restructure

**Split Definition of Done into two distinct things**

- *Completion criteria* — what must be true for the workflow to be considered finished
- *Quality criteria* — what standard must the outputs meet
- These are currently conflated and answer different questions

**Make Stakeholder Tension a standalone field**

- Currently buried in the impact table
- It is important enough — and consistent enough across workflows — to be explicit and prominent
- A workflow with unresolved stakeholder tension needs a different design approach than one without

**Separate "Triggers Another Workflow" from Definition of Done**

- Downstream triggers are about relationships between workflows, not about whether this one is complete
- Belongs in the Relationships section

***

## Simplify

**Collapse the Failure \& Abortion section**

- Failure modes, abortion triggers, and consequence of non-completion are three related but distinct things — but the current structure makes them feel like one long list
- Restructure as: *How it fails* / *How it stops legitimately* / *What non-completion costs*

**Make the Assumptions field actionable**

- Currently the Knowledge \& Learning section asks "what assumptions does this workflow rest on?"
- Add a companion field: *which of these assumptions is most likely to be wrong?*
- This creates an immediate test priority

***

## Remove or Defer

**Version number at this stage**

- Versioning adds no value until the contract is stable enough to change — creates false rigour in early drafts
- Replace with a simpler *Draft / In Review / Approved / Superseded* status

**Some Knowledge \& Learning fields**

- "Does performing this workflow build or degrade the capability of the actor?" is valuable but too detailed for the contract level
- Move to a post-run retrospective template rather than the contract itself

***

## Add as a Header Field

**Workflow Category**

- From the categorisation list we built earlier — by trigger type, value type, position in value chain, risk level etc.
- Even two or three category tags at the top of the contract would make contracts comparable and searchable across an organisation
- Also feeds pattern matching — similar workflows can share design approaches

**Rigour Level justification**

- Currently the rigour level appears as a result but the reasoning is stored separately in the triage
- Add a one-line justification to the contract header: *"Level 4 — irreversible outputs, regulatory obligation, high blast radius"*
- Makes the contract self-explanatory without needing to retrieve the triage record

## How These Relate to the Workflow Contract

Each workflow contract would reference a list of input and output IDs rather than describing them inline. This keeps the contract readable at the workflow level while allowing each input and output to be fully specified in its own record.

The practical benefit is that the same input or output can appear in multiple workflow contracts — an approved PO is an output of the PO approval workflow and an input to the supplier order placement workflow. Specifying it once and referencing it in both contracts avoids duplication and ensures consistency.

This also means that when an output changes — a new format, a new recipient, a new quality standard — only one record needs to be updated, and all workflows that reference it inherit the change automatically.

The dependency graph of inputs and outputs across all workflow contracts is, in effect, the organisation's value flow map.

Yes. This is a significant insight. Let me reflect it back clearly.

***

## The Insight

A workflow is fundamentally a **transformation** — it takes inputs and produces outputs. Everything else — the steps, the actors, the timing, the rules — exists only to perform that transformation reliably.

If you cannot specify the inputs and outputs, you do not yet understand the workflow well enough to design it.

If you can specify them completely, the workflow's internal design becomes a constrained problem — you know what you are starting from and what you must produce. The design space narrows dramatically.

***

## What This Changes About the Design Sequence

The current sequence implied by the contract is:
> Understand the workflow → then specify inputs and outputs

The sequence this insight suggests is:
> Specify inputs and outputs first → then design the workflow that connects them

This is not just a reordering. It is a fundamentally different starting point. You begin with the boundary conditions, not the process.

***

## The Manifest as Organisational Infrastructure

If inputs and outputs are specified as standalone records — referenced by workflows rather than owned by them — then over time the organisation builds a manifest of:

- Every data type, document, decision, and artefact that flows between workflows
- Where each one comes from and where it goes
- What quality standard it must meet
- Which workflows depend on it

This manifest is reusable infrastructure. When designing a new workflow, the designer starts by asking:

> Which of these inputs already exist in the manifest?
> Which of these outputs already exist as inputs somewhere else?

The design work is then scoped to what is genuinely new — not re-specified from scratch every time.

***

## The Design Entry Point This Creates

Rather than beginning with "what is this workflow?", the design process could begin with two questions:

**1. What must exist that does not exist yet?**
— This defines the outputs

**2. What already exists that this workflow can use?**
— This defines the available inputs

The workflow is then the designed mechanism that closes the gap between what exists and what must exist.

***

## The Practical Implication for CAWDP

The first act of workflow design is not completing the contract. It is:

1. **Specify the outputs** — what must this workflow produce, in what form, to what standard, for whom?
2. **Identify available inputs** — what already exists in the manifest that this workflow can consume?
3. **Identify missing inputs** — what does this workflow need that does not yet exist?
4. **Determine whether missing inputs are produced by other workflows** — or whether they represent a gap in the system

Only once inputs and outputs are specified does the internal design of the workflow begin. The contract then documents what the design decided — not the other way around.

This also gives you the minimum viable prototype entry point you were looking for. Before building anything else, build the input/output manifest and the two questions that use it. Everything else follows from that foundation.
