## What's Missing from the Existing Conversation

This is your strategic opening. The current discourse falls into fairly predictable camps — and your "trust stack" framing cuts across all of them:

| What's Being Said | What's Missing |
|---|---|
| "AI will boost GDP by $X trillion" (McKinsey, PwC) | Nobody quantifies how much of that is *locked behind the trust gap* |
| "AI adoption is stalling" (The Economist, Windfall) | Few explain *why* — the trust deficit is the mechanism, not just the observation |
| "We need AI regulation" (governments, UNESCO) | Missing: institutional distrust is itself a root cause; regulation alone can't fix a legitimacy crisis |
| "AI is destroying jobs" (Blakeley, popular media) | Rarely connected to how fear of job loss *itself* suppresses the trust needed for AI to create the new jobs |
| "Build responsible AI" (PwC, McKinsey) | Framed as corporate governance, not as an economic multiplier with measurable ROI |

## Key Pieces to Reference or Push Back Against

These are the posts and reports worth engaging with directly in your articles:

- **Brookings Institution (Aug 2025)** — *"Why People Mistrust AI Advancements"*: found that people consistently trust AI-driven progress *less* than identical non-AI progress in the same domain — fear and perceived societal benefit matter more than technical understanding. Useful for your framing that trust is emotional and institutional, not just technical [brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/why-people-mistrust-ai-advancements/)
- **All Tech Is Human (Sept 2025)** — *"The Missing Foundation in AI Governance: Building Trust Across..."*: surveyed AI governance professionals and found ten consistent concerns, with public engagement and literacy gaps explicitly named as **the missing foundation** [alltechishuman](https://alltechishuman.org/all-tech-is-human-blog/10-pressing-concerns-for-ai-governance-professionals)
- **Australian Government / PMC (2025)** — the Cabinet survey data  is extraordinarily useful: only **42%** of Australians trust government agencies to use AI responsibly for faster services; trust drops to just **29%** for personalised services; and critically, **70% of people with high AI knowledge trust government** vs just **12% of those with no knowledge** — proving your literacy argument with government's own data [pmc.gov](https://www.pmc.gov.au/sites/default/files/resource/download/ltbi-ai-surveys-summary.pdf)
- **Anthropic's productivity claim** — Anthropic's internal estimate that AI could double US productivity growth to 3.6% annually  has been widely cited but also widely criticised for methodological weaknesses. Engaging with this tension — grand claims vs. stalled adoption data — positions you as analytically rigorous rather than a booster or a sceptic [windfalltrust.substack](https://windfalltrust.substack.com/p/brief-3-if-ai-stays-jagged-it-could)

## Your Differentiated Angle

The conversation that barely exists yet — and where you have genuine IP from your own files — is the **"trust stack" framing**: that AI trust rests on institutional trust, which rests on social trust, and all three are degraded simultaneously. Van Geelen discusses economic hollowing, Blakeley discusses power concentration, governance commentators discuss regulatory gaps — but almost no one is connecting these as a *layered trust architecture problem* with an economic solution pathway. That's the gap your Substack series can own.
