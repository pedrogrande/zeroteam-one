## The Core Strategic Tension

You have **two different media jobs** that require different strategies:

1. **Category Creation** — making "Agent Production" a recognised category that journalists reference (like "DevOps" or "Agile" before they were categories). This is a *long campaign*, not a launch.

2. **Announcement Coverage** — getting ink for the working paper release, the 77% Challenge, Future's Edge launch. This is *timely, event-driven*.

These require different outlets, different timelines, and different journalist relationships. Conflating them is the most common PR mistake I see.

## What I Need Before Drafting Any Materials

Per my operating principles, I need to understand a few things about your intent and constraints:

1. **Timing** — When is the working paper realistically shipping? And is the 77% Challenge something you'd launch *with* it or separately?

2. **Spokespeople** — Are you the sole voice, or is Rifadh ready to speak publicly too? Any other voices (academic co-authors, early adopter testimonials)?

3. **Geographic focus** — Given your Australian base but global ambitions, are you targeting AUS media first (build local credibility), going straight to US/UK tech press, or both simultaneously?

4. **Risk appetite** — Category creation requires *provocation* (challenging the industry's current approach). Are you comfortable being quoted saying things like "77% of agentic AI projects are failing and the industry's response is more principles — that's the definition of insanity"? Or do you prefer a more measured tone?

5. **Embargo strategy** — Would you consider an exclusive with one outlet for the working paper release? This can create a "splash" but trades breadth for depth.

6. **Existing relationships** — Do you have any journalist relationships from the TEDx/ABC News/UN work, or are we starting from zero?

7. **The "so what" test** — When a journalist reads your pitch and thinks "this is interesting but what does it mean for my readers?", what's the one sentence answer? I have a candidate: *"There's a reason 77% of agentic AI deployments stall — and it's not the technology, it's the absence of a methodology. ## Initial Observations (Before You Answer)

While I wait for your responses, a few strategic observations from the journalist's perspective:

**The angle journalists will actually write** is not "Australian academic releases framework." It's *"The $5B industry that's failing itself"* — where your methodology is the *evidence* in the story, not the *subject* of the story. Journalists write about problems their readers have; you are the expert who explains the problem and offers the fix. Never pitch yourself as the story. Pitch the problem, and position yourself as the person who's solved it.

**The single strongest media asset you have** is the **77% data point combined with a specific, falsifiable claim.** "77% of agentic AI projects stall at pilot" + "we believe a methodology-based approach can cut that to X" is a story. "I have a framework" is not a story. The working paper's Claims and Evidence Table (that 4-tier hierarchy you designed) is a *media goldmine* — it's exactly the kind of evidence-based, honest-about-its-own-gaps positioning that earns respect from skeptical tech journalists.

**The "trust" word is a liability in headlines.** Every AI company on earth claims to deliver trust. Journalists are immune to it. Your positioning is stronger when you lead with *production* and let trust be the *result*: "The methodology that gets agents into production" is a headline. "Building trust in AI" is wallpaper.

**Your UN/ITU/IBM/TEDx provenance is a credibility shortcut** that most Australian tech founders don't have. This needs to be woven into every bio and every pitch — not as self-promotion, but as "this person has operated at scale internationally and seen the problem from multiple vantage points."
