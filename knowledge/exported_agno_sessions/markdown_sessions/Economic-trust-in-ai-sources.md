## Counter-Arguments Worth Addressing

A credible thought leader engages with dissenting views. Here are the strongest counter-positions:

- **"Trust isn't the primary bottleneck — infrastructure and skills are."** The OECD's firm-level analysis  and Finzarc's 2026 review  both argue that weak governance, unclear ownership, and outdated workflows are more fundamental than trust per se — trust is a *symptom*, not a *cause*[^6][^10]
- **"AI's economic benefits are overstated regardless of trust."** MIT's Daron Acemoglu estimates only 1.1–1.8% GDP growth over 10 years, suggesting AI's transformative economic impact is itself oversold — meaning even perfect trust won't unlock \$15 trillion[^11]
- **"Productivity gains don't require widespread trust — only adoption by a capable minority."** Goldman Sachs and PwC data shows benefits are already concentrating in the top 20% of AI-ready firms — meaning the economy might still benefit even if public trust stays low, just very unequally[^2]
- **"Too much trust is also dangerous."** UNESCO and academic research warn that *overtrust* — especially uncritical reliance on AI outputs — produces its own economic harms through errors, hallucinations, and accountability vacuums[^12][^13]

***

## Other Issues That Belong in the Series

Beyond trust, several structural forces shape AI's economic impact:

- **Infrastructure gaps** — legacy IT systems in government and enterprise block deployment regardless of trust[^14]
- **Data quality and governance** — AI's outputs are only as good as its data inputs; siloed, dirty data is a universal blocker[^15]
- **Regulatory fragmentation** — conflicting national AI rules raise compliance costs and distort investment flows[^16]
- **Equity concentration** — benefits accruing to a narrow band of organisations and geographies, independent of public trust[^17]
- **Measurement gaps** — without agreed ROI frameworks, institutions can't justify AI investment even when they want to[^14]

***

## Other Impacts of Low AI Trust

Beyond lost productivity, low trust has wider economic and social consequences:

- **Investment paralysis** — 46% of planned AI investments are on hold due to trust concerns[^5]
- **Talent misalignment** — workers who distrust AI resist reskilling, widening skill gaps over time[^18]
- **Stalled public services** — governments delay or abandon high-value AI deployments (in health, welfare, courts) to avoid backlash[^19]
- **Insurance and liability uncertainty** — Gallagher's 2026 benchmarking survey finds AI risk remains largely uninsured, creating financial exposure that suppresses adoption[^20]
- **Democratic erosion** — deepfake-driven distrust of information systems undermines civic participation alongside economic participation[^21]

***

## How Companies Can Build AI Trust

Research-backed strategies, not just platitudes:

- **Responsible AI governance frameworks** — PwC finds that companies with these frameworks are 1.7x more likely to capture AI's economic value[^2]
- **Explainability by design** — workers cite inability to explain AI outputs as their top trust barrier (32%); systems that show their reasoning reduce verification burden[^9]
- **Human-in-the-loop design** — visible human oversight checkpoints, especially for high-stakes decisions, materially increase both user and public trust[^22]
- **Role-specific AI training** — general AI awareness programs don't move the needle; task-specific upskilling does[^23]
- **Transparent failure reporting** — McKinsey's 2026 trust maturity data shows that organisations that openly report AI errors build more trust than those projecting infallibility[^3]
- **Co-design with end users** — involving frontline workers and affected communities in AI design is consistently identified as the highest-leverage trust intervention[^24]

***

## Institutional Trust Deficit: Your Most Differentiated Angle

You're right — and this is underexplored in most AI commentary. The problem isn't just trust in AI systems; it's trust in the **institutions deploying them**. This is where your series could genuinely stand out:

- Edelman's Trust Barometer has documented a long-run decline in institutional trust across government, media, and corporations — and AI is being deployed *into* this pre-existing deficit[^25]
- JP Morgan's May 2026 research explicitly frames institutional trustworthiness as a **competitive moat in the AI era** — companies already trusted by customers will extract far more AI value than equally capable but less-trusted rivals[^25]
- The KPMG/UoM study found **only 23% of Australians trust tech companies** to manage AI safely  — meaning even well-designed AI tools carry the reputational burden of deploying companies[^26]
- APS AI Plan documentation acknowledges this directly: *"AI adoption in the APS requires a strong authorising environment and the maintenance of public trust"* — framing institutional legitimacy as a prerequisite, not an afterthought[^27]
- UNESCO's AI ethics framework makes the same argument at global scale: AI governance must be grounded in pre-existing human rights and accountability norms, not bolted on top of broken institutions[^12]

This "trust stack" framing — where AI trust sits atop institutional trust, which sits atop broader social trust — could be the organising thesis of your entire series, and it's well-supported by the evidence above.
