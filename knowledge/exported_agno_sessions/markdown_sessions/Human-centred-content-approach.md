Senior leaders tend to be 45+; economists and builders skew 30–45. Mix of public sector (government, universities) and private sector (consulting, tech, financial services). High LinkedIn presence; increasingly Substack readers. |
| **What are their interests, needs, and pain points?** | Senior Leaders: need to govern AI responsibly without becoming technical experts; fear being the next Robodebt [^3]. Economists: need economic frameworks that apply to AI governance; frustrated by vague "responsible AI" discourse that doesn't engage with mechanism [^5]. Builders: want to build AI products that are genuinely ethical and commercially viable; worry about being associated with AI hype [^6]. All three share: frustration that AI promises don't match reality, and unease they can't fully articulate. |
| **What motivates them?** | Senior Leaders: protecting institutional reputation while unlocking competitive advantage. Economists: intellectual credibility and relevance in a fast-moving field. Builders: creating meaningful work that lasts — "profitable impact" [^7]. All three: being ahead of their peers, not behind them. |
| **What challenges do they face?** | Senior Leaders: governance frameworks that are declarative rather than structural — policies that *say* the right things but don't *enforce* them [^3]. Economists: regulatory lag and measurement gaps that make evidence-based AI policy nearly impossible [^8][^9]. Builders: the trust gap between what they build and whether affected communities will adopt it [^10]. |
| **How do they currently consume content?** | LinkedIn (primary discovery channel, increasingly noisy with AI-generated content — authentic voices stand out [^11]); Substack (growing fast for longform, especially among senior professionals); industry reports (KPMG, McKinsey, OECD); conferences and workshops. Economists also read academic journals and government policy papers. |
| **What are their expectations from content like this?** | Intellectual rigour — claims backed by named sources, not vibes. Practical applicability — frameworks they can carry into a Monday morning meeting. Honesty about complexity — they distrust content that oversimplifies. A distinctive point of view, not a summary of what everyone else is saying. |
| **What are their potential objections or concerns?** | "Is this just another AI ethics hot take?" — overcome by leading with hard economic data, not moral arguments. "Why should I trust Pete on this?" — overcome by citing peer-reviewed research, RMIT credibility, and the KPMG/University of Melbourne research base [^12][^2]. "Is the trust gap really *that* economically significant?" — overcome by Article 4's multiplier framework and the £29B/year verification burden figure [^13]. |

## Key Message and Context

| Questions to Define Your Message and Context | Answers and Insights |
| :-- | :-- |
| **What is the core message I want to convey?** | Trust is not a soft prerequisite for AI adoption — it is the primary economic variable determining whether AI's potential is realised or wasted. And trust in AI cannot be built in isolation: it rests on institutional trust, which rests on social trust — a "trust stack" that is simultaneously degraded. Solving it requires structural design, not better intentions. |
| **What do I want my audience to *feel*?** | Intellectually energised — that someone has finally named the problem clearly. Slightly uncomfortable — that their organisation may be one of the 80% not capturing AI's value, and they now know why. Cautiously optimistic — that the problem is solvable and the path forward is concrete. |
| **What do I want my audience to *think*?** | That the AI productivity debate has been missing its most important variable. That trust is an economic multiplier with measurable ROI, not a values statement. That structural ethics — building trustworthiness into architecture — is more efficient than declarative ethics at every scale [^3][^4]. That Pete Argent is the person in Australia who understands this at depth. |
| **What do I want my audience to *do*?** | Senior Leaders: reach out about the Structural Trust Framework or a leadership workshop. Economists: attend an ESA-style workshop or cite the series in policy work. Builders: join Vibe Club, register for Good Vibes Only, or apply Future's Edge's ethical AI framework to their work. All: subscribe, share, and reply to the closing question in each article. |
| **What information or research supports my message?** | KPMG/University of Melbourne 47-country trust study [^12]; PwC's 74%/20% concentration finding [^14]; £29B UK verification burden [^13]; Deloitte's 2.5x adoption multiplier [^15]; Gong's \$56B productivity loss [^16]; Pete's own Structural Trust Framework [^3] and Trust as a Multiplier research [^4]; Australian PMC government data [^17]; Robodebt as the definitive Australian case study [^3]. |
| **What context is necessary for my audience to understand the message?** | That AI's economic benefits are real but highly unequally distributed; that the mainstream AI debate focuses on regulation and skills but largely ignores the trust mechanism; that Australia has a measurably worse trust deficit than global peers; and that the trust problem is layered — AI trust, institutional trust, and social trust are all implicated simultaneously. |
| **How does my message relate to my audience's interests and needs?** | For Senior Leaders: explains the governance gap costing them competitive advantage and exposing them to liability. For Economists: provides a new economic lens (trust as multiplier, verification costs, transaction cost reduction) on a topic they must engage with. For Builders: reframes responsible AI from constraint to growth engine. |
| **How can I frame my message to resonate with my audience's values and beliefs?** | Lead with evidence, not exhortation — this audience distrusts moralising. Frame trust as an *efficiency* argument before an *ethics* argument. Position the trust gap as an *economic policy failure*, not a technology failure. Use Australian examples (Robodebt, KPMG/UoM data, APS mandatory requirements) to make it locally resonant rather than abstract. |

## Content Structure and Narrative

| Main Points and Narrative |  |
| :-- | :-- |
| **What are the main points you want to cover?** | (1) AI's economic benefits are real but stalled — and trust is the mechanism nobody is measuring. (2) The trust stack: AI trust cannot be built without institutional trust beneath it. (3) Australia as a case study: the "sceptical adopter" paradox and what it costs. (4) Trust as an economic multiplier: the research most people haven't read. (5) What actually builds trust — structural vs. declarative ethics. (6) The trust dividend: what a high-trust AI economy could look like, and the call to action. |
| **What is a narrative that weaves these points together?** | The series follows a detective arc: something is wrong with the AI economy (evidence: adoption flatlined, benefits concentrated) → the suspects are named and eliminated (regulation, skills, infrastructure) → the real culprit is revealed (the trust stack) → the mechanism is explained (multiplier effect) → the solution is specific (structural ethics, not declarative) → the verdict is delivered (here's what Australia gains if we get this right, and here's your role in it). Each article is a chapter. The tension builds from problem to resolution. |
| **Can the narrative build tension, create empathy, or evoke emotion?** | Yes — Robodebt builds genuine emotional stakes (hundreds of thousands of Australians harmed by a system with all the right policies in place). The "sceptical adopter" paradox creates recognition for Australian readers — they know this feeling. The \$60 billion figure creates urgency. The multiplier framework creates intellectual excitement. The closing provocation — "What in your institution still runs on trust that should run on structure?" — creates productive discomfort [^5]. |
| **Does the narrative align with your key message and resonate with your audience?** | Yes. The detective arc suits an audience that values rigour — they want to follow the logic, not be told the answer at the start. The local focus (Australia, Melbourne, Robodebt, KPMG/UoM) creates relevance. The economic framing respects their intelligence. The resolution — structural trust — offers a concrete path forward rather than leaving readers with anxiety and no direction. |

These function as lead magnets and workshop handouts simultaneously. |
