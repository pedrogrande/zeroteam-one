# what are the challenges faced by institutions and governments in their attempts to realise these benefits?

Governments and institutions face a cluster of interconnected structural, regulatory, and human challenges in converting AI's economic promise into reality.

## Skill Gaps \& Workforce Readiness

- Skill shortages are one of the most universal blockers — governments lag behind the private sector in AI talent, and many institutions lack staff who can meaningfully deploy or oversee AI systems [^1]
- Workers in AI-exposed roles need reskilling, but training pipelines are slow; the IMF warns that without deliberate investment, regions reliant on automatable jobs face sustained employment decline [^2]
- In higher education, faculty resistance, concerns over academic integrity, and lack of pedagogical training stall AI integration even where leadership supports it [^3]


## Legacy Infrastructure \& Data

- Most government agencies run on outdated IT systems incompatible with modern AI tooling, making adoption technically difficult and expensive [^1]
- Data is often fragmented across siloed departments, stored in incompatible formats, or restricted by privacy legislation — precisely when quality, interoperable data is the core input AI needs [^4]
- Without reliable data pipelines, many government AI pilots never scale beyond proof-of-concept [^1]


## The Regulation Dilemma

- Governments face a dual mandate: protect citizens from AI harms *and* foster innovation — and these two goals regularly pull in opposite directions [^5]
- The EU's prescriptive AI Act may stifle innovation and raise compliance costs for businesses, while lighter-touch regimes risk enabling harm and eroding public trust [^5]
- Global fragmentation is a serious problem: companies operating across borders must navigate a **patchwork of contradictory national regulations**, increasing costs and creating incentives to locate AI development in the least-regulated jurisdiction [^5]


## Measurement \& Accountability

- A critical missing piece is **impact measurement frameworks** — without the ability to demonstrate ROI, institutions struggle to justify scaling AI beyond pilots or to prioritise ongoing investment [^1]
- Opacity in AI systems (the "black box" problem) makes it difficult for regulators to audit decisions, assign accountability, or enforce rules even when they exist [^6]
- This drives risk aversion in public-sector bodies, which have stricter needs for transparency and public representation than private firms [^1]


## Equity \& Trust

- The benefits of AI are highly concentrated: 74% currently flow to just 20% of organisations [^7], raising political and social pressure that can slow adoption in equity-sensitive public institutions
- Data used in hiring algorithms, credit scoring, and benefits systems can **reinforce existing inequalities** — undermining public trust and inviting legal challenges [^5]
- Australia's National AI Plan (launched 2026) explicitly acknowledges this tension, framing the challenge as ensuring adoption leads to "shared prosperity" rather than concentrated gain [^8]


## Budget \& Prioritisation

- Tight public budgets mean AI investment competes with immediate service delivery needs, and without clear ROI evidence, AI projects are frequently deprioritised or underfunded [^1]
- The Australian Public Service only launched its formal APS AI Plan in May 2026, illustrating how slowly government-wide frameworks develop compared to private-sector adoption [^9]
