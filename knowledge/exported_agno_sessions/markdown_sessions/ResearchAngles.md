Several, and they are rich. Here are the most promising institutional-economics lenses you could use, with a sentence or two on what each paper would actually study.

***

### 1. Transaction Cost Economics (Coase, Williamson)

Lenses:
- Why do we need a *firm-like* framework (Nexus) at all, instead of ad hoc contracting between humans and agents?
- How does Nexus change the make–or–buy boundary when agents drive marginal transaction costs toward zero? [corpgov.law.harvard](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2024/07/02/williamson-and-coase-transactions-costs-or-rent-seeking-in-the-formation-of-institutions/)

Possible papers:
- "Agentic Coordination and the Nature of the Firm": use Coase’s question (why firms exist) to analyse whether a Nexus-governed organisation becomes *more* or *less* firm-like as coordination costs fall.
- "Proof Templates and the Governance of Contracts": map Williamson’s dimensions (asset specificity, uncertainty, frequency) onto your proof-template + reviewer structure and show how it reconfigures enforcement and monitoring costs.

***

### 2. New Institutional Economics / Governance Structures

Lenses:
- Nexus as a **new organisational form**: part hierarchy (FrameworkOwner, human gates), part market (self-chosen challenges in the DAO), part network (distributed verification). [jstor](https://www.jstor.org/stable/116895)
- How institutional rules (tools, gates, audit log) shape incentives, information flows, and power.

Possible papers:
- "The Nexus Framework as a Polycentric Governance Institution": compare its nested authority (Zone 0, projects, tasks) to polycentric governance models in NIE.
- "Agentic Institutions and Adaptive Integrity": adapt the DAO literature’s criteria for viability (incentive intensity, cooperative adaptation, adaptive integrity) to Nexus as a general-purpose governance tech. [cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-institutional-economics/article/nature-of-the-decentralised-autonomous-organisation/221DE3FF0F7E3CC3CF73CAEF8284DE28)

***

### 3. Agentic AI and Transaction Costs

Lenses:
- How autonomous agents change search, bargaining, and enforcement costs in knowledge work — analogous to work already being done for supply chains and procurement. [linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/impact-ai-agentic-transaction-costs-mro-supply-chains-isak-marais-u5hmf)
- Whether transaction costs fall *uniformly*, or simply shift from coordination to specification and verification.

Possible papers:
- "From Coordination Costs to Specification Costs: A Transaction-Cost View of Agentic Frameworks": show how proof templates, Pattern Libraries, and uncertainty handling move costs upstream into contract design.
- "Autonomous Agents and the Rebundling of Organisational Boundaries": study whether lower coordination costs favour smaller firms, DAOs, or new hybrids.

***

### 4. Complex Adaptive Systems + Institutional Change

Lenses:
- Nexus as a **designed CAS substrate** for institutions: rules that cause governance patterns to emerge and evolve. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8762444/)
- How institutional performance changes over time as patterns (institutional “genes”) are selected, promoted, and retired.

Possible papers:
- "Institutional Evolution in an Agentic Governance Framework": treat patterns in the Pattern Library as institutional rules, and study their emergence/selection/retirement as an evolutionary process.
- "Designing for the Edge of Chaos in Knowledge Institutions": analyse how your principles manage the order–adaptability trade-off, using CAS metrics.

***

### 5. Ostrom / Commons Governance Applied to Knowledge and Models

Lenses:
- Treat shared models, data, and Pattern Libraries as **common-pool resources** that can be overused, under-maintained, or captured. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11320366/table/T1/)
- Apply Ostrom’s design principles (boundaries, monitoring, graduated sanctions, nested enterprises) to governance of shared AI infrastructure.

Possible papers:
- "Governing the AI Knowledge Commons": map Ostrom’s eight principles to Nexus structures (tool boundaries, audit logs, pattern promotion, nested retros).
- "Youth-led DAOs and the Self-Governance of Digital Commons": Future’s Edge as a case where young members co-govern shared AI/knowledge resources using Nexus-like rules.

***

### 6. Principal–Agent and Agency Costs in a World of Agents

Lenses:
- Principal–agent problems change when “agents” are literal AI agents and proofs are structural, not trust-based.
- Agency costs don’t disappear; they shift: from shirking/hidden action to mis-specification, misaligned incentives, and governance capture. [homsy-staging.cambridgecore](https://homsy-staging.cambridgecore.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/221DE3FF0F7E3CC3CF73CAEF8284DE28/S1744137424000341a.pdf/nature_of_the_decentralised_autonomous_organisation.pdf)

Possible papers:
- "Do DAOs Eliminate Agency Costs? An Institutional Analysis of Agentic Verification": argue that Nexus reduces classic agency costs (shirking) but introduces new ones (mis-specified proof templates, pattern bias).
- "Structural Accountability vs. Relational Trust: Rethinking Agency Theory for AI Agents": contrast your structural-ethics model with classic principal–agent governance.

***

### 7. Institutional Design for Youth-Led DAOs (Future’s Edge)

Lenses:
- Future’s Edge as a **laboratory**: youth members choose challenges, earn rewards via verified proofs, and participate in governance.
- How institutional design affects creativity, participation, and perceived fairness.

Possible papers:
- "Institutional Design for Youth-Led Digital Organisations": use Future’s Edge to test whether transparent, proof-based reward structures increase effort and satisfaction compared to conventional youth programs.
- "Decentralised Verification and Trust in Youth Services DAOs": empirically study distributed verification mechanisms and their impact on trust, participation, and conflict.

***

### 8. Institutional Complementarities: Design Thinking + Formal Rules

Lenses:
- Design thinking (problem exploration, ideation, prototyping) as an **informal institution** embedded in formal rules (proof templates, zones).
- How institutionalised cognitive diversity (Six Hats-style agents) interacts with formal governance.

Possible papers:
- "Cognitive Diversity as an Institutional Design Parameter": analyse structured multi-perspective agents as an institutional feature, not just a process choice.
- "From Workshops to Protocols: Institutionalising Design Thinking in Agentic Organisations": how you turned human design rituals into enforceable, repeatable protocols.

***

### 9. Comparative Institutional Analysis: Nexus vs. Traditional Firms vs. DAOs

Lenses:
- Use comparative institutional analysis to ask: under what conditions is a Nexus-governed organisation more efficient/equitable/adaptive than:
  - A traditional firm hierarchy
  - A pure market
  - A blockchain-native DAO without rich agentic infrastructure [cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/governing-complexity/evolutionary-institutional-change-and-performance-in-polycentric-governance/D49F2410957F6E01B4CBB0F8D14E3525)

Possible papers:
- "Nexus, Firm, or DAO? A Comparative Institutional Analysis of Knowledge Work Governance"
- "Agentic Frameworks as a New Institutional Form": argue Nexus is a distinct organisational technology with its own efficiency and equity profile.

***

If you want a concrete starting point, the cleanest first paper is probably:

> **"From Coordination Costs to Specification Costs: An Institutional-Economics View of Agentic Frameworks."**

It lets you:
- Start from Coase/Williamson (transaction costs, firm boundaries) [josephmahoney.web.illinois](https://josephmahoney.web.illinois.edu/BA549_Fall%202010/Session%203/Williamson%20(1979).pdf)
- Introduce Nexus as a case where coordination and monitoring costs fall sharply
- Show how new costs arise (proof design, pattern governance, ethical gates)
- Position Future’s Edge as an applied testbed.
