# Does the original discussion on the 6-episode Substack series still fit as-is? Does it need refining or re-thinking?

What other media formats could we explore beyond written articles?

What press/media strategy could help amplify our message?

The original 6-article series holds up strategically, but it needs some targeted refinements now that we have a clearer strategy — and yes, there are meaningful media format expansions and a press angle worth pursuing seriously.

***

## Does the 6-Article Series Still Fit?

**Mostly yes — with three targeted refinements.**

The series was designed before the Playing to Win strategy was formalised. Now that the strategy is explicit, three misalignments emerge:

### What Still Holds

- The detective narrative arc (problem → diagnosis → mechanism → solution → call to action) is sound and distinctive[^1][^2]
- The evidence base is strong and current
- The three-audience structure (Senior Leaders, Economists, Builders) works across the series without fracturing it
- The paywall split at Articles 4–6 is strategically correct — it converts readers who've adopted the vocabulary into paid subscribers


### What Needs Refining

**1. Article 1 needs a sharper strategic opening gambit**

The original framing — *"The \$15 Trillion Problem Nobody Is Talking About"* — leads with scale. But the Playing to Win strategy says we win by *naming what others are circling around*, not by adding to the scale conversation. The revision: open with the paradox, not the number. Something like *"Australia Has an AI Policy. 9% of Australians Know It Exists."*  — a specific, local, undeniable failure that immediately separates Zero Team from the generic AI productivity discourse. The trillion-dollar data arrives inside the article, not as the hook.[^3]

**2. The series needs an explicit strategic anchor — a named concept introduced in Article 1 and carried through all six**

Right now the series has a theme (the trust gap) but not a *Zero Team concept* that readers will carry away and repeat. The Playing to Win strategy says vocabulary ownership is the mechanism of gravity. The fix: introduce the **Trust Stack** as the named Zero Team framework in Article 1, and ensure every subsequent article is explicitly framed as exploring one layer of that stack. By Article 6, readers don't just understand the argument — they own a model.[^2][^1]

**3. Article 6 is underloaded as a commercial converter**

The original Article 6 (*The Trust Dividend*) is visionary but passive — it imagines a better future without directing readers to act. Given the strategy's commercial intent, Article 6 should do three things the current version doesn't: (1) name Zero Team explicitly as the practitioner who can help organisations move from declarative to structural trust; (2) introduce the Structural Trust Framework as the implementation path; and (3) include a direct, low-friction call to action (a 30-minute diagnostic conversation, not a sales pitch). The article stays visionary — it just adds a door at the end of the corridor.[^4][^1]

## Media Format Expansion

The Substack series is the Foundation layer. These formats extend it into Signal and Proof — each chosen for a specific audience and a specific job.

### Format 1: The Companion Podcast — *"Off the Record"*

**What it is:** A short (20–25 min) audio conversation attached to each Substack article, released the same day. Not a standalone podcast — a companion that lets people who prefer audio consume the same ideas without reading 3,000 words.

**Why it works:** Podcasts build parasocial trust faster than text. The economist and senior leader audiences specifically over-index on audio during commutes. Substack natively supports podcast episodes within the same subscription  — existing subscribers get audio automatically, removing the need to build a separate audience.[^5][^6]

**Format:** Pete's voice only — not an interview. More like a "director's commentary" track. What's the thinking *behind* the article? What didn't make the final cut? What question is this article really trying to answer? This is distinct from the written piece and rewards both formats.

**Production reality check:** Given the Organiser weakness, this needs a simple, repeatable system — same recording setup, same 3-question structure each episode, batch recorded. Not a production endeavour; a thinking-out-loud endeavour.[^7]

***

### Format 2: The Framework Visual Series — LinkedIn Carousels

**What it is:** Each article's core framework rendered as a 6–8 slide LinkedIn carousel — the trust stack diagram, the multiplier model, the declarative vs. structural ethics distinction, the four conformance profiles.[^1][^2]

**Why it works:** Carousels are the highest-engagement format on LinkedIn for professional audiences. More importantly, they travel independently — someone shares the carousel without ever clicking through to the Substack, which is fine, because the carousel carries the Zero Team vocabulary and attribution. It's a framework distribution mechanism, not a traffic driver.[^8]

**Strategic purpose:** This is how the vocabulary gets seeded into conversations Pete isn't part of. A board director shares the trust stack carousel in a governance committee. A policy advisor drops it into a briefing note. The framework is in the room before Pete is.

***

### Format 3: The "Proof" Video — Short-Form Documentary Clips

**What it is:** 2–3 minute, single-camera, high-quality clips filmed at the Good Vibes Only hackathon, ESA Victoria workshop, and any speaking engagements. Not promotional content — captured thinking. Pete at a whiteboard. A workshop participant having a realisation. The moment a framework clicks for a room.[^9][^10]

**Why it works:** Video is now the primary format for building trust with audiences who don't yet know you. Critically, these clips serve as *proof* — they show Zero Team frameworks being used by real practitioners in real rooms, which is categorically more credible than describing the frameworks in text. Senior leaders deciding whether to hire a consultant watch video to assess presence and credibility before they read anything.[^5]

**Distribution:** LinkedIn native video (not YouTube links — LinkedIn deprioritises external links). Short clips teasing the article or framework, with the full conversation linked in comments.

***

### Format 4: The Annual Research Note — Not a Report, a *Provocation*

**What it is:** A single-page, beautifully designed annual document — *"The Australian AI Trust Index: [Year]"* — published December each year. Not 40 pages of analysis. One number, one trend, one question.

**Why it works:** The Big 4 publish massive annual reports that nobody reads. Zero Team publishes one page that everyone can quote. The format signals confidence — we're not hedging with volume, we're betting on precision. It becomes an annual media moment, a PR hook, and the document that practitioners cite in board papers and policy submissions.[^11][^8]

**The number:** An aggregated "AI Trust Score" for Australian organisations, built from the publicly available KPMG/UoM, Deloitte, PwC, and government data we're already using. Zero Team doesn't collect the data — we synthesise it into a single, citable, quotable index.[^12][^13]

***

### Format 5: The Substack Discussion Thread — *"Ask the Room"*

**What it is:** After each article, a pinned discussion thread (Substack's native community feature ) with a single question directed at subscribers: "What in your organisation still runs on trust that should run on structure?"  Or: "Which of your AI governance controls is declarative right now — and what would it take to make it structural?"[^6][^10]

**Why it works:** These threads do three things. They generate real-world intelligence from practitioners (which feeds subsequent articles). They create community — the feeling that subscribers are thinking together, not just reading one person's thoughts. And they surface the specific anxieties, language, and situations of the target audiences, which makes every subsequent piece sharper.

**Commercial signal:** Subscribers who reply substantively to discussion threads are the highest-intent prospects. They've demonstrated they're wrestling with the problem, not just interested in the topic.

## Press and Media Strategy

This is where the strategy can accelerate significantly — because the media landscape has a structural gap that Zero Team's positioning fills precisely.

### The Core Media Insight

Australian technology journalism is currently caught between two poles: uncritical AI boosterism (adoption is good, more is better) and vague AI scepticism (trust issues, ethical concerns). Neither pole has a rigorous economic mechanism for *why* the gap between promise and reality exists. Zero Team's trust gap framing gives journalists exactly what they're missing: a specific, evidence-backed, Australian-grounded explanation with a named culprit and a concrete solution. That is a story.[^14][^15][^16]

### Target Outlets and Angles

| Outlet | Audience | Angle | Hook |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Australian Financial Review** | Senior leaders, board directors, investors | Economic cost of the trust gap — \$60B opportunity, £29B verification burden [^17] | "The hidden cost of AI your P\&L isn't capturing" |
| **The Mandarin** | Public servants, policy advisors | APS mandatory requirements activating June 2026 — and why they won't be enough [^18][^1] | "Australia has an AI policy. Here's why it won't prevent the next Robodebt" |
| **InnovationAus** | Australian tech ecosystem | Structural Trust Framework as original Australian IP in the global AI governance market | "The Melbourne framework that's redefining how organisations build trustworthy AI" |
| **Company Director (AICD)** | Board directors | The governance gap — declarative vs. structural, and what boards need to ask their leadership [^19][^1] | "Your AI ethics policy isn't working. Here's the evidence — and the fix" |
| **The Guardian Australia** | General educated public, policy influencers | Robodebt as the case study — the architecture of AI harm, and what structural governance would have changed [^1] | Human interest meets systemic diagnosis |
| **ABC Tech/News** | Broad Australian public | Australia's sceptical adopter paradox — we use AI more than we trust it [^20] | "Why Australians are the world's most suspicious AI users — and what it's costing us" |


***

### The PR Playbook

**1. The Article-to-Pitch Pipeline**
Each Substack article generates one targeted media pitch — not a press release, a direct email to a specific journalist with a specific angle. The pitch includes: one counter-intuitive data point from the article, the Zero Team framework that explains it, and an offer to provide original commentary or an op-ed. This is journalist service, not publicity seeking.

**2. The Op-Ed Strategy**
Articles 2 (the trust stack) and 5 (structural vs. declarative ethics) are the strongest candidates for op-ed placement, because they make a genuinely contestable argument — not just a data summary. Target: AFR or The Mandarin for Article 5; The Guardian or ABC for Article 2 (Robodebt angle). The op-ed is a shortened, sharper version of the Substack piece — not a reprint, a distillation. This drives Substack subscribers and credibility simultaneously.[^11]

**3. The Expert Source Play**
Register with Muck Rack or Source Bottle as an expert source on: AI governance, AI trust economics, responsible AI, agentic AI systems, AI policy in Australia. When journalists are writing on these topics — and they will be, increasingly, with the APS requirements activating and every new AI failure becoming a media story — Pete gets called. One media quote in the AFR or The Mandarin is worth more for inbound enquiries than ten LinkedIn posts.

**4. The Regulatory Moment Strategy**
The APS mandatory AI requirements activate in June 2026. This is a predictable, dateable media moment — the kind journalists plan for in advance. Zero Team should be positioned as the go-to commentator for that moment: a prepared statement ready, a media list contacted two weeks in advance, a companion Substack piece published the same day the requirements go live. That piece — *"The APS AI Requirements Are Live. Here's What They Don't Cover"* — positions Zero Team as the expert who goes deeper than the announcement, not just faster.[^18]

**5. The Research Partnership Angle**
The KPMG/University of Melbourne trust research program is the most credible AI trust data source in Australia. A formal or informal association — even a single co-authored commentary piece or joint event appearance — gives Zero Team's frameworks the institutional credibility that accelerates media pickup. This is a relationship to actively cultivate, not a passive hope.[^12]

***

### The One Thing to Do First

Before any of the above: **write the 300-word pitch email for a single journalist at The Mandarin**, targeting the APS requirements activation in June. It has a hard news hook, a specific date, a Zero Team angle that nobody else has, and a clear expert offer. Send it. The response — or lack of one — will tell you more about what the media strategy needs than another month of planning.[^4][^11]
