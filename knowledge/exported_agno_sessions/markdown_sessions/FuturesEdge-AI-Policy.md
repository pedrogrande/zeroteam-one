# Future's Edge 'AI for good' values and policy

Future’s Edge can credibly lead in “AI for Good” if your values and policies do two things: (1) clearly align with UNESCO/UN/WEF responsible AI principles, and (2) are visibly operationalised through your existing DAO, trust score, and governance structures. [^7_1][^7_2][^7_3][^7_4][^7_5]

Below is a concise set of **AI for Good values** plus a **policy skeleton**, followed by what else you need in place to have authority.

***

## AI for good values

These should sit alongside (or as a focused subset of) your existing core values like trust, transparency, ethical innovation, and social impact. [^7_4][^7_3][^7_6]

1. **Do no harm, maximise shared benefit**
    - AI systems deployed in or via Future’s Edge must demonstrably avoid foreseeable harm and aim to advance human well-being, social equity, and environmental sustainability, in line with UNESCO’s “do no harm” and SDG-aligned framing. [^7_7][^7_8][^7_2]
2. **Trust by design**
    - AI must be designed to be understandable, contestable, and auditable; black-box systems in high-stakes contexts are not acceptable. Transparency, explainability, and human oversight are non‑negotiable trust features, not optional extras. [^7_7][^7_1][^7_8][^7_9]
3. **Fairness and inclusion**
    - AI initiatives must proactively address bias, discrimination, and exclusion, especially toward the marginalised communities many AI for Good projects claim to serve. Diverse data, participatory design, and equity impact checks are required. [^7_7][^7_8][^7_2]
4. **Human rights, autonomy, and dignity**
    - All AI work must respect privacy, freedom of expression, and agency, consistent with UNESCO and UN principles; no Future’s Edge project should contribute to surveillance, social scoring, or rights‑eroding uses. [^7_7][^7_10][^7_2]
5. **Accountability and shared responsibility**
    - There is always a clearly identified accountable human (or governance body) for each AI system’s purpose, data, and impact. The DAO’s governance mechanisms must be structured to handle AI-related responsibility, not diffuse it. [^7_11][^7_7][^7_2][^7_12]
6. **Open knowledge and reciprocal value**
    - Methods, lessons, and non-sensitive artefacts from AI for Good projects should be shared openly (where safe), so others can reuse and improve them. Benefits should be shared fairly with the communities whose data, labour, or lived experience contribute. [^7_7][^7_4][^7_3]
7. **Youth leadership with intergenerational stewardship**
    - Youth lead the design and experimentation; experienced advisors help ensure alignment with global ethical standards and legal frameworks (UNESCO, UN, WEF). This matches your “youth-led, advisory board supported” governance. [^7_13][^7_3][^7_12]

## AI for good policy – key sections

You already have a strong general policy framework and constitution; an AI-specific policy can plug into that and reference UNESCO/UN principles explicitly. [^7_3][^7_6][^7_14][^7_2]

**1. Scope and purpose**

- Define what the policy covers: any AI tools, models, datasets, or AI-enabled features used on the platform, in missions, or delivered to partners. [^7_11][^7_7]
- State the purpose: to ensure all AI work at Future’s Edge advances human rights, trust, and social good, in line with UNESCO’s Recommendation on the Ethics of AI and the UN’s principles on ethical AI use. [^7_10][^7_2]

**2. Alignment with external standards**

- Explicitly reference:
    - UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (human rights, transparency, fairness, human oversight). [^7_1][^7_10][^7_8]
    - UN Principles for the Ethical Use of AI (do no harm, defined purpose, safety, fairness, privacy, human oversight, accountability, inclusion). [^7_2]
    - WEF Responsible AI Playbook (strategy, governance, development “plays”). [^7_15][^7_16]

**3. Design and development principles**
For any AI project:

- **Defined purpose and proportionality**: clear statement of problem, beneficiaries, and why AI is necessary vs simpler tools. [^7_7][^7_2]
- **Data governance**: data minimisation, consent, rights of data subjects, storage rules, and clear bans on unsafe data uses (e.g. facial recognition for surveillance). [^7_7][^7_2][^7_8]
- **Bias and fairness checks**: minimum requirements for dataset review, bias testing, and community review, appropriate to the project’s risk level. [^7_7][^7_8]
- **Transparency \& explainability**: documentation (model cards), user-facing explanations, and clear disclosure whenever AI is used in decision support. [^7_7][^7_1][^7_8]
- **Human-in-the-loop**: for any high-stakes or rights‑affecting use, mandate human review and a clear appeal/escalation path. [^7_7][^7_2][^7_9]

**4. Governance and accountability structures**

Connect to your existing DAO and councils. [^7_12][^7_3][^7_6]

- **Responsible AI Council / squad**: a cross‑functional group (youth reps, technical members, ethics/legal advisor, impacted community rep when relevant) that:
    - Reviews higher‑risk AI proposals and missions.
    - Maintains an inventory of AI systems in use (risk‑tagged).
    - Oversees incident reporting and response for AI-related harms. [^7_7][^7_15][^7_17]
- **RACI for each AI system**: define model owner, data steward, governance steward, and escalation contacts. [^7_17]
- **Stage gates**: no deployment of medium/high‑risk AI features without passing checks for: data protection, bias review, documentation, and human oversight plan. [^7_17][^7_18]

**5. Participation, inclusion, and community voice**

- Require participatory design for AI for Good missions: affected communities or domain NGOs must be involved in problem definition and evaluation, not just testing. [^7_7][^7_19]
- Provide avenues for members and beneficiaries to raise concerns or contest AI‑influenced decisions; route these through your conflict resolution mechanisms. [^7_7][^7_6][^7_12]

**6. Education and capacity building**

- Commit to building AI literacy and ethical awareness in all members engaging with AI missions: short foundations on bias, privacy, and responsible use embedded in the platform. [^7_7][^7_15]
- Offer advanced pathways where youth can contribute to governance (e.g. serving on the Responsible AI Council) as part of missions, with trust score and recognition. [^7_5][^7_12]

**7. Economic fairness and anti‑exploitation**

- Ensure AI for Good projects don’t extract free labour or data from communities without fair benefit sharing. [^7_7][^7_2]
- For monetised outputs (e.g. tools developed in missions), define transparent rules for attribution and revenue sharing via your incentives framework and DAO treasury. [^7_5][^7_3]

**8. Monitoring, evaluation, and continuous improvement**

- Require basic “AI impact logs” for significant projects: what was deployed, where, metrics on benefits/harms, complaints, and changes made. [^7_7][^7_15]
- Commit to periodic review (e.g. annual) of the AI for Good policy in light of new regulations, UNESCO/UN updates, and community feedback. [^7_12][^7_14]

## What else you need to have authority

To be taken seriously as an AI for Good authority (not just well‑intentioned), you’ll want concrete structures and artefacts:

1. **Published AI for Good policy and values page**
    - Public, clearly written, linked from your main site and docs, explicitly aligned to UNESCO/UN/WEF frameworks. [^7_1][^7_2][^7_15]
    - Include a simple visual showing your “trust by design” pipeline: Purpose → Data → Design → Governance → Monitoring.
2. **Responsible AI governance body in practice**
    - A named Responsible AI Council (or equivalent) with youth and advisor membership, a visible mandate, and a basic charter. [^7_12][^7_15]
    - Documented process for how AI‑related proposals flow through that body.
3. **AI system inventory and risk classification**
    - Even if small to start, keep a live list of AI tools and models used in the platform, plus risk levels and owners; this is standard in mature organisations and aligns with WEF and industry playbooks. [^7_11][^7_17][^7_15]
4. **Starter templates and tools**
    - Lightweight but real artefacts:
        - AI project proposal template (with purpose, beneficiaries, risk, data plan).
        - Model card template.
        - AI impact assessment checklist (for higher‑risk use cases). [^7_7][^7_17][^7_18]
5. **External advisory relationships**
    - A small external advisory circle (e.g. someone linked to UNESCO AI work, a local AI ethics researcher, a practitioner from an NGO doing AI for Good) you can list as advisors or partners. This signals you’re plugged into the broader standards ecosystem. [^7_1][^7_8][^7_7]
6. **Demonstration projects with transparent write‑ups**
    - 1–3 flagship AI for Good missions where you can publish:
        - The problem, partners, data, risks.
        - The governance you applied.
        - What went right, what went wrong, and what you changed. [^7_7]
    - These case studies become proof you live your values.
7. **Member training as a differentiator**
    - Position Future’s Edge as a place where young people don’t just *use* AI for Good, but learn how to govern it responsibly. Tie this directly to UNESCO/UN principles and the WEF playbook. [^7_7][^7_8][^7_15]
