## Task 3.1.1 — AI in Our Industry: What's Actually Happening

***

### Beyond the headlines

AI coverage in the media tends toward two extremes: either AI is transforming everything right now, or the promises are overblown and the reality is disappointing. Neither framing is particularly useful for a professional trying to understand what AI means for their work.

The truth, across most industries, is more specific and more interesting than either headline. AI is already delivering genuine value in some areas. It is falling short of expectations in others. And the professionals who are best positioned are not those who have adopted the most tools, but those who have developed the judgment to distinguish productive use from hype.

This task gives you an accurate, grounded picture of where AI adoption sits in your sector right now, including what has worked, what hasn't, and what the realistic outlook is for your profession.

***

### The current state of AI adoption across industries

AI adoption across business sectors has moved through several distinct phases. Understanding where your industry sits in that arc is more useful than any generic claim about AI's transformative potential.

**Early adoption phase**
Organisations are experimenting with AI, typically through individual staff using general-purpose tools independently or through isolated pilot projects. There is limited organisational policy, variable quality of outputs, and significant variation in how individual employees engage with AI. Most value is captured informally rather than through structured processes.

**Active integration phase**
Organisations have moved beyond experimentation and are actively integrating AI into specific workflows. Approved tool lists exist. Some functions have developed standard prompt patterns or workflow templates. Policy frameworks are in place but still maturing. The organisation is learning what works and building the governance structures to support broader adoption.

**Embedded practice phase**
AI is a normal part of how work gets done in key functions. Workflows are designed with AI assistance in mind. Employees are expected to have baseline AI literacy as part of their professional competence. Governance, oversight, and accountability frameworks are established and regularly reviewed.

Most organisations across most industries are currently in the first or second phase, with pockets of more advanced practice in specific functions or teams. The pace of movement between phases is accelerating.

***

### Use cases delivering genuine value across sectors

The following use cases have demonstrated consistent value across a wide range of industries and professional contexts. They share a common characteristic: AI handles the generative or structural component of the work, while humans contribute the contextual judgment, professional knowledge, and accountability that the task requires.

**Document drafting and editing**
First-draft generation for reports, briefings, correspondence, proposals, and internal communications. Across industries from legal services to healthcare administration to financial advice, professionals are finding that AI-assisted drafting reduces the time spent on structure and language, freeing attention for the content and judgment that require expertise.

Value is highest where: the task is recurring, the output structure is predictable, and the human has clear domain knowledge to apply in reviewing and refining the draft.

**Summarisation and information extraction**
Condensing long documents, meeting transcripts, research reports, and regulatory updates into usable summaries. This is consistently among the most time-efficient AI applications across professional contexts because the underlying task is time-consuming and cognitively straightforward, but the volume of material involved makes it a significant burden without assistance.

Value is highest where: source materials are text-heavy, the audience needs accessible rather than comprehensive coverage, and the human can assess whether the summary has captured the right emphasis.

**Research support and horizon scanning**
Using AI to generate structured overviews of topics, identify relevant considerations, or map the landscape of a subject before diving into primary research. This is not a replacement for authoritative research, but it is an efficient way to establish initial orientation on a topic and identify the questions worth investigating.

Value is highest where: the human uses AI output as a starting point for further investigation rather than an endpoint, and where claims are verified before being acted on.

**Template and process documentation**
Generating first drafts of process guides, checklists, standard operating procedures, and communication templates. Across operations, HR, compliance, and administration functions, this use case delivers consistent efficiency gains because the underlying task is structured and repetitive.

Value is highest where: subject matter experts review outputs for accuracy and fitness for purpose before the templates enter active use.

**Meeting preparation and follow-up**
Drafting agendas, preparing briefing notes, generating action registers from meeting notes, and producing follow-up communications. This use case is particularly well-suited to AI assistance because the inputs are typically structured (attendees, topics, decisions, actions) and the outputs follow predictable formats.

Value is highest where: the human retains responsibility for the accuracy of attributed actions and decisions, and all outputs are reviewed before distribution.

***

### Where AI has fallen short of expectations

Honest accounts of AI adoption include the cases where the technology has not delivered the anticipated value. The patterns are instructive.

**Tasks requiring deep contextual judgment**
AI performs consistently below expectations in tasks where the quality of the output depends primarily on a nuanced understanding of organisational context, professional relationships, or sector-specific knowledge that is not in the training data. The output is generic where it needed to be specific.

**High-stakes professional advice**
Attempts to use AI to generate professional advice in regulated domains without adequate expert review have consistently created problems. In legal, financial, medical, and compliance contexts, the fluent confidence of AI outputs is particularly dangerous because it creates a false impression of reliability. The error rate in these contexts is unacceptably high for unsupervised use.

**Complex multi-step reasoning**
Tasks that require sustained logical reasoning across multiple steps, where errors in early steps compound through the analysis, have produced unreliable results. AI performs well on individual reasoning steps but poorly on complex chains of inference where the whole matters more than any single part.

**Situations requiring current or local information**
Any task that depends on recent events, live data, local regulatory context, or organisational specifics the model doesn't have access to will produce outputs that are plausibly framed but factually unreliable. This is a structural limitation, not a tool quality issue, and it applies regardless of which AI tool is used.

***

### What this means for your professional development

Across industries, AI literacy is becoming a baseline professional expectation rather than a specialist skill. The specific implications vary by sector and role, but several themes are consistent.

Professionals who develop the ability to use AI effectively, evaluate outputs critically, and apply appropriate oversight are becoming more productive and more valuable than those who either avoid AI or use it without adequate judgment. The gap between these groups is widening as AI capabilities develop.

At the same time, the professionals who are developing the most durable advantage are not those who have adopted the most tools. They are those who have built the underlying capability: the judgment to know when AI helps and when it doesn't, the habits to use it responsibly, and the ethical and legal awareness to understand the obligations their profession places on them.

This course is designed to build that capability. The tools will change. The capability won't.

## Task 3.1.2 — Your Organisation's AI Strategy and Priorities

***

### Why organisational context matters

Individual AI literacy is necessary but not sufficient. How you apply your AI skills at work is shaped by your organisation's strategy, the tools it has approved, the governance structures it has put in place, and the direction it is heading. Working with AI at work means working within that organisational context, not around it.

This task gives you the organisational picture: where this organisation is heading with AI, what that means for your role, and how the skills you've built in this course connect to the organisation's priorities.

***

### The spectrum of organisational AI maturity

Organisations approach AI adoption with significant variation in strategy, appetite, and governance sophistication. Note that this spectrum describes how an organisation chooses to manage risk, which is distinct from how far along the adoption journey it has travelled: a cautious organisation can still be deeply into active integration, applying careful governance to a wide range of AI use; an early-stage organisation can take an exploratory, low-governance approach that moves fast without adequate controls. Both dimensions matter, and understanding them separately helps you read your own organisation's position accurately.

**AI-cautious organisations**
These organisations are taking a deliberate, measured approach to AI adoption, often because of regulatory exposure, data sensitivity, or a considered view that the risk-benefit profile does not yet justify broad deployment. In these contexts, the appropriate employee posture is to follow guidance closely, use only explicitly approved tools, and raise questions through the right channels rather than experimenting independently.

**AI-exploring organisations**
These organisations are actively investigating AI's potential but have not yet established mature governance. Approved tool lists may be partial or evolving. Policy frameworks exist but may not cover every situation. In these contexts, the appropriate posture combines individual initiative with careful adherence to the spirit of available guidance, and a willingness to flag gaps and seek clarification.

**AI-integrating organisations**
These organisations have committed to AI as part of how work gets done and are actively building the infrastructure, governance, and capability to support it. Approved tools are clearly defined, expectations of employees are explicit, and there are established channels for questions, feedback, and incident reporting. This course is most likely to sit in this organisational context.

***

### What an organisational AI strategy typically covers

Regardless of where your organisation sits on the maturity spectrum, an AI strategy addresses four core questions. The content of the answers will be provided by your organisation in the section below.

**What we are trying to achieve**
The strategic rationale for AI adoption: efficiency gains, capability development, competitive positioning, service improvement, or a combination. Understanding the organisational goal gives your individual AI use purposeful direction.

**What tools are approved and for what purposes**
The approved tool list is the practical boundary within which your AI use should operate. Tools are approved based on assessments of data handling, security, regulatory compliance, and fitness for purpose. Using unapproved tools, even tools that appear similar to approved ones, bypasses that assessment and creates organisational risk.

**What the organisation expects from employees**
The baseline of AI literacy, responsible use, and governance adherence that the organisation requires from all staff. This course is typically part of meeting that expectation.

**How AI connects to your role and function**
The specific ways in which AI is expected to support the work of your team and function, including any current initiatives or priorities that are directly relevant to your day-to-day work.

## Task 3.1.3 — Task Decomposition in Your Role

***

### Returning to decomposition with a sharper lens

In Task 2.1.1, you learned to break any work task into components and classify each as AI-led, AI-assisted, or human-only. In Task 2.1.2, you applied that thinking to your work day in general terms.

This task applies decomposition more specifically: to the tasks most common in your role, in the context of your organisation's approved tools and AI strategy. The goal is a practical, role-specific picture of where AI fits into your work, grounded in what your organisation actually supports and what your professional context actually requires.

***

### How organisational context shapes decomposition

The same task can decompose differently depending on the organisational context. Two factors in particular shape how decomposition applies in your specific setting.

**Approved tools and their capabilities**
The decomposition of a task into AI-led, AI-assisted, and human-only components is partly determined by what tools are available and what they can do. A task that could be AI-led with a tool that has access to your internal documents may need to remain human-led if the only approved tool is a general-purpose assistant without that integration.

When you apply the decomposition framework in your role, do so with reference to the tools your organisation has approved, not tools you may have used elsewhere or seen demonstrated.

**Professional and regulatory obligations**
Some components that might technically be AI-led in a generic context must remain human-led in your professional context because of regulatory requirements, professional conduct obligations, or organisational policy. The classification of components is not purely a question of what AI can do. It is a question of what AI is permitted to do in your context.

***

### Decomposition walkthrough: common professional tasks

The following worked examples cover task types common across a wide range of professional roles. For each, the components are classified using the three-category framework, with notes on what human input is required and where organisational context shapes the classification.

***

**Task: Preparing a report for a senior audience**

| Component | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gathering and reviewing source materials | Human-only | Requires professional judgment about which sources are authoritative and relevant |
| Drafting the executive summary | AI-led | Human provides key conclusions; AI drafts the summary for review and editing |
| Structuring the body of the report | AI-assisted | AI can propose a structure; human confirms it reflects the actual argument and priorities |
| Writing the analysis sections | AI-assisted | AI contributes to drafting; human provides the analytical judgment and contextual knowledge |
| Formulating recommendations | Human-only | Recommendations carry professional accountability and must reflect organisational context |
| Final review and sign-off | Human-only | Non-delegable; the person who signs the report is responsible for its contents |

***

**Task: Responding to a complex stakeholder enquiry**

| Component | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Assessing the nature and urgency of the enquiry | Human-only | Requires judgment about relationship context, sensitivity, and appropriate escalation |
| Researching the factual basis for a response | AI-assisted | AI can help structure the research; human must verify accuracy of specific claims |
| Drafting the initial response | AI-led | Human provides the key facts and desired tone; AI drafts for review |
| Reviewing for tone, relationship context, and appropriateness | Human-only | Requires knowledge of the stakeholder, the history, and the organisational context |
| Final review and sending | Human-only | The sender is responsible for the communication |

***

**Task: Preparing for a team meeting**

| Component | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Identifying agenda items | Human-only | Requires knowledge of current priorities, team dynamics, and what needs discussion |
| Drafting the agenda document | AI-led | Standard format; human provides items and AI structures the document |
| Preparing briefing notes on agenda items | AI-assisted | AI can draft from provided inputs; human reviews for accuracy and completeness |
| Generating pre-reading summaries from existing documents | AI-led | Well-suited to AI with strong human review before distribution |

| Producing an action register from meeting notes | AI-led | AI drafts from structured notes; human verifies accuracy of attributed actions |

***

**Task: Developing a business case or proposal**

| Component | Classification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Defining the problem or opportunity | Human-only | Requires professional and organisational judgment |
| Researching comparable cases or options | AI-assisted | AI supports research; human evaluates relevance and reliability |
| Drafting the narrative sections | AI-led | Human provides the core argument; AI assists with structure and language |
| Financial or quantitative analysis | Human-only | Requires access to internal data and professional judgment about assumptions |

***

### Building your personal decomposition map

Using the framework and examples above, work through the role-based worksheet to map at least three of your own most frequent and significant tasks.

For each task:

1. List the main components (aim for 4 to 7 components per task)
2. Classify each component as AI-led, AI-assisted, or human-only
3. Note what approved tool you would use for AI-led and AI-assisted components
4. Note what specific human input or oversight is required at each step

This map is a practical tool, not a one-time exercise. Revisit it as your role evolves, as new tools are approved, and as your own AI skills develop.

## Task 3.1.4 — Use Cases and Templates for Your Work

***

### From principles to practice

Everything built in this course has been leading to this: a set of ready-to-use prompt patterns and workflow templates for the AI use cases most relevant to your work.

These templates are not scripts to follow without thinking. They are starting points: well-constructed prompt patterns that embody the four-element structure from Task 1.2.2, built around realistic professional use cases, ready to be adapted and added to the prompt library you started in Task 1.2.7.

Each template includes:

- A worked prompt example using the four-element structure
- A note on the human oversight required before the output is used
- A note on data handling considerations specific to this use case
- Guidance on how to adapt the template for your specific role and context

***

### Template 1: Summarising a document for a non-specialist audience

**Use case:** You have a lengthy report, policy document, regulation, or research paper and need to communicate its key points to an audience who will not read the full document.

**Worked prompt:**
> You are a professional communicator preparing a briefing for a senior leadership team. Summarise the following document in plain language. Identify the three most important points for a reader who needs to make a decision based on this content but will not read the full document. Use short paragraphs and avoid jargon. Where the document contains specific figures or statistics that are central to the key points, include them. Flag any sections you found ambiguous or that you think warrant further attention from a subject matter expert.
>
> [Paste document text here]

**Human oversight required:** Review the summary against the original document to confirm the three key points accurately represent the document's most important content. Verify any figures included. Assess whether the summary's emphasis reflects the actual priority for this specific audience.

**Data handling note:** Only paste document text into this prompt if the document does not contain confidential, personal, commercially sensitive, or client-specific information, or if you are using an approved internal tool with appropriate data protections. For sensitive documents, use anonymised or generalised versions.

**Adapting this template:**

- For a different audience, change "senior leadership team" to the specific audience (e.g. "a frontline team with limited background in this area," "a board with limited technical knowledge")
- For a different output format, change "three most important points" to "a five-point FAQ" or "a one-page briefing note with recommendations"
- To increase scrutiny of the summary, add: "After producing the summary, identify two things you have left out that a thorough reader of the original might consider important"

***

### Template 2: Drafting a professional communication

**Use case:** You need to draft an email, letter, memo, or message to a stakeholder, client, colleague, or external contact.

**Worked prompt:**
> Draft a professional email on my behalf. The email is from [role/title] to [recipient description and relationship]. The purpose of the email is [clear statement of objective]. The key points to communicate are: [list 2 to 4 key points]. The tone should be [formal/professional but warm/direct and brief]. The email should be no longer than [word count or number of paragraphs]. Do not include legal disclaimers, excessive hedging, or phrases like "I hope this finds you well." End with a clear call to action or next step.

**Human oversight required:** Review for tone and relationship appropriateness before sending. Confirm all factual claims are accurate. Assess whether the call to action is appropriate for this stage of the relationship or process. Edit for any personal or organisational voice elements the template will not capture.

**Data handling note:** Do not include the recipient's personal information, confidential business details, or sensitive content in the prompt unless you are using an approved internal tool.

**Adapting this template:**

- For a sensitive situation (complaint, difficult news, apology), add: "The tone must be empathetic and avoid any language that could sound defensive or dismissive"
- For a first contact, add: "Introduce [role/organisation] briefly in the opening sentence"
- For a follow-up, add: "Reference the previous conversation or meeting briefly in the opening"

***

### Template 3: Preparing a meeting agenda and briefing notes

**Use case:** You are preparing for a recurring or one-off meeting and need a structured agenda and supporting briefing notes for attendees.

**Worked prompt:**
> Prepare a meeting agenda and brief background note for each item. The meeting is [meeting type, e.g. monthly team review / project steering committee / client update]. Attendees are [brief description of attendees and their roles]. The meeting runs for [duration]. The agenda items are: [list items]. For each agenda item, produce: a one-sentence description of the purpose of the item, a list of no more than three questions or decisions the group needs to address, and any relevant background a participant should know before the meeting. Use a clear, scannable format.

**Human oversight required:** Review agenda items and briefing notes for accuracy. Confirm that the questions and decisions listed reflect what actually needs to be addressed, not a generic version of the topic. Remove or revise any background content that is inaccurate or does not reflect the current state of the issue.

**Data handling note:** Avoid including names, personal details, confidential business information, or client-specific content in the prompt unless using an approved internal tool.

**Adapting this template:**

- For a complex or high-stakes meeting, add: "For each agenda item, also note the likely points of disagreement or difficulty and suggest a framing for productive discussion"
- For a short stand-up or operational meeting, reduce the briefing note to a single sentence per item
- For an external meeting, add: "Assume attendees may not be familiar with our organisation's internal processes or terminology"

***

### Template 4: Reviewing and improving a document

**Use case:** You have a draft document (your own or a colleague's) that needs review for clarity, structure, tone, or completeness before it is finalised.

**Worked prompt:**
> Review the following draft document and provide structured feedback. Assess the document against these criteria: (1) Clarity: are there sentences or sections that are unnecessarily complex, ambiguous, or hard to follow? (2) Structure: does the document flow logically? Are there sections that are out of order or missing? (3) Tone: is the tone appropriate for [describe the intended audience]? (4) Completeness: is there anything material missing that the reader would expect to find? Present your feedback as a numbered list, with the specific issue, the location in the document, and a suggested improvement for each point. Do not rewrite the document. Provide feedback only.
>
> [Paste document text here]

**Human oversight required:** Apply professional judgment to each piece of feedback before acting on it. Not all suggestions will be appropriate for your specific context. The model does not know your organisation's style conventions, the history of the document, or the specific audience's preferences.

**Data handling note:** Apply the same caution as Template 1. Do not paste confidential or sensitive content into an external tool.

**Adapting this template:**

- To focus the review on one dimension, remove the other criteria: "Review only for clarity and plain language"
- To adjust the output format: "Present feedback as tracked changes suggestions rather than a numbered list"
- To add a compliance lens: "Also flag any language that could create legal or regulatory risk in [context]"

***

### Template 5: Generating options for a decision or problem

**Use case:** You need to think through the options available for a decision, a problem, or a planning question, and want to use AI to help map the possibility space before applying your own judgment.

**Worked prompt:**
> I am working on the following problem or decision: [clear description of the situation]. The key constraints are: [list relevant constraints, e.g. budget, timeline, regulatory requirements, organisational priorities]. Generate five distinct options for addressing this situation. For each option, provide: a brief description of the approach, the main advantages, the main risks or drawbacks, and the conditions under which this option would be most appropriate. Do not recommend a single best option. Present all five options as equally as possible so I can apply my own judgment to the selection.

**Human oversight required:** Treat the options generated as a starting point for your own analysis, not a complete picture of the possibility space. Apply professional and organisational judgment to assess which options are genuinely feasible in your context. Verify any factual claims embedded in the option descriptions. Add options the model has not generated if your professional knowledge suggests they are relevant.

**Data handling note:** Describe the situation in general terms. Do not include client names, personal information, confidential commercial details, or specific financial figures unless using an approved internal tool.

**Adapting this template:**

- To narrow the options: "Generate three options only, and make each one significantly different from the others"
- To add a risk focus: "For each option, give particular weight to the risks, and include a brief note on how each risk could be mitigated"
- To prepare for stakeholder discussion: "After generating the options, produce a one-paragraph framing I could use to introduce this decision to a group of stakeholders"

***

### Adding these templates to your prompt library

Each of these templates is a starting point for your personal prompt library from Task 1.2.7. As you use and adapt them, capture what works: the specific adjustments that produce better outputs for your audience, your organisation's conventions, or your most frequent task types.

A prompt library that reflects your actual work is significantly more valuable than any generic template set. These templates give you a foundation. Your experience and professional judgment build on it.

## Task 3.1.5 — Driving AI Adoption in Your Team

***

### Individual skills and team capability are different things

By this point in the course, you have built meaningful individual AI capability: the understanding, the skills, and the frameworks to use AI well in your own work. That matters. But it is only part of the picture.

How AI gets used in a team, a function, or an organisation is not just the sum of individual choices. It is shaped by culture, norms, leadership, and the informal practices that develop when people work together. A team where one person uses AI thoughtfully and four others either avoid it or use it carelessly is not a team with good AI capability. It is a team with an uneven and potentially risky relationship with a powerful tool.

This task addresses what you can do, as an individual contributor, to help your team develop better AI capability, whether or not you have formal authority or a designated AI role.

***

### What responsible AI adoption looks like at the team level

A team that uses AI well is not necessarily one that uses it most. It is one where:

- AI use is deliberate rather than habitual: people reach for AI tools because they are the right tool for a specific task, not because they are available
- Outputs are reviewed before use: AI-generated content is treated as a starting point, not a finished product, and human oversight is applied consistently
- There are shared norms: the team has informal or formal agreement about what AI is and isn't appropriate for, what tools are approved, and what oversight is expected
- Concerns are raised: people feel comfortable flagging AI use that seems inappropriate or risky, rather than either ignoring it or escalating it unnecessarily
- Learning is shared: when someone finds a prompt pattern that works well, or identifies a use case that isn't suitable for AI, they share that with the team rather than keeping it to themselves

None of these characteristics require a formal AI program. They emerge from the collective practices of individual team members who take their responsibilities seriously.

***

### Practical steps for contributing to team AI capability

**Share what works**
When you develop a prompt pattern that produces consistently useful results for a task your team does regularly, share it. This could be as simple as adding it to a shared document, mentioning it in a team meeting, or sending it to a colleague who is working on a similar task. Collective prompt libraries develop from this kind of informal sharing.

**Model good oversight practice**
When AI-assisted work is discussed in your team, talk about it as something that required review and judgment, not just generation. This normalises the expectation that outputs need evaluation, and creates a culture where cutting corners on oversight is the exception rather than the norm.

**Flag use cases that don't fit**
If you encounter a situation where someone is using AI for a task that seems inappropriate, involves sensitive data, or appears to fall outside your organisation's approved use framework, raising it is a contribution to team safety, not an act of policing. The appropriate approach is almost always a direct conversation rather than an escalation: "I wasn't sure whether AI was the right tool for that one, what's your thinking?" is usually sufficient.

**Help colleagues who are uncertain**
Not everyone in your team will have completed this course or have the same level of AI confidence. Colleagues who are uncertain about AI often default to either avoiding it entirely or using it without adequate judgment, both of which create problems. Sharing what you know, without being prescriptive or creating more anxiety, is a genuine contribution to team capability.

**Use approved channels for concerns**
If you encounter AI use that seems to breach your organisation's policy, involve confidential data inappropriately, or create meaningful risk, the right step is to raise it through the appropriate channel: your manager, your organisation's AI lead or compliance function, or the reporting pathway set out in your organisation's AI policy. Most concerns are best addressed early and informally. The ones that aren't are important enough to escalate properly.

***

### Managing common team dynamics around AI

Teams navigating AI adoption tend to contain a recognisable range of responses. Understanding them helps you contribute constructively rather than reactively.

**The enthusiastic adopter**
Uses AI extensively and enthusiastically, sometimes in ways that outpace their organisation's guidance or their own evaluation skills. The risk is outputs that are used without adequate review, or use cases that fall outside approved boundaries.

The constructive response: engage with the enthusiasm while surfacing the evaluation and oversight questions. "That's a great use case, have you checked the output against the source data?" is more useful than either endorsement or caution.

**The resistant non-adopter**
Avoids AI because of scepticism, concern about job security, unfamiliarity, or a principled objection. May also actively discourage others from using AI.

The constructive response: curiosity is more productive than persuasion. Understanding the specific concern, whether it is about accuracy, ethics, job displacement, or something else, allows for a more useful response than generic reassurance.

**The passive adopter**
Uses AI when asked or when it's very easy, but doesn't engage critically with outputs. Tends to rubber-stamp AI-generated content rather than applying genuine review.

The constructive response: normalise the expectation of review through your own practice and by making it part of team conversations about outputs.

**The policy-first employee**
Relies entirely on formal guidance and is uncomfortable acting without explicit authorisation. May avoid useful AI applications because they aren't explicitly listed in the approved use framework.

The constructive response: help them understand that the principles underlying good AI use are more durable than any specific policy provision, and that raising questions with the right people is always appropriate when guidance is unclear.

***

### The role of every employee in how AI develops in this organisation

Your organisation's approach to AI will continue to evolve. The tools will change, the policy will be updated, the use cases will expand, and the governance structures will mature. How well that evolution goes depends substantially on the collective practices and attitudes of the people doing the work.

Employees who engage thoughtfully with AI, use it responsibly, share what they learn, and raise concerns appropriately are the ones who shape that evolution productively. Those who either resist it entirely or adopt it uncritically create the conditions for both missed opportunity and avoidable risk.

The judgment, habits, and professional responsibility this course has been building are your contribution to that outcome. They are not just personal assets. They are organisational ones.
