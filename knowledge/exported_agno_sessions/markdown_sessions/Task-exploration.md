# Let's break down the concept of a task within a business context. Disregard files and other discussions in this space or knowledge for now. I want to explore this from first principles.

We'll define a task as a unit of work that can be completed by one person in less than 4 hours of non-stop focused activity.  If it takes longer than this for an appropriately capable actor, it should be decomposed further.

A task should only be performed if it has some business value to the organisation. Whether directly related to revenue generating activities, or the broader mission of the organisation.

A task is therefore related to parent entities like project, goal, strategic initiative etc.

A task should have a clear, understandable 'definition of done' so that there is no ambiguity about the completeness of a task.

An actor may require skills, knowledge, experience, tools or other capabilities to complete the task satisfactorily.

A task may be completed to a range of quality standards: eg. poor, satisfactory, great, excellent.

A task may have dependencies on other tasks being completed.

A task may have a priority score that indicates its importance. eg 'must-have' vs 'nice-to-have'

A task might only need to be done once, on a regular schedule, or ad hoc / as required.

A task might have several states: draft, backlog, planned, available, assigned, in-progress, in-review, done, cancelled, blocked, paused, archived

What other factors can be said about business-related tasks?

Great first-principles framing. Here are additional factors that can meaningfully describe a business task, organized by dimension:

## Effort \& Estimation

- **Estimated effort** — a time or story-point estimate for how long the task *should* take (distinct from the 4-hour ceiling, which is a cap)
- **Actual effort** — time actually spent, enabling variance tracking and future estimation improvement
- **Complexity rating** — independent of duration; a task can be short but cognitively complex (e.g., a critical architectural decision)
- **Cognitive load type** — whether the work is primarily analytical, creative, communicative, administrative, or procedural


## Ownership \& Accountability

- **Owner/assignee** — the specific actor responsible for completion; distinct from contributors or reviewers
- **Delegability** — whether the task can be reassigned or must be performed by a specific person (e.g., due to access rights, accountability, or trust)
- **Review/approval requirement** — whether output must be verified by another actor before the task is marked done, and who that reviewer is


## Context \& Traceability

- **Parent entity link** — explicit reference to the project, goal, OKR, or initiative this task serves
- **Outcome contribution** — a short statement of *how* this task moves the needle on the parent goal, not just *that* it belongs to it
- **Creation source** — was the task human-initiated, surfaced by a process review, generated by a tool/agent, or triggered by an event?
- **External reference** — links to tickets, documents, meeting notes, or systems of record (e.g. Jira, CRM, contract)


## Risk \& Impact

- **Risk if not done** — what is the consequence of skipping or delaying this task? Helps prioritise beyond just value-add
- **Blast radius** — how many downstream tasks, people, or processes are affected if this task is done poorly or not at all
- **Reversibility** — can the effects of this task be undone? Irreversible tasks (e.g. sending a communication, deploying to production) warrant higher scrutiny


## Inputs \& Outputs

- **Required inputs** — data, decisions, artefacts, or access that must exist before work begins (closely related to dependencies, but more granular)
- **Expected outputs/deliverables** — specific artefacts, decisions, or state changes that result from completion
- **Handoff target** — who receives the output and in what form, especially important in sequential workflows


## Time \& Scheduling

- **Due date / deadline** — when the task must be complete, and whether that deadline is hard (contractual, regulatory) or soft (internal preference)
- **Earliest start date** — the soonest the task *can* begin, accounting for dependencies or embargoed information
- **Time-sensitivity** — whether value decays if delayed (e.g., responding to a lead vs. updating internal documentation)
- **Estimated lead time** — total elapsed time from assignment to completion, including wait states, not just active work time


## Communication \& Visibility

- **Stakeholders/observers** — parties who need visibility into progress or completion without being responsible for the work
- **Notification triggers** — events that should prompt communication (e.g., status change, overdue, blocked)
- **Confidentiality level** — whether the task or its outputs are sensitive, restricted, or publicly shareable


## Learning \& Improvement

- **Retrospective flag** — a marker indicating the task warrants a debrief (e.g., it went significantly over time, produced unexpected results, or uncovered new information)
- **Knowledge output** — whether completing the task generates reusable knowledge (e.g., a process template, a decision record, a how-to guide)
- **Skill development opportunity** — whether the task is a good candidate for a less-experienced actor to attempt with oversight, creating deliberate growth


## Identity \& Metadata

- **Unique identifier** — a stable ID for traceability across systems and time
- **Version/iteration number** — particularly relevant for recurring tasks, to distinguish this instance from prior ones
- **Tags/labels** — freeform categorisation for filtering, reporting, and grouping (e.g., `client-facing`, `tech-debt`, `compliance`)
- **Audit trail** — a log of state changes, assignments, edits, and comments for accountability and retrospection

A few design notes worth flagging:

- `owner_id` and `assignee_id` are intentionally separate — ownership is accountability, assignment is execution. They can be the same person but often aren't.
- `depends_on` and `blocks` are inverses of each other and could be derived, but storing both avoids expensive graph traversals at query time.
- `audit_trail` as a JSON array is pragmatic for most systems; at scale this would likely become its own `TaskEvent` table with a foreign key back to `task.id`.
- `recurrence_schedule` as a cron expression is maximally flexible — a human-readable label (e.g. "Every Monday at 9am") can always be derived from it for display purposes.

***

## Definition of Done — Refined

Your instinct to decompose DoD is correct. It's not a single text field — it's a **structured verification protocol**:

- **Acceptance criteria** become their own child records (see Related Models below), each with a `verified_by`, `verified_at`, and `verification_method`
- **Artefacts** serve as *evidence* of completion — they don't just describe outputs, they *prove* them
- **Verification method** should be typed per artefact, since different artefact types demand fundamentally different checks:

| Artefact Type | Verification Method |
| :-- | :-- |
| Document / report | Human review against criteria |
| Code / PR | Automated test pass + peer review |
| Design file | Stakeholder sign-off |
| Data output | Automated schema/range validation |
| Communication sent | Delivery receipt / read confirmation |
| Decision record | Named approver acknowledgement |
| Physical deliverable | In-person inspection |

This means `ArterfactSubmission` needs: `artefact_type`, `verification_method`, `verifier_id`, `verified_at`, and `pass/fail` status.

***

## Additional Risk Dimensions

You're right that the schema only captured *blast radius* and *reversibility*. A more complete risk taxonomy for a task should include:

- **Non-completion risk** — probability and consequence of the task not finishing on time or at all (distinct from *risk if not done* which assumes a deliberate skip)
- **Execution risk** — likelihood of the task being done incorrectly or to insufficient quality
- **Safety risk** — physical, psychological, legal, or reputational harm to the performer or others (especially relevant for field work, client-facing tasks, or regulated industries)
- **Dependency risk** — risk that upstream tasks fail, cascade-blocking this one
- **Data/security risk** — whether the task involves sensitive data handling with breach potential
- **Compliance/regulatory risk** — whether failure to complete correctly exposes the organisation to legal or regulatory consequence
- **Reputational risk** — whether poor execution is externally visible (to clients, partners, public)

Each of these maps well to your `Risk` model with fields like `risk_type`, `likelihood`, `consequence_severity`, `mitigation_strategy`, and `risk_owner_id`.

***

## Rulesets — Strong Pattern

Your identification of **Rulesets** as a governance layer is a clean architectural separation. The principle is: the *task instance* stores a reference to a ruleset, while the ruleset holds the logic, definitions, thresholds, and scoring rules. This avoids embedding business logic in data and makes rules maintainable independently of tasks.

Here's how each maps:


| Attribute | Ruleset Purpose |
| :-- | :-- |
| `confidentiality` | Defines handling obligations, permitted actors, storage requirements, disclosure rules |
| `complexity_rating` | Defines scoring criteria: what makes a task "high" vs "medium" complexity |
| `cognitive_load_type` | Defines classification criteria and actor-matching logic |
| `time_sensitivity` | Defines thresholds: what elapsed time triggers each level |
| `priority_score` | Defines the scoring formula: weighted inputs from value, risk, urgency, dependencies |
| `risks` | Defines risk taxonomy, severity scales, and required mitigations per type |
| `time_value_decay` | Defines decay function: linear, exponential, cliff-edge, or binary |
| `capability_requirements` | Defines what qualifies as meeting a skill/knowledge/tool requirement |
| `experience_levels` | Defines what constitutes `junior`, `mid`, `senior`, `specialist` per domain |
| `quality` | Defines what `satisfactory`, `great`, `excellent` mean for different task types |
| `blast_radius` | Defines the scoping criteria for each level |
| `reversibility` | Defines classification rules and required safeguards per level |
| `retrospective_flag` | Defines auto-trigger conditions (e.g. effort variance > 50%, quality = poor) |
| `knowledge_reusability` | Defines what qualifies as reusable and required output format |
| `outcome_contribution` | Defines how contribution to parent goals is assessed and weighted |

The Ruleset model itself would have: `id`, `name`, `version`, `applies_to` (the attribute or domain), `rules` (structured logic — could be JSON or a DSL), `effective_from`, `effective_to`, `owner_id`, and `rationale`.

## Task Governance Assets

These are excellent — they form a **policy layer** that sits *above* individual tasks and informs both Rulesets and defaults. Think of them as organisational standards that tasks must conform to:

- **Quality Guidelines** — define what quality means per task type or domain; feed into quality Rulesets and reviewer checklists
- **Task Valuation Framework** — defines how business value is estimated and scored; feeds `priority_score` Ruleset and enables portfolio-level ROI analysis
- **Performer Safety Guidelines** — define risk thresholds, required safeguards, and escalation paths for safety risks; particularly critical when tasks involve physical, psychological, or regulatory exposure
- **Notification Rules** — define *who* gets notified, *when*, and *how* based on task type, status, urgency, and role — decouples notification logic from task data entirely
- **Retrospective Guidelines** — define when retros are mandatory vs optional, what format to follow, and how learnings are captured and fed back into Knowledge Building
- **Knowledge Building Guidelines** — define what qualifies as a reusable knowledge output, required format/structure, where it's stored, and how it's tagged for discoverability

These governance assets also create a natural **versioning and compliance** requirement — tasks should record *which version* of each governance asset was in effect when they were created or executed, so that audits remain valid even as policies evolve.

***

## Emerging Architecture Shape

Pulling this together, you're converging on a three-layer model:

- **Layer 1 — Governance Assets**: Org-wide policies (quality guidelines, safety, valuation, notification rules)
- **Layer 2 — Rulesets**: Typed, versioned business logic that interprets governance into computable rules
- **Layer 3 — Task + Related Models**: Instance data that references Rulesets for classification and Related Models for structured detail

This is a sound, extensible design — rules stay rules, data stays data, and tasks stay lean.

# What are the different ways we could categorise these tasks beyond their parent/source?

Great question. Categories beyond parent/source give you different *lenses* through which to filter, route, assign, and analyse tasks. Each lens serves a different operational or strategic purpose.

***

## By Work Type / Nature

What kind of mental or physical activity is being performed:

- **Creative** — generating something new (writing, designing, ideating)
- **Analytical** — interpreting data or evidence to reach a conclusion
- **Evaluative** — judging quality, correctness, or fit against a standard
- **Communicative** — conveying information to another party
- **Administrative** — organising, recording, or maintaining systems
- **Procedural / Operational** — executing a defined, repeatable process
- **Investigative** — exploring an unknown to surface information
- **Relational** — building or maintaining a human connection
- **Decisional** — making a choice with documented rationale
- **Transformative** — converting an input into a different form or format

***

## By Functional Domain

The business function the task belongs to, regardless of who performs it:

- Finance, Legal, HR, Sales, Marketing, Customer Success, Product, Engineering, Operations, Strategy, Governance, Learning \& Development, IT/Systems

***

## By Value Stream

Which part of the organisation's value delivery the task serves:

- **Value-creating** — directly produces something a customer or stakeholder receives
- **Value-enabling** — supports or unblocks value-creating work
- **Value-protecting** — prevents loss, risk, or degradation (compliance, security, QA)
- **Value-discovering** — generates insight that informs future value creation (research, experiments)
- **Waste** — tasks that consume resources without contributing to any of the above (a useful diagnostic category)

***

## By Actor Type

Who is expected to perform the task:

- **Human only** — requires judgement, empathy, or accountability that cannot be delegated
- **Human-led, tool-assisted** — human performs with AI, automation, or software support
- **Automated / agent-executable** — can be fully performed by a system or AI agent with no human involvement
- **Collaborative** — requires real-time coordination between multiple actors
- **Delegated** — can be assigned down to a less senior actor with oversight

***

## By Interaction Mode

How the task is engaged with:

- **Synchronous** — requires real-time presence (a meeting, a live call)
- **Asynchronous** — can be done independently at any time within a window
- **Reactive** — triggered by an external event (an inbound email, an alert, a request)
- **Proactive** — self-initiated based on context or judgement

***

## By Visibility / Audience

Who the output is for:

- **Internal** — for team or organisation only
- **Client-facing** — directly seen or received by a customer
- **Partner-facing** — received by a supplier, vendor, or collaborator
- **Public** — externally published
- **Regulatory** — submitted to a compliance or government body

- **One-off** — never repeated

- **Demand-driven** — occurs when requested by another party

How portable the required capability is:

- **Generic** — any reasonably capable adult could do it
- **Domain-general** — requires business literacy but no specialisation
- **Domain-specific** — requires knowledge of a particular industry or function

- **Expert** — requires formal qualification, deep experience, or rare capability

What the task produces:

- **Artefact** — a document, file, design, or physical object

- **Communication** — information is transmitted to another party
- **Experience** — an interaction is delivered (a meeting, a training session)
- **Insight** — knowledge or understanding is generated, not necessarily formalised

How closely tied to organisational direction:

- **Core mission** — central to what the organisation exists to do
- **Strategic priority** — tied to a current key initiative or OKR
- **Business-as-usual** — necessary to keep things running but not strategically differentiated
- **Exploratory** — testing a hypothesis about future direction
- **Maintenance** — preserving the value of existing assets or relationships

Combining reversibility and blast radius into an actionable tier:

- **Tier 1 — Safe to proceed** — low blast radius, reversible, low stakes
- **Tier 2 — Proceed with care** — moderate impact or partially reversible, review recommended
- **Tier 3 — Requires approval** — high blast radius or irreversible, must be authorised before execution
- **Tier 4 — Escalate first** — safety, legal, or reputational exposure requires senior sign-off

Useful for scheduling and workload design:

- **Deep work** — requires sustained, uninterrupted concentration
- **Shallow work** — can be done amid interruptions or batched efficiently

***

## Tasks as Information Processing

Every task can be reframed as an **information transformation event**:

- It takes inputs (data, context, prior decisions) and produces outputs (artefacts, decisions, state changes)
- The actor is essentially a **processor** — applying capability to transform information from one state to another
- This framing immediately surfaces questions like: *what is the minimum viable input to begin?* and *what is the irreducible output that proves transformation occurred?*
- It also challenges whether a human is the right processor — or whether the transformation can be automated, augmented, or eliminated

***

## Tasks as Trust Transactions

Every task involves an implicit or explicit **trust contract**:

- The assigner trusts the actor to perform to a quality standard without constant supervision
- The actor trusts the assigner that the task has genuine purpose and will be used
- The organisation trusts that completed tasks actually happened as recorded
- This framing suggests tasks are **trust-bearing objects** — and that the metadata around them (verification, audit trail, DoD) is actually trust infrastructure, not just admin overhead
- Low-trust organisations generate enormous task overhead through excessive review, approval chains, and redundant verification

***

## Tasks as Energy Expenditure

Drawing from physics and thermodynamics as a metaphor:

- Every task consumes finite **human energy** — cognitive, emotional, physical
- Energy is not uniformly available — it varies by time of day, workload, psychological state, and context-switching cost
- **Switching between tasks has a real energy cost** (cognitive switching tax) that is rarely accounted for in task scheduling
- This framing suggests that task *sequencing* and *batching* are as important as task *priority* — a sequence of similar cognitive load tasks costs less total energy than the same tasks interleaved
- It also introduces the concept of **task debt**: deferred tasks accumulate psychological load even before they are started

***

## Tasks as Coordination Signals

In a multi-actor system, tasks are not just units of work — they are **coordination mechanisms**:

- A task becoming `available` is a signal to the system that capacity can be deployed
- A task becoming `blocked` is a signal that a dependency has failed somewhere upstream
- A task being `assigned` is a signal about trust, capability allocation, and role clarity
- This framing suggests the *state transitions* of tasks are at least as important as the tasks themselves — they carry organisational signal about flow, bottlenecks, and system health
- It connects directly to **queueing theory**: tasks are jobs in a queue, actors are servers, and throughput is a function of arrival rate, processing time, and capacity utilisation

***

## Tasks as Commitments

From the **Getting Things Done** and **Commitment-Based Management** traditions:

- A task is not merely a description of work — it is a **promise made by one person to another**
- This reframes the assignee not as a passive recipient of work, but as an **active participant in a social contract**
- It means tasks have *requesters* and *performers*, and the relationship between them matters — unclear requests generate poor performance not because of incompetence but because of **broken commitment structures**
- Tasks without a named requester and a named performer with mutual understanding of the DoD are not real commitments — they are *wishes*
- This challenges the common practice of self-assigning tasks from backlogs without dialogue

***

## Tasks as Learning Opportunities

Every task is also a **capability event**:

- The organisation learns something from every completed task, whether it captures that learning or not
- The actor either grows, stagnates, or is mis-deployed depending on whether the task matches their development edge
- This framing suggests task allocation should consider not just *who can do this* but *who should do this to grow* — turning the task queue into a **learning curriculum**
- It also surfaces the concept of **institutional memory loss**: when tasks are done but not documented, the organisation performs the same learning work repeatedly

***

## Tasks as Experiments

Particularly powerful for knowledge work:

- Many tasks are actually **hypotheses in disguise** — the organisation *thinks* this action will produce value, but doesn't truly know
- Treating tasks as experiments means they should have an explicit **expected outcome**, and completion should include a **result vs expectation** assessment
- This closes the feedback loop that most task systems leave permanently open
- It also legitimises the outcome "we were wrong" as a valid task result — rather than forcing tasks to be declared `done` when the output was useless

***

## Tasks as System Interventions

From **systems thinking** and complexity theory:

- Every task is an intervention in a living system — it changes something, which changes other things
- **Leverage points** exist: some tasks have disproportionate systemic effect relative to their effort
- Most organisations cannot identify their highest-leverage tasks because they evaluate tasks in isolation rather than as nodes in a causal network
- This framing argues for a **task dependency graph** that is treated not just as a scheduling tool but as a **causal model of the organisation's work**
- It also introduces **unintended consequences** as a first-class concern — irreversible, high-blast-radius tasks deserve systems-level analysis before execution

***

## Tasks as Market Goods

An underexplored framing from **internal market** and **mechanism design** theory:

- Tasks have *cost* (effort, opportunity cost) and *value* (contribution to outcomes)
- If tasks were priced and actors could choose which to perform, you would immediately surface information about which tasks are *overvalued* (everyone wants them) and *undervalued* (nobody wants them)
- This is the intuition behind **prediction markets for task completion** and **bounty-based task systems** (common in open-source and DAO contexts)
- It challenges the assumption that hierarchy is the right allocation mechanism for work — in some contexts, a **market or auction mechanism** allocates tasks more efficiently than top-down assignment

***

## Tasks as Actors Themselves

A provocative framing for agentic AI contexts:

- As AI agents become capable of performing tasks autonomously, the distinction between *task* and *actor* begins to blur
- A sufficiently well-specified task (with DoD, inputs, capabilities, constraints) is essentially a **job description for an agent**
- This means the task schema you are designing is also, potentially, a **prompt engineering scaffold** or an **agent configuration spec**
- It raises the question: should your task model include an `execution_mode` — `human`, `human_assisted`, `agent_assisted`, `fully_automated` — as a first-class field?
- And further: should tasks have **autonomy boundaries** — explicit limits on what an agent executing this task is permitted to do without human escalation?

***

## Challenging Institutional Assumptions

Here are the assumptions most organisations never question about tasks:


| Common Assumption | The Challenge |
| :-- | :-- |
| Tasks flow from managers to workers | Tasks could be claimed, auctioned, or self-organised based on capability and capacity |
| A task is done when the assignee says so | Completion is a social agreement, not a unilateral declaration |
| All tasks are equally real | Many tasks exist to *signal effort* or *cover liability*, not to create value |
| Tasks should be completed | Some tasks should be *eliminated* — the best response to a task may be to question its existence |
| Smaller tasks are simpler | Decomposition can increase coordination overhead more than it reduces execution risk |
| Tasks are sequential | Many tasks that appear sequential are actually parallelisable with better information design |
| The task backlog is a to-do list | A backlog is a *portfolio of options* — and like financial options, they expire, depreciate, and have risk profiles |

The deepest challenge may be this: **most task systems are designed to track compliance, not to generate value**. The question worth sitting with is — what would a task system look like if it were designed primarily to *maximise learning and leverage*, rather than to prove that work was done?

Are there any seminal research theories around tasks as a unit of work? What fields of science are interested in tasks?

Tasks as a unit of analysis appear across a surprisingly wide range of disciplines, each with their own vocabulary and theoretical traditions.

***

## Foundational Theories

**Task-Technology Fit (TTF) — Goodhue \& Thompson, 1995**
One of the most cited frameworks in information systems research. Argues that performance improves when the capabilities of a technology match the requirements of the task. Introduced the idea that tasks have *profiles* — characteristics that determine what support they need — rather than being uniform units.

**Task Complexity Theory — Campbell, 1988**
A landmark taxonomy defining task complexity along four dimensions: multiple paths to solution, multiple desired outcomes, conflicting interdependence between paths, and uncertainty. Still the foundational reference when researchers need to operationalise "how hard is this task."

**Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller, 1988**
Originally from educational psychology but deeply influential in HCI and work design. Distinguishes between *intrinsic* load (complexity inherent in the task), *extraneous* load (complexity introduced by poor design), and *germane* load (effort that builds schema/skill). Directly informs your cognitive load type field.

**Job Characteristics Model — Hackman \& Oldham, 1976**
Foundational in organisational psychology. Identifies five core job dimensions — skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback — and links them to motivation and performance. Much of modern work design theory descends from this. The insight that *task identity* (doing a whole, meaningful piece of work) matters for motivation is directly relevant to your decomposition limit.

**Commitment-Based Management — Fernando Flores, 1980s**
Drawing on speech act theory and phenomenology, Flores argued that organisations are networks of commitments, not hierarchies of tasks. Every work request is a *speech act* — a promise between a requester and a performer — and breakdowns occur when that speech act is malformed. Highly influential in enterprise software design (he co-founded Action Technologies) and in later developments like Agile.

***

## Fields of Science That Study Tasks

**Cognitive Psychology \& Cognitive Science**
Probably the most prolific field. Studies how humans represent, plan, execute, and monitor tasks. Key areas include:

- **Goal-directed behaviour** — how tasks are mentally structured as hierarchies of goals and subgoals (GOMS model, Card, Moran \& Newell 1983)
- **Dual-process theory** — fast/automatic vs slow/deliberate task execution (Kahneman)
- **Prospective memory** — how people remember to perform tasks at the right time
- **Task interruption and resumption** — the cognitive cost of switching, highly relevant to your energy framing

**Human Factors \& Ergonomics**
Studies tasks from a *safety and performance* lens — how task design affects error rates, fatigue, and accidents. Key contributions:

- **Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA)** — a structured method for decomposing tasks into goals, subgoals, and operations; one of the most widely used tools in safety-critical industries
- **FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)** — applied to task sequences to identify where failures are most likely and most consequential
- Dominant in aviation, nuclear, healthcare, and military contexts

**Organisational Behaviour \& Management Science**
Studies tasks as units within larger social and organisational systems:

- **Work design theory** — how tasks should be structured to maximise performance and wellbeing
- **Role theory** — how tasks relate to formal and informal roles
- **Coordination theory** — Malone \& Crowston's work on how tasks with dependencies require coordination mechanisms; directly maps to your dependency model
- **Workflow theory** — formal modelling of task sequences, routing, and state machines

**Operations Research \& Management Science**
Treats tasks as quantitative objects to be optimised:

- **Queueing theory** — models task arrival, processing, and wait times; foundational for understanding throughput and bottlenecks
- **Scheduling theory** — optimal sequencing of tasks given constraints (deadlines, dependencies, resource limits)
- **Critical Path Method (CPM) and PERT** — network-based models of task dependencies for project planning; invented in the 1950s for the US Navy Polaris program
- **Theory of Constraints (Goldratt)** — argues that organisational performance is always limited by one bottleneck, and that optimising non-bottleneck tasks is waste

**Computer Science \& AI**
Tasks appear in multiple distinct subfields:

- **Task planning / automated planning** — AI systems that decompose high-level goals into executable task sequences (STRIPS, PDDL)
- **Multi-agent systems** — how autonomous agents negotiate, allocate, and execute tasks collectively
- **Workflow management systems** — formal models of task routing, state machines, and process execution (BPEL, BPMN)
- **Human-computer interaction (HCI)** — how interfaces should be designed around task structures; Task-Centred Design is a core methodology
- **Reinforcement learning** — agents learn to complete tasks through reward signals; the task is the environment

**Sociology \& Ethnomethodology**
Less quantitative but deeply insightful:

- **Workplace studies** — close observation of how work actually happens vs how it is formally described (often very different)
- **Situated action theory — Lucy Suchman, 1987** — argued that tasks are not executed from pre-formed plans but are constructed *in response to* the unfolding situation; a profound challenge to the rationalist planning model
- **Communities of practice — Lave \& Wenger** — tasks are not performed by isolated individuals but within social contexts that shape how they are interpreted and executed

**Neuroscience**
An emerging field of relevance:

- **Prefrontal cortex and executive function** — the neuroscience of task initiation, switching, and monitoring
- **Default mode network** — what the brain does *between* tasks; relevant to recovery and creativity
- **Flow states — Csikszentmihalyi** — the neurological and psychological conditions under which task performance peaks; directly challenges the assumption that more tasks = more output

**Behavioural Economics**
Studies how humans actually make decisions about tasks vs how rational models predict they should:

- **Present bias** — systematic tendency to overvalue immediate tasks and discount future ones; explains why high-priority long-term tasks are chronically deferred
- **Completion bias** — preference for completing small, easy tasks over starting large, important ones (the "small wins" trap)
- **Zeigarnik effect** — incomplete tasks occupy working memory disproportionately; unclosed tasks generate cognitive load even when not being worked on
- **Ego depletion** — decision-making and self-regulation quality degrades across a task day (though this finding has had replication challenges)

***

## Underappreciated Intersections

| Field Pair | What the Intersection Reveals |
| :-- | :-- |
| Cognitive psychology + Operations Research | Human processing constraints should inform scheduling algorithms, not just resource availability |
| Sociology + AI | Situated action theory suggests fully automated task execution will always encounter edge cases that require human judgement |
| Behavioural economics + Work design | Task list design (ordering, framing, chunking) has measurable effects on completion rates independent of priority |
| Neuroscience + Organisational behaviour | Optimal task throughput requires recovery time — organisations that treat humans as machines degrade their own processing capacity |

The goal of a great task system isn't to eliminate that gap — it's to make it *visible and navigable*.

## A Task-Centric Organisational Model

If tasks are the core unit of value creation, then every other organisational construct — roles, teams, processes, goals — should be *derived from and in service of* tasks, not the other way around. This inverts the conventional org design logic.

### The Conventional Model (Inverted)

Most organisations are designed like this:
**Strategy → Structure → Roles → Processes → Tasks**

Work is decomposed top-down from strategy into structures, then roles, then processes, then tasks. Tasks are the *residue* of organisational design — what's left after everything else is decided. They are assigned, not designed.

### The Task-Centric Model

**Tasks → Capability clusters → Dynamic teams → Emergent structure → Strategy feedback**

In this model:

- The atomic unit of design is the *task* — specifically its capability requirements, value contribution, and coordination needs
- **Roles** are defined as *recurring capability profiles* — clusters of task types a person is optimally suited to perform, not fixed job descriptions
- **Teams** form dynamically around task clusters that require coordination, rather than being permanent structures that tasks are forced into
- **Processes** are formalised only when task sequences recur with sufficient frequency and predictability to warrant it — otherwise they remain flexible
- **Strategy** is informed by aggregate task data — what is actually being done, what is generating value, what is being deferred or eliminated


### Core Design Principles

**1. Task as the unit of value accounting**
Every task carries an estimated and actual value contribution. The organisation can, for the first time, see where value is actually being created — not just where budget is being spent. This enables genuine prioritisation rather than political negotiation.

**2. Capability marketplace over fixed assignment**
Tasks broadcast their capability requirements. Actors — human or agent — are matched to tasks based on fit, availability, and development need. This replaces the inefficiency of fixed role-to-task mapping with a dynamic allocation system. Some tasks are claimed (actors self-select), some are assigned (routed by a system or manager), some are auctioned (highest-capability available actor is matched).

**3. Commitment as the unit of accountability**
Accountability is not attached to a role or a position — it is attached to a commitment. When a task is accepted, a commitment is formed. The organisation tracks commitment quality (was the DoD met? on time? to quality?) at the individual and team level. This makes performance visible without micromanagement.

**4. The task graph as the organisational nervous system**
The dependency graph of all active tasks is the real-time model of how the organisation is functioning. Bottlenecks appear as nodes with many downstream dependents. Value streams are visible as connected paths from task to outcome. Redundant work appears as duplicate task patterns. Leaders navigate the organisation by reading the task graph, not the org chart.

**5. Learning as a first-class output**
Every task is also a learning event. The organisation maintains a **task knowledge base** — a living repository of expected vs actual outcomes, reusable artefacts, process discoveries, and retrospective insights. Over time, this becomes the organisation's primary competitive asset: accumulated, structured, searchable operational intelligence.

**6. Waste as a visible category**
Because every task carries a `value_contribution` and a `parent_entity` link, tasks that cannot be justified — that exist to signal effort, satisfy bureaucracy, or cover liability — become visible. The organisation can conduct regular **task audits** asking: if we stopped doing this, what would actually break? This is a direct challenge to organisational bloat.

**7. Agents as first-class actors**
In this model, AI agents are not tools — they are actors in the same marketplace. They have capability profiles, capacity limits, trust levels, and autonomy boundaries. Tasks are allocated to the best-fit actor regardless of whether that actor is human or agent. The organisation's task system becomes the coordination layer for a hybrid human-agent workforce.

## The Deeper Shift

The most profound implication of centering the organisation on tasks is epistemological: it shifts the organisation from **managing people** to **managing commitments and value flow**.


| Conventional Organisation | Task-Centric Organisation |
| :-- | :-- |
| Accountability sits in roles | Accountability sits in commitments |
| Value is assumed from activity | Value is measured from outcomes |
| Structure is fixed, work adapts | Work is primary, structure adapts |
| Knowledge lives in people's heads | Knowledge is a task output |
| Performance is assessed annually | Performance is visible in real time |
| Bottlenecks are political | Bottlenecks are structural and visible |
| Strategy is set at the top | Strategy is informed by task data |
| Agents are tools | Agents are actors |

The task, properly modelled, is not just an administrative convenience. It is the **quantum of organisational intention** — the smallest unit at which a human or agent commits to creating value in the world. Build the right model for that quantum, and the organisational architecture that sits above it becomes far more legible, adaptable, and honest.

## The Ontological Hierarchy

You've just identified that tasks are **derivative**, not primary. The causal chain runs deeper:

**Human desire → Intent → Goal → Strategy → Work → Task**

Tasks don't exist in nature. They are *materialised intentions* — the moment a human desire to change the world becomes concrete enough to be assigned, scheduled, and executed. Without the desire, there is no task. The task is just the most granular visible expression of something that began in human consciousness.

This means the task model you've been building is actually the *bottom layer* of a much taller ontological stack.

## The Full Stack

| Layer | Entity | Core Question |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **1 — Being** | Human / Stakeholder | What do I value? What do I want to exist in the world? |
| **2 — Desire** | Need / Want / Problem | What is the gap between current reality and desired reality? |
| **3 — Intent** | Objective / Aspiration | What am I committing to change? |
| **4 — Direction** | Goal / OKR / Mission | How will I know I've moved toward what I want? |
| **5 — Strategy** | Strategic Initiative / Plan | What approach will I take to close the gap? |
| **6 — Structure** | Project / Programme / Epic | How do I organise the work required by that approach? |
| **7 — Coordination** | Process / Workflow | In what sequence and by whom will the work be done? |
| **8 — Action** | Task | What is the smallest committed unit of work that moves things forward? |
| **9 — Evidence** | Artefact / State Change | What proves that the action occurred and had effect? |

Nothing at layer 8 is legitimate unless it can be traced to layer 2. A task with no human desire behind it — however many levels of abstraction removed — is, by definition, waste.

## What This Changes

**Goals are not parents of tasks — they are the reason tasks exist at all.**
The relationship isn't structural (goal *contains* tasks) — it's *teleological* (goal *justifies* tasks). This is a subtle but important distinction. It means the most important question about any task is not "what project does this belong to?" but "whose desire does this serve, and how?"

**The human is not a resource — they are the origin.**
Conventional org design treats humans as inputs: skills and capacity to be allocated to tasks. But if desire is the source of all tasks, humans are the *generative layer* of the entire system. The organisation exists to amplify and coordinate human desire, not to consume human capacity.

**Tasks inherit meaning, they don't generate it.**
A task has no intrinsic meaning. It borrows meaning from the goal above it, which borrows meaning from the intent above that, which borrows meaning from the desire of a human being. This is why tasks without clear parent linkage feel hollow — they've been severed from their meaning chain.

**Automation has a hard boundary here.**
Agents and systems can execute tasks. They can even decompose goals into tasks. But they cannot *originate desire*. The layer 2 — the felt sense that something should be different — is irreducibly human. This means no matter how capable agents become, the human remains sovereign at the top of the stack. Every automated task is ultimately in service of a human want.

With this framing, you could now restate your original definition:

> *A task is the smallest unit of committed action through which a human desire to change the state of the world is made concrete, assigned, executed, and verified.*

This definition does something the original didn't: it **anchors the task to human will** rather than to time or decomposability. The 4-hour rule is a practical constraint. But the *reason* for the task's existence is always, somewhere up the chain, a person who wanted something to be different.

## The Implication for Organisational Design

If this is true, then the organisation's primary function is not to *manage tasks* — it is to:

1. **Understand and articulate human desires** — of customers, members, staff, community
2. **Translate those desires into clear goals** with measurable states of change
3. **Decompose those goals into work** that is coherent, coordinated, and purposeful
4. **Execute that work faithfully** through the best available actor — human or agent
5. **Verify that the desired state change actually occurred** — closing the loop back to the human whose desire started it all

The task system is just the machinery in the middle. The humans — with their desires, values, and capacity to judge whether the world has actually changed in the way they wanted — are the beginning and the end.

This also reframes what *organisational failure* actually is: not inefficiency, not missed deadlines, not poor quality — but **the persistent gap between what humans in and around the organisation desire, and what the organisation's task machinery actually produces**. Every broken task system is, at its root, a broken connection to human desire.

# In economics theory, a business can be seen as a nexus of contracts.

Could it be said that On another layer, a business is a nexus of tasks?

Yes — and the claim is defensible not just as metaphor but as a genuine theoretical proposition. Let me stress-test it properly.

***

## The Nexus of Contracts Theory (Baseline)

Jensen \& Meckling (1976) and the broader **contractarian theory of the firm** argues that a firm is not a real entity — it is a *legal fiction* that represents a nexus of voluntary contracts between individuals: shareholders, employees, suppliers, customers, creditors. The firm exists to reduce transaction costs (Coase, 1937) that would arise if all those contracts were negotiated individually in open markets.

The firm, in this view, is essentially an **information and enforcement structure** — a way of bundling contracts to reduce the cost of coordination.

***

## The Nexus of Tasks Proposition

You could argue, with equal rigour, that:

> *A firm is a nexus of tasks — a structured system for sourcing, allocating, executing, and verifying units of committed action in service of human desires that no single person could satisfy alone.*

This isn't a replacement of the contractarian view — it operates at a **different layer of resolution**. Contracts define the *terms of participation*. Tasks are what *actually happens* inside those terms.


| Theory | Unit of Analysis | What It Explains |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Nexus of Contracts | Contract | Why firms exist; how rights and obligations are structured |
| Nexus of Tasks | Task | What firms actually do; how value is created moment to moment |
| Transaction Cost Economics | Transaction | When to internalise vs outsource activity |
| Resource-Based View | Capability | Why some firms outperform others |
| **Nexus of Tasks** | **Task** | **How human desire becomes organisational output** |

The task layer sits *inside* the contract layer — contracts create the conditions under which tasks can be legitimately assigned and executed, but they say nothing about what those tasks are, whether they are well-designed, or whether they actually serve the desires that motivated the contracts in the first place.

***

## Where the Analogy Holds Strongly

**Firms exist because tasks require coordination.**
A single human can satisfy some desires alone. The moment a desire requires capabilities, tools, or scale beyond one person, you need a mechanism to coordinate multiple actors performing interdependent tasks. The firm is that mechanism. Coase's insight about transaction costs applies equally to task coordination costs — the firm internalises task allocation because doing so is cheaper than contracting each task individually in the open market.

**The boundary of the firm is a task boundary.**
What a firm *does* is defined by which tasks it performs internally vs sources externally. Outsourcing, offshoring, automation, and vertical integration are all fundamentally decisions about *where tasks are executed*. The firm's boundary is not a legal line — it is a task allocation decision.

**Firm capability is task capability in aggregate.**
The resource-based view argues that sustained competitive advantage comes from rare, inimitable capabilities. But capabilities are not abstract — they are the accumulated ability to execute certain tasks better than anyone else. A firm's core competency is really a cluster of tasks it can perform with superior consistency, speed, quality, or cost.

**Organisational culture is the informal layer of task governance.**
Culture is often described as "how we do things here." But more precisely, culture is the set of unwritten rules about *how tasks are interpreted, prioritised, and executed* when the formal system is silent. It is the gap-filling layer between the task specification and actual human behaviour.

***

## Where It Extends Beyond Contracts

The nexus of tasks framing captures something the contractarian view misses entirely:

**Contracts are static; tasks are dynamic.**
A contract specifies terms at a point in time. But the organisation's task graph is alive — tasks are created, blocked, completed, and cancelled continuously. The real-time state of the task system is a more accurate picture of organisational health than any contract could provide.

**Contracts don't capture meaning; tasks inherit it.**
As established in our previous exchange — tasks derive meaning from human desire. Contracts are silent on whether the work being contracted actually serves the underlying human need. A task-centric view keeps meaning visible in a way the contractarian frame does not.

**Contracts allocate rights; tasks allocate attention.**
The scarcest resource in a knowledge organisation is not capital, not labour in the abstract — it is *human attention*. The task system is the primary mechanism through which attention is allocated. No contract theory accounts for this. A nexus of tasks framing puts attention at the centre of organisational economics.

**Tasks reveal waste; contracts conceal it.**
A firm can be fully contractually compliant while performing enormous quantities of valueless tasks — tasks that exist to satisfy bureaucratic obligations, signal effort, or protect incumbents. The contractarian view has no mechanism to identify this. A task-centric view, with value contribution and desire traceability as first-class fields, makes waste structurally visible.

***

## A Potential Theoretical Synthesis

You could propose a **three-layer theory of the firm**:


| Layer | Theoretical Frame | What It Governs |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Constitutional** | Nexus of Contracts | Rights, obligations, ownership, participation terms |
| **Operational** | Nexus of Tasks | What work is done, by whom, in service of what desire |
| **Intentional** | Nexus of Desires | Why the firm exists; whose needs it serves; what it values |

Most economic theory of the firm lives at the constitutional layer. Most management theory lives at the operational layer. Most purpose-driven and stakeholder theory lives at the intentional layer. But they are rarely integrated into a single coherent model.

The task is the hinge between intention and contract — the moment where a human desire, operating within a set of contractual terms, becomes concrete action in the world. **That is not a trivial position in the architecture of economic life.** It is arguably the most important one.

***

## The Provocation

If every value-creating moment in an organisation is a task, and every task is traceable to a human desire, and the firm exists to coordinate tasks it cannot reduce to individual action — then:

> *The firm is not primarily a legal structure, a capital allocation mechanism, or a production function. It is a **desire-amplification system** — a social technology for turning what humans want into coordinated action at a scale no individual could achieve alone. The task is the atom of that system. And the health of the firm is ultimately measurable by how faithfully its task machinery serves the desires that called it into existence.*

That is a more demanding standard than contractual compliance. And it is one that most organisations, if honest, would admit they fall well short of.

## The Efficiency Architecture This Creates

Mapping these categories together reveals a **tiered instantiation model**:


| Tier | What Happens | Human Effort |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **1 — Select template** | Category 6 defaults pre-populate ~40% of contract | Near zero |
| **2 — Inherit from parent** | Category 3 values flow down automatically | Near zero |
| **3 — Confirm enumerations** | Category 5 values reviewed and selected | Seconds per field |
| **4 — Runtime computation** | Category 7 values calculated automatically | Zero |
| **5 — Author unique elements** | Category 4 fields drafted (AI-assisted) and confirmed | Minutes |
| **6 — Governance references** | Category 1 references resolved automatically from context | Zero |
| **7 — Ruleset evaluation** | Category 2 values computed and surfaced for review | Seconds to review |
| **8 — Human confirmation** | Category 8 items reviewed and signed | Focused human attention |

For a well-typed, routine task using a mature template — an invoice review, a blog post, a bug fix — the human touch points collapse to: *confirm the unique description, review the AI-drafted DoD, and sign the commitment*. Everything else is inherited, computed, or defaulted.

The complexity doesn't disappear. It is **absorbed by the system**, surfaced only when it matters, and always traceable when accountability requires it.

## Potential Negative Impacts

These are real and must be mitigated by design from the start — not treated as acceptable externalities:

- **Surveillance anxiety and gaming** among performers optimising for metrics rather than value
- **Algorithmic discrimination** encoding historical biases into capability matching
- **Relational work made invisible** — mentoring, culture, emotional labour are hard to task-contract
- **Bureaucratic ossification** — compliance culture replacing value culture
- **Labour precarity at scale** if task contracts replace employment without redistributing protections
- **Agent displacement without redistribution** concentrating AI productivity gains in platform owners
- **Single point of truth failure** making system outages catastrophic
