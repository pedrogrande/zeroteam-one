# The most profound realisation I had during the design thinking and ideation process here was that I was neglecting traditional trust building methods in my understanding of blockchain systems and applications.

And I believe most blockchain dapp and system builders miss this. They see the trust that blockchain brings, and think that is enough.

One needs to understand the "why" and "how" for ourselves so we can make our own judgement on it.
And blockchain is a multi-layered and complexity-riddled technology that can't be explained fully in a single sitting. It took me several years to really appreciate blockchain. And I'm still learning more every day.

If we instead to first establish the non-blockchain foundations of trust building and earn trust that way, the blockchain element just takes the trust level higher as people naturally start to understand its value.

This realization sits at the very heart of your design thinking session's closing postscript, and it's worth naming with precision: you didn't reject blockchain — you discovered its correct position in a trust sequence.[^1_1]

## The Sequence Error Most Builders Make

The conventional blockchain pitch inverts cause and effect. It positions smart contracts as the *source* of trust — replace human institutions with cryptographic ones, and trust is solved. But as your ideation session surfaced, this makes a category error: **blockchain solves the enforcement layer of trust, not its substrate.**  Smart contracts make defection detectable and automatically punishable. That is genuinely powerful. But enforcement only operates on top of something — a shared understanding of what was agreed, a verifiable record of what actually happened, an environment both parties inhabit together.[^1_1]

If those foundations don't exist, a smart contract is enforcing claims that were never reliably verified in the first place.

## What Trust Is Actually Made Of

Research consistently identifies three components that produce trust in any agent — human or non-human: **competence** (can they do what they claim?), **reliability** (do they do it consistently, not just once?), and **integrity** (do they behave the same when no one is watching?).  A smart contract addresses none of these directly — it can verify an outcome, but only if the outcome was honestly generated and accurately reported. The marble jar fills through repeated small acts. Blockchain can make those acts permanent and portable, but it cannot conjure them from nothing.[^1_1]

This is precisely why your four primitives — the Evidence Gate, Environment Contract, Context Card, and Observable Stream — are the substrate that smart contracts need to be meaningful.  Without them, blockchain enforces claims. With them, blockchain enforces *evidence*.[^1_1]

## The Complexity Problem You Named

Your second point — that people won't trust blockchain simply because experts say so — is actually a pedagogical truth that most blockchain educators skip. Trust in *technology* follows the same incremental pattern as trust in *people*: it is built through small, increasing commitments over time, not in one-shot demonstrations.  Asking someone to trust a multi-layered cryptoeconomic system before they have a felt sense of trust in the *work being done* is asking them to take the elevator straight to the top floor of a building whose lower floors they've never visited.[^1_1]

The design implication is significant: trust should be domain-specific and evidence-grounded before it is cryptographically enforced.[^1_1]

## The Sequence, Made Explicit

The postscript from your session names the full chain cleanly:[^1_1]


| Layer | Mechanism | What it provides |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **1. Understand trust** | Design session, human foundations | Phenomenology — what trust actually is |
| **2. Build systems that produce trust** | The four primitives | Competence, reliability, integrity at the task level |
| **3. Make trust verifiable and portable** | Smart contracts / blockchain | Cryptographic enforcement of verified evidence |
| **4. Make verifiable trust universally accessible** | The PhD's contribution | Economic participation in low-trust institutional environments |

The profound thing about this sequence is that each layer *requires* the one below it. Blockchain at layer 3 without the primitives at layer 2 is roof-without-foundation architecture.  And the ambition at layer 4 — unlocking economic participation for people in low-trust environments who currently can't access global knowledge work — is only achievable if layers 1 through 3 are in place first.[^1_1]

## Why This Is Rare

Most blockchain builders start at layer 3 and ask users to trust backward into layers 1 and 2 by inference. You've arrived at a framework that builds forward — earning trust through observable, repeated, evidence-based behaviour first, then letting blockchain *amplify and preserve* that earned trust rather than *substitute for* it.  That's a fundamentally different design philosophy, and it's one that makes the blockchain element far more powerful precisely because it arrives on top of an already trustworthy foundation rather than as a leap of faith.[^1_1]

## Phase 2 — Define

My job now is to run the three-level "why" ladder on each problem, find shared roots, map the design tensions, and ask the question that cuts to the core: *which problem, if solved, makes the others easier?*[^6_1]

***

### Problem Laddering

**Problem 1 — The Translation Problem**
*Intent can't be fully captured in formal specification*


| Level | Why is this a problem? |
| :-- | :-- |
| Why 1 | Because the executing mechanism — a court, or code — has access only to the specification, never to the intent behind it |
| Why 2 | Because discrepancies between specification and intent only become visible when a situation arises that the specification handles in an unexpected way |
| Why 3 | Because by then, the wrong thing has already executed — and the remedy (litigation, hard fork, or financial loss) is expensive and often irreversible |

**Root:** Intent and formal specification are fundamentally different kinds of thing. One is a mental state embedded in a relationship and a context. The other is a static text or program stripped of both. The gap is not a drafting failure — it is structural and irreducible.

***

**Problem 2 — The Futures Problem (Incomplete Contract Problem)**
*The world changes in ways that weren't anticipated at writing time*


| Level | Why is this a problem? |
| :-- | :-- |
| Why 1 | Because contracts specify actions for anticipated states of the world — unanticipated states either fall outside the contract (creating a gap) or get forced into the nearest specified state (creating a mismatch) |
| Why 2 | Because gaps require renegotiation or adjudication (both costly), and mismatches in smart contracts execute the wrong outcome automatically with no recourse |
| Why 3 | Because specifying every possible contingency in advance is genuinely impossible — not just expensive — since some future states are unknowable at writing time |

**Root:** The future is not expensive to specify — it is *unknowable*. This is Williamson's bounded rationality point taken to its limit. Every contract is written in a present that cannot see its own future. Incompleteness is not a design failure; it is a fundamental property of any agreement made across time.[^6_2]

***

**Problem 3 — The Trust Prerequisite Problem**
*Contracts require trust but cannot create it*


| Level | Why is this a problem? |
| :-- | :-- |
| Why 1 | Because when trust is absent or eroding, contracts become defensive instruments — parties design them to limit loss and preserve exit, not to enable cooperation |
| Why 2 | Because defensive contracts signal distrust and can accelerate the breakdown of the relationship they were meant to govern — the act of drafting becomes evidence of the problem it's trying to solve |
| Why 3 | Because the mechanism that's supposed to enable coordination in low-trust situations *requires* trust to function properly — shared interpretation of intent, good-faith renegotiation, and honest representation of circumstances all depend on trust that the contract was meant to replace |

**Root:** Contracts are a *workaround* for a trust problem, not a solution to it. Where trust is high, contracts are short, informal, or unnecessary. Where trust is low, contracts grow longer and more defensive — but a longer contract doesn't add trust; it documents its absence. The circular dependency is real: contracts need the very thing they're trying to substitute for.

***

### Shared Root Across All Three Problems

Running the ladders side by side, something surfaces:

All three problems are instances of the same underlying gap —

> **The gap between what parties actually share (intent, context, relationship, trust) and what any formal mechanism can capture (text, code, specified conditions).**

- Translation: gap between intent and specification
- Futures: gap between the world-as-anticipated and the world-as-it-becomes
- Trust prerequisite: gap between the formal record and the relational substrate

This matters because it means solving any one of these in isolation will push the problem into one of the others. A more complete specification (attacking Translation) still can't handle the unknowable future (Futures). A mechanism that adapts to the future (attacking Futures) still requires good-faith interpretation of what adapting means (Trust prerequisite). A mechanism that builds trust (attacking Trust prerequisite) still needs to translate shared understanding into enforceable commitments (Translation).

The shared root isn't any one of the three problems. **The shared root is that formal mechanisms cannot fully capture the informal substrate that gives them meaning.**

| **Completeness ↔ Flexibility** | More specified = less adaptable. More adaptable = more gaps. |
| **Automation ↔ Judgment** | Automatic execution removes trust in enforcement but removes capacity for contextual judgment. Human enforcement allows judgment but requires trust in the adjudicator. |
| **Specification ↔ Relationship** | More detailed specification signals distrust and can damage the relationship. Less specification relies on and builds relationship but creates legal risk. |
| **Trust-substitution ↔ Trust-building** | Mechanisms that substitute for trust (contracts) are fundamentally different from mechanisms that build trust (reputation, transparency, repeated interaction). Conflating them produces neither. |
| **Protection ↔ Coordination** | Contracts designed to protect individual interests (exit clauses, loopholes) undermine the collective coordination they're meant to enable. |


***

### The Key Question

*Which problem, if solved, makes the others easier?*

The Trust Prerequisite problem is the most fundamental. Here's why:

- If trust were present, Translation imprecision becomes tolerable — parties use good judgment to interpret intent and extend good faith when specifications fall short
- If trust were present, the Futures problem becomes manageable — parties renegotiate in good faith when circumstances change
- But if trust is absent, even a perfectly specified, complete contract fails — the DAO hack was a trust failure embedded in code, and your partnership story was a trust failure that a contract could only partially contain

The implication is significant: **contracts are not the primary coordination mechanism — trust is. Contracts are what you reach for when trust isn't available.** If you could make trust cheaper and faster to establish, you'd need contracts less.

This is the same conclusion your own framework ideation reached from a different direction: *"Trust is coordination infrastructure. When trust is cheap, coordination is cheap."*[^6_3]

## What Game Theory Adds to Phase 2

### The canonical case you're describing has a name

The situation you named — *"coordinating between strangers with no prior relationship and no expectation of future interaction"* — is the one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma. In this setup, the mathematically dominant strategy for a rational self-interested player is to defect. Not because people are bad, but because the payoff structure makes cooperation irrational. If you cooperate and I defect, you lose and I win. If we both defect, we both lose a little. The rational move is to defect.[^8_1]

**This is the exact problem contracts were invented to solve.** They change the payoff matrix. Defection (breach of contract) becomes costly through enforcement. Cooperation becomes safer because defection is punishable. The contract doesn't create trust — it changes the maths.

### The Folk Theorem is the game-theoretic answer

Robert Axelrod's research showed something profound: in *repeated* games — where the same parties interact again and again with no clear endpoint — cooperation emerges naturally without any contract or external enforcement. The most successful strategy across all his simulations was **Tit-for-Tat**: start by cooperating, then mirror whatever the other party did last time. It's nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and clear.[^8_2][^8_3]

The **Folk Theorem** formalises this: in an infinitely (or indefinitely) repeated game, any outcome that's mutually beneficial can be sustained as an equilibrium — as long as players are sufficiently patient and the probability of future interaction is high enough.[^8_1]

Axelrod's key line is worth sitting with:

> *"For cooperation to prove stable, the future must have a sufficiently large shadow."*[^8_2]

**The shadow of the future** is the concept. The bigger it is — the more likely and valuable future interactions are — the more cooperation becomes rational right now, even between self-interested parties, even without contracts.

### What contracts are, precisely restated

Contracts are a mechanism for **artificially extending the shadow of the future into one-shot interactions**. They do this by importing the consequences of future interactions (enforcement, reputation damage, financial penalty) into the present. They make a one-shot game behave more like a repeated game.[^8_1]

That's their actual function. Not coordination. Not trust. **Payoff transformation.**

### Ostrom: the most important counterevidence

Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize work showed something contracts miss entirely. Communities governing shared resources — fisheries, irrigation systems, forests — developed their own coordination mechanisms through communication, monitoring, and graduated sanctions. No contracts. No external authority. No courts.[^8_4]

Her finding: *"Isolated, anonymous individuals overharvest from common-pool resources. Simply allowing communication — 'cheap talk' — enables participants to reduce overharvesting."*[^8_4]

What they built instead were: shared rules developed by the community, monitoring by participants, graduated consequences for defection, and mechanisms for resolving conflicts locally. These are repeated-game mechanisms — they work because the parties expect to keep interacting.

**What this means for your design question:** Ostrom is evidence that coordination without contracts is not just theoretically possible — it has worked at scale, across centuries, across cultures. The mechanism isn't formal specification. It's **visibility + community + graduated consequence**.

***

### Round 1 — Within the Domain

Five approaches from familiar frames: contracts, economics, blockchain, coordination theory.

***

**Approach 1 — The Living Document**

*Core idea:* A contract is not a static artefact — it is a versioned record of evolving mutual intent. Instead of specifying all conditions upfront, parties agree to a lightweight initial agreement plus an explicit amendment protocol: who can propose changes, what triggers review, how consent is recorded. The contract's version history is as important as its current state.

*HMW connections:* HMW1 (preserve intent without full specification), HMW4 (adapt without unilateral rewriting)

*Tension introduced:* Who initiates an amendment? If one party can propose changes at any time, the other party faces permanent renegotiation pressure. The amendment protocol needs as much design as the contract itself.

***

**Approach 2 — The Outcomes Contract**

*Core idea:* Specify what success looks like, not what actions must be taken to achieve it. Parties agree on measurable outcomes and verification criteria — not prescribed behaviours. The path is left open. This is OKRs made enforceable.

*HMW connections:* HMW1 (intent over specification), HMW7 (coordinate without pretending to predict the path)

*Tension introduced:* Outcome measurement itself requires trust. What happens when the outcome is genuinely ambiguous? And who defines what "success looks like" when parties have different mental models of the goal?

***

**Approach 3 — The Escrow Ladder**

*Core idea:* Release value in small, staged increments tied to observable milestones. Each stage is small enough that defection is never more attractive than completion — the cost of walking away always exceeds the value of defecting. The contract becomes a sequence of small bets, not one large one.

*HMW connections:* HMW3 (make defection costly without courts or code), HMW8 (scale mechanism weight to actual risk)

*Tension introduced:* Requires capital to be locked up upfront. Parties with less capital are structurally disadvantaged. The ladder assumes milestones are clearly observable — which reintroduces the verification problem.

***

**Approach 4 — The Minimum Viable Agreement**

*Core idea:* Embrace incompleteness explicitly as a design principle. Specify only the conditions that cover the most probable situations — the 20% that handles 80% of what actually happens. For the rest, pre-agree a dispute resolution process rather than trying to anticipate outcomes. The agreement is small; the process for handling what it doesn't cover is robust.

*HMW connections:* HMW1 (preserve intent without exhaustive specification), HMW7 (coordinate without pretending to predict), HMW8 (proportionality — small agreement for small stakes)

*Tension introduced:* The uncovered 20% is almost always the most contested territory. Agreeing on a dispute process in advance still requires trust in the adjudicator — which loops back to the trust prerequisite problem.

***

**Approach 5 — The Graduated Commitment**

*Core idea:* Parties don't commit to the full agreement at signing. They commit to progressively deeper levels of obligation as trust accumulates through early execution. Phase 1 is low-stakes; good performance unlocks Phase 2 with higher stakes and correspondingly stronger obligations. The contract deepens as the relationship proves itself.

*HMW connections:* HMW5 (extend repeated-game benefits to new relationships), HMW3 (make defection costly — but only after trust has been established, not as a precondition), HMW8 (proportionality)

*Tension introduced:* Who defines the graduation criteria? Asymmetric power means one party might control the conditions for advancement, creating a new form of hold-up.

***

### Round 2 — Outside the Domain

Five mechanisms borrowed from completely different fields. For each: what is the source system, what is the core mechanism being borrowed, and what tension does it introduce.

***

**Approach 6 — The Constitutional Model**
*(Source: political philosophy and common law)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* A constitution is a document explicitly designed to be incomplete. Its power lies not in anticipating every situation, but in establishing foundational principles plus a deliberate amendment pathway. Common law builds on top — each case that falls outside existing law generates a precedent that becomes part of the system. The law evolves through encounter, not anticipation.

*Applied here:* Agreements specify non-negotiable principles (what this relationship can never do) and a precedent-building mechanism (how novel situations get resolved and recorded). Over time, the precedent layer handles the unknowable future by accumulating real decisions rather than hypothetical ones.

*HMW connections:* HMW4 (adapt without unilateral rewriting), HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty without pretending to predict)

*Tension introduced:* Common law is slow and expensive. Requires an interpretive body — a court, a community, an arbitrator — and trust in that body. Works at civilisational scale; unclear how it works for two parties in a single agreement.

***

**Approach 7 — The Jazz Standard**
*(Source: music performance)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* A jazz standard specifies the minimum shared structure — key, tempo, chord sequence — within which radical improvisation is possible. Every performance is different. The structure doesn't constrain; it enables. Musicians who know the standard can play together immediately, adapting in real time to each other, without specifying what will happen.

*Applied here:* An agreement specifies the minimal shared grammar — what game we're playing, what counts as a contribution, what constitutes defection — and leaves the rest open. Parties improvise within the grammar rather than executing a script. The agreement becomes a score, not a checklist.

*HMW connections:* HMW1 (preserve intent without full specification), HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty)

*Tension introduced:* Jazz requires significant shared background knowledge — years of practice with the same musical language. Strangers can't improvise together without that common grammar. The mechanism works beautifully in ongoing relationships; unclear how it helps with first encounters.

***

**Approach 8 — The Immune System**
*(Source: biology)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* An immune system doesn't pre-specify every pathogen it will ever encounter — that would be impossible. Instead, it maintains a system for recognising novelty, generating a response, and retaining memory of that response for future encounters. It builds adaptive capacity, not predictive completeness. The cost is paid on first encounter; every subsequent encounter with the same threat is handled cheaply.

*Applied here:* Rather than trying to specify all contingencies, an agreement includes a mechanism for recognising when an unspecified situation has arisen, generating a mutually acceptable response, and recording that response as part of the agreement for future use. The agreement learns.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty without pretending to predict), HMW4 (adapt without unilateral rewriting)

*Tension introduced:* The first encounter with a novel situation is unprotected — the system fails before it adapts. High cost of first exposure. Requires that both parties survive the initial failure and commit to staying in the relationship long enough for the memory to form.

***

**Approach 9 — The Options Contract**
*(Source: financial derivatives)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* An option is the right but not the obligation to act at a future point. It separates the right to participate from the obligation to complete. It is designed specifically for genuine uncertainty about whether future conditions will be favourable. Rather than betting on a specific future, you preserve the ability to respond when the future becomes clearer.

*Applied here:* Parties agree on the conditions under which they will proceed, rather than agreeing to proceed unconditionally. The agreement is a set of options, not obligations. Each party holds the option to activate the next stage. The agreement structures choice rather than eliminating it.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty), HMW4 (adapt to changed circumstances), HMW8 (proportionality — commit only what the current level of certainty justifies)

*Tension introduced:* Pricing optionality requires valuing uncertain futures — which is exactly what we said was impossible. The Black-Scholes model works for financial derivatives because markets provide price discovery. Without a market, how do you value the option?

***

**Approach 10 — The Andon Cord**
*(Source: Toyota production system)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Any worker on the production line can pull the andon cord to halt the entire line when they see a problem — without asking for permission, without a manager's approval, without a formal process. The response is immediate, collective, and visible to everyone. The line restarts only when the problem is resolved. It is distributed sensing with collective response.

*Applied here:* Any party to an agreement — or any observer with a stake in its outcome — can trigger a pause in execution when they detect that circumstances have diverged from what was anticipated. Execution halts; parties convene; the situation is assessed; execution resumes or the agreement is amended. The cord is the mechanism for handling the unknowable future in real time.

*HMW connections:* HMW4 (adapt to changed circumstances without unilateral rewriting), HMW6 (make the gap between agreed and actual visible without requiring parties to police each other), HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty by acknowledging it when it arrives)

*Tension introduced:* Requires a culture where pulling the cord is safe — psychologically and economically. If halting execution is penalised, the cord never gets pulled. Works in Toyota because the culture actively rewards problem detection. In most contracts, raising a problem is adversarial by default.

### Hidden Contracts — Things We Don't Call Contracts

This angle immediately opens something worth naming before we generate approaches:

**The covenant vs. contract distinction.**

In religious and philosophical tradition, these are fundamentally different instruments:

- A **contract** is conditional: *"I do X if you do Y."* It collapses when circumstances change or one party fails.
- A **covenant** is unconditional: *"I commit to X regardless of what you do or what happens."* Marriage vows are the canonical form — *"in sickness and in health, for better or worse."* A covenant is designed precisely to hold when the future becomes unknowable and hard.

That distinction is directly relevant to the Futures problem. A covenant doesn't need to anticipate every contingency because it's not contingent. It holds *because* the future is uncertain, not in spite of it.

It's also the structure underneath any genuine relationship — including what a brand actually promises, and what the social contract between a citizen and a state is supposed to be.

***

**Approach 11 — The Covenant**
*(Source: religious and philosophical tradition)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* An unconditional commitment that doesn't depend on reciprocity, specified conditions, or anticipated circumstances. Unlike a contract, it doesn't collapse when things go wrong — it's designed to hold precisely then.

*Applied here:* Parties commit to a shared purpose and to each other's flourishing, not to specified terms. The "agreement" is about who we are to each other, not what we will do. Renegotiation isn't required when circumstances change because the commitment was never circumstance-dependent.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty without pretending to predict), HMW1 (preserve intent — the intent is preserved in the relationship, not the specification)

*Tension introduced:* Covenants require genuine shared values, not just aligned incentives. They are deeply vulnerable to exploitation by a party who doesn't hold the same commitment. They can't be enforced — only held, or broken. They don't scale to stranger-coordination at all.

***

**Approach 12 — The Brand Promise**
*(Source: marketing / stakeholder theory)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* A unilateral public commitment to a community, enforced collectively by reputation and market behaviour rather than courts. The brand commits; the community holds them accountable through distributed action — buying, not buying, recommending, denouncing. Enforcement is emergent, not adjudicated.

*Applied here:* A party makes a public commitment to a set of principles and standards. The "contract" is enforced by the community's collective response to observed behaviour, not by a bilateral legal mechanism. Breach is punished by reputational loss at scale.

*HMW connections:* HMW3 (make defection costly without courts or code), HMW2 (no legal expertise required — community enforcement is accessible to everyone)

*Tension introduced:* Weak for high-stakes individual transactions. Can be gamed by performing the values rather than living them. Requires the community to have enough collective power and information to punish defection — which is not always present.

***

**Approach 13 — The Schelling Focal Point**
*(Source: game theory — Thomas Schelling)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Parties coordinate without any agreement, contract, or communication — by converging on the "obvious" outcome their shared cultural context makes salient. When asked to meet in New York with no prior arrangement, most people say Grand Central at noon. No contract. No negotiation. Just shared context producing convergent expectations.[^12_2]

*Applied here:* Design coordination situations so that the right action is so obvious — given shared values, shared context, or shared identity — that it doesn't need to be specified. Standards, norms, and cultural protocols are all focal-point mechanisms.

*HMW connections:* HMW2 (coordination without expertise), HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty — focal points handle novel situations by pointing at "what would obviously make sense here")

*Tension introduced:* Only works when there IS a clear focal point — when shared context exists. Diverse parties, novel situations, or high-stakes asymmetric outcomes may have no focal point. And deliberately designing focal points requires the very shared context that's missing when you need them most.

### Utopian and Dystopian Science Fiction

Two approaches — one aspiration, one warning. Both are design-relevant.

***

**Approach 14 — The Culture (Banks)**
*(Source: Iain M. Banks' Culture novels — utopian)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Post-scarcity coordination without contracts, money, or enforcement. In the Culture, there is no scarcity, so no coordination problem requiring formal agreement. AI Minds handle macro-coordination. Individuals coordinate through conversation, social norms, and genuine care for each other's flourishing. Contracts are unnecessary because the conditions that make them necessary — scarcity and distrust — have been dissolved.

For non-scarce goods — information, ideas, attention, code — the Culture model may already be operational. Open source software looks like this.

*Tension introduced:* Requires solving the scarcity problem as a prerequisite. But surfaces an important question: how much of what we use contracts for is *actually* about scarcity, and how much is about distrust that could be dissolved by other means?

**Approach 15 — Nosedive (Black Mirror)**
*(Source: Black Mirror, Season 3 — dystopian warning)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Universal, continuous, mutual social rating. Everyone rates every interaction; aggregate scores determine access to housing, transport, employment. Coordination is enforced by ubiquitous reputational stakes — defection becomes immediately costly because everyone can see your score.

*Why it's in the ideation:* This is the failure mode of HMW5 taken to its logical extreme. It shows what happens when you solve portable reputation without solving the authenticity problem — scores stop reflecting actual trustworthiness and start reflecting likability, social compliance, and performance of the right values. The marble jar gets gamed. The mechanism optimises for the signal rather than what the signal was supposed to represent.

*HMW connections:* Warning for HMW5 and HMW3 — the shape of what we don't want

*Tension introduced:* This IS the tension. When reputation becomes currency, it gets counterfeited. The failure mode isn't the mechanism itself — it's the absence of a way to verify that marbles are real.

### Game Theory — The Solution Space We Haven't Fully Explored


***

**Approach 16 — The Revelation Mechanism**
*(Source: mechanism design — Hurwicz, Maskin, Myerson, 2007 Nobel Prize)*[^12_3]

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Design the rules of interaction such that truthful revelation of private information becomes each party's dominant strategy. In a well-designed mechanism, you can't benefit from lying or concealing your true position. Coordination becomes possible even when parties have private information and self-interested motives — not by trusting them, but by making honesty their best rational option.

*Applied here:* Instead of trying to specify what parties must do, design the interaction structure so that their optimal self-interested strategy produces coordination. The "contract" is the game design, not the terms. Smart contracts are a crude version of this — they attempt to make the outcome of the game deterministic. Mechanism design asks the deeper question: can you make the *right* outcome the self-interested one?

*HMW connections:* HMW3 (make defection costly — structurally, not through punishment), HMW6 (make gaps visible — a well-designed mechanism surfaces private information automatically)

*Tension introduced:* Requires knowing enough about the underlying game structure to design the mechanism correctly. Misdesigned mechanisms produce perverse incentives that are worse than no mechanism. The complexity of design may exceed the complexity of the contract it replaces.[^12_3]

***

**Approach 17 — The Open Source Commons**
*(Source: Linux, Wikipedia, open source software)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Coordination among strangers at massive scale without contracts — through contribution norms, transparent public history, and the always-available option to fork. The "agreement" is a minimal permissive license plus social norms of contributing back. Quality is maintained by peer review and reputation. Anyone can verify what happened. Anyone can exit. No adjudicator required.

*Applied here:* What if coordination agreements worked like open source licenses — minimal, public, and enforced by community norms rather than legal machinery? The "terms" are the protocol; compliance is verified by the community; defection is handled by forking (exiting and continuing without the defector).

*HMW connections:* HMW2 (no expertise required), HMW6 (visibility — public history), HMW5 (portable reputation — your contribution history is public), HMW7 (emergent coordination without prediction — the protocol handles novel situations through community response)

*Tension introduced:* Works for non-rival goods — code, text, ideas. Unclear whether it translates to coordination around scarce or exclusive resources. Also requires a community with shared values strong enough to sustain contribution norms without formal enforcement.

# what about the other view we took earlier, that contracts were mainly about managing risk.

insurance is something that is another way to manage risk.

what other ways do we do this?

Right — and this is a genuinely different axis we haven't fully mapped.[^13_1]

Here's the distinction that makes it important before we generate:

**Contracts are risk prevention mechanisms.** They try to make failure expensive enough that it doesn't happen.

**Insurance is a risk absorption mechanism.** It accepts that failure *will* happen and designs for how the damage gets distributed.

These are philosophically opposite orientations to the future. A contract says: *specify what must not happen and penalise it.* Insurance says: *we don't know what will go wrong, but we agree in advance on how we'll handle it when something does.* Insurance is actually the most honest response to the unknowable future of any mechanism we've looked at — it doesn't require prediction at all.

That distinction opens several new approaches.

***

**Approach 18 — The Insurance Pool**
*(Source: actuarial science / mutual insurance)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Risk is distributed across many parties rather than concentrated in a bilateral agreement. No single party bears the full cost of a bad outcome. The mechanism doesn't require knowing *what* will go wrong — only *that* something will, at some rate. Premiums are priced to the probability distribution, not the specific future.

*Applied here:* Parties to coordination agreements contribute to a shared pool. When any party suffers a loss due to another's failure, the pool absorbs part of the cost. Individual exposure is bounded. The mechanism scales: the larger the pool, the more predictable the aggregate loss rate, the cheaper the individual premium.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty without predicting the future — insurance doesn't require it), HMW8 (proportionality — premiums scale to actual risk), HMW2 (accessible — mutual insurance societies predated legal infrastructure by centuries)

*Tension introduced:* Moral hazard. If your losses are covered by the pool, your incentive to avoid failure is reduced. Insurance creates the very risk it's insuring against, unless carefully designed with deductibles, co-payments, and monitoring. Also requires the pool to be large enough for the law of large numbers to work — bilateral insurance doesn't function.

***

**Approach 19 — The Performance Bond**
*(Source: construction / government contracting)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* A trusted third party — a bank, an insurer, a bonding company — guarantees your performance to the other party. If you fail, the bond pays. The third party has incentive to assess your reliability before issuing the bond (they're on the hook) — which means the bond itself functions as a credibility signal. You have the bond; therefore someone with skin in the game assessed you as reliable.

*Applied here:* Rather than parties trying to assess each other's trustworthiness directly, a third party with expertise and stake in the outcome performs that assessment and backs it with capital. The "contract" becomes simpler — almost just "do the thing" — because the performance guarantee is carried separately by the bond.

*HMW connections:* HMW5 (extend repeated-game benefits to strangers — the bonding company's assessment is portable across relationships), HMW3 (defection is costly — through the bond mechanism, not a court), HMW2 (potentially accessible — if bond infrastructure existed at fine-grained scale)

*Tension introduced:* Requires a trustworthy third party. Concentration of bonding capacity creates a new bottleneck and potential single point of failure. The 2008 financial crisis was partly an insurance/bond failure at exactly this mechanism — AIG's credit default swaps were performance bonds that failed because the pool wasn't large enough for correlated risk.

***

**Approach 20 — The Probationary Architecture**
*(Source: employment law / clinical trials)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Before full commitment, run a bounded exposure period. Clinical trials Phase I → Phase II → Phase III: each stage expands the commitment only if the previous stage produced acceptable evidence. Employment probation: full obligations don't attach until performance has been observed. The architecture manages risk by limiting exposure until information is available.

*Applied here:* Every coordination agreement starts as a probe — a small, bounded, low-stakes version of the full agreement. Information gained in the probe changes the terms of the next stage. Full commitment is unlocked by evidence, not promised upfront.

*HMW connections:* HMW5 (earn the repeated game — each probe is a marble), HMW8 (proportionality — exposure matches the information available), HMW7 (don't pretend to predict — commit only to what the current evidence supports)

*Tension introduced:* Both Approaches 5 (Graduated Commitment) and 20 have this shape — the distinction is the framing. Graduated Commitment is about trust accumulation; Probationary Architecture is about risk reduction through information. They may merge in Phase 5.

***

**Approach 21 — The Redundancy Architecture**
*(Source: aerospace / nuclear engineering)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Don't try to prevent failure — design systems that survive it. Redundant systems, independent failure modes, graceful degradation. A Boeing 777 doesn't rely on any single component not failing; it assumes components will fail and designs around that assumption. Resilience, not perfection.

*Applied here:* Rather than trying to write a contract that prevents all bad outcomes, design the coordination arrangement so that no single failure is catastrophic. Multiple overlapping agreements, alternative paths, reversible commitments. If one thing fails, the system degrades gracefully rather than collapsing.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty — by building for failure rather than against it), HMW4 (adapt when circumstances change — redundancy provides options when the primary path closes)

*Tension introduced:* Redundancy is expensive. It requires over-provisioning relative to the expected case. The discipline of contract law partly exists because redundancy in human coordination is very costly — having multiple backup agreements for every commitment isn't practical. But in some contexts (critical infrastructure, high-stakes multi-party coordination) it's clearly worth it.

***

**Approach 22 — The Mutual Aid Society**
*(Source: pre-insurance community history / labour movement)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Before insurance was industrialised, communities self-organised mutual aid — contributions to shared pools, informal assessment of need, community-enforced norms of reciprocity. The Victorian working class friendly societies, rotating credit associations across cultures, barn-raising in agrarian communities. Coordination without contracts, risk absorption without actuarial tables.

*Applied here:* A community of parties to coordination agreements maintain a shared commitment to support each other when things go wrong — not through a formal mechanism, but through a community norm of reciprocity that everyone monitors and enforces. The "agreement" is membership in the community and commitment to its norms.

*HMW connections:* HMW3 (defection costly — social exclusion from the community), HMW2 (no expertise required — mutual aid predates legal and financial infrastructure), HMW5 (portable reputation — community membership is the credential), HMW7 (no prediction required — just commitment to respond)

*Tension introduced:* Only works within the scale of community where social norms can be enforced. Dunbar's number lurks here — above roughly 150 people, the social fabric that enforces reciprocity becomes too thin. Scales through nested communities, but those introduce their own coordination problems.

***

### The New Axis This Opens

We now have three distinct orientations to risk in the table:


| Orientation | Logic | Examples |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Prevention** | Make failure expensive enough to deter it | Contracts (1–7), Escrow Ladder (3), Performance Bond (19) |
| **Absorption** | Accept failure; distribute or bound its cost | Insurance Pool (18), Mutual Aid (22), Redundancy (21) |
| **Reduction** | Reduce uncertainty before committing | Probationary Architecture (20), Graduated Commitment (5), Due diligence |

And Nassim Taleb's concept of **antifragility** suggests a fourth, which none of our approaches have quite named yet: mechanisms that don't just survive failure but *get stronger from it* — like the Immune System (8) or the Constitutional Model (6) when they accumulate precedents.

That may be a synthesis primitive in Phase 5.

# one aspect I've been fascinated and challenged by is the idea with smart contracts that proof must be supplied to execute. It is difficult to think of suitable evidence that must be supplied for some criteria as it must be verifiable in a decentralized way. For instance, if we wanted to incentivize people to exercise more, how could we get proof that they exercised using a method that can't be gamed and is not too inefficient to procure to be not worth the reward?

I know the core of this is related to other aspects of our discussion eg. Oracle problem etc. But maybe there's something to explore here

This is Phase 4 material, but it's also pointing at something that cuts across *all* of the approaches we've already generated. Let me name what it is before we generate new approaches.

***

## The Verification Problem — The Mirror of Translation

You've identified something structural. We named the **Translation Problem** earlier as: *intent → specification always loses something.* What you're pointing at now is its mirror image:

**The Verification Problem:** *real-world event → on-chain proof always loses authenticity.*

They're symmetrical failures. On the way *in*, you can't fully capture what you mean. On the way *out*, you can't fully prove what happened. Every smart contract sits between these two gaps.[^14_1]

This is exactly what the Evidence Gate primitive in your framework is trying to solve — *before* any automated execution can be trusted, the evidence triggering that execution must be reliably verified.  The exercise example is just a particularly vivid illustration of a problem that exists in every contract with any real-world condition.[^14_1]

***

## Goodhart's Law — The Fundamental Challenge

Before generating approaches, one principle needs to be on the table:

> **"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."** — Charles Goodhart

The moment you specify *"proof of exercise"* as the triggering criterion, you've created an optimisation target. Rational actors will optimise for the proxy rather than the underlying behaviour. The measure and the thing you care about are never perfectly correlated — and once money is attached, that correlation degrades further.

STEPN (move-to-earn protocol) is the real-world example here. It was gamed through GPS emulators, multiple accounts, and trading schemes within months of launch. The reward was large enough that gaming became rational. The verification mechanism — GPS + accelerometer data — was technically sophisticated but still gameable at scale.

This is a universal challenge, not a technical one. Any verification system that becomes valuable enough will attract enough adversarial creativity to erode it. The design question shifts from *"how do we make perfect proof?"* to *"how do we make gaming more expensive than the reward?"*

***

### New Approaches — The Verification Problem

**Approach 23 — Zero-Knowledge Attestation**
*(Source: cryptography / ZK proofs)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* A zero-knowledge proof allows you to prove that a statement is true without revealing the underlying data. You can prove you're over 18 without revealing your birthdate. You can prove your heart rate was elevated for 30 minutes without revealing your health records or location.

*Applied here:* A wearable device with a secure enclave generates a ZK proof that a certain biometric pattern (heart rate, movement, duration) occurred — without revealing the raw data. The proof is verifiable on-chain. The data never leaves the device.

*HMW connections:* HMW6 (make reality visible without parties policing each other), HMW2 (potentially accessible — no intermediary required)

*Tension introduced:* Requires trusted hardware with a genuine secure enclave. If the device itself is compromised, the proof is invalid. Also: ZK proofs prove the data matched a pattern — they don't prove the data was real. A device that generates fake biometric data still generates a valid proof.

***

**Approach 24 — Social Attestation with Stake**
*(Source: Ethereum Attestation Service / vouching systems)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Rather than hardware verification, trusted humans attest to each other's behaviour. Your workout partner attests that you ran. The attestation is on-chain and carries their reputation. If the attestation is challenged and found false, the attestor loses stake — creating genuine skin in the game.

*Applied here:* Any real-world event requiring verification is attested to by one or more people with established reputation and staked capital. Gaming requires corrupting your attestors, who are motivated not to be corrupted because their own reputation is at risk.

*HMW connections:* HMW5 (portable reputation — attestors carry reputation across attestations), HMW3 (defection costly — through attestor stake), HMW2 (no hardware required)

*Tension introduced:* Requires genuine social relationships. Collusion between close parties is easy. Also shifts the gaming surface — instead of gaming the hardware, you game the attestor network. And attestation becomes a burden on the attestor: why would your workout partner want to be your on-chain witness every morning?

***

**Approach 25 — The Optimistic Oracle**
*(Source: UMA Protocol / optimistic rollups)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Assume the claimed event occurred. Anyone can challenge the claim within a dispute window. If unchallenged, the claim is accepted and execution proceeds. If challenged, a resolution mechanism (jury, arbitration, automated check) adjudicates. The key insight: most claims are honest, so you only pay the cost of verification for the dishonest minority.

*Applied here:* Claimant asserts: *"I exercised for 30 minutes."* The claim triggers execution unless challenged within 24 hours. Challengers must stake something to challenge — preventing frivolous disputes. The cost of challenging is calibrated so that only credible disputes are raised.

*HMW connections:* HMW8 (proportionality — verification cost paid only when needed), HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty — don't require proof upfront), HMW4 (adapt when something goes wrong — the challenge window is the andon cord)

*Tension introduced:* The dispute window is an attack surface. Sophisticated actors can game the challenge mechanism itself. Also requires a credible resolution mechanism — which loops back to the trust prerequisite problem. Who arbitrates?

***

**Approach 26 — Aggregate Outcome Verification**
*(Source: insurance actuarial science / epidemiology)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Don't verify individual events. Verify aggregate outcomes over time. An insurer doesn't verify every safe drive — they verify your accident record over three years. Epidemiologists don't track every meal — they track health outcomes across populations.

*Applied here:* Instead of rewarding individual workouts (gameable), reward demonstrated health improvements over a defined period — verified by a medical professional or device at agreed intervals. You can't fake fitness improvements over 90 days. The granularity of verification shifts from per-event to per-period.

*HMW connections:* HMW7 (coordinate under uncertainty — accept that you can't verify every event), HMW8 (proportionality — verification cost paid periodically, not per-event), HMW1 (preserve intent — the intent was better health, not completed workouts; this verifies the intent directly)

*Tension introduced:* Delayed reward reduces incentive effectiveness — people respond more strongly to immediate feedback than deferred outcomes. Also requires agreement on what "health improvement" means and who measures it.

***

**Approach 27 — The Commitment Device with Inversion**
*(Source: Beeminder / behavioural economics)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* Invert the verification problem. Instead of proving you *did* something, you commit capital upfront and prove you *didn't fail*. Failure is automatically visible; success is the absence of verified failure. Beeminder: you set a goal, connect data, and automatically lose money when your data shows you missed it. The burden of proof is on the failure, not the success.

*Applied here:* Party stakes capital against a commitment. The default assumption is success. If failure can be demonstrated — through any verifiable signal, however imperfect — the stake is forfeit. The gaming challenge is now: can you prevent failure from being detected? Much harder than gaming a proof-of-success system, because failures are usually visible even without designed verification.

*HMW connections:* HMW3 (make defection costly — the stake is the cost), HMW8 (proportionality — stake size calibrated to commitment value), HMW7 (no prediction of success required — just a commitment)

*Tension introduced:* Requires the party to trust the data source that proves failure. If you're staking against your Fitbit data, the Fitbit company becomes a trusted third party. The decentralisation problem re-enters through the back door.

***

**Approach 28 — Composite Evidence Bundles**
*(Source: forensic science / anti-money laundering)*

*Core mechanism borrowed:* No single piece of evidence is definitive — but a composite of independent signals that would all need to be gamed simultaneously is much harder to fake. Forensic evidence works this way: fingerprints alone can be planted; fingerprints + DNA + CCTV + phone records together are nearly impossible to fabricate in concert. AML systems don't rely on any single transaction — they look for patterns across dozens of signals simultaneously.

*Applied here:* Verification requires a bundle of independent signals — GPS location, accelerometer pattern, heart rate curve, time of day, proximity to known gym locations, calendar data, social check-in — no single one of which is sufficient but together are expensive to game simultaneously. The gaming cost grows multiplicatively with each additional independent signal.

*HMW connections:* HMW6 (make gaps visible without policing each other — multiple signals create a picture), HMW3 (defection costly — gaming all signals simultaneously is expensive)

*Tension introduced:* Requires significant personal data disclosure. Privacy is sacrificed for verifiability. Who holds the bundle? Who has access? The aggregation of independent signals is also the architecture of surveillance — which is why this pattern is used in both forensics *and* authoritarian monitoring.

***

## The Pattern Across All Six

Looking at Approaches 23–28 together, there are really only **four verification strategies** — everything is a variant of one of these:


| Strategy | Logic | Gaming surface | Cost |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Hardware attestation** (23) | Trusted device generates proof | Compromise the device or its inputs | High upfront (secure hardware) |
| **Social attestation** (24) | Trusted humans vouch with stake | Collude with attestors | Ongoing social cost |
| **Optimistic default** (25) | Assume true; challenge if false | Game the challenge window | Paid only on dispute |
| **Outcome not event** (26, 27) | Verify results, not actions | Fake the outcome (harder) | Delayed reward |
| **Composite signals** (28) | Multiple independent proofs | Game all simultaneously | Privacy cost |

Appears in: Outcomes Contract (2), Constitutional Model (6), Immune System (8), Andon Cord (10), Brand Promise (12), Open Source Commons (17), all verification approaches (23–28)

The common logic: *trustworthiness is demonstrated through accumulated evidence of past behaviour, not asserted upfront.* The marble jar made portable. Every approach here is trying to carry past behaviour into new relationships as credible evidence.

### The Four Primitives

Every repeated mechanism collapses into one of four. These are the load-bearing pieces — the things the problem requires regardless of what form the solution takes.

***

**Primitive 1 — The Graduated Stake**

*What it solves:* The Futures problem and the Trust Prerequisite problem simultaneously.

*What it is:* Commitment and risk exposure scale proportionally to the information available and the trust demonstrated so far. No single large bet on an unknown future. A sequence of small, expanding commitments — each one only unlocked when the previous stage has produced evidence worth building on.

*Why it's essential:* The future is unknowable. Requiring full commitment upfront forces parties to pretend they can predict it. Graduated stakes accept uncertainty as a given and design within it rather than against it.

*Where it appears:* In every culture that practises courtship before marriage, probation before permanent employment, clinical trials before drug approval, phase-gating before full investment. The pattern is ancient. What's missing is its formal articulation as a coordination primitive.

***

**Primitive 2 — The Evidence Trail**

*What it solves:* The Verification Problem and the Translation Problem.

*What it is:* Real-world behaviour generates observable, verifiable evidence as a natural by-product of doing the thing — not as a separate proof-generation step. The evidence trail is the substrate that gives any coordination mechanism its grip on reality. Without it, execution is blind.

*Why it's essential:* You can't translate intent into code or legal language without loss. But you can observe and record behaviour. The evidence trail doesn't try to capture intent — it captures what actually happened, which is often the more important thing anyway.

*Where it appears:* Version control in software, chain of custody in forensics, contribution history in open source, double-entry bookkeeping in accounting, flight data recorders in aviation. Every domain that manages high-stakes coordination has invented this independently. It keeps reinventing itself because it's required by the problem.[^15_2]

***

**Primitive 3 — The Response Protocol**

*What it solves:* The Futures problem directly.

*What it is:* An explicit, pre-agreed mechanism for what happens when the agreement meets a situation it didn't anticipate. Not a contingency clause — those try to predict. A response protocol acknowledges that unspecified situations *will* arrive and specifies the *process* for handling them, not the *outcome*. Who convenes? Who proposes? How is consent given? What happens to execution while the response is being formulated?

*Why it's essential:* The incompleteness of contracts is not a drafting failure — it is a fundamental property of any agreement made across time. Every agreement needs a process for handling what it doesn't cover. The only question is whether that process is designed in advance or improvised under pressure when both parties are already in conflict.

*Where it appears:* The Andon Cord (halt and assess), the constitutional amendment process (deliberate pathway for the unanticipated), the jazz standard (minimal grammar enabling improvisation), common law precedent (novel situations become part of the system). The mechanism is: *acknowledge the gap, convene, decide, record.*[^15_3]

***

**Primitive 4 — The Portable Credential**

*What it solves:* The Trust Prerequisite problem. Specifically: how to extend the shadow of the future to strangers.

*What it is:* Past trustworthy behaviour generates verifiable evidence that can be carried into new relationships. Not a rating or a score — a verifiable record of specific behaviours in specific contexts, with enough information for a stranger to assess its relevance to what they're about to do together. The marble jar, made portable.

*Why it's essential:* Trust requires time and repeated interaction. But most coordination happens between parties with limited shared history. The Portable Credential doesn't simulate trust that doesn't exist — it carries forward evidence of trust that was genuinely earned elsewhere.

*Constraint it must honour:* The Nosedive warning. When credentials become currency, they get gamed. A portable credential only works if the underlying evidence is verifiable — which loops back to Primitive 2. The credential is downstream of the evidence trail.[^15_2]

***

### The Relationship Between Primitives

They're not independent. They form a sequence:

```
Evidence Trail → Portable Credential → Graduated Stake → Response Protocol
     ↑                                                           |
     └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                    (each execution feeds the trail)
```

The Evidence Trail is the substrate. The Portable Credential is what accumulates from it over time. The Graduated Stake is how you enter new relationships using it. The Response Protocol is what keeps the whole thing honest when reality diverges from plan — and its outputs feed back into the Evidence Trail.

This is an **agreement architecture**, not a contract. It's designed to learn through execution rather than predict before it.

***

### Design Principles

Specific enough to make a design decision — not values statements.[^15_1]

1. **Never require full commitment at the start of a relationship.** Any mechanism that demands full exposure upfront, before trust has been demonstrated, is misaligned with how trust actually works. Start small. Graduate deliberately.
2. **Design the response to the unanticipated before designing anything else.** The response protocol is more important than the terms of the agreement, because the terms will eventually be wrong and the protocol will be all that's left.
3. **Evidence is substrate; execution is superstructure.** No automated execution should trigger without a verified evidence trail. The trail is not documentation after the fact — it is the foundation the whole system rests on.
4. **The most important part of any agreement is its amendment protocol.** An agreement with good terms and no amendment pathway is more brittle than an agreement with minimal terms and a deliberate pathway for handling what wasn't anticipated.
5. **Portable credentials must carry context, not just scores.** A rating without context is gameable. Evidence of *what was done, under what conditions, with what result* is what makes a credential useful to a stranger. Strip the context and you get Nosedive.
6. **Gaming resistance scales multiplicatively with independent evidence sources.** A single verification signal is gameable. Every additional independent signal multiplies the cost of gaming. Design verification as a composite, not a single test.

### Probes

The minimum experiment that would validate or invalidate each primitive.[^15_1]

**Probe 1 — Graduated Stake**
*Hypothesis:* Two parties who enter a coordination agreement with an explicit graduation mechanism (small commitment → evidence of delivery → expanded commitment) will report higher psychological safety and lower exit rates than parties who commit fully upfront.
*Action:* Design two versions of a simple coordination agreement for one task in the Futures Edge pilot. One requires full commitment at signing. One stages commitment across three milestones. Run both with different pairs.
*Measurement:* Did the parties complete the agreement? Did either party feel the need to introduce defensive clauses? Would they use this structure again?
*Pass/fail:* Staged version produces 50%+ higher completion rate, or meaningfully lower reported distrust during execution.

**Probe 2 — Evidence Trail**
*Hypothesis:* 80% of coordination tasks in a software development context generate sufficient verifiable evidence as a natural by-product of doing the work — without requiring a separate proof-generation step.
*Action:* Take 20 tasks from the Futures Edge task graph. For each, identify what evidence exists *after* the task is complete that wasn't generated *specifically for verification*. Commit messages, pull requests, test results, deployment logs, user feedback.
*Measurement:* What percentage of tasks are self-evidencing? What percentage require additional proof steps?
*Pass/fail:* 80% of tasks are self-evidencing. If lower, identify what category of task creates the gap.

**Probe 3 — Response Protocol**
*Hypothesis:* Parties who agree on a response protocol before specifying what they're agreeing to do will have more productive disputes when circumstances change than parties who don't.
*Action:* Write a one-page response protocol template. Use it in the next Futures Edge contributor agreement. Compare conflict resolution quality against previous agreements without one.
*Measurement:* When an unanticipated situation arises, how long does resolution take? Is the resolution accepted by both parties? Does the relationship survive?
*Pass/fail:* At least one situation arises that the agreement didn't anticipate. Resolution is reached without the relationship ending.

**Probe 4 — Portable Credential**
*Hypothesis:* For a software development task, the evidence required for a stranger to trust a contributor is smaller than assumed, and mostly behavioural rather than credential-based.
*Action:* Take one experienced contributor with a track record in the system. Ask a stranger who has never worked with them: "What would you need to see to trust this person with a \$500 task?" Map the answer against what the evidence trail actually contains.
*Measurement:* Does the existing evidence trail satisfy the stranger's requirements? What's missing?
*Pass/fail:* Existing evidence trail satisfies 70%+ of the stranger's stated requirements without any additional verification step.

### Draft Abstract

> Smart contracts were theorised as mechanisms for reducing transaction costs through automated, trustless enforcement. This paper argues that this framing rests on a category error: contracts — whether traditional or smart — are risk management instruments, not coordination mechanisms. They transform payoffs to deter defection; they do not build the trust that coordination requires. Drawing on incomplete contract theory (Hart, Moore, Williamson), game theory (Axelrod's shadow of the future, the Folk Theorem), and the relational contracting literature (Macaulay, Macneil), we demonstrate that smart contracts inherit three irreducible challenges: the translation problem (intent cannot be fully captured in formal specification), the futures problem (the unknowable future cannot be fully anticipated), and the trust prerequisite problem (contracts require the trust they were designed to replace). We then derive, through systematic analysis of coordination mechanisms across multiple domains, four primitives constituting an *agreement architecture* that addresses these challenges at their roots: the Graduated Stake, the Evidence Trail, the Response Protocol, and the Portable Credential. We argue that smart contracts become trustworthy only when deployed as an enforcement layer *on top of* this architecture — not as a substitute for it. This reframing has significant implications for DAO design, institutional cryptoeconomics, and the broader project of building coordination infrastructure for inclusive, post-scarcity economic participation.

**Section 1 — Introduction (800 words)**

Open with the broken promise: smart contracts were supposed to make trust unnecessary. The DAO hack as the first major evidence that something fundamental was wrong — not technically, but architecturally. *"A bug-free smart contract will always execute as coded — although, perhaps, not necessarily as intended."*  That sentence contains the whole problem.[^19_2]

State the paper's contribution: a category error has been embedded in the institutional cryptoeconomics literature. Correcting it changes how we design coordination mechanisms, not just how we audit code.

***

**Section 2 — Literature Review: Three Unsolved Problems (2,000 words)**

*2.1 — The Incomplete Contract Problem*
Hart and Moore (1986, 1988), Williamson (1985). Why complete contracts are impossible: bounded rationality, description costs, verifiability limits. The Nobel Prize framing. Key insight: incompleteness is a property of any agreement made across time, not a drafting failure.[^19_3]

*2.2 — The Coordination-Enforcement Conflation*
The relational contracting literature (Macaulay 1963, Macneil 1978) showed decades ago that contract terms matter less than the relationship. Most business disputes are resolved without invoking contract terms. The institutional cryptoeconomics literature (Berg, Davidson, Potts 2019) extended TCE to blockchain but inherited the same conflation: enforcement and coordination treated as the same problem.

*2.3 — The Shadow of the Future*
Axelrod (1984), Folk Theorem. Cooperation emerges in repeated games without contracts. The shadow of the future is the mechanism. One-shot games require payoff transformation (what contracts do) precisely because the repeated-game mechanism is absent. This frames the design challenge: contracts are a workaround for the absence of repeated interaction, not a solution to the coordination problem itself.

*2.4 — The Trust Prerequisite*
Mayer, Davis, Schoorman (1995). Trust requires demonstrated ability, benevolence, and integrity — accumulated through repeated interaction. Contracts require good-faith interpretation, honest representation, and willingness to renegotiate — all of which depend on trust. The circular dependency is structural, not incidental. Ostrom (1990): communities govern commons successfully without contracts through visibility, community norms, and graduated sanctions.[^19_4]

***

**Section 3 — The Category Error (1,000 words)**

This is the paper's core theoretical intervention. Stated directly:

> **Contracts are risk management instruments. They transform the payoff matrix of potential defectors. They do not build trust, resolve incompleteness, or handle the unknowable future. They have been systematically misidentified as coordination mechanisms — and this misidentification explains why smart contracts, despite technical sophistication, reproduce the same failures as the agreements they were meant to replace.**

Support through three cases:

- The DAPO experiment: *"purely technical mechanisms cannot by themselves resolve what counts as fairness"* — social and trust factors became more important, not less, when structure was automated[^19_5]
- The DAO hack: correct code, violated intent — the translation problem in executable form[^19_2]
- Vatiero (2022): smart contracts may incur *higher* transaction costs than traditional contracts precisely because they eliminate the adaptive mechanisms that make traditional contracts workable

The implication: the design challenge is not *how to write better contracts* but *how to build the substrate that makes any enforcement mechanism trustworthy*.

***

**Section 4 — The Agreement Architecture (2,500 words)**

*Methodology note (300 words)*
Brief account of the derivation method: systematic cross-domain analysis of coordination mechanisms (legal, biological, musical, game-theoretic, financial, social), identifying mechanisms that appear across multiple domains as primitive candidates. The Essential vs. Accidental lens applied to distinguish what the problem requires from what historical design choices introduced.

*4.1 — Primitive 1: The Graduated Stake*
Commitment scales to available information and demonstrated trust. Evidence from: clinical trial phase-gating, venture capital staging, employment probation, options pricing. The shared logic: never require full commitment to an unknowable future. Addresses the Futures problem directly.

*4.2 — Primitive 2: The Evidence Trail*
Observable, verifiable records generated as a natural by-product of doing the work — not as a separate proof step. Evidence from: version control, chain of custody, double-entry bookkeeping, flight data recorders, open source contribution history. The shared logic: behaviour generates its own evidence when the environment is designed to capture it. Addresses the Verification Problem at its root.

*4.3 — Primitive 3: The Response Protocol*
A pre-agreed process for handling what the agreement didn't anticipate — specifying *how* novel situations get resolved, not *what* the resolution will be. Evidence from: constitutional amendment processes, common law precedent-building, the Toyota andon cord, jazz improvisation within shared grammar. The shared logic: incompleteness is irreducible, but the response to it is designable. Addresses the Futures problem at the meta-level.

*4.4 — Primitive 4: The Portable Credential*
Evidence of past trustworthy behaviour carried into new relationships as verifiable proof — extending the shadow of the future to strangers without simulating trust that doesn't exist. Evidence from: professional certification, open source contribution history, performance bonds, reputation systems. Critical design constraint: credentials must be *non-transferable*, earned only through genuine contribution — preventing Goodhart's Law degradation (the Nosedive failure mode). This is the Sybil resistance insight: by making credentials inseparable from genuine value contribution, the mechanism makes gaming more expensive than honest participation.

*4.5 — The Cyclic Architecture*
The four primitives are not independent. They form a learning cycle:

```
Evidence Trail → Portable Credential → Graduated Stake → Response Protocol
      ↑                                                          |
      └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each execution feeds the trail. Trust accumulates over time. Commitments deepen as evidence warrants. Unanticipated situations are handled and recorded. The system learns rather than predicts.

***

**Section 5 — Smart Contracts Repositioned (800 words)**

The paper's practical punchline. Smart contracts are not eliminated by this architecture — they are correctly positioned within it.

Current framing: smart contracts *are* the coordination mechanism. They replace trust with code.

Corrected framing: smart contracts are the *enforcement layer* that executes automatically *once* the agreement architecture has done its work. When the Evidence Trail is verified, the Portable Credential is genuine, the Graduated Stake is appropriately sized, and the Response Protocol is in place — a smart contract can execute with genuine confidence. Its execution is trustworthy because the substrate beneath it is trustworthy.

The design implication: building smart contracts without the architecture underneath is building the roof before the foundation. The technical mechanism works. The institutional mechanism doesn't.[^19_6]

***

**Section 6 — Implications (1,000 words)**

*6.1 — For Institutional Cryptoeconomics*
The Berg-Davidson-Potts framework needs a trust substrate layer beneath the blockchain institutional layer. The four primitives constitute that layer. DAOs that implement only the blockchain layer — smart contracts and token governance — will exhibit the governance failures Davidson (2025) identifies: agency costs reasserting themselves, collective action problems, whale dominance.  The agreement architecture is what makes the blockchain layer viable.[^19_2]

*6.2 — For DAO Design*
Governance threshold mechanisms (participation locked behind earned reputation, not purchased tokens) are a concrete implementation of the Portable Credential primitive with Sybil resistance built in. This is not just a security feature — it is a coordination design choice that makes the governance mechanism honest. The contribution-gated governance model is theoretically derivable from first principles, not just pragmatically motivated.

*6.3 — For Post-Scarcity Economics*
The deepest implication: economic exclusion in low-trust environments is not primarily a poverty problem or a legal infrastructure problem. It is a *coordination cost* problem. When the only available mechanism for coordinating between strangers requires legal expertise, technical sophistication, or capital — those without access are excluded. The agreement architecture provides a pathway to coordination that is accessible before those prerequisites are met. This is the accelerant toward post-scarcity participation: not better contracts, but cheaper trust infrastructure.[^19_7]

***

**Section 7 — Conclusion (400 words)**

The category error correction restated cleanly. The architecture summarised. The research agenda opened: empirical validation of the primitives, mechanism design for specific coordination contexts, measurement of trust infrastructure costs across institutional environments — which is the PhD proposal.

***

## The Novel Contributions, Clearly Stated

For the cover letter and response to reviewers:

1. **Category error correction** — the first paper to clearly separate coordination (trust-building) from enforcement (payoff transformation) in the institutional cryptoeconomics literature, explaining why smart contracts reproduce traditional contract failures
2. **Agreement architecture** — four primitives derived from cross-domain analysis, presented as *required by the problem* rather than chosen design options, with an explicit cyclic relationship not found in prior literature
3. **Smart contracts repositioned** — enforcement layer on top of trust substrate rather than substitute for it; changes the entire design logic for DAO governance
4. **Non-transferable credential as Sybil resistance** — articulated as a coordination design principle rather than a security countermeasure; connects Goodhart's Law, Axelrod's shadow of the future, and Nosedive-style failure modes in a single design insight

# I just saw a note I took during our session that I'd like you to explain more: "Most contracts ignore (or at least don't involve) non-party stakeholders." I also see this as a failing of contracts.

This is genuinely important and it's a failing distinct from the three we identified. It deserves a proper name.

***

## The Privity Problem

What you're observing is formally called the **doctrine of privity of contract** — one of the most foundational and contested principles in contract law. The doctrine states that only parties to a contract can enforce or be bound by it. A third party has no rights or obligations under the contract, even if it directly affects them, even if it was made partly for their benefit.[^20_1]

This isn't an oversight in contract drafting. It's a structural design choice embedded in contract law since Roman times, preserved in Australian law, and only partially eroded by statutory exceptions in jurisdictions like England (Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999)  and the US third-party beneficiary doctrine.[^20_2][^20_3][^20_4][^20_1]

The implication is stark: **two parties can enter a contract whose effects fall largely on people who had no seat at the table, no voice in the terms, and no legal remedy when things go wrong.** A supply chain contract that implicitly accepts labour exploitation. A development contract that displaces a community. A DeFi protocol governance vote that affects users who don't hold governance tokens.

***

## The Coase Theorem and Why It Doesn't Save Us

Ronald Coase's famous theorem says private bargaining between parties can solve the externality problem — the costs imposed on non-parties — without government intervention, as long as two conditions hold: property rights are clearly defined, and transaction costs are zero (or low).[^20_5][^20_6]

The theorem is elegant. The conditions never fully hold. The third condition Coase quietly added is the most important: *there must be few affected parties, otherwise the transaction costs of organising them become too great.*[^20_7]

This is the mechanism of exclusion. When non-party stakeholders are numerous, diffuse, or disorganised — communities, future generations, the environment — the cost of including them in the negotiation is prohibitive. So they're excluded. Not out of malice. Out of transaction cost logic.

Bilateral contracting systematically excludes those whose transaction costs of participation are highest. That is disproportionately: people with less power, less resource, less legal access, and less proximity to the contracting parties. The doctrine doesn't just exclude stakeholders — it excludes specific stakeholders in a predictable pattern.

***

## Ostrom's Answer

This is precisely what Ostrom's polycentric governance framework was designed to address. Her third design principle for sustainable commons governance states:[^20_8]

> *"Most individuals affected by a resource regime are authorised to participate in making and modifying its rules."*

Not *parties to the agreement*. **Individuals affected.** That's a completely different unit of inclusion. And her empirical finding was that communities applying this principle — not contracts, not state regulation — sustained commons governance successfully across centuries and cultures.[^20_9]

The architecture is polycentric: multiple overlapping governance centres, multi-level, adaptive, with information flowing between all affected parties.  It's the opposite of bilateral contracting — and it works for exactly the problems bilateral contracting fails at.[^20_10]

***

## Smart Contracts Amplify the Problem

Traditional contracts at least occur within legal systems that have developed some mechanisms — however imperfect — for third-party claims, public policy exceptions, and regulatory intervention.[^20_11]

Smart contracts have none of these. They execute between parties automatically, immutably, and without any formal channel for non-party stakeholders to observe, object, or seek remedy. The immutability that makes them "trustworthy" to parties makes them more dangerous to non-parties — there's no court to invoke, no adjudicator to appeal to, no amendment process accessible to those outside the contract.

DAOs inherit this problem directly. Davidson's paper notes that token holder voting theoretically suppresses agency costs — but token holders are a subset of affected stakeholders.  Users who depend on a DeFi protocol but don't hold governance tokens have no voice in decisions that directly affect them. This is the whale dominance problem from a different angle: not just unequal power among parties, but entire classes of affected people with no formal standing at all.[^20_12]

***

## This Is A Fourth Problem

Looking back at the three problems we identified in Phase 2:

1. **Translation** — intent can't be fully captured in specification
2. **Futures** — the world changes in ways that weren't anticipated
3. **Trust Prerequisite** — contracts require the trust they were designed to replace

What you've named is structurally different from all three. It's not about how parties relate to each other — it's about who counts as a party at all. Call it:

**Problem 4 — The Stakeholder Exclusion Problem**

> Contracts are bilateral instruments that legally and structurally exclude affected third parties. The privity doctrine means non-parties have no rights under a contract that affects them. Combined with Coasean transaction cost logic, this systematically externalises costs onto those with the least power to resist — communities, future generations, non-participants in the contracting economy.

This is not a minor failing. It may be the most consequential failing. Problems 1–3 explain why contracts fail the parties involved. Problem 4 explains why contracts, even when they succeed for the parties, can fail everyone else.

***

## Does This Generate a Fifth Primitive?

Almost certainly yes. If the four primitives respond to Problems 1–3, a fifth primitive would address Problem 4:

**Possible Primitive 5 — The Affected Party Register**

*What it would be:* A mechanism for identifying and including non-party stakeholders in a coordination agreement — not as full contracting parties, but as participants with defined rights to observe, object, and trigger the Response Protocol when the agreement's execution affects them.

*Where it already exists:*

- Ostrom's design principle \#3 (affected parties participate in rule-making)[^20_8]
- Environmental impact assessments (mandatory third-party notification before execution)
- Planning and development approvals (community consultation as a precondition)
- Some DAO governance experiments with "affected party" token classes separate from governance tokens

*What it would connect to in the existing architecture:*

- The Evidence Trail becomes public by default, not party-private — non-party stakeholders can observe
- The Response Protocol gains a new trigger: any affected party can pull the andon cord
- The Portable Credential extends to community reputation — not just bilateral transaction history, but evidence of broader stakeholder impact

*The constraint it must honour:* Including every possible affected party collapses into paralysis — Coase's transaction cost problem re-enters. So the primitive needs a scope condition: *which stakeholders are materially affected, and at what threshold does their stake warrant formal inclusion?*

***

## The Post-Scarcity Connection

This is where your research vision becomes its sharpest. You said earlier that what excites you most is the accelerated pathway to a post-scarcity future. Here's the direct line:

**Post-scarcity is not achievable through mechanisms that systematically externalise costs to the commons.**

Every bilateral contract that succeeds for its parties by shifting costs to non-party stakeholders — environmental, social, economic — is accumulating a debt that the commons eventually pays. The path to post-scarcity isn't just reducing transaction costs between parties. It's reducing the total coordination costs across *all* affected parties — including those who never signed anything.

An agreement architecture that includes Problem 4 as a structural concern isn't just more ethical. It's more efficient at the system level — because it internalises costs that bilateral contracting externalises, preventing the accumulation of commons debt that eventually produces the poverty and inequality your framework is trying to address.[^20_13]

***

## What This Means for the Paper

A fourth problem and a possible fifth primitive is a significant addition. There are two ways to treat it:

**Option A:** Incorporate it into the current paper as a fourth problem that reinforces the central argument — bilateral contracting is the unit of analysis that's limiting us, not just the quality of the contracts within that unit.

**Option B:** Reserve it for a second paper specifically on coordination mechanisms for inclusive post-scarcity economies — where the Stakeholder Exclusion Problem becomes the central argument rather than a supporting one. This might actually be the more significant paper, and it maps directly to the broader impacts section of your research proposal.[^20_13]

The instinct that led you to write that note during the session was right. This isn't a footnote to the design session. It's the reason the design session matters.
