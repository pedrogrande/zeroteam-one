## The Core Insight

**Relational learning** — understanding accelerates when new knowledge is *connected* to existing knowledge and future goals, not delivered in isolation. This is well-established in learning theory (constructivism, schema theory, Vygotsky's ZPD). But you're saying something more specific: **an agent can do this at scale, continuously, and personalized** — which is what makes it a design challenge, not just a pedagogical observation.

## What I See Connecting

This insight is the **same pattern you've already identified in three other places**, just applied to a different domain:

1. **The Accretive Workspace** — "build up from fragments the learner already has" is the accretive model. You don't start from a blank page. You start from what's already there and connect to it.

2. **The User Context Framework** — your 9 categories of what a design agent needs about a user? That IS a learner profile. "Existing knowledge" = categories 1 and 8 (Identity & Knowledge). "Future goals" = category 9 (Goals & Success Criteria). The framework already has the structure for this.

3. **PRISM's Intentions dimension** — goals, constraints, mandates, guardrails. A learning agent needs to know where the learner is *trying to go*, not just where they are. The PRISM architecture already captures this.

## Three Implications You Might Not Have Drawn Yet

**1. The agent doesn't explain — it relates.**

Current AI tutors try to *generate understanding* (explain, rephrase, give examples). Your insight suggests the agent's highest-value role is *connecting* — "This new concept relates to what you already know about X, and it's relevant to your goal of Y." The learner does the meaning-making. The agent does the *relating*.

This is **the same pattern** as "the agent prepares judgment; the human makes judgment" — just applied to learning instead of decision-making. **The agent prepares understanding; the learner makes meaning.**

**2. Type collision applies to learning too.**

Traditional learning content is an undifferentiated wall of text. But different knowledge *types* need different *connection strategies*:
- A **fact** connects to existing knowledge via *association* ("you know X — this is like that")
- A **procedure** connects via *analogy* ("you already do X — this is similar but with Y step added")
- A **concept** connects via *contrast* ("you thought X — actually it's more like Z because...")
- A **principle** connects via *application* ("remember when you faced X? This principle explains why that happened")

If the agent can't *type* the knowledge it's connecting, it can't choose the right connection strategy. Same type collision problem, different domain.

**3. There's a dependency trap that mirrors CAWDP's scaffold dependency.**

If the agent is *too* good at relating new knowledge to existing knowledge, the learner might stop building their own connections. This is the **Educational Tutor dependency insight** you already identified: worst failure looks like success (learner getting correct answers via dependency). Progressive autonomy applies to learning too — the agent should gradually reveal less and require the learner to make more connections independently.

## A Question This Raises

If relational learning is the future, then **what does the agent need to know about the learner?**

You already have a partial answer in the User Context Framework. But for learning specifically, I'd add:

- **Misconceptions** — existing knowledge that's *wrong* (the agent needs to know what to *un-relate* from)
- **Learning trajectory** — not just current knowledge, but the direction of travel (are they accelerating? plateauing? stuck?)
- **Emotional state around learning** — the 14 annoyances apply here too (identity threat, verification anxiety, feeling stupid)

This feels like it connects to Future's Edge in a direct way — the curriculum *is* the relational learning design, and the agent *is* the relational learning engine.

What angle do you want to explore? The pedagogical principle itself, the agent design for learning, the curriculum implications for Future's Edge, or something else?

### Response

The User Context Framework has two versions — an earlier 8-category version and a refined 9-category version. The 9-category one is the most complete:

## The 9-Category User Context Framework

**What a design agent needs to know about a user when designing agents/workflows.**

### 1. Identity & Authority
- Role, decision authority, domain expertise, technical fluency, cognitive style, what they've tried
- **Why it matters:** Determines how much design engagement they can handle, what language to use, and what past experience shapes their expectations

### 2. Current Workflows
- Task map, time distribution, bottlenecks, variation patterns, volume/frequency, seasonal peaks
- **Why it matters:** The actual work patterns, not idealised versions. Shows where time really goes

### 3. Environment & Tools
- Tool stack, data sources/sinks, integration points, data formats, network/access constraints
- **Why it matters:** What the agent must work within and connect to

### 4. Constraints & Boundaries
- Compliance, security, privacy, budget, time, contractual limits
- **Why it matters:** Hard limits the design cannot violate

### 5. Failure & Recovery
- Current failure modes, error tolerance, reversibility, current error-handling, escalation paths, cost of failure
- **Why it matters:** What goes wrong now and what happens when it does

### 6. Attitudes & Preferences
- Automation tolerance, risk appetite, control preferences, trust calibration, change tolerance, communication preferences, past AI experiences
- **Why it matters:** How the user relates to automation and AI

### 7. Stakeholders
- Other people in workflow, approval chain, client expectations, team capabilities, upstream/downstream dependencies
- **Why it matters:** Who else is affected and who else has authority

### 8. Knowledge & Expertise
- Tacit knowledge, decision criteria, edge case awareness, trusted knowledge sources, known knowledge gaps
- **Why it matters:** What the user knows that isn't written down

### 9. Goals & Success Criteria
- Primary goal, success metrics, non-negotiables, desired future state, willingness to invest, exit criteria
- **Why it matters:** What "done" looks like and what "good enough" means

---

## A Key Structural Insight

**Most of this information is about BOUNDARIES, not capabilities.** The design agent needs to know where the lines are far more than it needs a list of features. This aligns with the entire CAWDP philosophy: boundaries before capabilities.

## Priority Tiers

| Tier | Categories | Role |
|------|-----------|------|
| **Must-have** (gates design) | Identity & Authority, Constraints & Boundaries, Failure & Recovery, Goals & Success Criteria | Without these, you can't design safely |
| **Should-have** (shapes quality) | Current Workflows, Environment & Tools, Stakeholders, Attitudes & Preferences | Without these, design works but misses important context |
| **Nice-to-have** (refines over time) | Knowledge & Expertise depth, variation patterns, seasonal peaks | Adds refinement, can be learned during use |

## The Progressive Capture Principle

Three rules:
1. **Don't ask everything up front** — capture what's needed when it becomes relevant. Intake asks #1 + #9. Boundaries asks #4 + #5. Architecture asks #2 + #3.
2. **Derive what you can** — industry → regulations, role → authority level, team size → governance complexity
3. **Observe what you can** — language, pace, what they emphasise, what they skip. Behaviour reveals more than stated preferences.

---

And as I mentioned before — if you're applying this to **learning** specifically, you'd want to add: misconceptions (what to *un-relate* from), learning trajectory (direction of travel), and emotional state around learning (identity threat, verification anxiety).
