# Competitive analysis \- Five Forces model

## Porter's Five Forces Analysis Worksheet (Likert Scale Format)

For each statement below, please indicate your level of agreement on a scale of 1 to 5\. A higher rating (towards 5\) generally means that the factor contributes more strongly to the intensity of the specific competitive force being analyzed. After rating the individual factors, provide an overall assessment for each of Porter's Five Forces.

**Rating Scale for Statements:**

* **1: Strongly Disagree**  
* **2: Disagree**  
* **3: Neutral**  
* **4: Agree**  
* **5: Strongly Agree**

| Factor Statement (Rate how true this is, contributing to *high* supplier power) | Your Rating (1-5) |
| :---- | :---- |
| Suppliers are highly concentrated (few suppliers dominate the supply of key inputs). |  |
| The product/service provided by suppliers is unique, highly differentiated, or critical to buyers. |  |
| There are *few or no readily available substitutes* for the inputs suppliers provide. |  |
| Switching costs for your business (and others in the industry) to change suppliers are *high*. |  |
| Suppliers can realistically threaten to integrate forward (e.g., enter your industry and become a competitor). |  |
| Your industry is *not a crucial customer* for the suppliers (they have diverse customer bases). |  |
| The cost of supplier inputs represents a *significant portion* of your industry's total product cost. |  |
| **Overall Assessment: The Bargaining Power of Suppliers is:** (1=Very Low, 2=Low, 3=Medium, 4=High, 5=Very High) |  |

## Original questions

Porter's Five Forces is a framework for analyzing the competitive forces within an industry to understand its attractiveness and potential profitability. To help a new entrepreneur produce a Five Forces chart, prompt them with questions related to each of the five areas:

## **Threat of New Entrants**

This force examines how easy or difficult it is for new competitors to enter the market. High barriers to entry make an industry less vulnerable to new players.

Key questions to consider:

* What are the initial capital requirements to start a business in this industry? Are they high or low?

* Are there significant economies of scale that existing players benefit from?

* How strong is brand loyalty among existing customers? Is it difficult for new entrants to overcome this?

* Are there specific distribution channels that are hard for new companies to access?

* What government policies or regulations (e.g., licensing, legal requirements) could prevent or hinder new entries?

* Do existing companies possess proprietary technology, patents, or specialized knowledge that is difficult for new entrants to acquire?

* How high are the switching costs for customers to move from existing providers to a new entrant?

* What is the likelihood and potential impact of retaliation from established competitors if a new company enters the market?

* Is access to skilled labor or essential raw materials restricted or difficult for newcomers?

## **Bargaining Power of Buyers (Customers)**

This force assesses the power that customers have to drive down prices or demand higher quality or more services.

Key questions to consider:

* How many significant buyers are there in the market? Are they concentrated or fragmented?

* What is the size of an average buyer's order? Do large-volume buyers have more leverage?

* How significant are switching costs for buyers to change suppliers?

* How differentiated are the products or services in the industry? If products are standardized, buyers have more power.

* Can buyers realistically threaten to integrate backward (i.e., produce the product or service themselves)?

* How price-sensitive are the buyers?

* How critical is the quality of your product/service to the buyer's own product/service?

* How much information do buyers have about your costs and alternative suppliers?

## **Bargaining Power of Suppliers**

This force evaluates the power that suppliers have to raise prices or reduce the quality of goods and services they provide.

Key questions to consider:

* How many major suppliers are there for key inputs? Are they concentrated or fragmented?

* How unique or differentiated is the product or service provided by suppliers?

* Are there readily available substitute inputs for what suppliers provide?

* How significant are switching costs for your business to change suppliers?

* Can suppliers realistically threaten to integrate forward (i.e., become your competitor by selling directly to your customers)?

* How important is your industry or your business to the suppliers? Do they rely heavily on your purchases?

* What is the cost of the inputs relative to your total product cost?

## **Threat of Substitute Products or Services**

This force looks at the likelihood of customers finding a different way of doing what your product or service does.

Key questions to consider:

* Are there alternative products or services from other industries that can satisfy the same customer need?

* What is the price and performance of these substitutes compared to your industry's offerings?

* How high are the switching costs for customers to move to a substitute product or service?

* Are customers willing to switch to substitutes? What factors influence this willingness?

* Are there technological advancements that could lead to new substitutes emerging quickly?

* How do the perceived benefits of substitutes compare to your product/service?

## **Intensity of Rivalry Among Existing Competitors**

This force examines the level of competition among existing firms in the industry. High rivalry often leads to lower profitability.

Key questions to consider:

* How many competitors are there in the industry? Are they roughly equal in size and capability?

* What is the current industry growth rate? (Slow growth often intensifies competition for market share).

* Are products and services highly differentiated, or are they commodities?

* What are the fixed costs in the industry? (High fixed costs can pressure firms to cut prices when demand is low).

* How high are exit barriers? (If it's difficult or costly for firms to leave the industry, rivalry can be more intense).

* How diverse are the competitors in terms of their strategies, origins, and personalities?

* Is there significant overcapacity in the industry?

* How do competitors primarily compete (e.g., price, innovation, marketing, service)?

* What is the history of competitive behavior in this industry (e.g., price wars, aggressive advertising)?
