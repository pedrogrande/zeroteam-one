**1\. What 'implemented well' means**

Before painting the 2030 vision, it is worth being precise about what 'AI implemented well' in education actually means. It is not a technology question. It is an educational purpose question.

| What it is NOT Every school has deployed AI marking tools Students submit work faster with AI assistance AI has reduced the number of teachers needed Assessment has been fully automated Institutions have achieved ASQA/TEQSA compliance on AI policy Staff have attended an AI awareness workshop The credential process is more efficient | What it IS Graduates are genuinely prepared for AI-augmented employment The capability gap between what education develops and what work requires is closed Teachers have found a more valued, more fulfilling professional role The equity gap in AI literacy has narrowed, not widened Institutions are demonstrably fulfilling their missions — formation, mobility, professional competence — in a world where AI has changed what those missions require The community has a generation of AI-literate, ethically grounded citizens |
| :---- | :---- |

| 'Implemented well' is not about institutions adopting AI. It is about institutions using AI to better fulfil the purpose they already have. The measure of success is not what happens inside the institution. It is what happens to students after they leave it. |

**2\. Scenes from 2030 — the perfect world, made concrete**

Abstract visions are easy to agree with and easy to ignore. These vignettes make the 2030 vision specific and human — grounding it in the daily experiences of real people in concrete situations. They are the 'north star' images that should shape every design decision Zero Team makes.

| MONDAY MORNING, 2030 — CEM SECONDARY SCHOOL, MELBOURNE |
| :---- |
| **A teacher who has found her professional identity again** *Priya has been teaching English for eleven years. She used to spend her Sunday nights marking thirty drafts of the same essay, leaving comments that students skimmed before filing the assignment away. She doesn't do that anymore. Her students now write with AI as a thinking partner — and the essays she reads on Monday are more ambitious, more argued, more genuinely theirs than anything she saw five years ago, because she redesigned the task. She reviews AI-generated feedback alongside each piece, adds the observations that only she can see — the student who is writing about grief because her grandmother died, the one whose confidence has quietly shifted — and spends the time she used to spend marking in one-to-one conversations instead. The Catholic Education Melbourne AI framework gave her the governance and the permission she needed. The Zero Team programme gave her the pedagogy. She feels more like a teacher now than she did when she was marking essays until midnight.* |

| JOB INTERVIEW, 2030 — COMMUNITY SERVICES ORGANISATION, REGIONAL VICTORIA |
| :---- |
| **A graduate whose credential opens the door it promised to open** *Marcus completed his community services diploma through Open Colleges in 2029\. His interviewer asks him to walk through how he would use the case management platform's AI triage tool — and when he would override it. He gives a considered answer: the tool is useful for flagging resource options and generating draft case notes, but he describes two specific scenarios where he would not follow its recommendation, and why the human judgment matters more in those situations. The interviewer makes a note. She has interviewed twelve applicants today. Four of them knew the tool existed. Two of them, including Marcus, understood when not to trust it. He starts in two weeks. The credential did what it promised.* |

| CLIENT MEETING, 2030 — FINANCIAL PLANNING PRACTICE, SYDNEY |
| :---- |
| **A Kaplan graduate whose AI competence protects the client** *Anika is three years into her financial planning career. She has just run a client review using her firm's AI-assisted portfolio analysis tool. The tool has flagged a rebalancing recommendation. Anika recognises a pattern she was trained to watch for: the model is optimising on recent performance data in a way that doesn't account for this client's specific drawdown risk tolerance, which she knows from their conversations — not from the model. She documents her reasoning, adjusts the recommendation, and presents it to the client with an explanation. Later, reviewing the case, her principal notes the override in her file and marks it as good practice. The client's retirement savings are better protected because Anika learned — in her Kaplan training — that professional accountability does not transfer to the model.* |

| PARENT INFORMATION EVENING, 2030 — WESLEY COLLEGE MELBOURNE |

She doesn't spend much time on what AI students are allowed to use. She answers with employment and further education outcome data from the last two graduating cohorts, and with specific assessment redesign examples. Another parent — a GP — says quietly that she wishes someone had taught her to think this way about AI before she started using it in her practice. The room is engaged in a way that parent evenings rarely are.* |

**3\. The 2030 destination — stakeholder by stakeholder**

The vignettes give texture. This section gives structure — a systematic account of what the 2030 perfect world looks like for each stakeholder group, and what has changed to make it possible.

| INSTITUTIONS |
| :---- |

| Formation institutions (CEM · Wesley) A coherent, values-grounded AI framework that all schools operate within — not a blanket policy, but a living framework that guides real decisions in real classrooms. Curriculum explicitly develops the capabilities that matter in an AI era: judgment, ethical reasoning, relational intelligence, creative synthesis. Assessment is designed to measure these — not to measure pre-AI capabilities that AI can now perform. The formation mission is being achieved more fully than before AI — because the redesign forced institutions to articulate what they are actually trying to develop in students, and to build learning experiences that develop it deliberately. CEM is recognised as a national leader in responsible AI education. Wesley's approach is studied and replicated by other independent schools. | Access and professional competence institutions (Open Colleges · Acknowledge · Kaplan) Credentials demonstrably translate to employment at the level of capability employers need — in AI-transformed fields, at AI-era standards. Curriculum is kept current through systematic, AI-assisted monitoring of industry practice and employer expectations — not through five-year review cycles. Kaplan's professional education programmes are recognised by professional bodies and employers as the gold standard for AI-era professional competence — not because regulators mandated it, but because Kaplan moved first. Enrolments are stronger than before — because graduate employment outcomes are visibly better than competitors who did not make the transition. |
| :---- | :---- |

| EDUCATORS — TEACHERS AND TRAINERS |
| :---- |

The 2030 story for educators is the one that is hardest to believe from 2026 but matters most to get right. Teachers who feel threatened by AI — whose professional identity is tied to knowledge-holding — are the source of the most significant resistance to the transition. The 2030 vision requires that resistance to have been resolved, not suppressed.

| Professional identity — renewed Teachers have found and inhabited a professional identity that is more valued in an AI world, not less. They are not knowledge-holders competing with AI. They are developers of human capability — mentors, designers of learning experiences, ethical guides, and the adults in students' lives who help them develop a mature relationship with powerful technology. This identity is explicitly more valuable in an AI world than before it. This transition did not happen spontaneously. It happened because institutions invested in sustained professional learning that helped teachers work through the identity question — not just gave them tool training. | Daily experience — transformed Administrative burden is dramatically reduced. Routine marking, documentation, progress reporting and parent communication — AI handles the first pass, with teachers reviewing and adding the judgment and relationship that only they can provide. Teachers have more time for the work only they can do. The teaching workforce is more stable. Early career teachers find the profession more attractive because AI has reduced the administrative overwhelm that drives early attrition. The professionals who left between 2023 and 2026, burnt out and uncertain, were the loss. Those who remained and navigated the transition are now more professionally fulfilled than at any point in their careers. |

| STUDENTS AND ALUMNI |
| :---- |

| Current students in 2030 Have developed a mature, discerning relationship with AI — they know how to use it effectively, when to trust it, when to push back, and how to maintain their own intellectual agency when it would be easier to defer. Are assessed on capabilities that matter — judgment, ethical reasoning, creative synthesis — not on the ability to produce outputs that AI can generate without them. The equity gap has narrowed. AI literacy is explicitly and equitably developed in schools and institutions — it is not left to family networks, expensive tools, or the luck of having a technology-engaged teacher. Students from disadvantaged backgrounds have the same access to quality AI education as students from advantaged backgrounds. For many, the institutional setting is the only place they would have received it. | Alumni entering the workforce Are demonstrably more employable than peers who graduated from institutions that did not make this transition — the difference is visible in interviews, in early performance, and in employer preference over time. Professional alumni — Kaplan graduates — practice in ways that are measurably safer and more client-centred because they were trained to use AI responsibly, not just efficiently. Formation alumni — CEM and Wesley graduates — are active, thoughtful participants in civic conversations about AI. They vote, advocate, and contribute from a position of genuine understanding, not passive consumption of AI tools. The investment in the credential delivered the return it promised. The mobility mission worked. |

| EMPLOYERS |
| :---- |

The partnership is self-reinforcing: institutions with employer partnerships develop better curricula; employers with institutional partnerships get better graduates. |

**4\. Backcasting — the path from here to there**

Backcasting works backwards from a defined future state to identify what needs to be true at each point in time for that future to be reachable. It differs from forecasting — which extrapolates from current trends — by starting with the desired destination and asking what must happen to arrive there, regardless of whether current trends point that way.

The 2030 vision is four years away. That is not a long time in education — curriculum redesign, staff capability development, and assessment reform all take years to implement and show results. The path is tight.

| 2030 The destination | Graduates from AI-engaged institutions demonstrably outperforming peers on employment outcomes in AI-transformed fields — measurably, publicly. Curriculum across all five institution types has been redesigned to develop AI-era capabilities. Assessment measures the capabilities that matter, not pre-AI proxies. The educator workforce is professionally confident and capable with AI. Early career teacher attrition related to AI uncertainty has reversed. The equity gap in AI literacy has narrowed — AI capability is no longer strongly predicted by socioeconomic advantage. Professional licensing frameworks (financial advice, mortgage broking, real estate) include AI competence. Kaplan graduates meet the updated standard. Employer partnerships with AI-engaged institutions are established and self-reinforcing. The market signal is clear. CEM and Wesley are recognised nationally as leaders in responsible AI education. Open Colleges, Acknowledge and Kaplan are the benchmark for AI-ready vocational and professional credentials. |
| :---: | :---- |

| 2028–29 Delivery at scale | Redesigned curriculum is fully implemented and being delivered across all programme cohorts — not just pilots. First full cohorts of students who have completed AI-integrated programmes are graduating and entering the workforce. Their outcomes are being tracked. Staff capability programmes are complete — teachers and trainers across all institutions have developed genuinely new practice, not just awareness. Assessment redesign is implemented. Traditional essay and report formats have been substantially replaced or supplemented by assessment modes that develop and measure AI-era capabilities. Employer recognition is building — hiring managers are beginning to note the difference between graduates of AI-engaged and non-AI-engaged institutions. Professional bodies have either updated competency frameworks to include AI, or are in advanced consultation — with Kaplan providing input to the process. ASQA and TEQSA have developed clearer expectations around AI in curriculum — institutions that have moved are ahead of compliance, not scrambling to meet it. |
| :---: | :---- |

| 2027 Capability building | Staff capability programmes are well underway — cohorts of teachers and trainers are developing genuinely new pedagogical practice through sustained, community-of-practice-based professional learning. Curriculum frameworks are developed and beginning to be implemented — pilot programmes are running, evidence is being gathered, refinements are being made. Assessment redesign is in progress — new assessment models are being trialled, evaluated, and refined in collaboration with staff and employer partners. Institutional governance frameworks are in place — policies that govern AND develop, supported by implementation infrastructure. First employer partnerships around AI-ready graduates are being established — curriculum advisory relationships, work-integrated learning pilots, hiring pipeline conversations. Early outcome data from first cohorts is being collected and informing programme refinement. CEM's framework is being implemented across its school network. Wesley's approach is attracting sector attention. |
| :---: | :---- |

| 2026 The foundation year | Institutional leadership in each of the five organisations has committed to addressing the capability gap — not just the integrity problem. The mission-level conversation has happened. A trusted partner with deep expertise in both education and AI is engaged. Curriculum review has begun. The staff engagement strategy is in place — not a one-off PD session, but a sustained programme designed to move practice, not just awareness. Pilot programme design is complete. The first cohort of students and teachers is identified. Governance framework design is underway — a framework that governs AND develops, tailored to each institution's specific mission and community. Employer conversations have begun — not to announce a programme, but to understand what capability they need from graduates and in what timeframe. The decision to start has been made. This is the only year in which the 2030 destination is still reachable. |
| :---: | :---- |

| The students who will graduate in 2030 are already enrolled — in primary school, secondary school, RTOs and private higher education institutions — right now. The curriculum changes that will shape their capability need to begin in 2026\. There is no version of the 2030 vision that is reached without foundational decisions being made this year. |

**5\. The critical path — what cannot be skipped**

Not all steps in the backcasting pathway are equally critical. Some can be accelerated, deferred, or done in parallel. Others are load-bearing: if they are skipped or done poorly, subsequent steps cannot succeed. Understanding the critical path helps Zero Team design programmes that sequence correctly and focus on the dependencies that matter most.

**1\. The mission-level commitment must come first**

The most common failure in institutional AI adoption is starting with technology: deploying tools before working out what the tools should be achieving. The critical path begins not with a tool or a policy but with a leadership conversation about mission — what are we here to do, and how has AI changed what that requires of us?

This conversation must produce a genuine institutional commitment — not just to managing AI's disruption, but to closing the capability gap. Without that commitment, every subsequent step is built on sand: staff capability programmes that have no curriculum mandate to change, assessment redesign that has no leadership backing, governance frameworks that have no relationship to what's actually being taught.

| *Zero Team's entry point is this conversation. Not 'let us show you what AI can do.' But: 'Let us help you work out what good education looks like in a world AI has changed — and then build the capability to deliver it.'* |
| :---- |

**2\. Staff capability must be sustained, not single-session**

The research on teacher professional development is unambiguous: single-session awareness training produces awareness, not practice change. The critical path requires sustained, community-of-practice-based professional learning that gives teachers time to try new approaches, reflect on what happens, refine their practice, and support each other through the transition.

This is the step most institutions shortcut — because sustained professional learning is expensive and hard to schedule. It is also the step that determines whether the curriculum redesign actually reaches students or remains a document that nobody implements. A beautifully redesigned curriculum delivered by teachers who haven't changed their practice is the same as the old curriculum.

**3\. Assessment redesign must happen in parallel with curriculum redesign**

Assessment is the most powerful signal in any education system. If curriculum is redesigned to develop AI-era capabilities but assessment continues to measure pre-AI capabilities, students and teachers will rationally optimise for the assessment — and the curriculum redesign will have no effect. Assessment redesign is not a downstream step. It is a concurrent and equally important one.

This is also the hardest step — redesigning assessment at scale requires significant expertise, staff time, and a willingness to accept that some assessment approaches educators have used for decades are no longer fit for purpose. The institutions that succeed in the 2030 vision are the ones whose leadership made this step non-negotiable, not optional.

**4\. Employer partnerships must be built in, not added on**

The 2030 vision depends on employers recognising and preferring AI-literate graduates. That recognition does not emerge spontaneously — it is built through deliberate engagement: curriculum advisory relationships that give employers input into what capabilities are being developed, work-integrated learning that gives graduates and employers early exposure to each other, and explicit conversations about what AI-ready means in each specific field.

Employer partnerships that are built in from the beginning shape better curricula and produce faster recognition. Employer partnerships that are added on after the curriculum is redesigned are too late to shape the programme and too tentative to produce the hiring preference that validates the investment.

**5\. Equity must be designed in, not retrofitted**

The 2030 equity outcome — AI literacy distributed across socioeconomic groups, not concentrated in them — does not emerge from a programme designed for the average student and then adapted for disadvantaged ones. It requires equity to be a design principle from the beginning: What access do students have to AI tools outside school? What family support do they have for developing responsible AI use? What does AI-literate education look like for a student who is the first in their family to study beyond Year 10?

CEM's mission makes this a natural design constraint. Open Colleges' and Acknowledge Education's student populations make it existential. Zero Team's social enterprise values make it non-negotiable.

**6\. The foundation year — why 2026 is decisive**

The backcasting analysis produces an uncomfortable conclusion for institutions that have not yet made the mission-level commitment: 2026 is the last year in which the 2030 vision is still reachable. Not because the window will permanently close after this year — but because the students who will graduate in 2030 are already enrolled, and the curriculum changes that will shape their capability have to start now to have any meaningful effect by then.

| The enrolment reality A student who starts Year 7 at a CEM or Wesley school in 2026 will sit their VCE in 2031\. The curriculum they are taught from this year onward is the curriculum that will form them. Changes made in 2028 will reach them in Year 9 — better than nothing, but not the same as changes made in 2026 that shape their entire secondary experience. A student who enrols in an Open Colleges community services diploma in 2026 will complete it in 2028\. If the curriculum is redesigned in 2027, this student misses it. The next cohort benefits. This student — who made the investment — graduates with the old capability set. A Kaplan student who begins their financial planning certification in 2026 will be in practice by 2027 or 2028\. If the AI competence module is added in 2028, it was not there when they were trained. Their first clients are served by a practitioner who was trained in the gap. |
| :---- |

This is not an argument for panic. It is an argument for starting — specifically, for making the mission-level commitment and engaging the right partner in 2026, so that curriculum review, staff capability programme design, and assessment redesign can begin this year.

| What starting in 2026 makes possible Curriculum redesign completed and implemented by 2028, reaching current students before they graduate Staff capability built sustainably over 2026–2028 — not rushed Assessment redesign trialled in 2027, fully implemented by 2029 First AI-integrated cohorts graduating in 2029–2030 with measurable outcome differences Employer partnerships established in time to shape graduate outcomes, not just respond to them The institution is ahead of regulatory expectations, not scrambling to meet them | What waiting until 2028 means Curriculum redesign cannot be fully implemented before 2030 — the 2030 vision is unreachable for current students Staff capability is rushed — one-off programmes rather than sustained practice change Assessment redesign starts too late to produce reliable data before 2031 First AI-integrated cohorts don't graduate until 2031 — a full year of graduates have entered the workforce with the capability gap intact Employer partnerships form around competitor institutions that moved first The institution is behind regulatory expectations, not ahead of them |

**7\. Zero Team's role in the journey**

Zero Team does not appear in the 2030 destination. It appears in the path — specifically, in the foundation year and the capability building years that determine whether institutions arrive at the destination or not.

That positioning is deliberate and important. Zero Team is not the AI tool that institutions use in 2030\. It is the partner that helps institutions make the transition — the foundation-year conversation, the curriculum review, the sustained professional learning programme, the assessment redesign, the governance framework. Once the capability is built, it belongs to the institution. Zero Team moves on to the next institution that needs to make the transition.

**Where Zero Team operates in the backcasting timeline**

| 2026 — The conversation that makes the journey possible Zero Team opens and facilitates the mission-level leadership conversation that must happen before anything else. This is not a sales conversation. It is a design conversation: 'What are you here to do? How has AI changed what that requires? What does good education look like for your students, in your context, with your mission?' Most institutions have not had this conversation. They have had the integrity conversation, the policy conversation, the tool conversation. Zero Team's distinctive value at this stage is the ability to hold a conversation that starts with educational purpose, not with technology. | 2026–2028 — The capability building that makes the destination real Zero Team designs and delivers the sustained professional learning programme — the teacher and trainer capability building that is the single most important determinant of whether curriculum redesign reaches students or stays in a document. Zero Team supports curriculum and assessment redesign — not by writing the curriculum for the institution, but by providing the educational and AI expertise that the institution's own designers need to do the work well. Zero Team establishes the governance framework — the policy and governance structures that govern AI use AND create the conditions for ongoing capability development as AI continues to evolve. |
| :---- | :---- |

| The Zero Team proposition — restated for the 2030 vision In 2030, the institutions that fulfilled their missions in an AI-transformed world are the ones that asked the right question in 2026 — not 'how do we manage AI?' but 'what do our students need to be capable of, and how do we build the curriculum and the teachers to deliver it?' Zero Team is the partner that helps institutions ask that question well, and build the capability to answer it. The community benefit — AI literacy distributed equitably, professional practice that is safer, graduates who are prepared for the world they enter — flows from institutional decisions made now. Those decisions are where Zero Team operates. The 2030 vision is reachable. The path from here to there is clear. The only question is which institutions will choose to start. |
