**Director of Learning \& Innovation · Mid-to-large Australian education provider**


| Attribute | Detail |

| **Role** | Senior leader with cross-institutional authority — not purely academic, not purely operational |

***

## 🧠 Empathy Map

### What Alex **Thinks \& Feels**

- *"AI has arrived and I'm already behind — but I can't just react, I need a position I can actually defend."* [^1]
- Genuinely uncertain whether moving fast or moving carefully is the right call — both feel risky [^1]
- Carries a deep sense of professional responsibility: students, staff, families, and regulators are all watching [^1]
- Quietly worried that a misstep — a data breach, an integrity scandal, a staff revolt — will define their leadership tenure [^1]
- Values-driven: wants AI adoption to reflect what the institution *stands for*, not just what's convenient [^1]


### What Alex **Hears**

- Board and senior leadership saying *"we need an AI strategy"* without specifying what that means [^1]
- Teachers and trainers complaining that students are using AI in ways nobody has a clear policy on [^1]
- Vendors pitching technology-first solutions that don't understand education at all [^1]
- Parents and community members asking questions the institution can't yet answer coherently [^1]
- Peers at other institutions appearing confident publicly while admitting privately they're figuring it out too [^1]


### What Alex **Says \& Does**

- Has convened internal working groups on AI policy — but they stall because nobody has clear authority [^1]
- Makes ad hoc decisions at the margin while waiting for a proper framework [^1]
- Attends conferences and reads sector media looking for models to adapt [^1]
- Is cautious with external vendors — has been burned by edtech promises before [^1]
- Talks publicly about "responsible AI" without having a definition they're fully confident in [^1]


### What Alex **Sees**

- 300 schools / multiple campuses making inconsistent AI decisions in the absence of system guidance [^1]
- Students openly using AI tools that staff don't understand [^1]
- A compliance landscape (Privacy Act, TEQSA, ASQA, child safety) that is evolving faster than internal policy [^1]
- Other institutions getting media attention — both positive (sector leaders) and negative (integrity scandals) [^1]

***

## 😣 Pains (Prioritised)

1. **The governance vacuum** — AI is in classrooms and training environments right now, but policy is weeks or months behind; every day without a framework is a day of unmanaged risk [^1]
2. **Reputation exposure** — one high-profile failure (data breach, academic integrity scandal, harmful AI output) could damage years of institution-building [^1]
3. **Staff capability gap** — teachers and trainers are neither equipped nor confident to handle AI in their context; professional development demand is urgent and under-resourced [^1]
4. **Vendor trust deficit** — most AI vendors don't understand education regulation, student welfare obligations, or the difference between corporate L\&D and real teaching [^1]
5. **Academic integrity in crisis** — existing detection tools are blunt, contested, and educators hate using them; there's no replacement model yet [^1]
6. **Compliance burden** — ASQA/TEQSA obligations are demanding and growing; AI creates new compliance risks faster than existing frameworks can absorb [^1]

***

## 🎯 Gains Alex Is Looking For

- A **defensible, values-grounded AI position** they can communicate to board, staff, students, and community [^1]
- A **trusted partner who speaks education** — not a tech vendor who needs the sector explained to them [^1]
- **Quick wins** (a workshop, a pilot, a policy draft) that demonstrate momentum without requiring a long procurement cycle [^1]
- **Sector credibility** — being able to point to their AI approach as a model, not just a compliance response [^1]
- Staff who feel **supported and confident** navigating AI, rather than threatened or confused by it [^1]

***

## 💡 Implications for Zero Team

- Lead with **risk reduction**, not innovation excitement — Alex is not looking for a transformation pitch, they're looking for a way to stop the bleeding and build something solid [^1]
- The **"educator-led, trustworthy AI"** positioning directly addresses Alex's vendor trust deficit — but it needs to be demonstrated, not just claimed [^1]
- A **co-design framing** (not vendor-client) matches Alex's identity as a leader, not a buyer [^1]
- **Case studies and peer references** will be more persuasive than feature lists — Alex needs to see it working somewhere they respect [^1]
