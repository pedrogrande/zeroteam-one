How to use AI as a thinking partner — not just a tool — and leave every session richer than you started.

Why it works

What you can expect

This style is designed to leave you richer than you started — not just with an output, but with better thinking. The session itself is the value, not just what it produces.

🏗️

Production-ready artefacts

Schemas, frameworks, templates, code, and documentation that go directly into real work — not polished generics.

🔭

New framings & perspectives

The session surfaces angles and constraints you hadn't considered before you typed your first message.

✍️

Ideas made public-ready

Loosely held concepts get structured into communicable form — often without you asking for it explicitly.

🧪

Pre-tested decisions

Weaknesses in your logic get caught before you build or commit, not after. The session stress-tests your thinking.

🔗

Cross-domain synthesis

Ideas from adjacent fields surface connections your domain peers would miss — and your sessions become more than the sum of their parts.

🧠

Meta-insight about your thinking

The retro at the end of each session extracts what you learned about the problem — and about yourself.

Suitability

Is this style right for you?

Use the co-design style when your problem has depth, ambiguity, or stakes. Avoid it for simple lookups — a regular query is faster and produces the same result.

✓ Good fit ✗ Poor fit
You're designing something new You need a quick fact or definition
You have context and knowledge to bring You have no prior knowledge of the domain
You want to stress-test your thinking You just need a draft written fast
The output will inform real decisions The question has one clear answer
You can invest 20+ minutes in the session You have 2 minutes

Example use cases

  • Designing a product, curriculum, or workflow from scratch
  • Evaluating technical architecture options before committing
  • Developing a framework or methodology you'll publish or teach
  • Turning a rough idea into a structured, shareable document
  • Decomposing a complex task with risk mapping
  • Critiquing an artefact you've already produced
  • Running a retrospective on a project or system
  • Exploring a domain before writing or presenting
Before you start

Pre-session preparation

Work through these questions before you type your first message. You don't need to write them down — but consciously answering them shapes every prompt that follows.

Q1

What is my desired output?

Be specific: a schema, a decision, a critique, a structured list, a document? Knowing the output type shapes how you open the session.

Q2

What does a great outcome look like?

Set your own success criteria before you start — otherwise you won't know when to stop iterating. "A great outcome is something I could hand to a colleague tomorrow."

Q3

What context does the AI need that it won't have?

Think across three layers: project context (what you're building and why), constraints (stack, audience, budget, non-negotiables), and your current position (what you know and what the gap is).

Q4

Is this a divergent or convergent session?

Divergent: explore widely and generate options. Convergent: narrow toward a decision or finished artefact. Mixed sessions work — but you need to consciously shift between modes.

Q5

What is out of scope?

Knowing what to exclude is as important as knowing what to include. Stating it explicitly prevents the session from drifting.

Q6

Who is the audience for the output?

Outputs shaped for yourself look different from those shaped for a client, student, or executive. Declare this upfront — it changes the tone, structure, and depth of every response.

Q7

What comes next after this session?

If it feeds into a build, a presentation, or another session — say so. The AI will structure outputs to serve what comes next.

The 5-phase method

Priming Before Producing

Most people skip straight to Phase 5. They ask for the output before the context exists, get a generic answer, and conclude the tool isn't that useful. Priming is the differentiator.

The quality of your Phase 5 output is almost entirely determined by the quality of your Phase 1–4 conversation. Think of it like warming up clay before sculpting — you don't start shaping until the material is ready.

1

Prime the Field

Build shared understanding before revealing your purpose. Short, scene-setting questions that warm up the domain without tipping your hand. Keep prompts to one sentence — no explanation of why you're asking.

Tell me about the challenges businesses face when implementing AI.
How does lack of trust impact the business benefits of AI?
What does the research say about this?
2

Reveal Your Direction

Introduce what you're actually building — your existing ideas, document, or framework — and ask what's missing or weak. The AI is now responding to your specific work against a fully primed backdrop.

I'm working on a trust framework for AI adoption. Here's what I have. What am I missing?
Here's a document I've produced. Analyse it — strengths, weaknesses, and what you'd improve.
3

Probe Weak Spots

Anything underdeveloped gets its own focused thread. Use the AI as an intellectual sparring partner — probing weakness before committing is one of the highest-leverage moves in this style.

That third point feels thin. Let's explore it further.
What's the strongest argument against this position?
4

Apply Alternative Lenses

Deliberately shift the frame to uncover non-obvious insights. Each lens is a constraint that forces new connections — this is where unexpected insights emerge.

How would a sceptical CFO read this?
Apply a systems thinking lens to this problem.
What does this look like from the employee's perspective rather than the organisation's?
5

Produce the Output

Only now do you ask for the thing you came for. You're not asking the AI to generate from scratch — you're asking it to crystallise something you've already built together.

Now write the section on trust using everything we've discussed.
Turn this into a structured table I can use in my presentation.
Rewrite the document with your improvement recommendations incorporated.
Closing the loop

The Session Retrospective

Most people close the tab when they get the output they came for. The retro is what separates a good session from a great one. It extracts meta-value — the reframes, tensions, and implications that the session produced but never explicitly surfaced.

👍 Liked

What worked well? What outputs or exchanges were most valuable?

💡 Learned

What do you now understand that you didn't before? What surprised you?

⚠️ Lacked

What was missing? Where did the session fall short or go shallow?

🔭 Longed for

What do you wish had happened? What would make the next session better?

The single most valuable closing prompt:
"What did we learn in this session? What are the key insights and what would I do differently next time?"

This question costs 30 seconds and routinely produces the most valuable sentence in the entire transcript. It also seeds the opening context for your next session on the same topic.
