# AI Training & Governance Offerings for Education Leaders in Australia
### A Competitive Landscape Analysis for Zero Team · May 2026

***

## Executive Summary

Australia's education sector faces a well-documented gap between the pace of AI adoption and the availability of structured, sector-specific capability development for the leaders responsible for managing it. While a growing number of programs now exist — from free government-backed online modules to premium governance workshops — the landscape is fragmented, largely generic, and heavily weighted toward classroom teachers rather than the decision-makers who set institutional policy. Only 24% of Australians have undertaken any AI-related training or education, compared to 39% globally[^1], and Australia ranks near the bottom internationally for AI literacy. This report maps the current offerings available to education leaders across the five organisation types identified in Zero Team's opportunity sketches, assesses their focus and quality, and identifies the structural gaps that represent Zero Team's clearest opportunity.

***

## The Policy & Regulatory Backdrop

Understanding what is being offered requires understanding the policy framework these offerings are responding to.

The **Australian Framework for Generative AI in Schools** was released in November 2023 and reviewed in 2024, with Education Ministers endorsing the review findings in June 2025[^2]. The Framework outlines six principles: enhancing teaching and learning, safeguarding human and social wellbeing, promoting transparency and accountability, ensuring equity and inclusivity, protecting privacy and security, and fostering continuous improvement[^3]. It provides guidance rather than mandates, leaving individual systems and schools to determine implementation.

In the **higher education sector**, TEQSA took an educative approach from 2022 through to 2025, but in May 2025 announced a shift to a **regulatory-led framework beginning in 2026**[^4]. This reflects the widespread use of generative AI by students and the associated risks to assessment integrity, and means that from 2026, higher education providers must demonstrate how they are managing AI risks in alignment with the Higher Education Standards Framework[^5]. The **Australian Centre for Student Equity and Success (ACSES)** published a new *Australian Framework for Artificial Intelligence in Higher Education* in December 2025, focused on equity, human-centred learning, and research integrity[^6].

In the **VET sector**, the Standards for RTOs 2025 came into force on 1 July 2025, shifting ASQA's regulatory framework decisively toward outcomes-based quality and self-assurance[^7]. The ICT Training Package is being redesigned by the Future Skills Organisation to address urgent skills gaps in AI and cyber security[^7].

At the national level, the **2025 National AI Plan** (Action 5: 'Support and train Australians') aims to boost digital skills, expand training access, and grow an inclusive pipeline of AI-ready workers through industry and the skills and education sectors[^8]. The **Senate Select Committee on Adopting AI** report (2026) further reinforces education as a national priority for AI capability[^9].

## Current Offerings: Who Offers What

### 1. Government & Peak Body Programs

#### Australian Framework for Generative AI in Schools (Dept of Education)
The foundational policy document for K-12 schools — a principles-based guide rather than a training program[^10]. It provides a values framework and implementation guidance but does not itself deliver capability development. Education leaders are expected to operationalise it themselves.

#### ESA / Microsoft: AI Readiness Modules for Teachers
**Education Services Australia (ESA)** and **Microsoft** partnered in 2025 to deliver two free online modules aligned with the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST) and the federal AI Framework[^11][^12]. The modules cover:
- *AI Readiness: Preparing teachers for safe and effective use of GenAI* (60–90 mins)
- *AI Implementation: Strategies for guiding students in safe and effective use of GenAI* (60–90 mins, secondary teachers, ages 13+)[^13]

Both modules are free, self-paced, and certificate-bearing[^14]. They are part of Microsoft's broader goal to train one million people across Australia and New Zealand in GenAI skills by 2026[^11][^15]. Microsoft estimates teachers can save an average of 9.3 hours per week through GenAI use[^12].

#### Digital Technologies Hub (ESA)
Hosts nine free, self-paced professional learning modules for teachers (Foundation to Year 10), covering generative AI, machine learning, bias, responsible AI, programming, and ethics[^13]. No login required. Certificate available for GenAI modules. Comprehensive for classroom teachers but not designed for institutional leaders.

#### Victorian Academy of Teaching & Leadership
Published resources on AI in schools aligned with the Australian Framework, targeted at school leaders in Victoria[^16].

### 2. Education-Sector Specialist Providers

#### IAMAI (I Am AI)
**IAMAI** is the most directly comparable provider to Zero Team's proposed positioning. Describing itself as "the leading provider of AI readiness workshops for education systems"[^17], IAMAI offers:
- *Schools Ready AI* workshop series: four two-hour online workshops (or two in-person sessions) covering governance frameworks, risk guidance, staff capability development, and student AI use[^18]
- Programmes specifically built for Catholic dioceses in partnership with **CEnet**, including a dedicated dioceses program[^19]
- Services include outsourced AI leadership, coaching, short workshops, and full governance workshop series
- Pricing starts at $500 for coaching; full workshop programs from $1,990; shared programs across schools to reduce costs[^18]

IAMAI's model is workshop-based, governance-focused, and school-specific. It has worked with hundreds of schools[^17] and has established relationships in the Catholic sector that directly overlap with Zero Team's CEM opportunity.

#### AI Surge
**AI Surge** offers a *Professional Learning Group (PLG) for Principals* — a hands-on AI training program tailored to school leadership covering data-driven insights, attendance tracking, performance analysis, strategic planning, and leadership communication via AI[^20]. They also offer an *AI-Ready Principal Programme* designed as a year-long leadership journey for New Zealand principals, including Google-hosted leadership intensives and EduTech Sydney attendance[^21]. AI Surge is more operationally focused (using AI for admin tasks) than governance/values focused.

#### NextEd
**NextEd** launched free foundational AI training for all students enrolled across its education businesses (Greenwich College, AIT, Celtic Training, ISCD, Sero Institute) from early 2026[^22]. For organisations, it offers *AI in Practice: Building Workforce Capability*, a half-day hands-on workshop covering AI limitations, risks (hallucinations, bias, data privacy), and practical workflow integration[^22]. NextEd's offering is workforce-capability focused and not specifically designed for education governance contexts.

#### Code for Schools / AWS
In 2025, **Code for Schools** partnered with **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** to launch Australia's first locally developed AI literacy program, aiming to reach one million students over three years[^23]. The program covers ethical foundations, hands-on skills, industry insights, and critical thinking, with professional learning resources for teachers[^23]. Student-facing; teacher resources are supplementary.

#### CEnet (Catholic Education Network)
**CEnet** — the technology and services provider to Australian Catholic dioceses — has developed its own AI governance infrastructure, including:
- *cechat*: a generative AI solution built for Catholic schools, with over 114 institutions, 14 member dioceses, 2,033 staff and 8,239 students actively engaging as of late 2025[^24]
- An *AI Governance Framework* aligned to Catholic values and principles[^25]
- A generic *Sample School AI Policy* template[^25]
- CEnet has partnered with IAMAI specifically for diocese-level governance workshops[^19]

CEnet is not a training provider in the traditional sense but is an incumbent infrastructure provider for Catholic schools — a significant contextual factor for any approach to Catholic Education Melbourne.

### 3. Professional & Higher Education Providers

#### AIM (Australian Institute of Management)
**AIM** offers a *Responsible AI Leadership* two-day facilitator-led short course[^26] — one of the most directly relevant offerings in the market for the "Alex" persona. It covers:
- AI strategy development aligned to organisational values and risk tolerance
- Ethical frameworks for AI decision-making (bias, transparency, accountability, fairness)
- Australian regulatory and legal landscape for AI
- Leading AI adoption to build trust with employees, customers, and stakeholders
- Data privacy, security, and risk management
- Governance structures and human capabilities for sustained AI value[^26]

AIM's offering is cross-sector (not education-specific) and aimed at senior leaders and professionals. It is one of the few programs in Australia explicitly framed around *responsible* AI leadership rather than AI productivity.

#### KPMG AI Learning
**KPMG** offers an *AI Learning* program for leaders, delivered through workshops, hybrid learning, self-paced e-learning, and on-demand video[^27]. It focuses on AI fundamentals, strategy, change leadership, and governance/ethics/risk. Like AIM, it is not education-specific and is corporate in positioning and pricing.

#### Sydney Community College: AI Integration for the VET Sector
A short workshop specifically designed for RTOs covering ASQA compliance, AI tool integration, learning resource updates, and governance procedures[^28][^29]. Practical and sector-specific for the VET context, though limited in depth and not designed for senior leadership decision-making.

#### TAFE NSW: Responsible Artificial Intelligence (Microskill)
**TAFE NSW** offers a two-hour online self-paced *Responsible Artificial Intelligence* microskill covering principles of responsible AI, governance, responsible AI use in business, and case studies[^30]. Low depth, introductory level.

#### Generative AI for Education Leaders Summit
An annual conference event (2025 and 2026 editions) attracting senior leaders from K-12, VET, and higher education[^31]. Conference-format learning — useful for networking and case study exposure but not structured capability development.

#### National Education Summit: AI in the Classroom Stream
A conference stream at the 2026 National Education Summit covering foundational AI understanding, ethical frameworks, AI-enhanced teaching, curriculum integration, and professional learning[^32]. Similar to the Generative AI Summit — event-based, not programme-based.

***

### 4. Technology Vendor Programs

#### Microsoft Education
Microsoft's broader education AI strategy encompasses the ESA partnership for teacher training, Copilot integration across education platforms, and its commitment to train one million people across ANZ[^11][^33]. Microsoft's offering is tool-centric (Copilot adoption) rather than governance or pedagogy-centric, and is explicitly designed to drive platform adoption.

#### Google (Gemini / DeepMind)
Google has expanded Gemini to education settings with features like *Guided Learning*, designed to act as a tutor rather than a shortcut[^33]. Google DeepMind's education research acknowledges that "most tools fail to support teachers" and highlights weak evaluation practices as a major barrier to realising AI's educational potential[^34]. Google's programs are platform-adoption focused.

#### SupaHuman AI (RTO-specific)
**SupaHuman** offers a purpose-built *AI Workspace for RTOs*, covering ASQA compliance documentation, assessment generation, learning material creation, and a learner-facing AI assistant[^35]. An operational AI tool rather than a capability development program — it automates RTO work rather than building human understanding of AI.

#### RTOAiVoX
An AI-powered platform for VET professionals covering content creation, compliance, and digital literacy, with access to all 61 Training Packages and 14,833+ units of competency[^36]. Tool platform, not education program.

## Key Limitations in the Current Landscape

### 1. Almost Nothing is Designed for the Decision-Maker
The overwhelming majority of current offerings are designed for classroom teachers — not for the heads of systems, principals, CEOs, CLOs, and academic directors who are making institutional AI policy decisions. The ESA/Microsoft modules are explicitly for classroom teachers[^11]. The Digital Technologies Hub is for F-10 teachers[^13]. Conferences reach leaders but don't develop structured capability. AIM's Responsible AI Leadership and KPMG's program are the exceptions — but neither is education-specific.

### 2. Sector-Specific Depth is Shallow or Absent
Programs that are education-specific (IAMAI, AI Surge, Sydney Community College VET workshops) do not engage with the full regulatory complexity that education leaders actually face — ASQA audit obligations, TEQSA Higher Education Standards, child safety statutory requirements, the Privacy Act's application to student data, and the academic integrity frameworks that TEQSA's 2026 regulatory shift is demanding[^4][^5]. Programs that engage with regulatory depth (AIM, KPMG) are corporate and sector-agnostic.

### 3. Values Framing is Almost Entirely Absent
Only CEnet's offering (and to a lesser extent IAMAI's Catholic dioceses programme) engages with an institutional values framework — the Catholic social tradition, human dignity, the common good — in relation to AI[^19][^25]. No program in the market systematically addresses what responsible AI means in the context of a Uniting Church school, a faith-based higher education provider, or a community-values RTO. Frameworks tend to be regulatory-compliance-oriented, not values-grounded.

### 4. Governance Framework Building vs. Awareness Training
Most programs deliver *awareness* of AI — what it is, how it works, what the risks are. Almost none deliver what education leaders most need: support building a *governance framework* — the policies, processes, oversight structures, and escalation paths that allow an institution to manage AI systematically rather than reactively. IAMAI comes closest, but its workshop model produces a framework document rather than an embedded institutional capability.

### 5. Academic Integrity — A Live Crisis with No Adequate Response
Academic integrity in an AI-saturated assessment environment is one of the most urgent problems in Australian education[^4][^3], yet the market's response is almost entirely detection-tool-oriented. Detection-based approaches are widely contested — educators dislike them, students find ways around them, and they criminalise behaviour that is often ambiguous[^37]. No provider in the market offers a systematic, curriculum-redesign-based academic integrity framework as a standalone product. TEQSA's 2026 regulatory shift explicitly demands that institutions move beyond detection to demonstrate how their assessment design confirms learning outcomes[^4].

### 6. The RTO Compliance Gap is Significant and Unserved
The VET sector's compliance burden under the Standards for RTOs 2025 is substantial, and AI creates both new risks (AI-generated content misrepresenting qualification outcomes; AI learner support agents giving incorrect enrolment advice) and potential solutions (AI-assisted compliance documentation)[^7][^38]. Yet no provider in the market offers a comprehensive, RTO-specific AI governance and compliance capability program that combines leadership training, policy framework development, and compliant AI tool integration. Sydney Community College's workshop is introductory. SupaHuman and RTOAiVoX are tool platforms, not capability programs.

### 7. Leadership vs. Staff Programmes Are Not Differentiated
Across the market, AI training for education is overwhelmingly positioned at the teacher/trainer level. Leadership-specific programs — designed for the strategic governance decisions that principals, CEOs, and CLOs need to make — are not clearly differentiated from staff training. The same modules are often presented to all staff. The distinct capability requirements of a school principal (who needs to govern AI across an institution, communicate with parents, manage staff, and engage with the system office) versus a classroom teacher (who needs to use AI responsibly in delivery) are not recognised in any existing Australian education-specific offering.

### 8. The Tech-Vendor Trust Problem
Microsoft and Google's programs dominate in reach (free, scalable, well-resourced) but are explicitly tool-adoption programs — designed to embed Microsoft Copilot or Google Gemini, respectively, into educational workflows[^11][^33]. Education leaders who want a genuinely vendor-neutral, educator-led perspective on responsible AI adoption have almost nowhere to go. The concern among governance researchers is that most institutional responses are ad-hoc and lack a cohesive strategy, often guided by vendor relationships rather than pedagogical or governance principles[^37].

## Structural Gaps: Zero Team's Opportunity Space

The gaps in the current landscape map directly onto Zero Team's proposed positioning:

| Gap in Current Market | Zero Team's Potential Response |
|---|---|
| Nothing designed for the institutional decision-maker | Leadership-specific AI governance programme |
| No sector-specific depth for education regulation | ASQA/TEQSA/Privacy Act/child safety embedded from day one |
| Values framing almost entirely absent | Values-grounded co-design (Catholic, Uniting Church, community) |
| Governance framework building vs. awareness training | Framework-first engagement model |
| Academic integrity — no curriculum-redesign solution | Assessment redesign + integrity framework product |
| RTO compliance support unserved | RTO-specific AI governance + compliance programme |
| No differentiation between leadership and staff capability | Separate leadership and teacher/trainer pathways |
| Tech-vendor trust problem | Genuinely vendor-neutral, educator-led positioning |

The one provider that most directly occupies Zero Team's space is **IAMAI** — governance-focused, school-specific, and established in the Catholic sector. IAMAI's limitations are its relatively shallow regulatory depth, its workshop-rather-than-programme model, and its absence from the RTO and higher education segments. Understanding IAMAI's model and positioning in more detail is the most important competitive intelligence priority before Zero Team enters the market.

***

## Conclusion

The Australian market for AI capability development in education is active but structurally inadequate for the decision-makers who most need it. The dominant offerings are either too narrow (teacher-focused, awareness-level, tool-adoption-oriented) or too generic (corporate leadership programs with no education sector knowledge). The regulatory environment is tightening — TEQSA's 2026 shift to a regulatory-led framework and ASQA's strengthened Standards are creating urgent demand for sector-specific governance capability that the current market cannot adequately supply. The academic integrity crisis is unresolved. The values dimension is almost entirely unaddressed. And the institutional leader — the person who has to make decisions that affect thousands of students, manage regulators, and defend their institution's AI approach in public — is largely invisible to the existing provider landscape.

This is the gap Zero Team is positioned to fill.
