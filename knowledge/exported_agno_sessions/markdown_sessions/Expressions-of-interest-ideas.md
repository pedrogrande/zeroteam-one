There will also be at least two full time leaders working with me now who bring different strengths, knowledge and experience.

For now, I want to design an application form I can invite students to complete if they are interested in taking on one or more of the roles on offer.

This form should not be like a traditional job application form, it should really give students the feeling that I recognise their unique value and that if they demonstrate their energy, passion and commitment, they will be successful in becoming a team member.

What questions could I ask in this form?

I now have deep context on Pete's design philosophy, student program thinking, and Zero Team's ethos. Let me craft the application form.

***

The form below is designed to *feel like an invitation*, not a filter. Every question signals that Zero Team already respects the person reading it — the form itself is the first experience of the organisation's values.[^1]

# Zero Team — Expression of Interest Form

*Suggested intro copy to set the tone before Question 1:*

> **You don't need to be an AI expert. You don't need a perfect CV. You need to be curious, honest, and ready to build something real.**
>
> Zero Team is assembling its founding cohort — a group of people who will shape Australia's most important conversation about AI, trust, and what responsible technology actually looks like in practice. Most of the roles don't exist anywhere else yet. That's the point.
>
> This form isn't a job application. It's the start of a conversation. There are no trick questions and no right answers. We're looking for energy, self-awareness, and genuine commitment — not a polished performance.
>
> It takes about 20 minutes. Be honest. That's what earns a place here.

***

## Section 1: You, Right Now

These questions establish who the person is *before* they've been shaped by Zero Team — not their credentials, but their current vantage point.[^1]

**1. In one sentence: what are you studying, and why did you choose it?**
*(Not what your degree is called — why did that field pull at you?)*

**2. What's something you've taught yourself recently — not for class, just because you wanted to know?**
*(Can be anything. The subject matters less than the fact that you went looking.)*

**3. Describe a moment when you realised something you believed was wrong. What did you do with that realisation?**
*(We're not looking for a dramatic story. We're looking for intellectual honesty.)*

***

## Section 2: Your Relationship with AI

This section deliberately avoids testing AI knowledge — it tests *orientation* toward uncertainty, which is far more predictive of success.[^2][^1]

**4. What's your honest assessment of where you're at with AI tools right now?**
*Choose the description that fits most closely:*

- I've heard a lot but haven't used much yet
- I use ChatGPT or similar tools occasionally, mostly for study
- I use AI tools regularly and have explored beyond the basics
- I've built something with AI, even if it was small or imperfect
- Something else (please describe)

**5. What's one thing about AI that genuinely worries you — not in a vague "it'll take jobs" way, but something specific that you've actually thought about?**

**6. What's one thing about AI that genuinely excites you — again, something specific, not just "the possibilities"?**

***

## Section 3: How You Work

These questions reveal operating style and self-awareness — the two things that predict whether someone thrives in an autonomous, low-supervision environment.[^3][^1]

**7. Tell us about a time you took initiative on something nobody asked you to do. What happened?**

**8. We'll be honest with you: during semester, you'll have about 5 hours a week for this. That's not much. How do you intend to make 5 hours count?**
*(This question is real — we want to see how you actually think about constraint, not whether you give an impressive answer.)*

**9. Describe how you work when you're stuck. Not what you *should* do — what you actually do.**

***

## Section 4: Why Zero Team, Why Now

This section surfaces motivation quality — the difference between someone who wants the CV line and someone who wants to build something.[^4][^1]

**10. What specifically about Zero Team's work resonates with you?**
*(If you haven't looked us up properly yet, please do before answering this. We can tell the difference.)*

**11. A year from now, what do you hope you'll be able to say you contributed — not learned, *contributed*?**

**12. Is there anything about AI, trust, governance, youth empowerment, or responsible technology that you already care about? Tell us what it is and why you care.**

***

## Section 5: The Role(s) You're Drawn To

**13. Review the list of Zero Team roles. Which one or two feel most like *you* — not the most impressive ones, the ones that actually match how your mind works?**
*(Link or list of roles provided here)*

**14. For your first-choice role: what do you already bring to it that we wouldn't find on your CV?**

**15. What's the thing about that role that would genuinely stretch you — the part you're not sure you can do yet?**

***

## Section 6: One Thing We Should Know

**16. Is there something about you — your background, your circumstances, your perspective, the way you think — that isn't visible anywhere else in this form but that you want us to know?**
*(This is the most open question we'll ask. Some people leave it blank. The best responses come from people who've thought about what makes them genuinely different.)*

***

## Section 7: The Commitment Question

This is the only section where there is, in a sense, a right answer — not what they say, but whether they've thought about it honestly.[^1]

**17. Zero Team is not a class and it's not a traditional internship. There are real stakes — real clients, real deadlines, real work that matters. Given that, are you in?**

*Choose one:*

- Yes, fully — I've thought about this and I'm committing
- I'm interested but I have some questions first *(tell us what they are)*
- I need to think about it more *(that's fine — what would help you decide?)*

**18. Finally: what's the most honest thing you can say about why you're doing this?**
*(No points for the right-sounding answer. Points for the true one.)*

***

## Form Closing Copy

> **What happens next:** We'll read every response personally. If we'd like to have a conversation with you, we'll reach out within [X] days. Whether or not you join this cohort, we'll give you a genuine response — not a form letter.
>
> Thank you for taking the time to be honest with us. That's already telling us something.

***

## A Note on What to *Exclude*

The form deliberately omits:[^1]

- GPA or academic results
- Employment history or CV upload
- References at this stage
- Any question that rewards a polished answer over a true one
- Demographic information beyond what's voluntarily shared in Q16

The selection signal you're looking for is **intellectual honesty + self-awareness + genuine motivation** — none of which show up in a transcript. The form is designed so that someone who tries to perform well gives a weaker application than someone who answers plainly and truthfully. That asymmetry is intentional.[^3]
