***

### The obligations that shape responsible AI use

**Currency notice:** The regulatory content in this activity reflects the state of Australian privacy law, automated decision-making (ADM) provisions, and court guidance on AI disclosure as at June 2026. This is a rapidly evolving area. Learners should treat this content as a starting point for awareness, not a definitive legal reference, and should check with their organisation's legal or compliance function for current guidance. **The client content slots in Tasks 3.2.1 and 3.2.5 should be reviewed and updated by the deploying organisation before each course cohort.**

Everything built in this course so far has focused on capability: understanding AI, using it effectively, evaluating outputs critically, and applying it strategically in your work. This final activity addresses the dimension that capability alone cannot cover: the obligations that govern how AI must be used in a professional context.

Those obligations are of two distinct kinds. Legal and regulatory obligations are defined by legislation, regulation, and organisational policy. They set boundaries that are not discretionary. Ethical obligations are defined by professional values, duty of care, and the kind of professional you want to be. The two kinds of obligation overlap significantly, but they are not identical. Something can be technically permissible and still be professionally wrong.

Both kinds of obligation apply to you when you use AI at work. Neither is suspended because AI was involved in producing an output. Neither diminishes as AI tools become more capable. If anything, as AI makes it easier to produce large volumes of professional-grade content quickly, the importance of understanding and applying these obligations clearly becomes greater, not less.

This activity covers the regulatory landscape relevant to your industry, the privacy and confidentiality obligations that shape what data you can use with AI tools, the intellectual property questions that arise from AI-generated content, the personal liability implications of AI use in professional contexts, and your organisation's own AI policy and approved use framework.

## Task 3.2.1 — The Regulatory Landscape for AI in Your Industry

***

### Why regulation matters for everyday AI use

Regulatory compliance can feel like a specialist concern, something managed by legal and compliance teams rather than something relevant to everyday professional practice. AI changes that perception.

When you use an AI tool at work, you are making decisions that may have direct regulatory implications: what data you input, what outputs you act on, what disclosures you make, and what oversight you apply. These decisions sit within a regulatory framework whether or not you are aware of it. Awareness is not optional for professionals who use AI.

This task gives you the foundational regulatory picture for your industry: the key frameworks you need to know, what they require at the individual employee level, and where to go when you need guidance on specific questions.

***

### The broader regulatory context for AI

AI-specific regulation is still developing in most jurisdictions. Australia, the European Union, the United Kingdom, and the United States are all at different stages of developing frameworks specifically directed at AI systems and their use. The direction of travel across most jurisdictions is toward greater regulation, more explicit accountability requirements, and clearer obligations around transparency, fairness, and human oversight.

In the current period, the most directly relevant regulatory obligations for most Australian professionals are not AI-specific. They are the existing frameworks that apply to data, privacy, professional conduct, and organisational governance, now applied to a context that includes AI tools.

Understanding the existing frameworks that govern your work, and how AI use interacts with them, is the foundation of regulatory compliance in an AI-assisted workplace.

***

### Key regulatory frameworks relevant to most Australian workplaces

**The Privacy Act 1988 (Cth), the Australian Privacy Principles, and the 2024 Reforms**
The Privacy Act governs how organisations collect, use, store, and disclose personal information. The Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) set out specific obligations that apply to most private sector organisations and Australian Government agencies.

The relevance to AI use is direct. When you input personal information into an AI tool, you are using personal information in a way that must be consistent with the APPs. Depending on how the tool processes and stores that information, inputting it may constitute a disclosure to a third party. The question of whether that disclosure is authorised, and whether it is consistent with the purpose for which the information was collected, is a genuine compliance question, not a hypothetical one.

[minterellison](https://www.minterellison.com/articles/privacy-and-other-legislation-amendment-act-2024-now-in-effect)

Key obligations relevant to AI use:

- Personal information should only be used for the purpose for which it was collected, or a directly related purpose
- Personal information should not be disclosed to third parties without appropriate consent or authorisation
- Organisations must take reasonable steps to protect personal information from misuse, interference, loss, and unauthorised access
- Individuals generally have the right to know how their personal information is being used

**The Notifiable Data Breaches scheme**
Under the Privacy Act, organisations subject to the Act are required to notify the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) and affected individuals when a data breach is likely to result in serious harm. Inputting personal information into an unsecured or unapproved AI tool could, in certain circumstances, constitute a notifiable data breach.

**Sector-specific privacy and confidentiality obligations**
Many industries have privacy and confidentiality obligations that go beyond the Privacy Act. Healthcare professionals are subject to obligations under the My Health Records Act 2012 and various state and territory health records legislation. Legal professionals are subject to confidentiality obligations under their professional conduct rules. Financial services professionals are subject to obligations under the Corporations Act 2001 and relevant ASIC guidance. These sector-specific obligations apply to AI use in those contexts.

**Professional conduct and standards frameworks**
Most regulated professions have conduct rules or standards that are relevant to AI use, even where those rules were not written with AI in mind. The obligations to act with competence, to avoid misleading conduct, to maintain confidentiality, to supervise delegated work, and to take personal responsibility for professional outputs all apply when AI is part of the workflow.

**Consumer protection and misleading conduct**
The Australian Consumer Law prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct in trade and commerce. Organisations that use AI to generate consumer-facing content, including marketing, product information, advice, and automated responses, carry obligations to ensure that content is not misleading or deceptive. The fact that AI generated the content is not a defence.

**Workplace laws and employment obligations**
Using AI in ways that affect employees, including in performance management, hiring, surveillance, or workload allocation, may engage obligations under the Fair Work Act 2009 and relevant anti-discrimination legislation. These are primarily organisational obligations, but employees who are involved in implementing AI-assisted HR processes should be aware that they exist.

***

### What employees are required to know and comply with personally

The regulatory obligations above are primarily organisational obligations. The organisation, as the data controller and the responsible entity, bears the primary compliance burden. However, individual employees carry personal obligations that sit within that framework.

At the individual level, you are generally required to:

- Act consistently with your organisation's data handling policies when using AI tools
- Not input personal, confidential, or sensitive information into tools that have not been approved for that purpose
- Raise concerns about AI use that appears to create regulatory risk through appropriate channels
- Not use AI to produce outputs that you submit as professional advice, assessments, or representations without the oversight and verification your professional obligations require
- Comply with any disclosure obligations that apply in your professional context when AI has been used to produce work product

You are generally not required to be a regulatory expert. You are required to act within the guidance your organisation provides, to ask when you are uncertain, and not to take actions you know or suspect create regulatory risk without seeking clarification first.

***

### Where to go for regulatory guidance

Regulatory questions about AI use in a specific professional context are best addressed by someone with the relevant expertise. The following are the appropriate starting points for different kinds of questions.

**General privacy questions:** Your organisation's privacy officer, legal team, or the OAIC's guidance materials at oaic.gov.au.

**Sector-specific regulatory questions:** Your organisation's compliance function, your professional body's guidance, or the relevant regulator's published guidance for your sector.

**Questions about your organisation's specific obligations:** Your manager, your organisation's legal or compliance team, or the contact specified in your organisation's AI policy.

**Urgent concerns about potential breaches:** Follow your organisation's incident reporting process. Do not delay and do not attempt to resolve a potential breach independently.

## Task 3.2.2 — Data Privacy and Confidentiality at Work

***

### The privacy question every AI user needs to answer

Every time you type something into an AI tool, you are making a privacy and confidentiality decision. You are sending information to a system that processes it, may store it, and may use it in ways that go beyond your immediate interaction. The question of whether what you are sending is appropriate to send is one you need to answer before you type it, not after.

This is not an argument against using AI. It is an argument for being deliberate about what you put into it. Most of the tasks where AI delivers genuine value do not require you to input sensitive data. Where they do, there are usually alternatives. And where no alternative exists, that is often a signal that the task is not appropriate for AI assistance in the current context.

***

### Three categories of sensitive information

The following three categories cover the types of information that require particular care in any AI-assisted workflow. They are not exhaustive, but they cover the situations that most commonly create problems.

**Personal information**
Personal information is information that identifies or could reasonably identify an individual. Under the Privacy Act, this includes names, contact details, dates of birth, financial information, health information, identification numbers, and any other information that connects to a specific person.

Personal information should not be entered into an external AI tool unless:

- The tool is approved by your organisation specifically for use with personal information
- The data handling arrangements for that tool are consistent with your organisation's privacy obligations
- Entering the information is necessary for the task and not just convenient

In practice, most AI-assisted drafting and analytical tasks can be completed using anonymised or generalised descriptions rather than actual personal information. "A 45-year-old employee with a performance concern" carries the same informational value for most prompting purposes as including the person's name and employee number.

**Confidential and commercially sensitive information**
Confidential information includes client information, business strategy documents, financial data, pricing and commercial terms, intellectual property, and any information your organisation or its clients would expect to be treated as private.

The question to ask before including confidential information in a prompt is: has this information been shared with me on the understanding that it would be kept within my organisation? If the answer is yes, entering it into an external AI tool almost certainly breaches that expectation.

Commercially sensitive information includes information about the organisation's competitive position, strategic plans, unannounced initiatives, financial performance, and any other information that could cause harm to the organisation or its clients if disclosed.

**Sensitive categories of personal information**
Some categories of personal information carry heightened protection under the Privacy Act and in most professional conduct frameworks. These include health information, information about a person's racial or ethnic origin, religious or political beliefs, sexual orientation, criminal history, financial circumstances beyond general financial information, and information about government-issued identifiers.

These categories should be treated with particular caution. In most professional contexts, there is no legitimate reason to include this type of information in a general-purpose AI prompt.

***

### The practical test

Before including any information in an AI prompt, apply this test:

> If I printed this prompt and left it on a desk in a public area, would I be comfortable with anyone reading it?

If the answer is no, the information in the prompt is likely too sensitive to include. Find an alternative approach: anonymise the data, use generalised descriptions, complete the task without AI assistance, or use an approved internal tool with appropriate data protections.

This test is deliberately simple. In practice, most privacy and confidentiality decisions are not genuinely difficult. The hard cases are usually the ones where the answer is clearly no but the convenience of including the real data is tempting.

***

### What to do when a task requires sensitive data

The following approaches allow you to capture the benefits of AI assistance on tasks involving sensitive information while managing the privacy and confidentiality risks.

**Anonymise the data**
Replace personal identifiers with generic descriptions. "Client A" instead of the client's name. "The employee" instead of a name and employee number. "A financial services firm with approximately 200 staff" instead of your organisation's name. In most cases, the AI's output will be equally useful.

**Use synthetic examples**
For tasks involving the analysis of specific cases, generate a synthetic version of the scenario that captures the structural features without the actual data. This is particularly useful for training materials, template development, and process testing.

**Use an approved internal tool**
If your organisation has approved an AI tool with appropriate data handling controls, and the task genuinely requires the actual data, use that tool rather than a general-purpose external one. Understand what data protections it provides before proceeding.

**Complete the task without AI assistance**
For tasks involving information that is so sensitive that no approach adequately manages the risk, the appropriate response is to complete the task without AI assistance. This is not a failure of AI literacy. It is the exercise of professional judgment.

***

### Recognising the privacy risks specific to AI tools

Using AI tools creates privacy risks that are different in kind from most other workplace technology risks, and worth understanding specifically.

**Input retention**
Many AI tools retain inputs for some period of time after the conversation ends. The duration and purpose of retention varies by tool and by your organisation's contractual arrangements with the tool provider. Before entering sensitive data into any tool, understand your organisation's guidance on whether inputs to that tool are retained.

**Training data use**
Some AI tools use inputs to improve their models. If an input is used as training data, the information in it may influence the model's outputs for other users. Most enterprise AI tools offer options to opt out of training data use. Your organisation's approved tool guidance should address whether this is configured.

**Data residency**
AI tools process data on servers that may be located in different jurisdictions with different legal frameworks governing data access and protection. For organisations with data residency requirements, this is a specific compliance consideration that shapes which tools are appropriate for which data types.

**Third-party access**
Tool providers may have contractual rights to access input data for purposes including technical support, safety monitoring, and legal compliance. Understanding what rights the tool provider has over your inputs is part of responsible tool selection.

***

### What to do if you suspect a privacy issue has occurred

If you believe you may have inadvertently entered sensitive information into an inappropriate tool, or if you become aware of a potential privacy or confidentiality breach related to AI use, follow your organisation's incident reporting process promptly.

Do not attempt to assess independently whether a breach has occurred or whether it is serious enough to report. The appropriate people to make that assessment are your privacy officer, legal team, or compliance function. Your responsibility is to report promptly and accurately, and to provide whatever information is needed to support the assessment.

## Task 3.2.3 — Intellectual Property and AI-Generated Content

***

### A rapidly evolving area with immediate practical implications

Intellectual property law as it applies to AI-generated content is developing quickly, and some of the most significant questions have not yet been definitively resolved by courts or legislators in Australia or elsewhere. This task does not attempt to resolve those questions. It gives you the practical principles that apply now, based on current law and professional guidance, so you can make sensible decisions about IP in your AI-assisted work without waiting for legal certainty that may be some years away.

The core practical principles are relatively clear, even where the law at the edges is not.

***

### Who owns the content AI generates at work

In most employment contexts in Australia, content created by an employee in the course of their employment using work-provided tools is owned by the employer, not the employee. This is a default position under Australian copyright law, and it applies regardless of how the content was produced.

AI-generated content produced during work, using work-approved tools, for work purposes, is generally treated the same way. The organisation owns it.

This has practical implications in several directions:

**You cannot claim personal ownership of AI-generated work product.** Content generated using your employer's approved tools in the course of your employment belongs to the organisation, not to you personally.

**AI-generated work product is a professional asset, not a personal one.** Sharing it externally, using it for personal purposes, or reproducing it in ways that weren't authorised by the organisation may create the same issues as doing the same with any other organisational work product.

**The organisation's obligations extend to AI-generated content.** The fact that the content was generated by AI does not reduce the organisation's obligations around accuracy, appropriateness, and compliance. If anything, it increases the importance of review and oversight.

***

### What happens when you input third-party content into an AI tool

One of the most commonly overlooked IP risks in AI-assisted work is the risk created not by what AI generates, but by what you put into it.

When you paste a third party's document, report, article, or code into an AI tool, you are inputting content that may be protected by that third party's intellectual property rights. Depending on the tool's terms of service and data handling arrangements, that content may be retained, used for model training, or otherwise processed in ways that the original rights holder did not authorise.

This applies to:

- Client documents shared with you in confidence
- Research papers and published reports
- Software code belonging to third parties
- Content licensed to your organisation for specific purposes that do not include inputting it into AI tools
- Any content where the rights holder has not authorised this kind of use

The practical rule is the same as for personal data: if you would not be comfortable explaining to the rights holder how you used their content, do not input it into an external AI tool without checking first.

***

### Copyright considerations for AI-generated content

Whether AI-generated content can be protected by copyright is a genuinely contested legal question. Under current Australian law, copyright protection generally requires a human author. Content generated entirely by AI without meaningful human creative input may not attract copyright protection, meaning it could potentially be copied and used by others without infringement.

There is also a converse risk worth noting: AI outputs can inadvertently reproduce or closely resemble protected material present in the training data, meaning the same output that may not be protectable as your organisation's own work could simultaneously infringe a third party's copyright. This exposure is most acute for marketing copy, creative content, and code, and is one reason why AI-generated content intended for external use or commercial publication warrants a closer IP review than internal drafting.

This is an evolving area, and the practical implications for most professional AI use are limited in the short term. But it is worth knowing that the assumption that "I created it, therefore I own it" does not straightforwardly apply to AI-generated content in the way it does to traditionally authored work.

Where copyright in AI-assisted work does attach, it is most likely to attach to the human-authored elements: the prompt design, the selection and arrangement of AI outputs, the editing and refinement, and any original content the human contributed to the final work. The more a professional invests genuine creative judgment in directing and refining AI output, the stronger the case for copyright protection in the result.

***

### Specific considerations for images and code

**Images**
AI-generated images are produced by models trained on large datasets of existing images. There is ongoing litigation and regulatory debate about whether training on copyright-protected images constitutes infringement, and about whether AI-generated images can infringe the copyright of images in the training data.

For professional purposes, the practical precautions are:

- Do not use AI-generated images in external communications without understanding your organisation's guidance on this
- Do not use AI image tools to reproduce the style of a specific named artist without considering the reputational and legal implications
- Be aware that stock image licensing terms typically do not cover AI-generated images, and that AI image tools have their own terms governing commercial use of outputs

**Code**
AI code generation tools are trained on large corpora of existing code, including open-source code with various licensing terms. Code generated by AI tools may contain fragments that closely resemble licensed open-source code, which can create obligations around attribution and licensing compliance.

For professional purposes:

- AI-generated code used in production software should be reviewed by someone with the relevant expertise, including for potential licensing issues
- Your organisation's software development policy should address how AI-generated code is reviewed and documented
- Do not assume that AI-generated code is licensing-free just because the AI tool generated it

***

### Practical rules for protecting intellectual property in AI-assisted work

- Do not input third-party content into external AI tools without checking whether your use is authorised
- Do not input your organisation's proprietary content, client work, or confidential processes into external tools without checking your organisation's guidance
- Do not pass off AI-generated content as independently created human work in contexts where that distinction matters professionally or contractually
- For AI-generated content that will be used externally, understand your organisation's policy on attribution and disclosure
- For AI-generated images and code used in a professional context, apply the specific precautions above and seek guidance from your organisation's legal team on edge cases

## Task 3.2.4 — Personal Liability and Professional Accountability

***

### The accountability principle, fully applied

In Task 1.2.6, you encountered the accountability principle for the first time: the person who submits or acts on an AI output is professionally responsible for it, regardless of how it was produced.

In Task 2.2.5, you saw how this principle applies to the appropriateness of outputs, and what genuine oversight requires.

In this task, the principle is applied fully: to the professional, regulatory, and personal legal consequences that can follow when AI use goes wrong and adequate oversight was not in place.

This is not intended to create anxiety about AI use. It is intended to make the accountability principle concrete enough to be motivating, so that the oversight habits built throughout this course are understood not just as good practice but as professional protection.

***

### When personal liability arises from AI use at work

Personal liability in the context of AI use at work arises in the same circumstances as personal liability in any professional context: when a professional fails to meet the standard of care their role, their profession, and their obligations require, and that failure causes or contributes to harm.

AI does not create new categories of professional liability. It creates new ways in which existing standards can be met or breached.

The circumstances most likely to create personal liability exposure in AI-assisted work are:

**Submitting AI-generated content as professional advice without adequate review**
In most regulated professions, the standard of care requires that advice given to clients reflects the professional's genuine, independently formed judgment based on adequate investigation and analysis. Passing on AI-generated content as professional advice without that underlying judgment does not meet that standard. The professional who does so may be liable for the consequences in the same way they would be if they had given negligent advice by any other means.

**Acting on AI-generated analysis that has not been verified**
When a professional acts on AI-generated analysis, particularly in high-stakes domains such as finance, law, healthcare, engineering, or regulatory compliance, and that analysis contains errors, the professional's liability depends on whether their reliance on the analysis was reasonable and whether appropriate verification steps were taken. Reliance without verification is generally not reasonable in high-stakes professional contexts.

**Inputting sensitive data into an unapproved tool in breach of policy**
An employee who inputs personal or confidential information into an AI tool in breach of their organisation's policy, and where that breach causes or contributes to a privacy incident or data breach, may face disciplinary action and potentially personal legal consequences, particularly if the action was deliberate or reckless.

**Failing to disclose AI use when disclosure is required**
Some professional contexts require disclosure of AI assistance. Lawyers submitting court documents in some jurisdictions are now required to certify whether AI was used and what review was performed. Professionals who fail to make required disclosures face the consequences applicable to non-disclosure in their professional context.

***

### Professional standards that apply in this context

Most regulated professions have a core accountability principle embedded in their conduct rules, even where those rules don't yet explicitly address AI. The language varies, but the substance is consistent: the professional is responsible for the quality of their work product, regardless of how it was produced.

**Legal profession:** Lawyers have duties of competence, candour, and supervision. A lawyer who relies on AI-generated legal research without adequate review risks breaching their duty of competence and, where errors cause harm to a client, may face professional discipline and civil liability.

**Financial advice:** Financial advisers have an obligation to provide advice that is appropriate to the client's circumstances and based on adequate investigation. AI-generated financial content cannot substitute for the adviser's independent professional judgment. Acting on or passing on AI-generated financial analysis without that judgment is inconsistent with the adviser's obligations.

**Healthcare:** Health professionals have duty of care obligations that require clinical judgment, not algorithmic output. AI tools may support clinical workflows in specific, validated applications, but clinical responsibility cannot be delegated to an AI system. Health professionals who rely on AI outputs in clinical decision-making without applying their own professional judgment are not meeting their duty of care.

**Accounting and audit:** Accountants and auditors are required to exercise professional skepticism and maintain professional standards in all their work. Using AI to generate financial analysis or audit documentation without adequate review and professional judgment does not meet those standards.

**Engineering and technical professions:** Engineers carrying statutory or contractual obligations for the safety and fitness of designed systems retain those obligations regardless of whether AI tools contributed to the design process. Professional indemnity and liability attach to the engineer, not the tool.

***

### What "I used AI" does not protect you from

It is worth being direct about the limits of AI as an explanation for professional failure, because there is a cultural tendency to treat AI involvement as a form of shared responsibility that reduces individual accountability.

"I used AI" does not protect you from:

- Professional disciplinary action for submitting work that does not meet your professional standards
- Civil liability for advice, analysis, or outputs that cause harm to clients or third parties
- Employment consequences for breaching your organisation's AI policy
- Regulatory consequences for breaches of privacy, confidentiality, or conduct obligations that occurred in the course of AI use
- Personal liability for a notifiable data breach caused by inputting sensitive data into an unapproved tool

In each of these situations, the relevant question is not whether AI was involved. It is whether you, as the responsible professional, exercised the judgment, oversight, and care that your obligations required. If you did, AI involvement is generally not a liability. If you didn't, AI involvement does not reduce your exposure.

***

### Building personal habits that protect your professional standing

The most effective protection against the liability risks described in this task is not legal knowledge. It is professional habit. The practices developed throughout this course are the foundation of that protection.

The professional who uses AI well, in a liability sense, is the one who:

- Applies AI to appropriate task components and retains human judgment for those that require it
- Reviews outputs genuinely, not perfunctorily, before submitting or acting on them
- Verifies specific claims in high-stakes outputs against independent sources
- Applies the three questions before any output leaves their hands: is it accurate, is it appropriate, am I accountable for it?
- Follows their organisation's AI policy and escalates situations it doesn't clearly address
- Keeps a record of their process for high-stakes AI-assisted work, so that adequate oversight can be demonstrated if it is ever questioned

None of these practices require exceptional effort. They require consistency. Consistent, documented, professional-grade oversight is the difference between AI use that creates liability exposure and AI use that reflects professional competence.

## Task 3.2.5 — Our AI Policy and Approved Use Framework

***

### Policy as enablement, not constraint

An AI policy is sometimes experienced as a list of things you can't do. That framing is understandable but counterproductive.

Your organisation's AI policy exists because the organisation has made a considered assessment of how AI can be used to benefit the work, what governance structures are needed to manage the risks, and what boundaries are necessary to protect employees, clients, and the organisation itself. The policy is the output of that assessment. Understanding it fully is what allows you to use AI confidently, within a framework that has been designed to support you.

Not knowing the policy is not a neutral position. It creates the risk of inadvertent breaches and the professional consequences that go with them. Knowing the policy well is what enables productive, confident AI use without the uncertainty of operating in undefined territory.

***

### What a well-designed AI policy covers

AI policies vary across organisations in length, scope, and maturity. The most useful ones address the following consistently.

**The rationale**
Why the organisation has adopted AI and what it is trying to achieve. Understanding the rationale gives individual AI use purposeful direction and helps employees make sensible decisions in situations the policy doesn't explicitly address.

**Approved tools and their intended uses**
A specific list of AI tools the organisation has approved, with notes on what they are approved for, what data handling arrangements apply, and whether any specific use restrictions exist. This is the most operationally important section of the policy for most employees.

**Tools requiring approval before use**
Some tools may be appropriate for certain use cases or certain roles but not others, and require sign-off before use. Understanding this category prevents the situation where an employee uses a tool that is not prohibited but was not intended for their use case.

**Prohibited uses**
The specific uses of AI that the organisation does not permit under any circumstances. These typically include uses involving confidential data in unapproved tools, uses that breach regulatory obligations, uses in regulated advice contexts without appropriate oversight, and uses that are inconsistent with the organisation's values or legal obligations.

**Oversight and review requirements**
What level of human review is required before AI-assisted outputs are submitted or acted on. This may be general guidance or specific requirements for particular output types.

**Disclosure obligations**
Whether and when employees are required to disclose that AI was used in producing a piece of work. This varies by organisation and by professional context.

**Incident reporting**
How to report AI-related concerns, potential policy breaches, or incidents. Who to contact, what to report, and what the process is.

**Policy review and updates**
How frequently the policy is reviewed, how changes are communicated, and where to find the current version.

***

### What is permitted: using AI confidently within the framework

The permitted uses section of an AI policy defines the space within which you can operate confidently. When you use an approved tool for an approved purpose with the oversight required by the policy, you are operating as your organisation intends.

The general principles that apply to permitted use across most organisational policies:

- Use approved tools only
- Do not input data types the policy identifies as restricted or prohibited
- Apply human review to outputs before they are submitted or acted on
- Apply additional scrutiny in high-stakes, external-facing, or regulated contexts
- Comply with any disclosure requirements that apply to your professional context

Within these principles, the space for productive AI use is substantial. The policy is not trying to prevent AI use. It is trying to ensure that AI use is governed well.

***

### What requires approval: navigating the grey areas

Most AI policies include a category of uses that are potentially appropriate but require judgment or sign-off before proceeding. These are the situations where the general principles don't give a clear answer and where acting without checking creates unnecessary risk.

Common examples of uses that typically require approval or clarification:

- Using AI for a task or data type not explicitly addressed in the policy
- Using an AI feature in a software tool that the organisation hasn't specifically evaluated
- Using AI in a client-facing context in a way not previously done in this organisation
- Using AI to generate content that will be submitted under a professional's name in a regulated context
- Using AI in a workflow that involves data types close to but not clearly within the restricted categories

The right response in each of these situations is to ask before acting. The appropriate contact is usually your manager, your organisation's AI lead, or the compliance or legal function, depending on the nature of the question.

Asking is not a sign of uncertainty or incompetence. It is good professional practice, and it is specifically what the policy is designed to facilitate.

***

### What is prohibited: the non-negotiable boundaries

Prohibited uses are not grey areas. They are uses the organisation has determined create unacceptable risk, breach regulatory obligations, or are inconsistent with its values or legal commitments.

Common categories of prohibited use in most organisational AI policies:

- Inputting personal information about identifiable individuals into unapproved external tools
- Inputting confidential client, patient, or commercially sensitive information into unapproved external tools
- Using AI to generate professional advice in regulated domains without the oversight and verification the professional's obligations require
- Using AI in ways that breach the organisation's confidentiality obligations to clients or partners
- Using AI to produce content that is misleading, discriminatory, or that could bring the organisation into disrepute
- Attempting to circumvent the policy by using personal accounts or devices to access AI tools the organisation hasn't approved

Prohibited uses are not subject to individual judgment about whether the specific circumstances justify an exception. If you believe a prohibited use is genuinely necessary in a specific situation, the right step is to escalate for a considered organisational decision, not to proceed on your own assessment.

***

### How to raise a concern or report an incident

AI-related concerns and incidents should be reported through the same channels as other professional concerns and incidents in your organisation. The specific process will be set out in your organisation's policy, but the general principles are consistent:

**Report promptly.** The sooner a concern or incident is reported, the more options the organisation has to manage it. Delayed reporting limits the available responses and, in the case of potential privacy breaches, may itself create additional obligations.

**Report accurately.** Describe what happened as clearly and specifically as possible. What tool was used, what was input, what output was produced, what action was taken on that output, and what concern has arisen. Do not speculate about whether a breach has occurred or how serious it is. Those assessments are for the appropriate specialists.

**Do not attempt to resolve it independently.** If you suspect a privacy breach, a confidentiality issue, or a policy violation, the right response is to report it, not to investigate it yourself, conceal it, or attempt to address it without involving the appropriate people.

**Report in good faith.** Good-faith reporting of genuine concerns is what the reporting process is there for. You will not face adverse consequences for reporting a concern that turns out not to be a breach, provided the report was made honestly and in good faith.

***

### Keeping up to date as the policy evolves

AI policy is not static. As tools develop, as the regulatory landscape evolves, and as the organisation learns from its own experience with AI adoption, the policy will be updated. Staying aware of current guidance is part of your professional responsibility.

In practice, this means:

- Knowing where to find the current version of the policy
- Being alert to communications from your organisation about policy updates
- Checking the policy before undertaking a new or unfamiliar AI use case, rather than relying on your recollection of a version you read some time ago
- Asking your manager or the relevant contact if you are uncertain whether your intended use is consistent with current guidance

The organisation commits to communicating significant policy changes clearly and giving employees reasonable notice of changes that affect their everyday AI use. Your responsibility is to engage with those communications and to act on them.

### Three commitments to take into your work

Before you complete the course, identify three specific commitments to take forward: one about how you will use AI, one about how you will evaluate what it produces, and one about how you will maintain your ethical and legal awareness as AI continues to evolve in your workplace.

The habits you build now, when the learning is fresh, are the ones most likely to persist. The professionals who get the most durable value from this course are not those who scored highest on the assessment. They are those who leave with two or three concrete habits they consistently apply.

### How to stay current

AI is changing quickly. The tools available today will be different in twelve months. The regulatory framework is still developing. Your organisation's policy will be updated as it learns from experience.

Staying current does not require constant attention to every development. It requires a sustainable approach:

- Follow one or two trusted sources relevant to AI in your industry
- When your organisation communicates AI policy updates, engage with them
- Revisit your prompt library and personal use map periodically to check whether your practices still reflect the best available approach
- When a new tool is introduced, apply the orientation framework from Task 2.1.3 before using it for substantive work
- When you are uncertain whether a use case is appropriate, ask before acting

The professionals who navigate AI most successfully over time are not those who know the most at any given moment. They are those who maintain the habits of critical engagement, continuous learning, and professional accountability that this course has been building toward.
