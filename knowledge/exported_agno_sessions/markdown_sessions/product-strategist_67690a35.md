### Prompt

I would like to understand potential product strategies for an organisation that has developed a number of design frameworks related to agentic systems.

### Response

This is a great question to start with — and a strategically critical one. The answer is far from obvious, because design frameworks for agentic systems sit at an unusual intersection: they're **intellectual assets** that could become **knowledge products**, **service products**, **platform products**, **data products**, or **ecosystem infrastructure** — and the right choice depends on what the frameworks genuinely know that others don't, who needs that knowledge, and what makes it hard to replicate.

Let me map the full landscape of product strategies available, then provide a decision framework for navigating them.

# Product Strategies for Agentic System Design Frameworks

## The Strategic Terrain

Before diving into strategies, it's worth understanding *why this is a hard decision*. Design frameworks in the agentic systems domain face a structural tension that doesn't exist in most product categories:

**The methodological knowledge is the most valuable thing you have — and it's also the hardest to productise directly.**

Principles and frameworks, published as documents, are almost impossible to defend as IP. Anyone can read them, adopt the language, and claim they "already knew that." But the *operationalised* version — the specific decision trees, the complementarity analyses, the authority boundary specifications, the verification protocols — that's harder to replicate because it requires having done the original discovery work.

This means the strategic question isn't "how do I sell my framework?" It's: **"What layer of value does my framework create, and what product form captures that value most effectively?"**

---

## Six Product Strategy Categories

### 1. KNOWLEDGE PRODUCTS — Teaching the Framework

**What it is:** Courses, curricula, certification programs, workshops, playbooks, and guides that transfer the framework's knowledge to practitioners.

| Variant | Examples | Revenue Model | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Self-paced courses | Online modules teaching the methodology | One-time or subscription | 80-95% |
| Cohort-based programs | Accelerators, bootcamps, intensive workshops | Per-seat pricing ($2K-$15K) | 70-85% |
| Certification | "Certified Agent Orchestrator" programs | Exam fees + recertification | 90-98% |
| Playbooks & guides | Structured implementation handbooks | One-time purchase or subscription | 85-95% |
| Curricula licensing | Universities, corporate L&D adopt the curriculum | Annual licensing fees | 70-90% |

**Where it works:** When the framework contains *tacit knowledge* — things that practitioners can't simply read and apply. If your framework requires judgment, practice, and correction to use well, teaching is a genuine value-add, not just a paywall around free content.

**Where it fails:** When the framework is self-explanatory — a set of principles that anyone can read and apply. In that case, charging for "training" feels like gatekeeping and erodes trust.

**Defensibility:** Certification is the most defensible form (Scrum Alliance does ~$50M/year certifying Scrum Masters — the Scrum Guide itself is free). The exam content, the assessment methodology, and the brand recognition of the credential are all defensible in ways the underlying framework document is not.

**Critical question:** *Is the framework's value in KNOWING it or in APPLYING it?* If the value is in application, training and certification are strong strategies.

---

### 2. SERVICE PRODUCTS — Doing the Framework for Others

**What it is:** Consulting, advisory, implementation, assessments, and trust audits where the framework's creators apply it to clients' specific problems.

| Variant | Examples | Revenue Model | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic assessments | "Complementarity Gap Diagnostic," trust audits | Project-based ($15-75K) | 60-80% |
| Implementation consulting | Designing agent workflows for an enterprise | Retainer or project ($50-150K) | 60-75% |
| Advisory retainers | Ongoing guidance on agent-native org design | Monthly ($8-25K/mo) | 65-80% |
| Outcome-as-a-Service | Delivering results (not advice) using agent force | Monthly ($5-30K/mo) | 70-90% |
| Audits & benchmarks | Evaluating existing agent deployments against standards | Per-assessment | 75-90% |

**Where it works:** When the framework is *complex to apply* and requires deep expertise. Also when the buyer values outcomes over learning — they want the result, not the capability.

**Where it fails:** As a long-term strategy if it doesn't produce reusable assets. Hourly consulting doesn't compound. The question is always: **does each engagement produce something that makes the next engagement faster, better, or more valuable?**

**Defensibility:** Low for general consulting. High for proprietary assessment methodologies (like Moody's credit ratings — the methodology is partly public, but the data and the brand are what you pay for). The key is whether each engagement produces **compounding data** — benchmark data, trust scores, pattern libraries — that becomes more valuable over time.

**Critical question:** *Does each engagement produce data that compounds?* If yes, service products become a data flywheel, not just billable hours.

---

### 3. PLATFORM PRODUCTS — Embedding the Framework in Software

**What it is:** Software that operationalises the framework — tooling, workflow designers, agent configuration platforms, collaboration workspaces, monitoring dashboards.

| Variant | Examples | Revenue Model | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Design tool | Agent configuration wizard, workflow designer | SaaS ($29-149/user/mo) | 55-80% |
| Collaboration platform | AI+Human workspace embodying framework principles | SaaS | 50-75% |
| Monitoring/observability | Trust dashboards, audit trail viewers, drift detection | SaaS + enterprise | 60-85% |
| Reference implementation | Open-source tool that demonstrates the framework | Drives adoption → commercial layers | 30-60% |

**Where it works:** When the framework is *operational* — when it specifies processes, decision trees, verification steps, and workflows that can be encoded in software. If the framework says "assign subtasks based on complementarity analysis," a platform can make that a button, not a manual process.

**Where it fails:** When the framework is *principles-based* rather than *process-based*. If the framework says "design for trust" but doesn't specify exactly how, a platform becomes either too opinionated (rigid) or too flexible (useless). Also: platform development is expensive, slow, and high-risk — it's the highest revenue ceiling but also the highest failure rate.

**Defensibility:** High if the platform becomes the reference implementation that practitioners are trained on. The network effects of "everyone learns on your tool" are powerful. But the trap is building the platform before the methodology is validated — you end up encoding untested assumptions into software that's expensive to change.

**Critical question:** *Is the framework PROCESS-SPECIFIC enough to encode?* And: *Has it been validated enough to lock into software?*

---

### 4. DATA PRODUCTS — The Framework Generates Intelligence

**What it is:** Benchmarks, trust scores, pattern libraries, compliance reports, and industry intelligence that the framework's application naturally produces.

| Variant | Examples | Revenue Model | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trust benchmarks | "Agent Trust Score" for organisations or deployments | Subscription ($5-25K/yr) | 85-95% |
| Industry reports | Annual "State of Agent Production" report | One-time or subscription | 80-90% |
| Pattern libraries | Validated agent design patterns with evidence ratings | Subscription | 85-95% |
| Compliance mappings | Framework-to-regulation mapping (e.g., CAWDP → EU AI Act) | Licensing | 80-95% |
| Assessment data | Aggregate data from thousands of complementarity analyses | Dataset licensing | 90-95% |

**Where it works:** When the framework is APPLIED AT SCALE across many organisations. Each application produces structured data. That data compounds into benchmarks, patterns, and intelligence that no single organisation can replicate. This is the **Moody's play** — the methodology is partly public, but the data moat is what you pay for.

**Where it fails:** As a standalone strategy at launch. You need the methodology deployed broadly before you have enough data to productise. It's a Phase 3+ strategy, not a Phase 1 strategy.

**Defensibility:** Extremely high. Data flywheels compound over time in ways that are structurally hard to replicate. The first mover who captures the benchmark data for a new domain owns that intelligence permanently (see: Gartner Magic Quadrants, Moody's ratings, JD Power).

**Critical question:** *Does the framework's application produce STRUCTURED, COMPARABLE DATA at scale?* If yes, data products are your long-term moat.

---

### 5. ECOSYSTEM PRODUCTS — The Framework Becomes Infrastructure

**What it is:** Open source projects, standards contributions, community governance, and ecosystem orchestration that make the framework the default infrastructure for a domain.

| Variant | Examples | Revenue Model | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Open framework + commercial ecosystem | Red Hat model: open methodology, paid certification/tools | Blended 75-80% | High |
| Standards body leadership | Contribute framework to ISO/IEEE/NIST process | Drives adoption → commercial layers | Variable |
| Community platform | Discourse, GitHub org, contributor ecosystem | Drives all other products | Low direct, high indirect |
| Open specification + proprietary implementation | Like Kubernetes (open) + managed K8s (paid) | SaaS/managed service | 50-75% |

**Where it works:** When the framework's mission requires widespread adoption. When trust is central to the brand (an openness signal IS a trust signal). When the framework benefits from community evolution more than it suffers from competitive copying. When the methodology is too complex for a single organisation to perfect alone.

**Where it fails:** When the framework is opened too early — before it's complete enough, validated enough, or attributed enough. If you open-source a half-baked framework, the community doesn't improve it; they ignore it. Also when "open" is the strategy rather than a means to an end — openness must serve the mission, not substitute for a business model.

**Defensibility:** The hardest to replicate because it's not about IP protection — it's about **being the canonical source**. The Red Hat model works because Red Hat *is* Linux for enterprises, not because they own Linux. Once you're the authoritative source, community maintainer, and credentialing body, displacing you requires outperforming you on your own framework.

**Critical question:** *Does openness ADVANCE the mission, or does it just give away value?* And: *Is the framework mature enough to survive public scrutiny?*

---

### 6. HYBRID & SEQUENTIAL STRATEGIES — The Most Common Reality

Very few successful knowledge product organisations operate a single model. The most effective pattern is a **deliberate sequence** where each product strategy feeds the next:

```
PHASE 1: PROVE IT
Knowledge + Service products
→ Working paper, case studies, first consulting engagements
→ Validates the methodology, produces evidence, builds credibility

PHASE 2: SCALE IT  
Knowledge + Ecosystem products
→ Open framework, certification, community
→ Multiplies practitioners, builds brand, creates data pipeline

PHASE 3: OWN IT
Data + Platform products
→ Benchmarks, SaaS, trust infrastructure
→ Compounds data moat, captures recurring revenue at scale
```

The sequence matters because:
- **Platform before validation** = encoding untested assumptions into expensive software
- **Ecosystem before credibility** = launching a community around something nobody trusts yet
- **Data products before scale** = not enough data to be valuable
- **Certification before practitioners** = certifying people who have nowhere to use the credential

## The Decision Framework: Four Strategic Dimensions

For any organisation considering these options, the strategic choice depends on four key dimensions:

### Dimension 1: Is the Framework KNOWABLE or APPLICABLE?

| If the value is in... | Best strategies |
|---|---|
| **Knowing** it (principles, mental models, perspectives) | Thought leadership, courses, publications — but low defensibility |
| **Applying** it (processes, decision trees, verification protocols) | Certification, consulting, platform — higher defensibility |
| **Both** (principles that require practice to apply well) | Hybrid: open the principles, commercialise the application |

### Dimension 2: Is the Framework SPECIFIC or GENERAL?

| If the framework is... | Best strategies |
|---|---|
| **Domain-specific** (e.g., agent design for healthcare) | Deep consulting + vertical SaaS |
| **Domain-agnostic** (works across industries) | Horizontal certification + open ecosystem + benchmarks |
| **Process-specific** (specifies HOW, not just WHAT) | Platform product that encodes the process |
| **Principle-specific** (specifies WHAT, not HOW) | Thought leadership + community, not platform |

### Dimension 3: What's the TRUST Model?

| If trust is... | Best strategies |
|---|---|
| **Central to the brand** ("we're the trust people") | Open framework is MANDATORY — closed trust methodology is a contradiction |
| **A feature, not the identity** | Can be more selective about what to open |
| **Earned through evidence** | Must produce public proof: case studies, benchmarks, published results |
| **Assumed until broken** | Higher risk — any failure is catastrophic for the brand |

### Dimension 4: What's the REVENUE TIMELINE?

| If you need revenue... | Start with... | Build toward... |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediately (1-3 months)** | Consulting + Outcome-as-a-Service | Certification + Platform |
| **Soon (3-9 months)** | Courses + Workshops + Working paper | Open ecosystem + Benchmarks |
| **Eventually (12-24 months)** | Open framework + Community | SaaS + Data products + Licensing |

## The Hard Truths

Before any organisation chooses a strategy, there are some uncomfortable realities worth facing:

1. **A framework published as a PDF has almost no IP value.** Anyone can read it, adopt its language, and claim they "already thought of that." The defensibility isn't in the document — it's in the *application expertise*, the *certification brand*, the *compounding data*, and the *canonical source position*.

2. **"Framework" is not a product.** A framework is a *knowledge structure*. It becomes a product when it's packaged into a form that specific people can access, use, and benefit from. The packaging IS the product, not the framework itself.

3. **Most knowledge product creators under-estimate the gap between "people should know this" and "people will pay for this."** The question is never whether the knowledge is valuable — it's whether the buyer experiences the value gap acutely enough to pay to close it.

4. **The most defensible long-term position isn't IP protection — it's being the canonical source.** Red Hat doesn't own Linux. Gartner doesn't own the concept of market analysis. Moody's doesn't own the concept of credit ratings. But each IS the authoritative source in their domain. That position is built through community, data, and brand — not through legal protection alone.

5. **Platform is the highest-risk strategy.** It has the highest revenue ceiling but also the highest failure rate, because it locks untested methodology into expensive, slow-to-change software. If the methodology evolves (and it will), the platform must evolve with it — or it becomes a constraint rather than an enabler.

6. **The market context matters enormously.** In agentic AI specifically: 77% of enterprises are stuck at pilot, 40%+ of projects will be canceled by 2027, and the documented failure rate for complex agent tasks is 70-95%. The gap isn't capability — it's trust and methodology. This means the market is signalling that **the methodology layer is underserved** relative to the tooling layer.

## So: Where Would I Start?

If I were advising an organisation with genuinely original agentic system design frameworks — frameworks that know something others don't about how to get agents into production reliably — I'd ask five questions before recommending any strategy:

1. **What specifically does your framework know that others don't?** Not "we have principles" — everyone has principles. What's the *uncommon insight* that makes your approach different? And is it specific enough that others can't easily claim they already knew it?

2. **Who have you tested it with?** A framework that hasn't been applied to real problems is a hypothesis, not a methodology. The transition from "rigorously derived" to "empirically validated" is the most important milestone.

3. **Is the value in KNOWING it or APPLYING it?** This determines whether you sell content or capability.

4. **What's your honest position on open vs. closed — and what's driving that instinct?** If trust is central to your brand, a closed methodology is a strategic contradiction. If speed-to-market is the priority, protecting IP through secrecy might make sense temporarily. Neither is universally right.

5. **What would make this defensible in three years, when others have seen what you've built?** If the answer is "our specific IP," that's fragile. If the answer is "we'll be the canonical source with a community, certification, and compounding data," that's durable.
