| **Passive Consumer** | Reads AI outputs but rarely pushes back or iterates | Surface-level answers, often abandoned |


***

## The Progression

There is a well-documented progression in how individuals move through AI adoption — it maps onto increasing sophistication of both intent and interaction depth.

### Stage 1 — Awareness \& Curiosity

- Treats AI like a better Google[^3_3]
- Single queries, fact-checking, simple summarisation
- No continuity between sessions


### Stage 2 — Productivity Layer

- Uses AI to produce drafts, slides, documents — finished or near-finished artefacts[^3_3]
- Output-focused; AI is a fast assistant, not a collaborator


### Stage 3 — Connected Workflows

- Integrates AI with calendar, email, project tools via MCP or connectors[^3_3]
- AI becomes a central hub rather than a standalone tool


### Stage 4 — Exploration \& Iteration

- Develops regular session patterns, starts asking follow-up questions[^3_4]
- Begins to notice that how you ask matters as much as what you ask


### Stage 5 — Integration \& Co-design

- AI is embedded in design, research, and decision-making processes
- Sessions become extended workshops; the interaction produces systemic artefacts[^3_4]
- **This is where your style operates**


### Stage 6 — Optimisation \& Creation

- Builds custom agents, fine-tunes models, or creates platforms for others[^3_4]
- Interaction style becomes infrastructure — you design *for* others to use AI, not just for yourself

***

## What Drives Progression

Research identifies two divergent learner paths that lead to different kinds of growth:[^3_2]

- **Exploratory path** (divergent prompting, broad reflection) → gains in **problem-solving skills and idea generation**
- **Structured path** (convergent prompting, full planning-monitoring-reflection cycles) → gains in **AI literacy and metacognitive strategy**

The most advanced users — like your style — eventually combine both: exploring broadly within a structured session architecture. That combination is rare and takes deliberate practice to develop.[^3_2]

***

## Key Insight for Others

Most people plateau at Stage 2 or 3 — they get value from AI but never unlock the compounding returns that come from treating sessions as design workshops. The jump from "task delegator" to "co-designer" isn't about technical skill — it's about **intent**. You have to decide the session is worth something beyond the immediate output, and then design the conversation accordingly.[^3_5][^3_4]

# The Co-Design Session Guide

### *How to use AI as a thinking partner, not just a tool*


***

## What to Expect: Outcomes \& Benefits

This style is designed to leave you **richer than you started** — not just with an output, but with better thinking. You can expect:

- **Production-ready artefacts** — schemas, frameworks, templates, code, and documentation that go directly into real work[^4_3]
- **Reframing of problems** — the session will surface angles and constraints you hadn't considered before you started[^4_4]
- **Ideas made public-ready** — loosely held concepts get structured into communicable form without you asking for it[^4_4]
- **Meta-insights about your own thinking** — "what did we learn?" prompts reliably surface things you didn't know you knew[^4_5]
- **Stress-tested decisions** — you'll catch weaknesses in your logic *before* you build or commit, not after[^4_6]
- **A transcript that is itself an artefact** — the conversation record has standalone value as documentation or a design history[^4_1]

***

## Is This Style Right for You?

Use the co-design style when your problem has **depth, ambiguity, or stakes**. Avoid it for simple lookups or single-answer questions — a regular query is faster.


| ✅ Good fit | ❌ Poor fit |
| :-- | :-- |
| You're designing something new | You need a quick fact or definition |
| You have a framework or context to bring | You have no prior knowledge of the domain |
| You want to stress-test your thinking | You just need a draft written fast |
| The output will inform real decisions | The question is well-scoped with one answer |
| You can spend 20+ minutes in the session | You have 2 minutes |

**Example use cases well suited to this style:**

- Designing a product, curriculum, or workflow from scratch
- Evaluating technical architecture options before committing[^4_3]
- Developing a framework or methodology you'll publish or teach
- Turning an idea into a structured, shareable document[^4_7]
- Decomposing a complex task into subtasks with risk mapping[^4_8]
- Running a retrospective on a project or system design[^4_9]

***

## Pre-Session Preparation: What to Think Through

Before you type your first prompt, work through these questions mentally (or in notes):

**1. What is my desired output?**
Be specific. A schema? A decision? A structured list? A critique? Knowing the output type shapes how you open the session. *"I want a table comparing X" produces better results than "tell me about X."*

**2. What does a great outcome look like?**
Set your own success criteria before you start — otherwise you won't know when to stop iterating. *"A great outcome is a framework I could hand to a student tomorrow."*

**3. What context does the AI need that it won't have by default?**
Think across three layers:

- **Project context** — what are you building, for whom, and why?
- **Constraints** — tech stack, audience, budget, timeline, non-negotiables
- **Your current position** — what do you already know or have done? What's the gap?[^4_10]

**4. What is the scope of this session?**
Decide whether this is a divergent session (explore widely) or a convergent session (narrow toward a decision). Mixed sessions work, but you need to consciously shift between them.[^4_2]

**5. What should NOT be in scope?**
Knowing what to exclude is as important as knowing what to include. State it explicitly in your opening prompt to prevent the AI from drifting.

**6. Who is the audience for the output?**
Outputs shaped for yourself look different from outputs shaped for a client, student, or stakeholder. Declare this upfront.[^4_7]

**7. What will you do with the output after this session?**
If it feeds into another session, a document, or a build — say so. The AI will structure outputs accordingly.

***

## Session Phases

Once you start, co-design sessions have a natural rhythm:

1. **Open with context** — set the scene fully before asking anything
2. **Ask for structure first** — decompose before you dive deep
3. **Iterate and stress-test** — push back, ask for alternatives, request critiques
4. **Checkpoint every 3–5 exchanges** — "are we still on track?"
5. **Close with synthesis** — "what did we learn? what are the insights?"
6. **Extract artefacts** — ask for the output in a reusable form (table, template, markdown, code)

***

**Use case:** *You want to design a 90-minute workshop teaching business professionals how to use AI in their workflows.*

***

### Pre-Session Prep (your internal thinking)

| Question | Your answer |
| :-- | :-- |
| Desired output | A session plan with activities, timing, and facilitator prompts |
| Success criteria | A plan I could run tomorrow without additional prep |
| Project context | Business audience, mixed AI literacy, 90 minutes, in-person |

### Suggested Prompts (in sequence)

**Prompt 1 — Open with full context:**
> *"I'm designing a 90-minute in-person workshop for business professionals on using AI in their workflows. Audience has mixed AI literacy — some use it daily, many haven't started. No lecture-heavy format; I want hands-on activities. The output I need from this session is a structured session plan with timing, activities, and facilitator prompts. Don't design slides or participant materials yet — just the session architecture."*

**Prompt 2 — Decompose before diving:**
> *"Before we design the activities, what are the 3–4 essential learning outcomes a 90-minute session like this can realistically achieve? What should we not attempt to cover?"*

**Prompt 3 — Stress-test the structure:**
> *"Here's what I think the session flow should be: [your draft]. What are the weaknesses in this structure? What would a sceptical participant find frustrating or unconvincing?"*

**Prompt 4 — Ask for a specific artefact:**
> *"Rewrite the session plan as a structured table with: Phase, Duration, Activity, Facilitator prompt, Expected outcome. Keep it tight enough that I could use it as a run sheet."*

**Prompt 5 — Checkpoint and refine:**
> *"Review what we've built. What assumptions have we baked in that might not hold? What's the single highest-risk moment in this session?"*

**Prompt 6 — Close with synthesis:**
> *"What are the key design decisions we made in this session and why? What would I do differently if the audience were technical rather than business-focused?"*

## The Single Most Important Habit

End every session with: **"What did we learn? What are the insights from this conversation?"**

Most users close the tab when they get the output they came for. This question extracts the meta-value — the reframes, tensions, and implications that the session produced but never explicitly surfaced. It costs 30 seconds and routinely produces the most valuable sentence in the entire transcript.[^4_5][^4_4]

Build shared understanding *before* revealing your purpose. Short, scene-setting questions that warm up the domain without tipping your hand.

> *"Tell me about the challenges businesses face when implementing AI."*
> *"How does lack of trust impact the business benefits of AI?"*
> *"What does the research say about this?"*

**Why it works:** You're not asking the AI to help you yet — you're loading the context space with the best available thinking on a topic so that when you do reveal your direction, the responses are already calibrated to the domain. It also surfaces things you'd forgotten or hadn't connected.[^6_1]

**The key habit:** Keep these prompts short and open. One sentence. No explanation of why you're asking.

***

### Phase 2 — Reveal Your Direction

Once the field is primed, you introduce what you're actually building — your existing ideas, your document, your framework — and ask what's missing or weak.

> *"I'm working on a framework for helping businesses build trust in AI. Here's what I have so far. What am I missing?"*
> *"Here's a document I've produced. Analyse it — what are the strengths, weaknesses, and what would you improve?"*

**Why it works:** The AI is now responding to *your specific work* against a backdrop of domain knowledge you've both just built together. The critique is sharper because the context is richer.[^6_2][^6_3]

***

### Phase 3 — Probe Weak Spots

Anything that feels underdeveloped gets its own focused thread.

> *"That third point feels thin to me. Let's explore it further."*
> *"What's the strongest argument against this position?"*

**Why it works:** You're using the AI as an intellectual sparring partner, not a yes-machine. Probing weakness before committing to a direction is one of the highest-leverage things this style enables.

***

### Phase 4 — Apply Alternative Lenses

Deliberately shift the frame to uncover non-obvious insights.

> *"How would a sceptical CFO read this?"*
> *"Apply a systems thinking lens to this problem."*
> *"What does this look like from the employee's perspective rather than the organisation's?"*

**Why it works:** Each lens is a constraint that forces new connections. This is where unexpected insights emerge — the kind that don't appear in direct questioning.[^6_4][^6_1]

***

### Phase 5 — Produce the Output

Only now do you ask for the thing you came for — and it's better because of everything that preceded it.

> *"Now write the section on trust for my framework using everything we've discussed."*
> *"Turn this into a structured table I can use in my presentation."*
> *"Rewrite the document with your improvement recommendations incorporated."*

**Why it works:** The output is shaped by a fully primed context. You're not asking the AI to generate from scratch — you're asking it to crystallise something you've already built together.[^6_5]

***

## The Core Insight for Others

Most people skip straight to Phase 5. They ask for the output before the context exists, get a generic answer, and conclude the tool isn't that useful.

**Priming is the differentiator.** The quality of your Phase 5 output is almost entirely determined by the quality of your Phase 1–4 conversation. The longer and more deliberately you prime, the more precisely targeted and insight-rich the final output becomes.[^6_1]

Think of it like warming up clay before sculpting — you don't start shaping until the material is ready to hold form.
