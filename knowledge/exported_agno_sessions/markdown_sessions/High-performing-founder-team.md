# I want to design a partnership agreement with Gagan that sets up a strong foundation for working together successfully.

What are the characteristics of a high performing founder team that places the health and wellbeing of both as the priority?
What do they do to make it successful?
How do they communicate?
What do they know about each other?
How do they support each other?
How do they motivate each other? etc

A high-performing founder team that truly prioritises both people’s health and wellbeing is deliberate about how they design the relationship, not just the company. Below is a practical blueprint you and Gagan can use as inputs to your partnership agreement.

***

## 1. Core characteristics of this kind of founder team

These teams treat wellbeing as infrastructure for performance, not a “nice to have”.[^1][^2][^3]

- They explicitly agree that *the relationship and health come before the startup*, and are willing to slow down or change direction to protect that.
- They build **psychological** safety: both can admit mistakes, say “I’m not okay”, and share half-baked ideas without fear of ridicule or punishment.[^4][^5]
- They design sustainable ways of working (paced sprints, recovery time, realistic expectations) instead of glorifying overwork.[^3][^6][^1]
- They play to complementary strengths and are honest about weaknesses, so neither is constantly operating in de‑energising territory.[^7][^8]
- They see mental health as “always on” maintenance (sleep, therapy, coaching, exercise, reflection) instead of crisis response.[^6][^9][^3]
- They invest in trust as a *practice*: reliability, keeping promises, fair decision-making, transparent communication.[^5][^10][^4]

You can hard-code many of these into the agreement: for example, a clause that either founder can call a “yellow flag” on wellbeing that triggers a structured conversation and temporary scope reduction.

***

## 2. What they *do* to make it successful

Think of this as your operating system: recurring behaviours and rituals that protect both the business and the relationship.

- Co-founder health check-ins.
    - Schedule a monthly 60–90 minute “relationship retro” that is *only* about the partnership, not product or metrics.[^11]
    - Prompts: “What gave you energy this month?”, “Where did you feel friction with me?”, “How supported did you feel from 1–10?”.
- Clear role and decision boundaries.
    - Write down who is DRI (directly responsible individual) for each domain: product, tech, capital, hiring, partnerships, etc.[^12]
    - Agree decision rules: what each can decide alone, what requires consent, and how to break ties. This reduces chronic stress and conflict.
- Wellbeing guardrails and norms.
    - Define default work hours, “no meeting” blocks, and off-limits times (e.g., no non-urgent comms after 7pm or on Sundays).
    - Pre-agree rules for fundraising or crunch periods (temporary exceptions, how long they can last, how you recover afterwards).[^1][^3]
- Personal support systems, not just each other.
    - Encourage and normalise therapy, coaching, peer founder groups, or mentoring; founders who proactively manage mental health report better performance and growth.[^9][^6]
    - Make it acceptable (even expected) to use these supports during tough phases.
- Purpose and values grounding.
    - Revisit shared purpose and values every 6–12 months; high-performing teams with clear meaning and aligned values are more resilient and engaged.[^4][^3][^5]
    - Use these values when making big calls (e.g., “Does taking this deal support or erode our wellbeing and integrity?”).
- Proactive conflict practices.
    - Use structured conflict tools: e.g., “I feel / I need” statements; agreeing to pause and return if either is flooded.[^11]
    - Treat conflict as a design bug in the system, not a character flaw in the other person.

***

## 3. How they communicate

Healthy co-founders are boringly explicit about how they communicate, and they practice it before there’s a big blow-up.[^10][^11]

- Communication playbook.
    - Agree: preferred channels for what (urgent = phone, decisions = email/Notion, async updates = Slack), expected response times, and topics that must never be handled by text.
    - Document this and revisit every few months as the company evolves.[^11]
- Regular cadences with different purposes.
    - Daily or twice-weekly 15–20 min standup: priorities, blockers, quick emotional check-in.
    - Weekly 60–90 min founder meeting: strategy, key decisions, bigger tensions.
    - Monthly “relationship retro” as above, to talk about *how we are*, not just *what we’re doing*.[^11]
- Psychological safety in action.
    - Normalise sharing uncertainty, e.g., “I’m at 5/10 energy today, I might need more time to decide.”[^5][^4]
    - Celebrate the other owning mistakes: “Thanks for flagging that early, that helps us both.” This reduces shame and burnout risk.
- Clarity and directness with care.
    - Use direct language about impact: “When X happens, I feel Y, and I need Z,” instead of passive-aggressive hints.[^11]
    - Set a shared intention before difficult conversations: “Our goal is to protect both our relationship and the company.”

***

## 4. What they know about each other

You already have a head start: your strengths profiles tell you a lot about how each of you likes to work.[^8][^7]

- Strengths, drains, and growth edges.
    - You: Futuristic, Strategic, Activator, Ideation, Self-Assurance, plus realised strengths like Unconditionality, Strategic Awareness, and Mission mean you’re naturally a visionary, pattern-spotter, and catalyst who draws energy from future-oriented thinking and big-picture connection.[^7]
    - Gagan: Incubator, Implementer, and Technology Focus as realised strengths suggest a reflective, practical, tech-oriented builder who enjoys turning ideas into efficient, real-world solutions.[^8]
    - Together: you’re a “vision + translation” pair; that’s powerful if the agreement protects both from being over-stretched into their weaker zones.
- Stress signatures and early warning signs.
    - Each of you should explicitly list: “When I’m starting to struggle, you’ll notice…”, “What helps me then”, “What *doesn’t* help.”
    - Teams that notice early and act before crisis are more likely to keep both health and performance high.[^6][^9]
- Needs, boundaries, and non-negotiables.
    - Energy patterns (morning vs evening person, deep work preferences), family responsibilities, health conditions, and absolute limits (e.g., minimum sleep, max travel).
    - Lines you won’t cross for success (e.g., “I will not sacrifice therapy and exercise; we may accept slower growth instead”).
- Motivators and meaning.
    - Why each of you is doing this (money, impact, craft mastery, autonomy, legacy).
    - How each likes to be recognised: private appreciation vs public praise, opportunities vs gifts. Meaningful recognition fuels motivation and resilience.[^13][^3]
- Conflict styles and attachment histories (optional but powerful).
    - How you each tended to react in past conflicts (withdraw, attack, appease, over-intellectualise).
    - Agreements like “If I withdraw, I’ll say I need 30 minutes and then come back at X time.”

These can all be turned into a short “Founder User Manual” for each of you, appended to the agreement.

***

## 5. How they support and motivate each other

Support and motivation in a high-performing, wellbeing-first team are quiet, consistent, and embedded into how you work.

- Normalising care and vulnerability.
    - Make “How are you really?” a legitimate business question; leaders who prioritise wellbeing foster trust, resilience, and mutual respect.[^3][^9][^4]
    - Treat taking a rest day or therapy session as a *win* for the company, not a personal failure.[^3][^6]
- Sharing the load intelligently.
    - Adjust responsibilities when one is under more stress: e.g., if Gagan is deep in implementation, you might shield them from external demands, and vice versa.[^7][^8]
    - Be explicit: “I can carry more of X for the next two weeks if you can carry more of Y.”
- Micro-motivations and rituals.
    - Weekly “wins review”: each shares 2–3 things they’re proud of about themselves and the other; this builds positive affect and motivation.[^4][^3]
    - Couple progress celebration with rest (e.g., after a big milestone, schedule a half-day off or shared non-work activity).
- Shared purpose as motivation.
    - Regularly connect mundane tasks back to your mission—especially important for someone high in Futuristic and Mission like you, and Legacy for Gagan.[^8][^7][^3]
    - When either is demotivated, revisit: “How does this tie back to the world we want to help create?”
- Encouraging growth, not just output.
    - Allocate budget/time for both to pursue learning that nourishes them (courses, conferences, coaching). High-performing teams that invest in emotional intelligence and purpose alignment see improved collaboration and innovation.[^5][^3]
    - Frame feedback around growth: “I see you’re strong at X; if we refine Y a bit, it’ll reduce your stress and increase your impact.”

***

## 6. Turning this into a concrete partnership agreement

To make this real, your partnership agreement (and a lighter “Founder Charter” alongside it) can include:

- Shared principles section.
    - “We agree that the health and wellbeing of each founder, and the quality of our relationship, are foundational to the company’s success and cannot be traded away for short-term wins.”
- Roles, decision rights, and escalation.
    - Map Pete vs Gagan domains based on your strengths documents; note where you must co-decide.[^12][^7][^8]
    - Include a conflict resolution pathway: direct conversation → scheduled mediation-style session → trusted third-party coach if needed.[^10][^11]
- Wellbeing and time norms.
    - Default work patterns, availability, vacation policy for founders, “red flag” and “yellow flag” mechanisms (e.g., either can call one to trigger slowing or pausing certain work).
    - Agreement to proactive mental health maintenance (each chooses their mode: exercise, therapy, spiritual practice, etc.).[^6][^3]
- Communication cadences and expectations.
    - Explicit list of regular meetings and their purposes, your communication playbook, and how you’ll update it.[^10][^11]
- Founder User Manuals appendix.
    - 2–3 pages each covering strengths, drains, stress signals, boundaries, needs, best ways to give feedback, and motivators, drawing on your strengths profiles.[^7][^8]
