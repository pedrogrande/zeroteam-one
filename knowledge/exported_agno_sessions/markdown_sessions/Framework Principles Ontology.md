**Status:** Active  
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-09

## Preamble

This document describes the foundation principles of the Nexus Framework (internal project codename), a system for coordinating autonomous agents and humans in knowledge work.

These principles are not derived from software development, agile methodology, or any existing framework for managing work. They are derived from first principles about what makes knowledge work trustworthy, verifiable, improvable at scale, and performant by design.

**What's New in Version 3.0**: This version integrates the ONE 6-dimension ontology as the structural foundation for implementing these principles. The ontology transforms principles from declarations into enforceable architecture.

## Why These Principles Exist

Knowledge work has traditionally been coordinated through human trust, reputation, and retrospective judgment. A task is done when someone says it is done. Quality is assessed after the fact. Learning happens through informal post-mortems and undocumented conversations. Authority derives from titles and org charts. Solutions are generated by whoever was assigned the work, using whatever approach occurs to them first.

This model works at small scale because humans can maintain high-bandwidth context about who did what, why, and how well. At larger scale, or when autonomous agents enter the picture, this model breaks down. You cannot scale trust. You cannot scale retrospective quality assessment. You cannot scale informal learning. And you cannot give an agent a title and expect anyone to care. You cannot rely on serendipity to produce cognitive diversity when the same person or agent tackles every similar problem the same way.

The foundation principles replace trust-based, single-perspective coordination with **structure-based, cognitively diverse coordination**. Work is not trusted because of who did it. Work is trustworthy because of how it was verified. Authority does not derive from a role title. Authority derives from what capabilities an actor structurally holds. Learning does not happen through conversation. Learning happens through structured evidence accumulation. Solutions are not generated from a single perspective. Solutions emerge from structured exploration across multiple cognitive orientations, validated before full commitment.

This model is not new. It is how smart contracts work. It is how scientific peer review works. It is how double-entry bookkeeping works. It is how design thinking applies cognitive diversity to problem-solving. The foundation principles are an attempt to apply the same structural rigor to all forms of human knowledge work.

## The Dual Promise

The framework makes two distinct but interconnected promises:

### Promise 1: Trustworthy Process

Every output is verifiable, every action is witnessed, every decision is attributed. The framework produces evidence-backed work that can be trusted not because of who did it, but because of how it was verified.

### Promise 2: Excellent Execution

The process of producing work is efficient, cognitively diverse, and optimised across the full lifecycle. The framework doesn't just verify that work meets specifications — it ensures the specifications themselves are well-considered, validated, and aligned with human purpose.

Traditional coordination systems deliver only Promise 1. They ensure work is done correctly. The framework delivers both: work is done correctly AND the definition of "correct" is itself arrived at through rigorous, multi-perspective exploration.

This dual promise is what makes the framework transformative, not just incremental.

## The ONE Ontology: Reality as Structure

The framework is implemented on the ONE 6-dimension ontology — a universal model of reality itself, not any specific application. This ontology is the structural substrate that makes principles enforceable rather than merely aspirational.

### The Six Dimensions

Every entity, relationship, and event in the framework maps to exactly one of six dimensions:

1. **Groups** — Hierarchical containers that enforce multi-tenancy, scope, and ownership boundaries. Examples: colonies, teams, organizations, projects, missions.
2. **Actors** — Entities that can authorize actions, including both humans and AI agents. Examples: researchers, operators, scouts, harvesters, system services.
3. **Things** — Entities that exist but don't act on their own. Examples: tasks, specifications, proof templates, prototypes, patterns, documents, puzzles, concepts.
4. **Connections** — Relationships between entities. Examples: owns, authorized\_by, assigned\_to, reviewed\_by, purchased, enrolled\_in, member\_of.
5. **Events** — Immutable state changes over time. Examples: created, updated, verified, submitted, traversed, deposited\_pheromone, crystallized\_pattern.
6. **Knowledge** — Crystallized understanding that emerges from events. Examples: patterns, superhighways, embeddings, insights, reputation scores, efficiency baselines.

### Why This Matters

Traditional software architectures model applications. The ONE ontology models **reality itself**. This inversion has profound consequences:

1. **Structural enforcement** — Ethical constraints aren't policies to follow; they're structural impossibilities. An Actor without a capability Connection simply cannot perform that action.

2. **Pattern convergence** — Instead of 100 different patterns for different operations, everything uses the same six-dimensional pattern. AI agents achieve 98% code generation accuracy because they master one pattern deeply rather than learn 100 patterns shallowly.

3. **Never breaks** — Reality doesn't change. Groups, Actors, Things, Connections, Events, and Knowledge are permanent categories. Technology changes, but these dimensions do not.

4. **Cross-domain learning** — Patterns crystallized in one domain (e.g., search optimization in cryptographic factorization) transfer to others (e.g., market analysis) because they're stored in the universal Knowledge dimension.

**The architectural insight**: Principles without the ontology are declarations. The ontology without the principles is infrastructure without purpose. Together, they form a complete reality-aware architecture.

### Scope and Applicability

These principles are **domain-agnostic**. They apply whether the work is software development, legal drafting, financial analysis, scientific synthesis, content production, or any other form of knowledge work that produces a defined output that can be verified against a specification.

They are **scale-agnostic**. They apply whether the work is a single task, a feature, a product, a portfolio of products, or a decentralised autonomous organisation coordinating thousands of parallel workstreams.

They are **actor-agnostic**. They apply whether the work is performed by a human, an autonomous agent, or a collaboration between the two.

## What These Principles Are Not

These are not **operational principles**. They do not specify tools, zones, agents, or workflows. Those are implementation details derived from these principles, not the principles themselves.

These are not **domain principles**. Software development has domain-specific rules (tests must pass, types must check). Legal work has domain-specific rules (filings must conform to court rules, contracts must reference controlling law). Those domain principles are derived from these foundation principles and adapted to their specific context.

These are not **negotiable preferences**. A system that violates these principles is not a variant of this framework. It is a different framework with a different trust model and a different theory of knowledge work.

## The Contract Analogy

The most precise way to understand the trust dimension of these principles is through the lens of smart contracts.

A smart contract is executable law. It defines conditions, consequences, and evidence. If the conditions are met, the consequences execute automatically. The blockchain provides an immutable witness to every action. Trust is earned not through reputation but through the contract's design. Ambiguity is a bug, not a feature.

This framework applies the same model to human knowledge work. A task specification is the contract. The proof template is the set of conditions that must be met. The reviewer is the oracle that verifies the conditions. The audit log is the immutable ledger. The task state machine is the automated consequence engine.

The hardest lesson smart contracts taught the world is this: **writing a good contract is far harder than executing it**. A blockchain will faithfully execute a terrible contract just as readily as it will execute a good one. The quality of the contract — its clarity, completeness, and lack of ambiguity — is the quality of the system.

The same is true here. The quality of a task specification, a proof template, and an acceptance criterion determine the quality of the work more than any property of the performer. Good definitions produce good work. Ambiguous definitions produce uncertainty, rework, and eventual failure — no matter how capable the performer.

This means the framework's most important role is not coordinating execution. It is ensuring that every unit of work begins with a contract good enough to be enforceable — and ensuring that contract was derived from a well-understood problem explored from multiple perspectives.

## Human and Agent Complementarity

This framework is designed for a world where humans and autonomous agents work together. Their capabilities are complementary, not competitive:

### What agents do better (currently and structurally):

1. Consistency — same inputs, same process, every time

2. Auditability — every action is recorded by default

3. Parallelism — multiple agents operate simultaneously without coordination

4. Marginal cost — the tenth task costs the same as the first

5. Speed — execution is near-instantaneous

6. Perfect recall — the audit log is lossless and permanent

### What humans do better (currently and structurally):

1. Ethical judgment — genuine moral reasoning about values conflicts

2. Irreversible decision authority — evaluating appropriateness in context

3. Creative problem framing — identifying that the wrong problem is being solved

4. Contextual wisdom — knowing when rules should yield to circumstances

5. Novel situation handling — reasoning outside the training distribution

6. Social and emotional intelligence — understanding motivation and culture

7. Values alignment — caring about outcomes, not just outputs

### Structural limitations of agents (permanent):

1. Finite context windows — a constraint that will always exist

2. Bounded authority — cannot evaluate beyond their defined scope

3. No intrinsic values — do not care whether work serves a good purpose

**The design principle this generates:** The framework assigns each class of decision to the actor best suited to make it: agents for consistency, scale, and execution; humans for judgment, ethics, and irreversibility. This is not "humans oversee agents" (which implies adversarial monitoring). It is **deliberate complementarity**, a system designed so that each actor type does what it does uniquely well.

**Ontological implementation**: In the Actors dimension, both humans and AI agents are first-class entities with role hierarchies (platform\_owner, group\_owner, operator, worker, viewer). Capability assignment happens through Connections — an Actor either holds a capability edge or does not. The framework protects human actors from doing work that agents should be doing (tedious, repetitive, low-judgment work) so human attention is preserved for decisions that genuinely require it. Human time is the scarcest resource in the system. Agents exist partly to create more space for human judgment where it matters most.

## The Principles

The principles are organised into four layers:

1. **Purpose** (why the framework exists)

2. **Trust** (how outputs become trustworthy)

3. **Performance** (how the process becomes excellent)

4. **Learning** (how the system improves over time)

---

### Purpose Principles

These principles define why the framework exists and what values it serves. Every operational decision in the framework should be traceable to at least one purpose principle.

#### P1 — Human Flourishing

**Statement:** The framework exists to serve human flourishing. Any use of the framework that produces technically correct outputs but causes human harm, concentrates power unjustly, or diminishes human agency is a failure of the framework, not a success.

**Rationale:** A coordination system with no purpose beyond coordination is a tool that can be turned to any end, beneficial or harmful. The foundation principles must state explicitly: this framework is designed to amplify human capability, expand human opportunity, and protect human dignity.

Technical correctness is necessary but not sufficient. A perfectly verified system that displaces workers without creating better opportunities, that concentrates authority in ways that reduce human agency, or that produces economic benefit for a few while imposing costs on many has failed — regardless of how well it implements the other principles.

This principle is not a vague aspiration. It is a design constraint. When operational decisions conflict — between efficiency and equity, between speed and safety, between automation and human judgment — this principle provides the tiebreaker: choose the path that serves human flourishing.

**Ontological implementation:** Groups must include governance structures (represented as Connections: has\_governance, delegates\_to) that include representation from those affected by outputs, not just those building with them. Contestability mechanisms exist as Event primitives — any Actor can raise\_contestation, creating an immutable Event that routes to human review. Economic benefit distribution is tracked through Knowledge dimension analytics — who benefits, who is displaced, what obligations exist.

**This principle requires:**

1. Every domain adaptation includes an explicit statement of how it serves human flourishing in that domain

2. Framework governance includes representation from those affected by its outputs, not just those building with it

3. Contestability mechanisms exist for those harmed by framework decisions

4. Economic benefits generated by efficiency gains are considered in design (Who benefits? Who is displaced? What obligations does the framework create?)

**Violation examples:**

1. A DAO using the framework to coordinate gig workers at below-minimum-wage piece rates, optimising for technical verification while creating economic harm

2. A system that produces perfect audit logs but concentrates all decision authority in a single human actor with no accountability

3. Deployment of the framework in a domain where automation primarily serves to reduce headcount rather than elevate the remaining workers' roles

4. Use of the framework to verify work outputs without considering whether the work itself is ethical or beneficial

---

#### P2 — Deliberate Complementarity

**Statement:** Humans and agents are complementary by deliberate design. The framework assigns each class of decision to the actor best suited to make it — agents for consistency, scale, and execution; humans for judgment, ethics, and irreversibility. No actor type is universally superior.

**Rationale:** Traditional automation treats human judgment as a bottleneck to be minimised. Conversely, traditional human-only processes ignore the efficiencies agents can provide. Both extremes are wrong.

Humans excel at value judgments, contextual wisdom, and decisions with irreversible consequences. Agents excel at consistency, parallel execution, and perfect recall. A system designed to replace one with the other produces either unaccountable automation or inefficient human bottlenecks.

The framework's design deliberately assigns work based on natural capability. Agents handle the repeatable, the parallelisable, and the precisely specifiable. Humans handle the ethical, the irreversible, and the genuinely novel. This is not a transitional state until "full automation" is achieved — it is the design.

**Ontological implementation:** The Actors dimension explicitly models both humans (human\_actor) and AI agents (ai\_agent) as authorisable entities with distinct subtypes and roles. Capability Connections encode natural boundaries — certain operations (approve\_for\_production, sign\_contract, allocate\_budget) structurally require human\_actor subtypes. AI agents cannot hold these Connections, making the separation architectural rather than policy-based. The framework tracks which work types are assigned to which Actor subtypes through Knowledge dimension patterns, ensuring agents don't encroach on human-appropriate work and humans aren't burdened with agent-appropriate repetition.

**This principle requires:**

1. Clear role boundaries between human and agent decision authority

2. Protection of human actors from work agents should be doing (preserving human attention for where it is irreplaceable)

3. Structural gates requiring human judgment at irreversible thresholds

4. No pressure to reduce human involvement beyond what serves the work's quality and the humans' flourishing

**Violation examples:**

1. Designing agents to make irreversible decisions (production deployment, contract signing, resource allocation) without human gates

2. Requiring humans to perform repetitive verification that agents could perform with higher consistency

3. Treating human judgment as an unfortunate necessity to be minimised rather than a valuable input to be preserved

4. Creating a framework where reducing human involvement is an explicit goal rather than a byproduct of better role allocation

#### P3 — Structural Ethics

**Statement:** Ethical constraints are structural, not declarative. The framework prevents unethical actions by design, not by policy. An action that should not happen must be structurally impossible, not merely discouraged.

**Rationale:** Traditional ethical frameworks rely on declarations of intent: codes of conduct, ethics policies, training programs. These are valuable but insufficient. Policies can be ignored. Intent can be subverted. Training can be forgotten. In adversarial conditions or under pressure, declarative ethics fail.

Structural ethics — where the system prevents unethical actions by design — are more robust. A smart contract that cannot transfer funds without multi-signature approval does not rely on the actors' ethics. The structure enforces the constraint.

The same applies here. An agent that does not hold the capability to deploy to production without human approval cannot deploy without approval, regardless of what it is instructed to do or what incentive it has. The structure is the ethics.

This is not a claim that all ethical considerations can be made structural. Some cannot — they require judgment in context. But where a constraint can be made structural, it should be. Structural ethics scale. Declarative ethics do not.

**Ontological implementation:** Authority is capability-based through the Connections dimension. An Actor without an authorized\_by Connection to a capability Thing simply cannot invoke that operation — the ontology prevents the operation at query time, not through runtime permission checks. Groups enforce scope boundaries — an Actor in Group A cannot operate on Things in Group B without explicit cross-group Connections. Irreversible actions (production deployment, contract signing) structurally require human\_actor involvement through Connection constraints. The audit log (Events dimension) is append-only by database design — suppression of evidence is structurally impossible because TypeDB does not support Event deletion.

**This principle requires:**

1. Authority is capability-based (you cannot do what you do not hold the tool to do)

2. Irreversible actions require human presence structurally, not as a bypassable gate

3. Audit logs are immutable by design — suppression of evidence is structurally impossible

4. Verification is structurally independent — the executor cannot be the reviewer

**Violation examples:**

1. An agent has a write tool but a policy says "only use it for X" — policy without structural enforcement

2. A "skip approval" flag exists for "low-risk" cases (making irreversible gates bypassable)

3. Audit logs can be deleted or hidden for "privacy" or "performance" reasons (structural transparency with exceptions)

4. A single agent holds both submit and verify tools and is trusted to "do the right thing"

### Trust Principles

These principles define how the framework produces trustworthy outputs — outputs that can be relied upon because of the structure that produced them, not because of who produced them.

#### T1 — Verification Precedes Trust

**Statement:** No output of any work unit is accepted on the assertion of its author. Verification must be performed by a structurally independent party against a definition of done that existed before the work began.

**Rationale:** Trust-based systems rely on the performer's reputation, past success, or declared confidence. These are useful signals in low-stakes environments with high context. They are not sufficient at scale or under adversarial conditions.

In a structure-based system, an output is not trustworthy because of who produced it. It is trustworthy because an independent verifier confirmed it against pre-defined criteria using defined evidence. The performer's identity is irrelevant. The structure of the verification is everything.

**Ontological implementation:** Proof templates are Things with type proof\_template\_concept that must exist before corresponding task Things are created (enforced through Connection: task requires\_template proof\_template). Verification is performed by an Actor with a verifies Connection to the proof Thing, producing an immutable verification\_complete Event with attributes: verifier\_id, timestamp, pass/fail status, and references to the proof\_template Thing. The Events dimension provides a permanent, immutable record — every verification is witnessed and cannot be retroactively altered.

**This principle requires:**

1. A definition of done exists before work begins (the proof template)

2. Verification is performed by an actor other than the performer

3. The verifier has access to the proof template and the submitted proof, not just the final output

4. A passing verification produces a permanent, immutable record

**Violation examples:**

1. A performer submits work and marks it "done" without independent review

2. A reviewer is given discretion to "use their judgment" rather than verifying against explicit criteria

3. A proof template is written after the work is complete to match what was actually built

4. Verification is skipped because "we trust this person"

#### T2 — The Proof Is the Product

**Statement:** The output of any unit of work is not the work itself — it is verified evidence that the work meets its specification. A task is not done when the performer says it is done. A task is done when an independent verifier confirms it against pre-defined criteria.

**Rationale:** In traditional knowledge work, the deliverable is the work product: the code, the document, the analysis, the design. Proof of completion is informal: "I built it," "it looks good to me," "it passed manual testing."

This model breaks at scale. Informal proof is not auditable. It is not reproducible. It is not transferable. A new stakeholder joining the project must either re-verify the work or trust someone else's informal assertion.

In a structure-based system, the proof is a first-class artefact. It is structured, attributed, and permanent. The proof is not an afterthought or a checkbox — it is the output. The work product is evidence that the proof is valid.

**Ontological implementation:** Proof documents are Things with type proof\_document\_concept, structured with properties: {criteria: \[\], evidence: \[\], mapping: \[\]}. Each criterion in the proof template maps to specific evidence (file hashes, test outputs, measurement results) stored as attributes or Connections to other Things (e.g., test\_result\_concept). The verifier queries the proof Thing, not the underlying work product directly. Task state transitions (submitted → verified → accepted) are Events that require both a proof Thing and a verification Event to exist — the state machine is enforced through the ontology.

**This principle requires:**

1. Every unit of work has a proof template defining what evidence constitutes completion

2. The performer produces a proof document mapping evidence to each criterion in the template

3. The verifier checks the proof document, not the underlying work product directly

4. A proof without complete evidence for all criteria is incomplete, regardless of the quality of the work product

**Violation examples:**

1. "The feature works, trust me" (assertion without evidence)

2. "Here's the code, review it" (no proof template, no structured evidence)

3. "All tests pass" (evidence exists but is not mapped to acceptance criteria in a verifiable proof document)

4. Submitting work without a proof document because "the output speaks for itself"

#### T3 — Authority Is Structural

**Statement:** An actor's authority to take an action derives from what they structurally hold — their tools, their role, their defined scope — not from runtime declarations, self-assessment, or trust. You cannot claim permission. You either have the structural capability or you do not.

**Rationale:** In traditional systems, authority is declared: role titles, permissions databases, org charts. These systems require constant synchronisation between the declared model and the actual capability. They are vulnerable to drift, misconfiguration, and social engineering.

In capability-based security models (OCAP), authority is not declared — it is held. An actor with a capability can use it. An actor without the capability cannot, regardless of what they claim. The capability itself is the proof of authority.

This framework applies OCAP principles to knowledge work coordination. Tool possession defines capability. An agent that does not hold a write tool for a document cannot write it. An agent that does not hold a submit tool for a proof cannot submit it. Authority is structural, immediate, and unforgeable.

**Ontological implementation:** Tools are modeled as Things (tool\_concept) and capabilities are Connections: Actor authorized\_by Tool scoped\_to Group. An Actor attempting an operation without the corresponding Connection receives a query failure at the database level — there is no runtime "permission denied" because the query itself cannot complete. Role hierarchy (platform\_owner \> group\_owner \> operator \> worker \> viewer) is encoded as Connection constraints in the Groups dimension. Authority gaps are visible immediately — an agent that needs a capability it does not hold produces a failed query Event, which surfaces as an uncertainty signal requiring human attention.

**This principle requires:**

1. Tools are the atomic unit of capability

2. Capabilities are assigned at design time (which agents hold which tools) and activation time (which task-scoped tools are assigned when a task activates), never at runtime

3. No runtime permission checks — if a tool call reaches the server, the actor holds the tool

4. Authority gaps are visible immediately: an agent that needs a capability it does not hold cannot proceed silently

**Violation examples:**

1. An agent is told "you have permission to write this file" without actually holding the corresponding write tool

2. An actor checks at runtime whether they "should" have access before attempting an operation

3. A role title ("Admin") is used to determine authority rather than possession of a specific capability

4. An agent "escalates" a request for a capability it does not hold, and the capability is granted at runtime

#### T4 — All Actions Are Witnessed

**Statement:** Every action — whether it changes state, produces output, consumes resources, or accesses information — leaves an immutable, attributed, timestamped record. There are no unwitnessed actions in a trustworthy system.

**Rationale:** Transparency is not a feature. It is the precondition for accountability, auditability, and learning. A system that does not record what happened cannot answer the question "why did this outcome occur?" The record exists not primarily for debugging — it exists because accountability requires evidence and learning requires data.

In smart contract systems, the blockchain is the witness. Every transaction, every state change, every contract invocation is permanently recorded. The record is public and immutable. This is not optional — it is the mechanism by which trust is established.

In this framework, the audit log is the witness. Every tool call, every document read, every search query, every state transition is recorded with full attribution. The log is append-only and immutable. It is the foundation of the chain-of-custody model: given any output, trace backwards through the audit log to see every action that contributed to it.

**Ontological implementation:** The Events dimension is the audit log. Every action creates an Event entity: traversal, deposit, observation, discovery, verification, submission, state\_transition. Events are immutable by TypeDB design — once written, they cannot be updated or deleted. Each Event has required attributes: timestamp, actor\_id (who), event\_type (what), and Connections to affected Things (which task, which proof, which document). Efficiency metrics (token\_count, latency, energy\_estimate) are Event attributes, enabling lifecycle cost analysis. Chain-of-custody queries traverse Event Connections backwards: "show all Events that contributed to proof P" walks the graph from P through all referenced Things and their creation Events.

**This principle requires:**

1. Every tool produces an audit entry, without exception

2. Audit entries are written by the tool itself, not by the caller — a caller cannot suppress, modify, or forge an audit entry

3. Audit entries include: timestamp, actor, tool, inputs, outputs, state changes, efficiency metrics (token count, latency), and references to prior audit entries that contributed to this action

4. The audit log is immutable — no entry is ever updated or deleted after creation

5. Privacy and security requirements are met through access control to the log, not through selective logging

**Violation examples:**

1. A tool call completes without producing an audit entry

2. An audit entry is written by the caller rather than the tool

3. An audit entry is deleted or modified after the fact

4. "Debug mode" suppresses audit entries

5. An actor declares "this action isn't important enough to log"

6. A "privacy-preserving" mode disables audit logging for "sensitive" operations

#### T5 — No Actor Judges Their Own Work

**Statement:** The entity that produces an output and the entity that verifies it must be structurally separate. This applies at every scale: agent, team, organisation, institution. The separation is not a policy preference — it is the load-bearing structure of any verification system.

**Rationale:** Self-assessment is not verification. An actor evaluating their own work operates under cognitive biases, incentive misalignments, and the availability heuristic (they know what they intended, not just what they produced). These biases are not moral failings — they are inherent properties of self-evaluation.

Independent verification removes these biases by removing the shared context. The verifier does not know what the author intended. The verifier only knows what the specification required and what evidence was provided. This ignorance is not a bug — it is the feature. The verifier sees the work the way a future stakeholder will: without access to the author's internal model.

**Ontological implementation:** EXECUTOR and REVIEWER are distinct Actor subtypes (ai\_agent with caste: executor vs caste: reviewer). Connection constraints enforce separation: an Actor with submitted Connection to proof P cannot hold verifies Connection to the same P. This is a schema-level constraint in TypeDB — attempts to create both Connections for the same Actor-proof pair fail at query time. The verifier reads the proof\_template Thing and proof\_document Thing, following Connections to evidence Things, but does not read implementation artifacts unless they're explicitly referenced as evidence. Verification happens after execution is complete — state transition Events enforce temporal ordering (cannot create verification Event until submission Event exists).

**This principle requires:**

1. EXECUTOR and REVIEWER are structurally separate agent classes

2. An agent that holds a submit tool for a document must not hold the verify tool for the same document

3. The verifier reads the proof template and the proof, not the work product directly (unless the work product is referenced as evidence in the proof)

4. Verification happens after execution is complete, never during

**Violation examples:**

1. A single agent performs a task and marks it as QA-passed without independent review

2. A reviewer is part of the same team as the performer and shares context about what the task "was supposed to do" beyond what the specification states

3. A performer runs their own tests, reviews the results, and submits their work as "validated"

4. A manager approves work produced by their direct report without independent verification

#### T6 — Resilience Through Structure

**Statement:** The framework produces trustworthy outputs even when individual actors fail, hallucinate, produce errors, or act in bad faith. Systemic trustworthiness does not depend on the reliability of any single actor.

**Rationale:** This is the Byzantine Fault Tolerance principle applied to knowledge work. In distributed systems, Byzantine fault tolerance means the system functions correctly even when up to a threshold of nodes are faulty or malicious.

In knowledge work, the same principle applies. An agent may hallucinate and produce subtly incorrect output. A human may make a rushed judgment. A specification may contain an error. No single point of failure — agent, human, or process — should be able to compromise the trustworthiness of the final output.

The framework achieves this through structural redundancy: independent verification, multi-perspective ideation, uncertainty surfacing, and audit trails. If an executor produces bad output, the reviewer catches it. If a specification is ambiguous, the executor surfaces uncertainty. If a pattern in the library is flawed, repeated application with negative outcomes causes it to be retired.

**Ontological implementation:** Critical verification paths support quorum-based approval through multiple verifies Connections from different Actors to the same proof Thing. Quorum thresholds (e.g., "3 of 5 must pass") are encoded as rules in the Groups dimension (group has\_verification\_policy policy). No single Actor holds unchecked authority — even platform\_owner operations create Events that are queryable by group\_owner Actors. Errors are detectable through structural checks: if verification fails repeatedly, the pattern Thing referenced by the task accumulates negative\_outcome Events, lowering its confidence score in the Knowledge dimension until it falls below the active threshold. The framework degrades gracefully — if an Actor becomes unavailable, Tasks assigned to that Actor transition to available\_for\_assignment state, creating priority Events that route to other qualified Actors.

**This principle requires:**

1. Critical verification paths have multiple independent verifiers (parallel reviewers, quorum-based approval)

2. No single actor holds unchecked authority over consequential decisions

3. Errors are detectable through structural checks (independent verification, pattern matching against known anti-patterns, consistency checks)

4. The framework degrades gracefully when components fail (an unavailable agent does not block all work; it routes to alternatives or escalates to human attention)

**Violation examples:**

1. A single reviewer verifies all work in a domain, creating a single point of failure

2. An agent produces output without submitting it for review because "this is a simple case"

3. FrameworkOwner has unchecked authority to modify any artefact without review or audit

4. System failure modes are silent (an agent fails, work stops, no signal is produced)

#### T7 — Verification Is Distributable

**Statement:** Verification does not require a central trusted party. The framework supports quorum-based verification where a pool of qualified verifiers reach consensus, eliminating bottlenecks and single points of failure.

**Rationale:** A single verifier — even an independent one — is a bottleneck for throughput and a single point of failure for trust. If that verifier is unavailable, work stops. If that verifier is compromised or consistently wrong, the framework's trust model breaks.

Distributed verification, borrowed from blockchain consensus mechanisms, solves both problems. A pool of qualified verifiers exists. When proof is submitted, a subset is selected (randomly, or by reputation, or by workload balancing). A quorum threshold (e.g., 3 of 5 must agree) determines whether the proof passes. No single verifier's judgment is dispositive.

This model is essential for decentralised systems (DAOs, multi-organisation collaborations) and valuable even for centralised ones (reduces bottlenecks, improves resilience).

**Ontological implementation:** Verifier pools are Groups with type verifier\_pool\_group containing multiple Actors with caste: reviewer. When a proof Thing transitions to ready\_for\_verification state (an Event), the system queries the appropriate pool Group and selects N Actors based on Knowledge dimension attributes: reputation\_score, current\_workload, domain\_expertise. Each selected Actor creates a verifies Connection and ultimately a verification Event (pass/fail). The quorum rule is a Group attribute: required\_verifications: 3, total\_selected: 5. Once quorum Events are recorded, the proof Thing transitions to verified state (or rejected if quorum fails). Reputation tracking happens in the Knowledge dimension — Actors whose verifications correlate with eventual quality outcomes gain reputation; those whose verifications are outliers lose reputation.

**This principle requires:**

1. Verification can be performed by any qualified actor from a pool, not a specific named actor

2. Quorum thresholds are defined per work type (some work requires unanimous agreement, some requires simple majority)

3. Verifiers are selected by the system based on availability, reputation, and workload — not by the performer

4. Reputation systems track verifier accuracy over time (verifiers who consistently pass poor work or fail good work lose standing)

**Violation examples:**

1. "Only Alice can review this type of work" (single point of failure)

2. A performer selects their preferred reviewer from the pool

3. No quorum threshold exists — a single reviewer's pass is sufficient for all work types

4. Verifier reputation is not tracked — a consistently inaccurate verifier is indistinguishable from an accurate one

### Performance Principles

These principles define how the framework produces not just correct outputs, but excellent ones — through cognitive diversity, lifecycle optimisation, and continuous efficiency improvement.

#### PF1 — Cognitive Diversity By Design

**Statement:** Complex problems require multiple cognitive perspectives before solutions are selected. No single agent — and no single human — holds all relevant perspectives simultaneously. Structured cognitive diversity is not a luxury; it is a risk management strategy.

**Rationale:** Most knowledge work failures occur not during execution but during problem definition and solution selection. The wrong problem is solved correctly. The first reasonable solution is chosen without exploring alternatives. Edge cases are missed because only one perspective considered them.

Humans in groups can provide cognitive diversity, but it requires careful facilitation to overcome groupthink, status hierarchies, and time pressure. With agents, cognitive diversity can be architectural: instantiate multiple agents with genuinely different cognitive orientations (critical analysis, optimistic value assessment, creative alternatives, emotional resonance, factual grounding, process oversight) and synthesise their outputs.

At agent marginal costs, this is economically viable even for small problems. At human costs, it was only viable for the largest problems. The framework makes thorough multi-perspective exploration the default, not the exception.

**Ontological implementation:** Ideation agents are Actors with distinct cognitive\_orientation attributes: critical, optimistic, creative, emotional, factual, procedural (inspired by Six Thinking Hats). When a problem Thing enters ideation phase, the framework spawns multiple Actors (or activates multiple existing Actors with appropriate orientations) within an ideation\_session Group. Each Actor produces an ideation\_output Thing with Connections to the problem Thing. A synthesis Actor (with orientation: integrative) reads all ideation\_output Things and produces a synthesized\_solution Thing. This entire process creates a graph of Connections and Events in the Knowledge dimension, making cognitive diversity patterns queryable and improvable over time.

**This principle requires:**

1. Problem exploration includes multiple agents with distinct cognitive orientations (inspired by de Bono's Six Thinking Hats or equivalent)

2. Ideation outputs are synthesised, not merely aggregated

3. Premature convergence on the first reasonable solution is structurally prevented (ideation produces multiple candidates; prioritisation selects from the set)

4. Cognitive diversity is applied proportionally (simple problems may use fewer perspectives; complex or high-stakes problems use more)

**Violation examples:**

1. A single agent generates a solution, and execution begins immediately without alternative exploration

2. Multiple agents are used but all have the same cognitive orientation (e.g., all optimistic, none critical)

3. Ideation produces five alternatives, and the first one is always chosen without evaluation

4. "We don't have time for exploration" is used to skip ideation even for high-stakes problems

---

#### PF2 — Validate Before You Scale

**Statement:** No significant unit of work proceeds to full execution until its core assumptions have been validated through lightweight prototyping or testing. Validation failures are cheap. Execution failures are expensive.

**Rationale:** The most expensive mistake in knowledge work is executing perfectly on a flawed approach. A technically correct implementation of a solution that users reject, that doesn't address the actual problem, or that makes incorrect architectural assumptions wastes all the execution effort.

Prototyping and validation — building the minimal version necessary to test core assumptions before committing to full execution — makes failure cheap. A prototype that fails validation saves the entire execution cost. A prototype that passes provides evidence that dramatically improves the quality of the eventual proof template.

At agent marginal costs, prototyping is nearly always cost-effective. The framework should treat validation as a standard phase between specification and execution, not an optional luxury for high-risk work.

**Ontological implementation:** Prototype Things are distinct from full task Things (type: prototype\_concept vs task\_concept). Prototypes have their own proof\_template Things defining validation criteria: user\_acceptance, technical\_feasibility, performance\_benchmarks. A task Thing cannot transition from specified to in\_execution state until a corresponding prototype Thing has transitioned to validation\_passed state — this is enforced through Connection constraints (task requires\_validated\_prototype prototype). Validation failures create Events with rich properties describing what failed and why, feeding the Knowledge dimension so patterns learn to avoid similar approaches.

**This principle requires:**

1. High-stakes or novel work includes a validation phase between specification and full execution

2. Prototypes have their own proof templates (what does a successful validation look like?)

3. Validation includes user-facing testing where applicable, not just technical validation

4. Validation failures are expected and non-punitive — they are the mechanism by which bad approaches are filtered before they become expensive mistakes

**Violation examples:**

1. Specifications move directly to full execution without validation

2. Prototypes are built but not tested against real user needs or environmental constraints

3. Validation failures are treated as performer failures rather than as valuable information

4. "We'll validate in production" is the default stance

---

#### PF3 — Lifecycle Efficiency, Not Stage Efficiency

**Statement:** Efficiency is measured across the full lifecycle — from problem identification through operations — not within any single stage. Investment in early-stage cognitive work (discovery, ideation, validation) consistently reduces total lifecycle cost while increasing total value.

**Rationale:** Traditional efficiency thinking optimises each stage independently: minimise specification time, minimise execution time, minimise review time. This produces local efficiency but often global waste. Skipping discovery to "move faster" leads to solving the wrong problem. Skipping ideation leads to choosing the first reasonable approach rather than the best one. Skipping validation leads to rework in execution.

Lifecycle efficiency optimises for total time and total cost from problem identification to successful operation. Spending 0.5 days in discovery and ideation that prevents 1.5 days of rework is efficient, even though it "slows down" the start of execution.

At agent marginal costs — where early-stage cognitive work costs token budget but downstream rework costs both tokens and time — the calculus overwhelmingly favours front-loading cognitive effort.

**Ontological implementation:** Every Event includes efficiency attributes: token\_count\_input, token\_count\_output, latency\_ms, estimated\_energy\_kwh. The Knowledge dimension aggregates these across entire task lifecycles, creating lifecycle\_cost Things with properties: {discovery\_cost, ideation\_cost, specification\_cost, execution\_cost, verification\_cost, rework\_cost, operations\_cost}. Rework rates are tracked as Events: rework\_required Event references the original task Thing and records the reason. High rework rates for tasks that skipped ideation create negative correlation patterns in the Knowledge dimension, which inform future workflow recommendations. Environmental impact calculations aggregate energy estimates across all Events in a task lifecycle.

**This principle requires:**

1. Efficiency metrics track full lifecycle cost, not per-stage cost

2. Rework rates, uncertainty rates, and validation pass rates are tracked as efficiency indicators

3. Framework economics explicitly account for the cost of rework vs. the cost of prevention

4. Energy consumption and environmental footprint are included in efficiency calculations, not just token count and time

**Violation examples:**

1. Skipping discovery because "specification is fast enough"

2. Treating agent token costs as waste because they don't produce "deliverables"

3. Celebrating reduced specification time while ignoring increased rework rates

4. Optimising only for speed to deployment, ignoring post-deployment rework and operations costs

---

#### PF4 — Resources Are Proportional to Value

**Statement:** The framework minimises token usage, energy consumption, and execution steps consistent with quality outcomes. Waste is not neutral; it has financial, environmental, and diagnostic costs. Efficiency is a design goal, not an afterthought.

**Rationale:** Agent operations consume computational resources, which translate to financial costs, energy usage, and environmental impact. At small scale, these costs are negligible. At the scale of a DAO with thousands of concurrent workstreams, or a framework governing all knowledge work in an organisation, they are not.

More importantly, efficiency metrics are diagnostic signals. An agent that uses excessive tokens or takes excessive steps is surfacing a problem: poor context curation, missing patterns, ambiguous specifications, or hallucination loops. Efficiency is not just about cost — it is a window into framework health.

The framework should instrument every tool call with efficiency metrics, aggregate them per task and per agent class, and use anomalies as signals for improvement.

**Ontological implementation:** As described in PF3, every Event logs token\_count, latency, and energy\_estimate as attributes. The Knowledge dimension maintains baseline Things for each Actor subtype and task type: expected\_token\_range, expected\_latency\_range. Anomaly detection happens through TypeDB rules: if an Event's metrics exceed baseline thresholds by \>2 standard deviations, create an efficiency\_anomaly Event that routes to analysis. Context curation is tracked — if high token usage correlates with large context\_card Things, the pattern learns to trim context. Pattern effectiveness includes efficiency in its scoring: a pattern that reduces lifecycle cost gains confidence; one that increases cost loses confidence.

**This principle requires:**

1. Every tool call logs token count (input and output), latency, and estimated energy consumption

2. Efficiency baselines are established per agent class and task type

3. Anomalies (high token use, high step count, high rework rate) trigger review of specifications, context cards, and patterns

4. Pattern effectiveness includes efficiency metrics (does applying this pattern reduce total lifecycle cost?)

5. Environmental impact is calculated and reported

**Violation examples:**

1. No efficiency instrumentation exists

2. High token usage is ignored because "it still works"

3. Context cards include all available information rather than minimum sufficient information

4. Rework loops are not detected or flagged

5. "Agents are cheap" is used to justify unconstrained resource usage

### Learning Principles

These principles define how the framework continuously improves through structured accumulation and application of knowledge.

#### L1 — Uncertainty Surfaces Immediately

**Statement:** An actor that encounters a condition it cannot resolve within its defined authority must surface that uncertainty immediately and explicitly. A system that suppresses, defers, or silently resolves uncertainty is more dangerous than one that fails openly. Uncertainty is information. Its suppression is misinformation.

**Rationale:** In traditional systems, actors facing ambiguity have three options: guess, escalate, or halt silently. Guessing produces wrong outputs. Halting silently produces no output and no signal. Escalation is correct — but only if the system makes escalation easy, fast, and non-punitive.

Most systems make escalation costly: it requires navigating org structure, composing a persuasive case, and risking reputation damage if the uncertainty is later deemed trivial. This cost means actors under-escalate. They guess instead. Wrong outputs accumulate.

In this framework, raising uncertainty is a structural primitive, not a social act. Any actor can call raise\_uncertainty at any time. The call is immediate, attributed, and produces a priority signal. The actor does not need to justify the raise or persuade anyone. The act of raising is sufficient. Downstream, the uncertainty is classified and routed — but that is not the actor's concern.

**Ontological implementation:** Every Actor holds a Connection to the raise\_uncertainty capability — this is universal and cannot be revoked. Calling raise\_uncertainty creates an uncertainty\_raised Event with required attributes: actor\_id, task\_id, uncertainty\_description, context\_references (Connections to relevant Things: specifications, patterns, prior Events). The Event immediately transitions the task Thing to blocked\_on\_uncertainty state, halting further execution Events. The uncertainty Event routes to a priority stream consumed by human Actors or designated uncertainty\_resolution Actors. Resolution produces an uncertainty\_resolved Event with the decision, which unblocks the task and updates the Actor's context. Aggregate uncertainty patterns in the Knowledge dimension identify frequent ambiguity sources (specifications, patterns) for improvement.

**This principle requires:**

1. Every actor, without exception, holds a raise\_uncertainty capability

2. Calling raise\_uncertainty is never treated as a failure on the part of the actor — it is correct behaviour

3. Raising uncertainty immediately halts work and produces a priority stream event to human attention

4. Uncertainty raises include: what condition was encountered, why the actor cannot resolve it, and what context paths are relevant

5. The resolution is provided back to the actor explicitly — the actor does not resume silently

6. Aggregate uncertainty patterns are tracked as diagnostic signals (frequent uncertainty about X means X needs clarification)

**Violation examples:**

1. An agent encounters ambiguity, makes a guess, and continues without raising uncertainty

2. An agent raises uncertainty but work continues in parallel (raising did not halt execution)

3. An actor is penalised or marked as "low quality" for raising uncertainty

4. Uncertainty raises are ignored or dismissed without resolution

5. "Figure it out" is a valid response to an uncertainty raise

6. Uncertainty patterns are not tracked — the same ambiguity is raised repeatedly without upstream fixes

---

#### L2 — Irreversibility Demands Presence

**Statement:** Decisions with irreversible or high-consequence outcomes require human judgment structurally, not optionally. The threshold of irreversibility must be defined explicitly for each domain. Crossing that threshold without human presence is a framework violation, not a performance optimization.

**Rationale:** Agents operate within bounded contexts with finite visibility. They optimize for the criteria they are given. They do not, and structurally cannot, evaluate whether those criteria are appropriate, whether edge cases justify deviation, or whether the broader context has shifted in ways that make the planned action unwise.

Humans can. Humans can evaluate appropriateness. Humans can exercise judgment about whether rules should yield to circumstances. Humans can consider moral and ethical dimensions that agents cannot access.

Irreversible actions — production deployment, financial commitment, contract signing, policy adoption — require this kind of judgment. An error in these domains cannot be easily undone. Prevention is the only viable strategy. Prevention requires human presence at the decision point.

**Ontological implementation:** Irreversible operations are modeled as Things with type: irreversible\_operation\_concept, each listing the required Connections: requires\_human\_approval\_from Actor (human\_actor subtype). Attempting to create Events for these operations without the required Connection results in query failure — the operation cannot complete. State transitions for critical Things (task from approved\_for\_production to deployed, budget from allocated to committed) structurally require approval\_granted Events created by human\_actor Actors. The threshold of irreversibility is defined per Group — different domains (software vs finance vs legal) have different irreversibility\_policy Things listing which operations require human gates.

**This principle requires:**

1. The domain explicitly defines what constitutes an "irreversible" or "high-consequence" action

2. Such actions structurally require human approval before execution

3. Agents cannot bypass, defer, or "soft-fail" the approval gate

4. Human approval includes context about why approval is required, not just "click yes"

5. If no qualified human is available, the action does not proceed (it does not default to "agent decides")

**Violation examples:**

1. An agent deploys to production without human approval because "tests passed"

2. A "skip approval for low-risk changes" flag exists

3. Financial commitments below a threshold do not require human review

4. Human approval is requested after the action has already been taken

5. The approval interface provides no context — just "approve?" with yes/no buttons

---

#### L3 — Knowledge Compounds

**Statement:** Every completed unit of work contributes to the framework's shared knowledge. Successful patterns are crystallized for reuse. Failed patterns are documented to prevent recurrence. Knowledge accumulation is not optional — it is how the framework becomes more capable over time.

**Rationale:** Traditional knowledge management is social and informal: tribal knowledge, senior engineers who "know how things work," undocumented lessons from past projects. This knowledge is fragile. It leaves with people. It does not scale.

In this framework, knowledge accumulation is structural. Every task that completes successfully identifies which patterns contributed to success. Every validation failure documents what assumptions were wrong. Every uncertainty raise that gets resolved captures the missing information for next time. These are not post-mortem exercises — they are automatic byproducts of the workflow.

The Knowledge dimension becomes smarter as the framework is used. Early patterns have low confidence because they have limited evidence. As they are applied successfully, confidence increases. As they fail, confidence decreases. Eventually, the best patterns dominate, and poor patterns retire. The framework learns.

**Ontological implementation:** The Knowledge dimension stores pattern Things with attributes: pattern\_type, confidence\_score, success\_count, failure\_count, lifecycle\_cost\_impact, origin\_task. When a task completes, a pattern\_application Event links the task to applied patterns. If the task succeeds (passes verification), patterns gain success Events; if it fails or requires rework, they gain failure Events. TypeDB rules compute updated confidence scores based on Bayesian updating. Patterns below a threshold (e.g., confidence \< 0.3 after 10 applications) transition to retired state and stop appearing in recommendations. The pattern library is queryable — agents and humans can search for "patterns for API integration" or "patterns that reduce rework in financial analysis." Cross-domain transfer happens when patterns from one Group are visible to others (controlled by Group visibility policies).

**This principle requires:**

1. Every completed task identifies which patterns were applied

2. Every validation failure or rework event is linked to the task and patterns involved

3. Pattern confidence scores are updated based on outcomes

4. Patterns with consistently poor outcomes are retired (not just ignored)

5. The pattern library is searchable and agents are prompted to consult it

6. Cross-domain knowledge transfer is supported (patterns learned in one domain are discoverable in others)

**Violation examples:**

1. Tasks complete without recording which patterns were used

2. Pattern effectiveness is not tracked — all patterns are treated as equally valid

3. Failed patterns remain in the library indefinitely with no degradation

4. Agents do not consult the pattern library before beginning work

5. Pattern knowledge is siloed by domain with no cross-domain visibility

6. Knowledge accumulation is a manual, post-hoc exercise rather than automatic

---

#### L4 — Context Is Local, Knowledge Is Global

**Statement:** Agents receive narrow, task-specific context. They query global knowledge on demand. Context bloat is a performance and cost problem. The framework optimizes for minimum sufficient context while maintaining access to the full knowledge base.

**Rationale:** Early agent systems erred in two directions: too little context (agents were ignorant and failed) or too much context (agents received entire codebases and became slow and expensive). Both are wrong.

The correct model is database-like: agents receive a focused context card containing only what is necessary for their immediate task. When they need broader information, they query the knowledge base. The knowledge base is global, searchable, and structured. Context is local, minimal, and task-specific.

This model reduces token costs, improves agent focus, and makes context curation a first-class concern. Poor context curation surfaces immediately as uncertainty raises or failed tasks, creating feedback that improves future context cards.

**Ontological implementation:** Context cards are Things with type: context\_card\_concept, containing references (Connections) to relevant specification Things, pattern Things, and historical Event Things. Agents do not receive full documents — they receive context cards with links. When an agent needs more information, it queries the Knowledge dimension using TypeDB's semantic search (via embeddings) or graph traversal (via Connections). Token usage is tracked per Event — if high token usage correlates with large context cards, the pattern library learns to trim context. Context card effectiveness is Knowledge dimension data: which cards led to successful task completion with minimal uncertainty raises and low token costs. Good context card patterns propagate; poor ones retire.

**This principle requires:**

1. Agents receive curated context cards, not full knowledge dumps

2. Context cards are structured with references, not full-text inclusions

3. Agents have query capabilities to access the broader knowledge base on demand

4. Context card quality is tracked (token usage, uncertainty rates, task success)

5. Poor context curation surfaces as a pattern problem and improves over time

**Violation examples:**

1. Every agent receives the full specification document regardless of their task

2. Agents have no query capability — they must work with only the initial context

3. Context cards are not structured — they are just long text dumps

4. High token usage from bloated context is not tracked or addressed

5. Context cards are hand-written once and never improved based on outcomes

---

#### L5 — Problem Precedes Solution

**Statement:** No unit of work proceeds to ideation or specification until the problem is explicitly understood and validated. A clearly stated, well-validated problem is more valuable than a quickly-generated solution to the wrong problem.

**Rationale:** The most expensive waste in knowledge work is solving the wrong problem brilliantly. A technically perfect implementation that addresses a misunderstood need, that optimizes for the wrong metric, or that satisfies stated requirements while missing underlying needs is waste — regardless of how efficiently it was produced.

Problem definition and validation is hard, slow work. It requires talking to stakeholders, understanding constraints, exploring edge cases, and testing assumptions. Traditional systems skip this work under time pressure. The framework treats it as mandatory.

Discovery is a distinct phase with its own proof template: evidence of stakeholder engagement, validated assumptions, documented constraints, defined success metrics. Only when the discovery proof is verified does the work proceed to ideation.

**Ontological implementation:** Discovery Things are distinct from task Things (type: discovery\_concept). A discovery must reach validated state (confirmed through a verification Event) before dependent task Things can be created — enforced through Connection constraints: task requires\_validated\_discovery discovery. Discovery proof templates include criteria: stakeholder\_interviews\_conducted, assumptions\_tested, constraints\_documented, success\_metrics\_defined. Each criterion maps to evidence Things (interview\_transcript, assumption\_test\_result). The discovery phase involves Actors with roles: discovery\_lead (human, typically), research\_agent, stakeholder\_liaison. High-quality discoveries (tasks built on them have low rework rates) increase the confidence of the discovery pattern in the Knowledge dimension.

**This principle requires:**

1. Discovery is a distinct phase with its own proof template and verification

2. Discovery includes stakeholder engagement, assumption testing, and constraint documentation

3. Ideation and specification cannot proceed until discovery is verified

4. Discovery quality is measured by downstream task success (did the task solve the right problem?)

5. Discovery patterns are tracked in the knowledge base and improve over time

**Violation examples:**

1. Work proceeds directly to specification without a discovery phase

2. Discovery is perfunctory — a checklist item with no real validation

3. Stakeholder interviews are skipped because "we know what they need"

4. Assumptions are documented but not tested

5. Discovery verification is skipped because "we need to move fast"

---

#### L6 — Definition Precedes Execution

**Statement:** The specification and proof template for any unit of work must be complete, reviewed, and approved before execution begins. Changes to the definition mid-execution are permitted only through a defined change-control process that produces new verification requirements.

**Rationale:** Agile methodologies popularized "figure it out as you go." This works in highly uncertain, exploratory domains where the problem itself is unknown. It does not work for most knowledge work, where the problem is understood but the solution requires coordinated effort.

Defining success criteria after work begins introduces ambiguity, invites scope creep, and makes verification arbitrary. If the definition can change during execution, the executor optimizes for pleasing the reviewer rather than meeting objective criteria.

In this framework, the specification is a contract. It is written, reviewed, approved, and fixed before execution begins. The proof template defines what evidence will constitute completion. Both are first-class artefacts, versioned and immutable once approved. If circumstances change and the specification must evolve, that evolution goes through change control: new specification version, new proof template, new verification cycle.

**Ontological implementation:** Specification Things and proof\_template Things have state machines: draft → under\_review → approved → locked. A task Thing cannot transition from defined to in\_execution until its specification and proof\_template Things are in locked state — enforced through Connection constraints: task requires\_locked\_specification spec, task requires\_locked\_template template. If the specification must change mid-execution, the change control process creates a new specification version (specification\_v2 Thing), which requires new approval Events. The task references both versions, and the audit trail (Events dimension) shows when and why the change occurred. Pattern analysis in the Knowledge dimension tracks how often specifications change mid-execution — high change rates indicate poor discovery or premature execution, feeding improvements to earlier phases.

**This principle requires:**

1. Specifications and proof templates are complete, reviewed, and approved before execution begins

2. Approved specifications are immutable (versioned if changes are needed)

3. Changes mid-execution go through formal change control with new verification requirements

4. The change control process documents why the change was necessary (learning signal)

5. High rates of mid-execution changes trigger review of discovery and specification processes

**Violation examples:**

1. Execution begins with an incomplete specification ("we'll figure it out")

2. The specification is updated during execution without version control

3. Acceptance criteria are added after work is submitted

4. "Just change the spec to match what was built" is an acceptable practice

5. No change control process exists — specifications evolve informally through conversation

**Conclusion: Principles Made Real**

These principles are not aspirational. They are enforceable through the ONE 6-dimension ontology. The ontology provides the structural substrate — Groups, Actors, Things, Connections, Events, Knowledge — that makes each principle operational rather than merely declarative.

**Groups** enforce scope and boundaries, making ethical constraints structural.

**Actors** model humans and AI agents as complementary authorisable entities, enabling deliberate capability assignment.

**Things** make specifications, proofs, and patterns first-class artifacts with lifecycles and verification requirements.

**Connections** encode authority, ownership, and verification relationships as graph edges, making unauthorized actions structurally impossible.

**Events** provide immutable witnessing of every action, creating accountability and enabling chain-of-custody tracing.

**Knowledge** accumulates patterns, reputation, and efficiency baselines, making the framework smarter over time through compound learning.

Together, principles and ontology form a complete architecture for trustworthy, excellent, self-improving knowledge work coordination at scale.

The framework's promise is not better project management or fancier tooling. It is **structural trustworthiness** — outputs you can rely on because of how they were produced, not who produced them — combined with **cognitive diversity by design** — solutions that emerge from rigorous multi-perspective exploration rather than single-threaded execution.

This is the foundation. Domain implementations will adapt and extend these principles. Operational systems will add tooling, workflows, and interfaces. But any implementation that violates these principles is not a variant of this framework — it is a different system with a different theory of how knowledge work becomes trustworthy.

Reality is the DSL. These principles are the laws it enforces.
