## Counter-Arguments Worth Addressing

A credible thought leader engages with dissenting views. Here are the strongest counter-positions:

- **"Trust isn't the primary bottleneck — infrastructure and skills are."** The OECD's firm-level analysis  and Finzarc's 2026 review  both argue that weak governance, unclear ownership, and outdated workflows are more fundamental than trust per se — trust is a *symptom*, not a *cause* [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/the-adoption-of-artificial-intelligence-in-firms_f9ef33c3-en.html)
- **"AI's economic benefits are overstated regardless of trust."** MIT's Daron Acemoglu estimates only 1.1–1.8% GDP growth over 10 years, suggesting AI's transformative economic impact is itself oversold — meaning even perfect trust won't unlock $15 trillion [mitsloan.mit](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/a-new-look-economics-ai)
- **"Productivity gains don't require widespread trust — only adoption by a capable minority."** Goldman Sachs and PwC data shows benefits are already concentrating in the top 20% of AI-ready firms — meaning the economy might still benefit even if public trust stays low, just very unequally [pwc](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html)
- **"Too much trust is also dangerous."** UNESCO and academic research warn that *overtrust* — especially uncritical reliance on AI outputs — produces its own economic harms through errors, hallucinations, and accountability vacuums [unesco](https://www.unesco.org/en/artificial-intelligence/recommendation-ethics)

## Other Issues That Belong in the Series

Beyond trust, several structural forces shape AI's economic impact:

- **Infrastructure gaps** — legacy IT systems in government and enterprise block deployment regardless of trust [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)
- **Data quality and governance** — AI's outputs are only as good as its data inputs; siloed, dirty data is a universal blocker [newicon](https://newicon.net/ai-adoption)
- **Regulatory fragmentation** — conflicting national AI rules raise compliance costs and distort investment flows [theconversation](https://theconversation.com/regulating-ai-seems-like-an-impossible-task-but-ethically-and-economically-its-a-vital-one-250816)
- **Equity concentration** — benefits accruing to a narrow band of organisations and geographies, independent of public trust [weforum](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/01/creating-economic-opportunities-for-all-in-the-intelligent-age/)
- **Measurement gaps** — without agreed ROI frameworks, institutions can't justify AI investment even when they want to [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)

## Other Impacts of Low AI Trust

Beyond lost productivity, low trust has wider economic and social consequences:

- **Investment paralysis** — 46% of planned AI investments are on hold due to trust concerns [revenuebrew](https://www.revenuebrew.com/stories/2026/04/16/ai-trust-barrier)
- **Talent misalignment** — workers who distrust AI resist reskilling, widening skill gaps over time [kpmg](https://kpmg.com/au/en/insights/artificial-intelligence-ai/trust-in-ai-global-insights-2025.html)
- **Stalled public services** — governments delay or abandon high-value AI deployments (in health, welfare, courts) to avoid backlash [lawreform.vic.gov](https://www.lawreform.vic.gov.au/publication/artificial-intelligence-in-victorias-courts-and-tribunals-consultation-paper/3-benefits-and-risks-of-ai/)
- **Insurance and liability uncertainty** — Gallagher's 2026 benchmarking survey finds AI risk remains largely uninsured, creating financial exposure that suppresses adoption [ajg](https://www.ajg.com/au/news-and-insights/features/ai-adoption-and-risk-benchmarking-2026/)
- **Democratic erosion** — deepfake-driven distrust of information systems undermines civic participation alongside economic participation [minderoo](https://www.minderoo.org/stories/ai-research-feb-2026/)

## How Companies Can Build AI Trust

Research-backed strategies, not just platitudes:

- **Responsible AI governance frameworks** — PwC finds that companies with these frameworks are 1.7x more likely to capture AI's economic value [pwc](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html)
- **Explainability by design** — workers cite inability to explain AI outputs as their top trust barrier (32%); systems that show their reasoning reduce verification burden [onrec](https://www.onrec.com/news/statistics/ai-mistrust-is-making-work-slower-not-faster-%E2%80%93-uk-employees-spend-almost-as-long)
- **Human-in-the-loop design** — visible human oversight checkpoints, especially for high-stakes decisions, materially increase both user and public trust [dta.gov](https://www.dta.gov.au/articles/responsible-choices-new-policy-using-ai-australian-government)
- **Role-specific AI training** — general AI awareness programs don't move the needle; task-specific upskilling does [agile-insights.com](https://www.agile-insights.com.au/australias-trust-in-ai-2025/)
- **Transparent failure reporting** — McKinsey's 2026 trust maturity data shows that organisations that openly report AI errors build more trust than those projecting infallibility [mckinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/tech-forward/state-of-ai-trust-in-2026-shifting-to-the-agentic-era)
- **Co-design with end users** — involving frontline workers and affected communities in AI design is consistently identified as the highest-leverage trust intervention [ia.acs.org](https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2025/aussies-more-scared-than-excited-about-ai.html)

## Institutional Trust Deficit: Your Most Differentiated Angle

You're right — and this is underexplored in most AI commentary. The problem isn't just trust in AI systems; it's trust in the **institutions deploying them**. This is where your series could genuinely stand out:

- Edelman's Trust Barometer has documented a long-run decline in institutional trust across government, media, and corporations — and AI is being deployed *into* this pre-existing deficit [privatebank.jpmorgan](https://privatebank.jpmorgan.com/nam/en/services/portfolio-management/sustainable-investing/insights/how-trust-may-drive-a-competitive-edge-in-the-age-of-ai)
- JP Morgan's May 2026 research explicitly frames institutional trustworthiness as a **competitive moat in the AI era** — companies already trusted by customers will extract far more AI value than equally capable but less-trusted rivals [privatebank.jpmorgan](https://privatebank.jpmorgan.com/nam/en/services/portfolio-management/sustainable-investing/insights/how-trust-may-drive-a-competitive-edge-in-the-age-of-ai)
- The KPMG/UoM study found **only 23% of Australians trust tech companies** to manage AI safely  — meaning even well-designed AI tools carry the reputational burden of deploying companies [news.uq.edu](https://news.uq.edu.au/2026-01-australias-ai-safety-gap-4000-times-bigger-you-think)
- APS AI Plan documentation acknowledges this directly: *"AI adoption in the APS requires a strong authorising environment and the maintenance of public trust"* — framing institutional legitimacy as a prerequisite, not an afterthought [digital.gov](https://www.digital.gov.au/policy/ai/australian-public-service-ai-plan-2025/trust)
- UNESCO's AI ethics framework makes the same argument at global scale: AI governance must be grounded in pre-existing human rights and accountability norms, not bolted on top of broken institutions [unesco](https://www.unesco.org/en/artificial-intelligence/recommendation-ethics)

This "trust stack" framing — where AI trust sits atop institutional trust, which sits atop broader social trust — could be the organising thesis of your entire series, and it's well-supported by the evidence above.
