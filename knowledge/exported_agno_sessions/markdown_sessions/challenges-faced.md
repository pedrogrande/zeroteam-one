# Challenges Faced

## Skill Gaps & Workforce Readiness

- Skill shortages are one of the most universal blockers — governments lag behind the private sector in AI talent, and many institutions lack staff who can meaningfully deploy or oversee AI systems  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)
- Workers in AI-exposed roles need reskilling, but training pipelines are slow; the IMF warns that without deliberate investment, regions reliant on automatable jobs face sustained employment decline  [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2026/01/14/new-skills-and-ai-are-reshaping-the-future-of-work)
- In higher education, faculty resistance, concerns over academic integrity, and lack of pedagogical training stall AI integration even where leadership supports it  [digitaleducationcouncil](https://www.digitaleducationcouncil.com/post/breaking-faculty-barriers-to-ai-literacy-adoption-incentives-and-scaling-strategies)

## Legacy Infrastructure & Data

- Most government agencies run on outdated IT systems incompatible with modern AI tooling, making adoption technically difficult and expensive  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)
- Data is often fragmented across siloed departments, stored in incompatible formats, or restricted by privacy legislation — precisely when quality, interoperable data is the core input AI needs  [thedecisionlab](https://thedecisionlab.com/reference-guide/management/organizational-barriers-to-ai-adoption)
- Without reliable data pipelines, many government AI pilots never scale beyond proof-of-concept  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)

## The Regulation Dilemma

- Governments face a dual mandate: protect citizens from AI harms *and* foster innovation — and these two goals regularly pull in opposite directions  [theconversation](https://theconversation.com/regulating-ai-seems-like-an-impossible-task-but-ethically-and-economically-its-a-vital-one-250816)
- The EU's prescriptive AI Act may stifle innovation and raise compliance costs for businesses, while lighter-touch regimes risk enabling harm and eroding public trust  [theconversation](https://theconversation.com/regulating-ai-seems-like-an-impossible-task-but-ethically-and-economically-its-a-vital-one-250816)
- Global fragmentation is a serious problem: companies operating across borders must navigate a **patchwork of contradictory national regulations**, increasing costs and creating incentives to locate AI development in the least-regulated jurisdiction  [theconversation](https://theconversation.com/regulating-ai-seems-like-an-impossible-task-but-ethically-and-economically-its-a-vital-one-250816)

## Measurement & Accountability

- A critical missing piece is **impact measurement frameworks** — without the ability to demonstrate ROI, institutions struggle to justify scaling AI beyond pilots or to prioritise ongoing investment  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)
- Opacity in AI systems (the "black box" problem) makes it difficult for regulators to audit decisions, assign accountability, or enforce rules even when they exist  [governanceinstitute.com](https://www.governanceinstitute.com.au/news_media/can-you-navigate-the-challenges-of-ai-regulation/)
- This drives risk aversion in public-sector bodies, which have stricter needs for transparency and public representation than private firms  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)

## Equity & Trust

- The benefits of AI are highly concentrated: 74% currently flow to just 20% of organisations  [pwc](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html), raising political and social pressure that can slow adoption in equity-sensitive public institutions
- Data used in hiring algorithms, credit scoring, and benefits systems can **reinforce existing inequalities** — undermining public trust and inviting legal challenges  [theconversation](https://theconversation.com/regulating-ai-seems-like-an-impossible-task-but-ethically-and-economically-its-a-vital-one-250816)
- Australia's National AI Plan (launched 2026) explicitly acknowledges this tension, framing the challenge as ensuring adoption leads to "shared prosperity" rather than concentrated gain  [industry.gov](https://www.industry.gov.au/publications/australian-government-response-senate-select-committee-adopting-artificial-intelligence-ai-report)

## Budget & Prioritisation

- Tight public budgets mean AI investment competes with immediate service delivery needs, and without clear ROI evidence, AI projects are frequently deprioritised or underfunded  [oecd](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/06/governing-with-artificial-intelligence_398fa287.html)
- The Australian Public Service only launched its formal APS AI Plan in May 2026, illustrating how slowly government-wide frameworks develop compared to private-sector adoption  [finance.gov](https://www.finance.gov.au/about-us/news/2025/introducing-aps-ai-plan)
