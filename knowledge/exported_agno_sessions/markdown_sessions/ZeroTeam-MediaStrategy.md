# Zero Team Thought Leadership Strategy

## A Playing to Win Analysis — May 2026


***

## Part 1: Landscape Review

Before any strategic choice, we need to understand the territory honestly.

### The Dominant AI Narrative Clusters

The AI media and content landscape in 2026 is loud, crowded, and converging around five well-worn narrative clusters:

- **The Productivity Promise** — AI will add trillions to GDP; every organisation needs an AI strategy. Dominated by McKinsey, PwC, Deloitte reports and their LinkedIn echo chambers. Lots of data, little discrimination about *why* some organisations are capturing the gains and most aren't.
- **The Jobs Disruption Story** — which roles will AI replace, which will it create. Perpetually recycled. Low signal-to-noise ratio. Generates clicks but rarely generates thinking.[^2]
- **The Regulation Race** — EU AI Act enforcement, Australia's APS mandatory requirements, ISO 42001 compliance. Dominated by legal and risk consultancies. Important but technical; creates anxiety without frameworks for action.[^3][^4]
- **The Ethics Discourse** — responsible AI, human-centred AI, AI bias. Well-intentioned but largely declarative: "here are principles organisations should follow." No architecture. No mechanism. No way to tell whether compliance is real.[^5][^6]
- **The Hype Correction** — "AI isn't living up to its promise." Adoption flatlined. ROI elusive. This narrative is growing. It's the most honest cluster but it mostly diagnoses without prescribing.[^3]

**The gap:** Nobody is connecting these clusters through a single explanatory mechanism. The productivity gap, the adoption flatline, the ethics failure, and the governance crisis all have a common root cause — and nobody in the content landscape has named it, framed it, or made it economically legible. That root cause is the trust gap.[^4][^7]

***

### The Thought Leader Landscape

| Voice / Organisation | What They Own | What They Miss |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Big 4 consulting firms** (McKinsey, PwC, Deloitte, KPMG) | Data at scale; executive credibility; sector reports | They describe the problem; they sell implementation. They don't generate original frameworks. Declarative ethics, not structural [^8][^3] |
| **AI Ethics academics** (UTS HTI, Melbourne, Oxford) | Rigour; research credibility; policy access | Rarely practitioner-facing; frameworks are abstract; slow publication cycles [^6] |
| **LinkedIn AI influencers** (Allie K. Miller, Lenny Rachitsky, etc.) | Reach; accessibility; trend currency | Reactive commentary, not framework-building. High volume, low gravity [^9][^10] |
| **AI governance commentators** (ISO 42001 community, AICD) | Compliance framing; board-level language | Checklist-oriented; declarative not structural; treats governance as documentation [^11][^5] |
| **Australian AI policy voices** (APS, DISR, AI Safety Institute) | Official authority; policy reach | Slow; bureaucratic; not practitioner-trusted; low brand recognition (only 9% of Australians have heard of the AI Safety Institute) [^12] |
| **Substack AI writers** | Longform depth; direct audience relationships | Mostly US-focused; mostly hype/prediction; few original frameworks |

**The white space:** No one in Australia — and very few globally — is operating at the intersection of *economic rigour + architectural governance + practitioner accessibility*. The Big 4 have the reach but not the frameworks. The academics have the frameworks but not the accessibility. The influencers have the accessibility but not the rigour. Zero Team can own the intersection.[^13][^4]

***

### The Macro Trends Working in Our Favour

- **Governance anxiety is peaking:** APS mandatory requirements activate June 2026; EU AI Act enforcement is live; boards are being urged to become "futurists" — but nobody is showing them how[^11][^3]
- **Declarative ethics fatigue is setting in:** Organisations have published responsible AI policies; they're not working; leaders are beginning to ask the harder question[^6][^5]
- **Australia is measurably behind:** 12% of Australian leaders say GenAI is transforming their business vs. 25% globally — a specific, local anxiety that creates specific, local demand[^3]
- **Substack is becoming the primary platform for serious longform thought leadership** — not a content channel but a media ecosystem[^14][^15]
- **Human-made authenticity is winning on LinkedIn** as AI-generated content floods the feed — the competitive advantage is a distinctive, expert, human voice[^16]
- **The trust framing is emerging but not yet owned:** Multiple voices are gesturing toward trust as important (James Patto: "trust is the thing that opens the door to AI opportunity"; Alexis Sakova: "architecture enforces it" ) — but nobody has built a comprehensive framework around it. The concept is live in the conversation; the intellectual territory is still unclaimed.[^17][^5]

## Part 2: Zero Team Strengths Analysis

Playing to Win begins with an honest inventory of what you actually have that's hard to replicate.[^1]

### Genuine Strengths

**1. Original frameworks that don't exist elsewhere**
The Structural Trust Framework  and the Trust as a Multiplier model  are not derivative summaries of others' work — they are original intellectual property built from first principles. This is the rarest asset in the thought leadership landscape. Most commentators aggregate; you synthesise.[^7][^4]

**2. The bridge no one else is standing on**
Pete uniquely spans: economic reasoning (RMIT, ESA workshop design), architectural governance (Structural Trust Framework), learning experience design (LX Truths, UN ITU programs), community building (Vibe Club, Future's Edge), and practitioner credibility (20+ years, KPMG, Telstra, UN). No competitor sits at this intersection. Each of these individually is table stakes; together, they are a defensible position.[^13]

**3. Clifton profile that suits this exact game**
Futuristic + Strategic + Activator + Ideation + Self-Assurance  is the exact combination for a thought leader who needs to: see where the conversation is going (Futuristic), find the pattern nobody else sees (Strategic + Ideation), publish before the moment passes (Activator), and hold the position under criticism (Self-Assurance). The unrealised strength flagged is *Writer* — which is particularly relevant given the Substack strategy.[^18]

**4. The Australian case study advantage**
Robodebt is the world's most documented case of declarative AI governance failure from a democratic government. It happened here. You can use it in a way no overseas commentator can — with local resonance, specific detail, and moral authority.[^4]

**5. An ecosystem that generates proof assets**
Good Vibes Only, Vibe Club, RMIT, Future's Edge — these are not marketing vehicles. They are live laboratories that generate case studies, participant insights, and community credibility that pure commentators cannot manufacture.[^19][^13]

### Honest Weaknesses

- **Organiser and Persistence** are flagged weaknesses  — a 12-month content strategy requires systematic execution, not just ideation. This is the risk that turns strategy into a plan that doesn't get executed.[^18]
- **Brand recognition is nascent** — Zero Team is not yet a named brand in the Australian AI governance conversation. The Structural Trust Framework has no public citations yet.
- **Solo practitioner constraints** — the strategy requires volume and consistency that a single person with other commitments (RMIT teaching, Future's Edge, Vibe Club) will find difficult to sustain without systems or support.

## Part 3: The Playing to Win Strategy

Roger Martin's framework asks five integrated questions. Here are the Zero Team answers — stated as choices, not intentions.[^1]

***

### Aspiration: What Does Winning Look Like?

By May 2027, when an Australian board director, government economist, or AI governance practitioner asks "who should I read on AI trust and governance?", Zero Team is the first name they give — not because we have the most followers, but because we wrote the framework they use.

Specifically: inbound enquiries that name our frameworks, third-party citations of our published work, and speaking briefs that name our positioning rather than our topic.[^20]

***

### Where to Play

This is the most important strategic choice. We are explicitly choosing *some* playing fields and declining others.

**We play in:**

- **The governance architecture layer** — the space between "we have an AI ethics policy" and "our AI systems are actually trustworthy by design." This is underserved, high-stakes, and directly connected to regulatory pressure[^4][^3]
- **Australia-first, globally relevant** — we use Australia's specific context (Robodebt, KPMG/UoM data, APS requirements, sceptical adopter paradox) as the entry point, but frame everything to travel internationally[^21][^22]
- **Three audience segments simultaneously:** Senior Leaders, Professional Economists/Policy Advisors, and Ethical AI Builders — connected by the same trust gap diagnosis, converted by different content registers[^23][^19]
- **Longform + LinkedIn** as the primary distribution channels — Substack for gravity, LinkedIn for reach[^15][^16]

**We explicitly do not play in:**

- The **AI hype/prediction space** — who wins the model race, what's the next breakthrough. This is crowded, requires no original thinking, and attracts audiences who don't buy what we sell
- The **AI tools tutorial space** — how to use ChatGPT, prompt engineering tips. High volume, zero differentiation, wrong audience
- The **pure compliance consulting space** — ISO 42001 checklists, risk registers. This is what we argue *doesn't work*. Playing here would undermine the positioning
- **Global markets first** — the temptation is to target the US market where the audience is larger. But we have no credibility anchor there yet. Australia-first gives us a beachhead, a specific case study advantage, and a media infrastructure that knows us

***

### How to Win

The theory of competitive advantage here is **framework ownership** — a specific, rare form of differentiation in the thought leadership market.

Most thought leaders win on *reach* (volume of distribution) or *access* (proximity to power and data). Zero Team wins on neither of those — yet. We win by being the only practitioner in the space who has built a *structural* answer to the problem everyone else is describing declaratively.

The mechanism:

1. **Name what others are circling around.** The trust gap, the trust stack, declarative vs. structural ethics — these are concepts that smart practitioners are sensing but haven't articulated. We give them the vocabulary first. Whoever names a concept owns it[^7][^4]
2. **Give the frameworks away to make them indispensable.** The Structural Trust Framework is published openly. This is the open-source play: the framework becomes the industry standard precisely *because* it's free to use. People cite it, which builds Zero Team's authority, which creates demand for Zero Team to implement it[^4]
3. **Use Australia's specific pain as the entry point.** The Robodebt case study, the KPMG/UoM sceptical adopter data, the APS June 2026 deadline — these create urgency and local relevance that generalist global voices can't match. Australian audiences trust people who understand their context[^21][^3]
4. **Convert gravity into relationships through live experiences.** The ESA workshop, Good Vibes Only, and RMIT give us proof assets and relationships that pure content players cannot generate. Every event produces content; every participant becomes a potential advocate[^19][^23]

***

### Capabilities Required

Playing this way requires three capabilities that must be actively built — not assumed:[^1]

**1. Systematic publishing infrastructure**
The Activator strength means ideas flow readily; the Organiser weakness means execution systems must be *externally scaffolded*, not internally disciplined. Concretely: a content calendar locked 4 weeks in advance, a research-to-draft workflow, and a distribution checklist that runs on systems not willpower. This is the single most important operational capability to build.

**2. Framework versioning and citation tracking**
The Structural Trust Framework must be versioned publicly (v0.05 is already in documents ) and every piece of content must reference the version. This builds the citation trail that makes the framework citable by others. A simple Google Scholar alert and a spreadsheet tracking every external mention is the minimum viable system.[^4]

**3. The Writer strength, activated**
The Strengths Profile identifies Writer as an *unrealised* strength — performing well but underused. The Substack strategy is the activation mechanism. The risk is that Ideation produces more concepts than Writer can turn into finished pieces. The mitigation: constrain to one core idea per piece, drafted to completion before the next concept begins.[^18]

***

### Management Systems Required

Two systems are non-negotiable for this strategy to work:[^1]

**The Monthly Signal Check**
On the first of each month: (1) count inbound enquiries that named a Zero Team framework; (2) log any third-party citations or media mentions; (3) review subscriber growth and article open rates; (4) assess one assumption from the strategy — is it still holding? This takes 30 minutes and is the difference between a strategy that learns and a plan that doesn't.

**The Assumption Register**
This strategy rests on five key assumptions that must be monitored:

- *The trust gap is the right diagnosis* — if research emerges that contradicts the trust mechanism, the framework must evolve
- *Australian organisations are ready to invest in structural governance* — if the market stays in declarative-ethics mode for longer than expected, the commercial timeline shifts
- *Longform thought leadership generates inbound enquiries in this market* — if the conversion mechanism doesn't activate by Q4 2026, the content mix needs rebalancing toward shorter, more direct commercial content
- *Pete can sustain the publishing cadence alongside other commitments* — the single highest-risk assumption; needs a honest quarterly review
- *The Structural Trust Framework stays ahead of the competition* — given how fast others are circling the same territory, the framework must be actively evolved, not just published once[^17][^5]

***

### The Logic: What Must Be True for This to Work

For this strategy to deliver the aspiration, the following must all hold:[^1]

- Australian senior leaders and policy economists *will* pay for implementation help once they've adopted a framework — the consulting pipeline follows the intellectual credibility, not the other way around
- The trust gap framing *resonates* more strongly than the "responsible AI" or "AI governance" framings already in the market — because it adds an economic mechanism to a conversation that currently has only ethical and compliance mechanisms
- Publishing frameworks openly *does* create authority and demand rather than giving competitors a free ride — this is the open-source bet, and it has precedent (Creative Commons, IDEO's design thinking, the Lean Startup framework)
- The Australian context *is* specific enough to create differentiation, but universal enough that the frameworks travel internationally — this is the beachhead theory, and it requires the Australian content to be framed globally from the start

***

## The One-Sentence Strategy

> **Zero Team will become the global reference point for structural AI trust by publishing the frameworks that senior leaders, economists, and builders in Australia adopt as their vocabulary — winning through intellectual precision in a space dominated by declarative noise.**

Everything that supports that sentence stays. Everything that doesn't, goes.
