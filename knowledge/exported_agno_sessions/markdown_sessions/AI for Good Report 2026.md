# AI for Good: Landscape, Applications, Ethics, and Opportunities in 2026

## Executive Summary

"AI for Good" describes the deliberate application of artificial intelligence to advance human well-being, social equity, environmental sustainability, and global development goals—particularly the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). It is simultaneously a movement, a platform, a design philosophy, and an emerging practice discipline. The ITU's AI for Good initiative, organized in partnership with over 50 UN agencies and the Swiss Government, has become the primary global convening point, bringing together innovators, policy-makers, civil society, and problem-owners to identify and scale practical AI solutions for humanity's most pressing challenges.[^1][^2]

The 2025 ITU AI for Good Innovate for Impact Report curated 160 AI use cases from 32 countries across eleven domains, confirming that AI for Good has matured from aspiration to deployment—but also revealing persistent gaps in equity, access, and governance. The UN's AI for Good Impact Report documents AI as a potential positive enabler for all seven targets of SDG 1 (No Poverty), while simultaneously warning it could act as an inhibitor for 86% of those same targets if poorly governed—a dual-nature dynamic that runs across nearly every SDG.[^3][^4]

***

## What Is "AI for Good"?

AI for Good refers to the application of AI technologies to generate demonstrable positive social, environmental, or humanitarian outcomes—particularly in areas that market incentives alone would under-serve. It is distinguished from commercial AI by its intentionality around impact and its orientation toward equity, inclusion, and long-term sustainability rather than short-term returns.[^5][^6]

The concept encompasses three interconnected dimensions:

- **AI for People:** Applications advancing health, education, safety, equity, and accessibility—particularly for underserved and marginalized populations
- **AI for Planet:** Solutions addressing climate change, biodiversity loss, environmental monitoring, and sustainable resource management
- **AI for Prosperity:** Innovations that create economic opportunity, reduce inequality, and enable digital inclusion especially in emerging markets[^7]

It also increasingly encompasses **AI governance itself**—the design of AI systems and institutions that are themselves fair, transparent, accountable, and trustworthy. UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence establishes a global normative framework that positions responsible AI governance as inseparable from AI's potential for good.[^8]

### Geographic Markets Under Greatest Pressure

**Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia** face the most acute pressure: both are home to the majority of the world's extreme poor, have the highest exposure to climate shocks, and face severe healthcare and education workforce deficits. AI leapfrog opportunities are significant—mobile-first infrastructure, large youth populations, and rapidly growing digital ecosystems create conditions for rapid AI adoption without the constraints of legacy systems.[^9][^3]

**Southeast Asia** faces rising climate exposure, food security pressure, and growing demand for financial inclusion tools, particularly in rural and island communities.

**Latin America** is experiencing growing inequality, urban safety challenges, and post-pandemic health system strain, creating demand for AI-enabled service delivery.

**OECD countries** face AI for Good pressure primarily around social cohesion, mental health, aging populations, and ensuring AI transformation does not deepen structural inequality—particularly for workers in AI-disrupted industries.[^10][^11]

### Organisation Types Under Pressure

- **UN agencies and international development organisations:** Under pressure to demonstrate AI can accelerate SDG progress before the 2030 deadline
- **National governments:** Increasingly expected to deploy AI in public services while ensuring digital equity and responsible governance
- **Healthcare systems:** Facing both capacity pressures and the opportunity of AI diagnostics, especially in low-resource settings
- **NGOs and civil society:** Expected to adopt AI to increase operational efficiency and mission reach in a constrained funding environment
- **Academic and research institutions:** Under pressure to produce AI solutions with real-world deployment pathways, not just publications[^12][^4]

## Successful Examples of AI for Good Initiatives

### Healthcare: Disease Detection and Access

**Topaz AI (Cancer Diagnostics):** AI-assisted pathology tools have demonstrated the ability to detect cancers—including rare types—with accuracy comparable to specialist clinicians. In contexts with severe oncologist shortages (most of Sub-Saharan Africa and rural South Asia), this represents a potential step-change in early detection capacity.[^13]

**WHO Early Warning and Response System (EWARS) – Ethiopia:** The WHO piloted an AI-based climate-health training system in Ethiopia, using AI and mobile platforms to equip healthcare providers with personalised, time-efficient training on climate-related health risks, vector-borne diseases, and extreme weather response—addressing a critical capacity gap identified in the Sharm El-Sheikh Adaptation Agenda.[^14]

**Key learning:** AI diagnostics succeed in low-resource settings when they are designed from the outset for the infrastructure constraints of those settings (low-bandwidth, mobile-first, offline-capable).

***

### Climate and Environment

**AI-Optimised Renewable Energy Grids:** Machine learning systems are now being applied to optimise the distribution of energy from variable renewables (solar, wind), reducing curtailment and improving grid reliability in contexts from Kenya to California. This directly supports SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy).[^13]

**Precision Agricultural Forecasting (India):** Farmers in India have adopted AI models for localised rain forecasting and soil analytics, enabling more effective irrigation decisions and significantly reducing water and fertiliser use—a case study in AI democratising advanced agricultural science for smallholders.[^13]

**CSIRO AI for Climate R&D Roadmap (Australia):** Australia's national science agency CSIRO published a dedicated AI for Climate R&D roadmap, establishing a structured research agenda for applying AI to emissions modelling, extreme weather forecasting, ecosystem monitoring, and climate adaptation.[^15]

**Key learning:** Climate AI generates the most durable impact when it is embedded in existing community decision-making workflows—not deployed as a standalone tool—and when it is co-designed with the communities it is meant to serve.

***

### Financial Inclusion

**AI-Powered Microfinance:** AI is being used to accelerate credit decisioning for microfinance institutions serving the estimated 1.7 billion adults globally who lack access to formal financial services. By analysing alternative data (mobile usage, transaction patterns, social network proximity), AI-enabled credit scoring extends access to populations with no formal credit history.[^3]

**Good360 Donation Matching:** The NGO Good360 deployed machine learning to optimally match product donations from corporate partners to community organisations based on verified need, location, and type—reporting a 25%+ improvement in operational efficiency and significant reduction in mismatched or wasted donations.[^16]

**Key learning:** AI for financial inclusion is most powerful when it replaces biased or exclusionary proxies (like formal credit history) with richer, more relevant alternative data—but requires rigorous governance to ensure new data sources don't introduce new forms of bias.

***

### Education

**Personalised Learning Platforms:** AI-driven adaptive learning platforms are providing personalised educational experiences at scale in contexts ranging from refugee camps (where teacher shortages are acute) to mainstream schools seeking differentiated instruction. These platforms continuously adjust content difficulty, format, and pacing based on student response patterns.[^9][^13]

**Language Barrier Bridging:** AI translation and natural language processing tools are enabling education content delivery across language barriers in multilingual societies, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia where instruction must reach communities speaking dozens of distinct languages.[^13]

**Key learning:** AI in education requires careful attention to the "last mile"—digital infrastructure, device access, and teacher readiness. Technology-only approaches consistently underperform co-designed, human-centred implementations.

***

### Nonprofits: Fundraising and Operations

**Charity: Water Donor Engagement:** Charity: water implemented AI-powered chatbots for donor engagement, achieving a 30% increase in donor retention rates through personalised, timely communication at a scale impossible with manual staffing.[^16]

**HIAS (Hebrew Immigrant Aid Society) Campaign Optimisation:** HIAS used AI to analyse email campaigns and predict which appeals would drive highest donations across donor segments, achieving a 230% increase in contributions—demonstrating that AI-driven fundraising intelligence can be transformative even for organisations without large data science teams.[^16]

**Key learning from both cases:** AI fundraising tools generate outsized returns when they are used to personalise engagement, not just automate it. The human voice of the mission must remain at the centre of the communication strategy.

## Ethical Considerations and Potential Risks

### 1. Bias, Discrimination, and Unfairness

AI systems trained on historical data inherit and often amplify historical patterns of discrimination. Documented cases of bias exist across lending, hiring, criminal justice, healthcare triage, and educational assessment. Critically, AI can discriminate at a "speed and scale far beyond traditional discriminatory practices," as noted in the NIST framework—meaning biased AI affects far more people, more quickly, than biased human decisions.[^17][^18]

In AI for Good contexts, this risk is particularly acute: AI systems designed to *help* underserved populations can end up *reinforcing their exclusion* if training data reflects existing inequity and the development process doesn't include affected communities.[^19][^3]

### 2. Concentration of Power and Wealth

AI infrastructure, data, and capabilities are highly concentrated among a small number of large technology companies and wealthy nations. AI for Good initiatives risk becoming a form of "digital colonialism" where solutions designed elsewhere are deployed in communities without genuine local ownership, governance, or benefit-sharing. The UN's AI for Good Impact Report explicitly warns that AI ownership could "further create monopolies, leading to a further concentration of wealth and power without equitable compensation for the content providers".[^3]

### 3. Data Privacy, Consent, and Surveillance

AI systems require data, and many AI for Good applications operate in contexts where data subjects have limited legal protections, digital literacy, or ability to give meaningful informed consent. Healthcare AI applied in low-income settings, predictive policing in marginalised communities, and financial scoring of informal workers all create conditions where good-faith applications can become surveillance infrastructure if governance is inadequate.[^20][^19]

### 4. Accountability Gaps and Opacity

When AI systems make or inform high-stakes decisions (medical diagnosis, credit access, educational tracking, benefit eligibility), the "black box" nature of many ML systems makes it difficult to audit, contest, or correct errors. This is particularly problematic in AI for Good contexts where affected populations often lack the legal or technical resources to challenge algorithmic decisions.[^19][^17]

### 5. Dependency and Fragility

Organisations—particularly smaller NGOs and development agencies—risk building programmatic models dependent on AI tools and platforms they do not own, control, or fully understand. If tools are discontinued, repriced, or their outputs change, mission-critical services can be disrupted.[^16]

### 6. Dual-Use and Misuse Risk

AI capabilities developed for good can be repurposed for harm. Facial recognition developed for humanitarian registration can enable authoritarian surveillance. Predictive analytics designed for social services targeting can be weaponised for discriminatory enforcement.[^17][^19]

## Responsible and Ethical AI: How Organisations Can Act

### Five Foundational Principles

Research from Harvard, UNESCO, GitLab, and the World Economic Forum converges on five principles that distinguish organisations generating responsible AI outcomes from those that struggle:[^21][^22][^8][^19]

1. **Fairness and Bias Mitigation:** Embed bias auditing throughout the development cycle—not as a one-time check, but as a continuous practice. Use diverse, representative training data and include affected communities in design and evaluation processes.

2. **Transparency and Explainability:** Design AI systems so that decisions can be explained to the people they affect. Maintain human-readable audit trails. Publish model cards and system documentation.

3. **Human Oversight and Accountability:** Define clearly who is responsible for each element of an AI system. Establish protocols for when human review is mandatory. Do not deploy AI in high-stakes decisions without human-in-the-loop mechanisms.

4. **Data Governance and Privacy:** Apply data minimisation principles (collect only what is needed). Implement robust consent mechanisms. Map data flows and ensure compliance with relevant privacy frameworks. Consider the rights of data subjects in all deployment contexts.

5. **Ongoing Monitoring and Iteration:** AI systems are not set-and-forget. Establish continuous monitoring for drift, unexpected outputs, and emerging harms. Create feedback channels for affected communities. Treat AI governance as infrastructure, not a launch checklist.

### The WEF Nine Plays for Responsible AI

The World Economic Forum's 2025 AI Governance Alliance Playbook, developed in partnership with Accenture, offers nine actionable strategies for operationalising responsible AI principles at scale:[^22]

1. Establish and activate an AI governance function with clear authority
2. Embed responsible AI into product and service development processes
3. Build AI literacy at every level—from board to frontline
4. Develop transparent documentation practices for AI systems in use
5. Engage external stakeholders, including affected communities, in AI oversight
6. Align AI governance with regulatory frameworks across all operating jurisdictions
7. Foster a culture where raising AI concerns is safe and welcomed
8. Partner across sectors to develop shared standards, especially for high-risk domains
9. Treat responsible AI as a competitive differentiator, not a compliance tax

### Governance Structures That Work

- **Responsible AI Committees:** Cross-functional bodies (including ethics, legal, technical, and community representation) that review high-risk AI deployments before launch and monitor them post-deployment[^21]
- **AI Impact Assessments:** Structured pre-deployment analysis of potential harms analogous to environmental impact assessments, now increasingly required by regulation
- **Community advisory processes:** Involving affected communities as genuine co-designers, not just consultation targets—particularly critical in humanitarian and development contexts[^23]
- **Third-party auditing:** External bias audits and algorithmic impact assessments, particularly for high-stakes systems in healthcare, criminal justice, and financial inclusion

## How Nonprofits, Social Enterprises, and NGOs Can Benefit

### The Current State

AI adoption among nonprofits has accelerated dramatically: 82% of nonprofits now use AI in some form, up from a minority position three years ago. Yet only 10% have formal AI governance policies—creating significant operational and reputational risk alongside real operational opportunity.[^16]

### Highest-Value Applications for the Sector

**Fundraising and Donor Engagement**
Nonprofits using AI for fundraising see 20–30% increases in donation amounts through predictive analytics, personalised outreach, and automated engagement. AI tools can identify major gift potential in existing donor databases, optimise ask amounts, predict lapse risk before it occurs, and personalise communications at scale that would be impossible with staff resources alone.[^16]

**Operational Efficiency**
AI automation saves organisations 15–20 hours per week on administrative tasks—data entry, volunteer scheduling, compliance reporting, donor follow-ups, and grant tracking. For under-resourced organisations where staff capacity is perpetually constrained, this is the equivalent of gaining a part-time staff member without the cost.[^16]

**Impact Measurement and Reporting**
AI-powered analytics enable real-time program monitoring, predictive modelling of service demand, and automated impact reporting. As funders increasingly require sophisticated outcome measurement, AI tools make rigorous impact evidence more accessible to smaller organisations.[^16]

**Program Delivery and Service Matching**
Resource allocation algorithms can match beneficiaries to services, identify at-risk individuals for early intervention, and optimise geographic service delivery. Good360's 25% efficiency gain in donation matching is a replicable model for any organisation managing complex resource allocation.[^16]

**Content Creation and Communication**
Generative AI tools reduce the cost of producing high-quality fundraising copy, grant applications, social media content, and stakeholder reports—allowing small communications teams to maintain professional output across multiple channels.[^24]

### AI-Native Nonprofits: The Cost-Effectiveness Advantage

Research suggests that AI-native nonprofits—those that have systematically integrated AI across core functions rather than deploying it in pockets—achieve cost-effectiveness ratios 300–500% better than traditionally operated organisations. This is a striking figure: it implies that AI adoption is not merely an efficiency improvement but a potential step-change in mission impact per dollar raised.[^16]

### Platforms and Resources Available to the Sector

- **AI4NGO:** A dedicated platform providing workshops, ethical AI tools, and guidance specifically for non-governmental organisations[^25]
- **Tech To The Rescue:** A global connector matching social impact organisations with technology companies willing to provide pro bono AI implementation support—formalised through the ITU partnership in 2025[^7]
- **ITU AI for Good Platform:** Year-round digital platform connecting AI innovators with problem-owners in social impact contexts, with structured pathways from dialogue to deployment[^1]
- **Microsoft AI for Nonprofits / Google.org:** Both provide discounted or free access to AI infrastructure and technical support for qualifying nonprofits
- **The AI for Good Foundation:** A dedicated nonprofit convening minds and technologies for urgent global challenges[^5]

### Critical Success Factors for NFP AI Adoption

Based on available evidence from successful implementations, five factors consistently determine whether AI creates durable value for nonprofits:[^26][^22][^16]

1. **Mission alignment first:** AI adoption decisions should be driven by mission impact questions, not technology curiosity. Start by asking "what would we do better if we had more capacity?" rather than "what can AI do?"

2. **Governance before deployment:** Even small organisations need a basic AI policy. Without one, well-intentioned staff will make inconsistent and potentially harmful decisions about what data is fed into AI systems and how outputs are used.

3. **Data quality investment:** The impact of AI is bounded by the quality of the data it works with. Many nonprofits need to invest in data cleaning, consolidation, and standards before AI tools can deliver their potential.

4. **Human-centred implementation:** AI should augment the human relationships that drive nonprofit effectiveness—it should free staff to spend *more* time in meaningful human interaction, not replace that interaction with automated communication.

5. **Equity as a design criterion:** Nonprofits serving marginalised populations must ensure their AI tools don't replicate or amplify the structural inequities their programs are designed to address. This requires active attention, not passive assumption of neutrality.

***

## Conclusion: The Dual-Use Imperative

AI for Good is not a separate category of AI from commercial or enterprise AI—it is the same technology applied with different intentionality, governance, and design values. Every AI deployment decision embeds choices about who benefits, whose data is used, who bears the risks, and who has recourse when things go wrong.

The evidence is clear that AI has genuine, demonstrated potential to accelerate progress on humanity's most pressing challenges—from diagnosing cancer in resource-limited settings to connecting smallholder farmers with precision agricultural intelligence, to personalising education at scales no human workforce could match.[^4][^9][^13]

The evidence is equally clear that poorly governed AI systematically worsens outcomes for already-vulnerable populations—amplifying bias at scale, concentrating wealth and power, and creating accountability gaps that leave those most harmed with the least recourse.[^18][^8][^3]

The difference between these two outcomes is not the technology. It is the governance, the design process, the intentionality, and the distribution of power in who gets to decide how AI is built and deployed. That is the central challenge and the central opportunity of AI for Good in 2026.
