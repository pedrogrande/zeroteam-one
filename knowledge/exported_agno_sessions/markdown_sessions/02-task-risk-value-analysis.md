# 1. Ideal Outcomes of the Task

Ideal outcomes are what **perfect execution produces for each stakeholder** — the end state, not the process.

## For Students
| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 1 | Each student receives a **grade that accurately reflects the quality of their work** relative to the published rubric — no student is advantaged or disadvantaged by when they were marked or which question the marker read first |
| 2 | Each student receives **actionable feedback** that identifies specific strengths and specific areas for improvement, using rubric language they can trace back to the descriptor they met or missed |
| 3 | Students who performed well on **different dimensions** (e.g., strong technical understanding but weak communication) can see that distinction in their per-criterion scores — the grade doesn't collapse into a single opaque number |
| 4 | Students understand **why** they received their grade — the feedback is traceable to evidence in their submission and language in the rubric |

## For the Institution / Course Coordinator
| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 5 | The **grade distribution is defensible** — it aligns with institutional norms, can withstand moderation or appeal, and accurately signals cohort capability |
| 6 | **Cohort-level patterns** are identified — common misconceptions, recurring weaknesses, areas of strength — enabling targeted teaching adjustments for subsequent weeks |
| 7 | The marking process is **auditable** — any individual grade can be justified with reference to specific rubric descriptors and specific evidence from the submission |
| 8 | **Equity is maintained** — identical violations receive identical treatment; identical quality receives identical scores regardless of submission order |

## For the Marker
| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 9 | The marker completes the task within a reasonable timeframe without excessive re-work or second-guessing |
| 10 | The marker can **defend any individual grade** to a student, moderator, or appeals committee with confidence and specificity |

## For Future Relying Parties
| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 11 | Each grade is a **valid signal of demonstrated competence** in the assessed learning outcomes (AOL3b, AOL4, AOL5, AOL6) — not an artifact of marking inconsistency |

# 2. Risks Associated with the Task

## Category A: Systemic Risks (affect all 50 grades)

| # | Risk | Mechanism | Impact |
|---|------|-----------|--------|
| A1 | **Rubric miscalibration** | Marker interprets rubric language differently from institutional intent (e.g., reads "comprehensive" as "covers everything" when it means "depth + breadth + application") | All 50 grades shifted up or down; invisible until moderation |
| A2 | **Rubric-to-question mapping error** | Marker scores per-question and aggregates to criteria, but C1 and C2 require **holistic** assessment across questions — the mapping is non-obvious | Systematic mis-scoring on the two highest-weight criteria (60 pts combined) |
| A3 | **Boundary definition failure** | Marker never explicitly defines what separates HD from DI, DI from CR — relies on "gut feel" | Inconsistent grade band assignments; no defensible basis for borderline decisions |
| A4 | **Format compliance policy ambiguity** | Brief sets hard constraints (2 slides/question, sources per question, date/time on market data) but doesn't specify penalties for violation | Some students penalised, others not — equity violation |

## Category B: Per-Session Risks (affect marking quality within a marking session)

| # | Risk | Mechanism | Impact |
|---|------|-----------|--------|
| B1 | **Marker fatigue** | Cognitive depletion after 25–30 submissions; finer-grained distinctions (synthesis vs extraction, critical vs comprehensive) become harder to apply | Later submissions receive coarser, less discriminating scores |
| B2 | **Anchor effect** | First 3–5 submissions set an unconscious standard; subsequent submissions judged relative to that anchor rather than the rubric | Early cohort bias — high if first papers are strong, low if first papers are weak |
| B3 | **Criterion conflation** | Marker allows C1 (technical accuracy) to influence C2 (audience translation) or C4 (communication) — a technically impressive but poorly explained deck scores higher on C2 than it should | Inflation on criteria that measure different constructs |
| B4 | **Holistic skip** | Marker skips the full read-through (2.1) and scores question-by-question to save time | Structurally invalid C1 and C2 scores (these criteria cannot be accurately assessed per-question) |
| B5 | **Recency bias** | Most recently read submission unconsciously influences scoring of the next one | Adjacent-pair distortion; two students of equal ability receive different grades |

## Category C: Per-Student Risks (affect individual grades)

| # | Risk | Mechanism | Impact |
|---|------|-----------|--------|
| C1 | **Grade entry error** | Transcription error when entering scores into grading system | Catastrophic for the individual student; invisible unless student queries it |
| C2 | **Feedback vagueness** | Marker writes generic feedback ("good work", "needs improvement") instead of specific, rubric-linked feedback | Student cannot learn or improve; feedback is the primary value for the student stakeholder |
| C3 | **C3 (AI evaluation) under-weighting** | Marker treats Q5 as a "soft" reflection and doesn't apply the rubric's full rigour (accuracy, bias, limitations, verification, transparency, sophistication) | C3 is systematically inflated — students who wrote superficial reflections score the same as those who wrote critical ones |
| C4 | **Per-section Q5 requirement missed** | Marker doesn't check whether Q5 addresses each of Q1–Q4 individually (as the brief requires) | Students who skipped per-section reflection aren't penalised; those who complied aren't rewarded |

## Category D: Post-Marking Risks

| # | Risk | Mechanism | Impact |
|---|------|-----------|--------|
| D1 | **Drift undetected** | No drift check (3.3) is performed | Systematic grade inflation or deflation in the second half of the cohort goes uncorrected |
| D2 | **Distribution accepted without scrutiny** | Marker doesn't review the score distribution for anomalies | Unrealistic distributions (e.g., 80% HD) are released, triggering moderation or institutional concern |
| D3 | **Rubric performance not documented** | Marker doesn't note where the rubric was ambiguous or hard to apply | Same ambiguities recur in future iterations; no institutional learning |

# 3. Highest-Value Subtasks (by Stakeholder)

Value = the degree to which the stakeholder's outcome degrades if the subtask is done poorly or omitted.

| Rank | Subtask | Primary Stakeholder | Why It's High-Value |
|------|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **1** | **1.2 — Rubric calibration + rubric-to-question mapping resolution** | Institution, Marker, Students | This is the **single point of failure**. If the mapping is wrong (e.g., scoring C1 per-question instead of holistically), ALL 50 C1 scores are structurally invalid. Affects 60% of total marks (C1+C2 = 60pts). Wrong calibration shifts every grade. |
| **2** | **2.1 — Full deck read-through before scoring** | Students | Skipping this makes it **impossible** to score C1 (comprehensiveness), C2 (synthesis across questions), and C4 (coherence) validly. These three criteria total 80 pts. This subtask is the prerequisite for valid judgment on 80% of the marks. |
| **3** | **2.7 — Write per-student feedback** | Students | For students, feedback is the **primary learning output**, not the grade. A grade without specific feedback is a number; with specific feedback, it's a learning intervention. This is where the marking task creates educational value. |
| **4** | **2.4 — Score C2 (Synthesis & Audience Translation)** | Students, Institution | C2 is the hardest criterion to score accurately (synthesis vs extraction; insightful vs reasonably insightful are fine-grained distinctions), it carries 30 pts, and it measures the most professionally relevant skill (translating complexity for decision-makers). Inaccurate C2 scoring is both most likely and most consequential. |
| **5** | **3.3 — Check for marker drift** | Institution, Students | This is the **only mechanism** that can detect and correct the biggest systemic risk (B1 — fatigue). Without it, the last 20 students are graded on a different standard than the first 20. It protects equity across the cohort. |
| **6** | **2.2 — Format compliance check** | Students, Institution | This is high-value because inconsistency here is the **most visible equity violation** — if one student is penalised for exceeding the 2-slide limit and another isn't, the discrepancy is discoverable and indefensible. |
| **7** | **4.2 — Prepare cohort-level feedback** | Institution, Future Cohorts | This converts the marking exercise from a grading event into a **teaching intelligence** event. Common misconceptions identified here directly improve instruction in subsequent weeks. |
| **8** | **2.5 — Score C3 (AI Critical Evaluation)** | Students | C3 is the criterion most likely to be **under-marked** (treated as a "soft" reflection exercise) because markers may not have a clear standard for "critical vs comprehensive" or "transparent vs reasonably transparent." Rigorous C3 scoring sends the signal that AI literacy is a serious assessed capability. |

# 4. Riskiest Subtasks (by Expected Loss = Probability × Impact)

| Rank | Subtask | Risk Type | Probability | Impact | Why It's Risky |
|------|---------|-----------|-------------|--------|---------------|
| **1** | **1.2 — Rubric calibration (specifically: rubric-to-question mapping)** | Systematic error | **Medium** — the mapping is non-obvious and the brief doesn't specify it | **Catastrophic** — wrong mapping invalidates 60% of marks for ALL 50 students | This is a **one-time decision with 50× multiplier**. The marker may not even recognise they need to make this decision. It's the highest-risk subtask because it's both non-obvious AND affects every student. |
| **2** | **2.4 — Score C2** | Judgment error | **High** — "synthesised vs extracted" and "insightful vs reasonably insightful" are the finest-grained distinctions in the rubric | **High** — 30 pts per student; inconsistency across 50 students is probable | C2 is the criterion where the grade band boundaries are **most ambiguous**. The difference between HD and DI hinges on whether the student *discussed and synthesised* or *explained and extracted* — this is a genuinely difficult distinction to apply consistently. |
| **3** | **2.1 — Full deck read-through** | Process shortcut | **High** — time pressure and 50 submissions create strong incentive to skip | **High** — 80 pts of criteria require holistic judgment | The risk is **omission**, not error. Under time pressure, the marker will be tempted to score question-by-question, which structurally cannot produce valid C1/C2/C4 scores. |
| **4** | **2.3-2.6 — All scoring subtasks for submissions 30–50** | Fatigue-driven drift | **High** — cognitive depletion is well-documented after ~25-30 similar judgments | **Medium-High** — systematic grade shift for 40% of the cohort | The risk compounds: fatigue reduces the marker's ability to apply fine-grained distinctions, which means C2 and C3 scores drift toward coarser, more generous bands. This is undetectable without 3.3. |
| **5** | **2.5 — Score C3** | Under-application of rigour | **High** — markers tend to treat reflection tasks as "soft" and don't apply the same rigour as technical criteria | **Medium** — 20 pts per student; signals that AI literacy isn't serious | The rubric has real teeth (critical vs comprehensive; transparent vs reasonably transparent; sophisticated use), but the marker may default to a looser interpretation because the content is reflective rather than technical. |
| **6** | **2.2 — Format compliance check** | Inconsistent application | **Medium** — 50 submissions, multiple format rules, fatigue | **High for affected individuals** — equity violation is discoverable and indefensible | If one student gets a C4 penalty for 3 slides on Q1 and another doesn't, the inconsistency is a legitimate grievance. Format compliance is mechanically simple but requires **consistent enforcement** across 50 submissions, which is harder than it sounds. |
| **7** | **4.1 — Grade entry** | Transcription error | **Low** — but non-zero | **Catastrophic for the individual** — wrong grade released to student | This is the classic low-probability, high-impact risk. One wrong keystroke changes a student's grade. It's risky because there's **no self-correcting mechanism** — the error persists until the student notices and appeals. |
| **8** | **3.3 — Drift check** | Omission | **High** — no one likes re-reading papers they've already marked | **Medium-High** — undetected drift means 20 students are graded on a different standard | The risk is that the marker, fatigued from 50 submissions, **doesn't do** the drift check. If omitted, the biggest quality risk (B1) goes undetected. |

## Summary: Value × Risk Matrix

| Subtask | Value Rank | Risk Rank | Strategic Implication |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1.2 Rubric calibration + mapping | **1** | **1** | **Invest disproportionate time here** — this is where 1 hour of work prevents systematic error across all 50 grades |
| 2.1 Full read-through | **2** | **3** | **Never skip** — build it into the process as a mandatory gate before scoring |
| 2.7 Per-student feedback | **3** | — | High-value, lower-risk; but invest in quality because this is what students actually read |
| 2.4 Score C2 | **4** | **2** | **Develop explicit anchors** — pre-identify what "synthesised" looks like vs "extracted" to reduce judgment drift |
| 3.3 Drift check | **5** | **8** | **Schedule it, don't skip it** — it's the only backstop against the biggest systemic risk |
| 2.5 Score C3 | **8** | **5** | **Don't under-weight reflection** — treat C3 with the same rigour as C1/C2; the rubric has real teeth |
| 2.2 Format compliance | **6** | **6** | **Create a checklist, apply it uniformly** — mechanical consistency prevents equity issues |

The critical strategic insight: **the highest-value and highest-risk subtask is the same one** — rubric calibration and mapping (1.2). It's a one-time decision that determines the validity of 100% of the downstream work. The second insight: **the two highest-risk judgment tasks (C2 scoring, C3 scoring) are also the two most vulnerable to fatigue drift**, which means their risk compounds across the 50-student cohort.
