# Decomposition: Marking Assessment 1 for 50 Students

---

## Phase 1: Pre-Marking Preparation

These tasks establish the conditions for consistent, defensible marking across all 50 submissions.

### 1.1 Gather and organise submissions
- [ ] Download all 50 student submissions from the submission platform
- [ ] Check file formats (PDF, PPTX, Google Slides links) and convert where necessary to a consistent reviewable format
- [ ] Verify each submission is accessible and not corrupted
- [ ] Log any late submissions and note applicable penalty policies
- [ ] Create a tracking spreadsheet with student ID, file reference, and score columns for C1–C4

### 1.2 Calibrate the rubric
- [ ] Re-read the rubric and the HD descriptors alongside the full assignment brief
- [ ] Identify the **rubric-to-question mapping** (this is non-obvious and must be explicitly resolved):
  - **C1** (Technical Information) → Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 primarily; Q5 if it touches technical concepts
  - **C2** (Synthesis & Audience Translation) → Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 primarily; audience = "manager with limited reading time"
  - **C3** (AI Critical Evaluation) → Q5 primarily; but implicit presence in Q1-Q4 (did AI use affect technical accuracy in C1?)
  - **C4** (Communication & Format) → the entire deck holistically
- [ ] Define boundary conditions between grade bands (e.g., what distinguishes "comprehensive" from "effective" for C1? What makes synthesis "insightful" vs "reasonably insightful" for C2?)
- [ ] If co-marking, conduct a **calibration session**: each marker independently scores 3–5 sample submissions, then compare and discuss discrepancies to align standards

### 1.3 Establish format compliance checks
From the brief, there are **hard constraints** that should be checked before or during marking:
- [ ] Maximum 2 slides per question (enforced? penalty if exceeded?)
- [ ] Questions clearly identified on slides
- [ ] Sources listed for each question
- [ ] Market data sources include website + date/time accessed
- [ ] Q5 includes reflection per previous section (Q1–Q4 individually)

## Phase 2: Per-Student Marking (×50)

This is the core loop. Each student submission requires the following subtasks.

### 2.1 First pass — Read the whole deck
- [ ] Open the submission and read the entire deck end-to-end **without scoring**
- [ ] Note initial impression: overall quality signal, obvious strengths/weaknesses
- [ ] This prevents premature anchoring on individual questions before seeing the full picture (since C1, C2, and C4 are holistic)

### 2.2 Format compliance check
- [ ] Count slides per question — flag any exceeding the 2-slide limit
- [ ] Verify each question is clearly identified
- [ ] Check that sources are listed for each question
- [ ] Verify market data citations include website and date/time
- [ ] Check Q5 addresses each of Q1–Q4 individually
- [ ] Record any non-compliance — decide whether this affects C4 score or warrants a separate penalty per institutional policy

### 2.3 Score C1 — Technical Information (30 pts)
- [ ] Assess Q1: Are the four terms (Immutability, Decentralised Consensus, Scalability, Bitcoin Mining) **accurately** defined? Is the **importance** of each explained (i.e., application, not just definition)?
- [ ] Assess Q2: Is the **purpose** of Taproot correctly explained? Is the link to blockchain adaptation/development demonstrated?
- [ ] Assess Q3: Are the price movements of BTC/ETH/USDT described? Are relevant market events identified and connected to the movements? (technical + market information)
- [ ] Assess Q4: Is first-mover advantage conceptually applied to Bitcoin? Are other factors identified?
- [ ] Synthesise C1 score: Is the technical reporting **comprehensive** across all questions, or are there gaps? Is the application shown, not just definitions? Weight each question's contribution to the holistic C1 score.

### 2.4 Score C2 — Synthesis & Audience Translation (30 pts)
- [ ] Assess across Q1–Q4: Is information **synthesised** (combined from multiple sources into integrated insights) or merely **extracted** (listed from individual sources)?
- [ ] Check all three domains are present: **technical** information, **ecosystem** information, **market** information
- [ ] Assess audience translation: Could a non-specialist manager understand this? Is jargon explained or avoided? Are complex ideas made **clear and accessible**?
- [ ] Assess **insightfulness**: Does the student reveal connections or significance that go beyond surface-level translation?
- [ ] Check source quality: Are sources **relevant** (not just "reasonably relevant" or "limited")?
- [ ] Synthesise C2 score: The marker must judge the **degree** of synthesis, clarity, insight, and audience-tailoring across the whole deck.

### 2.5 Score C3 — AI Critical Evaluation (20 pts)
- [ ] Assess Q5: Does the student evaluate AI outputs for **accuracy** (specific instances, not general statements)?
- [ ] Does the student identify **bias** in AI outputs?
- [ ] Does the student articulate **limitations** of AI in this task?
- [ ] Does the student describe **verification processes** (how they checked AI outputs, not just that they did)?
- [ ] Is the evaluation **critical and reflective** (not just descriptive)? Does the student interrogate *why* AI performed as it did and reflect on their own process?
- [ ] Is the use of AI **transparent** — can the reader see exactly how AI was involved?
- [ ] Is the use of AI **sophisticated** — strategic, discerning, appropriate to the task?
- [ ] Check per-section coverage: Does Q5 address Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q4 individually?
- [ ] Synthesise C3 score

### 2.6 Score C4 — Communication & Format (20 pts)
- [ ] Assess **visual coherence**: Is there a consistent visual language throughout the deck?
- [ ] Assess **logical structure**: Does the flow of slides serve comprehension? Is the progression rational?
- [ ] Assess **effective communication**: Do the key messages land? Is the intended understanding conveyed without effort or ambiguity?
- [ ] Assess **conciseness**: Is the content appropriately brief for a manager with limited reading time?
- [ ] Assess **factual accuracy** as a communication quality (inaccurate information clearly communicated is not effective)
- [ ] Synthesise C4 score

### 2.7 Write per-student feedback
- [ ] For each criterion (C1–C4), write 1–3 sentences explaining the score with reference to specific rubric language
- [ ] Identify at least one specific strength and one specific area for improvement
- [ ] Ensure feedback is actionable (not just "good" or "needs improvement")

### 2.8 Quality check the scores
- [ ] Do the four criterion scores sum to a total that reflects your overall impression of the submission?
- [ ] Are any scores internally inconsistent (e.g., C1 = HD but C2 = Pass — is the technical information really comprehensive but the explanation terrible?)
- [ ] Flag any borderline cases for Phase 3 review

## Phase 3: Across-Cohort Quality Assurance

After marking all 50, the marker must ensure consistency and fairness at the cohort level.

### 3.1 Review score distribution
- [ ] Plot the distribution of total scores and per-criterion scores
- [ ] Check for unexpected clustering or skewing (e.g., too many HDs or too many fails in any criterion)
- [ ] Compare distribution against institutional norms or expectations for the cohort

### 3.2 Review flagged borderline cases
- [ ] Re-read submissions where criterion scores sit on grade boundaries (e.g., 79% total, or individual criterion at the HD/DI boundary)
- [ ] Apply the boundary definitions established in 1.2 to make a final determination
- [ ] Document the reasoning for any adjusted scores

### 3.3 Check for marker drift
- [ ] Compare scores from the first 10 submissions marked against the last 10 — has strictness or leniency drifted?
- [ ] If drift is detected, re-mark a sample from the drifted range
- [ ] If co-marking, compare score distributions between markers and moderate where necessary

### 3.4 Verify format compliance penalties
- [ ] Review the format compliance log from 2.2 across all 50 submissions
- [ ] Ensure consistent treatment (same penalty for same violation across all students)

---

## Phase 4: Post-Marking Administration

### 4.1 Finalise grades
- [ ] Calculate final total score for each student
- [ ] Convert to institutional grade scale if required
- [ ] Enter all scores into the grading system

### 4.2 Prepare cohort-level feedback
- [ ] Identify common strengths across the cohort
- [ ] Identify common weaknesses or misunderstandings
- [ ] Prepare brief cohort-level feedback notes for release with results

### 4.3 Handle re-mark requests and academic integrity
- [ ] Retain all submissions and marking notes for the retention period
- [ ] Be prepared to defend marking decisions with reference to rubric language and specific evidence from submissions
- [ ] Flag any suspected AI misuse (e.g., AI output presented without critical evaluation) or academic integrity concerns

### 4.4 Reflect on rubric performance
- [ ] Note any rubric criteria that proved difficult to apply consistently
- [ ] Note any ambiguities in the brief that caused varied student interpretations
- [ ] Document recommendations for refining the rubric or brief for next iteration
