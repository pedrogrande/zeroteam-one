# tell me about the potential economic benefits of ai

The potential economic benefits of AI are significant and wide-ranging, though estimates vary considerably depending on the pace and breadth of adoption.

## GDP \& Productivity Growth

Forecasts for AI's macro impact span a broad range:

- **Goldman Sachs** estimates AI could boost global GDP by \$7 trillion (7%) over 10 years, with US productivity growth rising 1.5% per year [^1]
- **McKinsey** projects AI could add \$13–\$25.6 trillion in global economic activity, equating to ~1.2% additional GDP growth per year [^2]
- **PwC** puts the figure at up to **\$15.7 trillion** added to the global economy by 2030, with \$6.6 trillion from productivity gains alone [^3]
- More conservative estimates from MIT's Daron Acemoglu suggest a **1.1–1.8% GDP increase** over 10 years, arguing only ~5% of tasks will be profitably automated in the near term [^1]
- CBA economists forecast AI lifting productivity by **0.8–1.0 percentage points** in advanced economies over time, including Australia [^4]


## Labour Market Effects

AI's impact on employment is nuanced — it both displaces and creates jobs:

- The World Economic Forum predicts AI will create **170 million new jobs** this decade while displacing 92 million, resulting in a **net gain of 78 million jobs** [^5]
- Firms that use AI extensively show **~6% higher employment growth** and **9.5% more sales growth** over five years [^6]
- Job postings requiring new AI-related skills pay **up to 15% more** in the UK and 8.5% more in the US, boosting local consumer spending [^7]


## Business \& Sector Productivity

- AI is already lifting real economic activity — in the US, AI-related investment has become a key driver of GDP resilience [^4]
- Companies using AI to automate portions of roles (rather than entire roles) tend to see employment *grow* in those positions, as firm productivity expands faster than headcount is cut [^6]
- However, the gains are unevenly distributed: **74% of AI's economic value** is currently captured by just 20% of organisations — those who have moved beyond pilots to full deployment [^8]


## Key Sectors Benefiting

- **Healthcare** — AI accelerates drug discovery, diagnostics, and care delivery
- **Finance \& logistics** — automation of high-volume, rules-based tasks [^9]
- **Software development** — code generation dramatically increases developer output
- **Green energy** — AI optimises grid management and accelerates clean tech R\&D [^5]


## The Equity Challenge

The economic upside is real, but not automatic or evenly shared. [^7] Regions with high AI skill demand have seen employment in AI-vulnerable occupations fall **3.6% after five years**, meaning policy choices around reskilling and labour transitions will heavily determine whether the productivity dividend is broadly shared [^7][^5].
