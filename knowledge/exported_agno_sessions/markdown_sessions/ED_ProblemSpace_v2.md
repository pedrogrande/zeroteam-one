**1\. The missions — what success actually looks like**

These five organisations do not have the same mission. Understanding that distinction is essential, because the way AI threatens each mission is different — and the stakes of getting AI adoption wrong are different.

| FORMATION MISSIONS — CATHOLIC EDUCATION MELBOURNE · WESLEY COLLEGE MELBOURNE |
| :---- |

These institutions exist to form human beings, not simply to credential them. Their founding purpose — one rooted in Catholic social teaching, the other in a Uniting Church tradition of whole-person development — is to help young people develop the intellectual, moral, relational and spiritual capacities to live well and contribute to society.

Success, for a formation institution, looks like graduates who are: genuinely curious and capable of sustained independent thought; equipped with ethical reasoning they can apply in ambiguous real-world situations; relationally capable — able to form, maintain and contribute to communities; and equipped for civic participation in a complex society. The academic credential is a byproduct of this formation, not its goal.

These institutions are preparing students for a 40+ year working life in a world they cannot fully see. That has always been true. What is new is that AI is transforming that world faster and more fundamentally than anything in living memory — and the formation mission requires preparing students for it.

| *Success means graduates who can navigate an AI-saturated world with judgment, ethics, and genuine capability — not graduates who have learned to produce credentials while outsourcing the thinking that credentials are supposed to represent.* |
| :---- |

| ACCESS AND MOBILITY MISSIONS — OPEN COLLEGES · ACKNOWLEDGE EDUCATION |
| :---- |

These organisations exist to democratise opportunity. They serve adult career-changers, international students, first-generation learners, and people for whom education is an economic lever — often a significant financial and personal investment. The credential is the mechanism of mobility. It is supposed to open a door that would otherwise be closed.

Success means: the student completes. The credential is recognised. The employer values it. The graduate gets the job, earns the wage, builds the career. The return on investment — in time, money, and often in the sacrifice of leaving a previous life behind — is realised.

| *Success is not the award of a credential. Success is employment in the field the credential claims to prepare students for, at the level of capability the employer needs. Everything between enrolment and that outcome is means, not end.* |
| :---- |

| PROFESSIONAL COMPETENCE MISSIONS — KAPLAN AUSTRALIA |
| :---- |

Kaplan exists at the intersection of education and professional regulation. It trains the practitioners — financial advisers, mortgage brokers, real estate agents — who will go on to hold Australian licences and advise real clients on consequential financial and property decisions. The mission is not to pass students through an exam. It is to produce practitioners who are genuinely competent in their profession.

Success means a graduate who can be trusted with a client's retirement savings, or who can explain a mortgage product's risks without misleading them. Professional competence is ultimately judged not by exam results but by what happens to clients in the real world.

| *Success means practitioners who are competent by the standards the profession actually operates at — not by the standards that were codified before AI changed how the profession works.* |
| :---- |

**2\. The world graduates enter — AI-transformed employment**

The missions of these institutions are future-oriented by definition. They are preparing people for a world they will inhabit for decades. That world is being transformed by AI faster than any previous technology shift, and the transformation is already visible in the workplaces that these graduates enter.

**For CEM and Wesley graduates — every professional career**

The young people graduating from Melbourne's Catholic and independent schools in the next five years will enter workplaces where AI tools are embedded in almost every knowledge-work role — legal, medical, financial, engineering, creative, administrative. By the time the youngest current students begin their careers, the workplaces they enter will be more AI-transformed than the ones that exist today.

What will matter in those workplaces is not the ability to retrieve information, draft documents, or recall facts — AI handles those tasks fluently. What will matter is judgment: the ability to direct AI effectively, to evaluate its outputs critically, to know when to trust it and when to override it, and to maintain personal accountability for decisions that AI contributed to. Beyond that, the distinctly human capabilities that AI cannot replicate — ethical reasoning, relational intelligence, creative synthesis, the ability to navigate genuine ambiguity — become more valuable, not less, as AI handles more of the routine cognitive work.

Civic life matters too. These graduates will vote on AI governance, serve on boards and councils making AI-related decisions, raise children in AI-saturated environments, and participate in communities grappling with the social consequences of AI. A formation education that does not prepare them for this civic dimension of AI has left something essential out.

**For Open Colleges graduates — community services, health, business, IT**

| Community services Case management platforms are integrating AI for documentation, risk assessment, resource matching and service navigation. A community services worker in 2028 will routinely interact with AI tools as part of their daily workflow. Workers who haven't developed the judgment to use them responsibly — and to know when human judgment must prevail — will be less effective and potentially harmful. Business Every business function — analysis, communication, project management, customer service, reporting — now has AI tools embedded or available. Graduates entering SMEs in particular are expected to be productive immediately, and AI tool fluency is increasingly part of what 'productive' means. | Allied health & health administration Clinical documentation, patient communication, scheduling, and administrative workflow are all being reshaped by AI. Allied health and support workers who can't navigate AI-assisted tools are less competitive in a workforce that is adopting them at pace. Information technology AI is now a core tool in software development, data analysis, systems management and cybersecurity. IT graduates who haven't used AI tools in their training enter roles where their peers who have will outperform them from day one. |
| :---- | :---- |

**For Acknowledge Education graduates — the stakes of the investment**

International students and career-changers make substantial personal and financial investments in their credentials. For many, the cost is significant relative to their means. The expectation — reasonable, and often explicitly promised — is that the credential will translate to employment in their chosen field.

If the fields those students are entering are transforming faster than the credential reflects, the investment fails. Not dramatically, not immediately, but quietly — in job applications that don't advance, in interviews where capability gaps surface, in early performance reviews where expectations aren't met. The students most harmed are those who relied most heavily on the institution to close a capability gap that the institution, unknowingly, left open.

**For Kaplan graduates — professions being transformed in real time**

| Financial advice | Mortgage broking | Real estate |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| AI is generating investment analysis, portfolio recommendations, and statement of advice drafts. The adviser's role is shifting from information-gathering and analysis — which AI does faster — to judgment, ethics, client relationship and oversight. An adviser who doesn't understand what the AI is doing well enough to oversee it cannot perform that oversight function. The client risk is real and personal. | AI credit assessment and document processing are compressing the analytical work that brokers traditionally performed. It is a present reality — already visible in graduate employment experiences, employer hiring behaviour, and the early career performance of graduates across every field these institutions serve. |

**3\. The capability gap — what education develops vs. what employment now requires**

The gap between what education currently develops in students and what AI-era employment actually requires is the structural problem that underlies every other challenge in this sector. It is not primarily a policy problem, a technology problem, or an integrity problem. It is a curriculum and pedagogy problem.

| What education currently develops Information retrieval and recall Document production — essays, reports, assignments Domain knowledge as defined by pre-AI curricula and competency standards Research and synthesis in pre-AI formats Basic professional skills as codified by regulatory frameworks designed before AI Assessment performance — doing well in tasks that AI can now complete *These remain valuable. The problem is not that they are worthless. The problem is that they are no longer sufficient — and in some cases, the way they are being developed is actively counterproductive if AI is doing the work that builds the underlying capability.* | What AI-era employment now requires AI tool proficiency — knowing how to direct AI effectively for tasks it handles well AI discernment — knowing when to trust AI outputs, when to verify, and when to override Critical evaluation of AI outputs — identifying hallucination, bias, and contextual inappropriateness AI-augmented judgment — using AI to enhance thinking without substituting for it Professional accountability in AI-assisted work — understanding that using AI doesn't transfer responsibility Ethical reasoning about AI — when use is appropriate, what disclosure is required, what oversight looks like Distinctly human capabilities at a higher standard — ethical reasoning, relational intelligence, creative synthesis, genuine ambiguity navigation Adaptability — the meta-capability to learn new tools as the landscape evolves |
| :---- | :---- |

| The gap between these two columns is not a future problem to be planned for. It is a present reality for every graduate these institutions are producing right now. Each year that closes without curriculum and pedagogy change is another cohort of students entering a world they were not fully prepared for. |
| :---- |

**Why the gap is structural, not incidental**

This gap has not emerged from negligence. It has emerged from the collision of two systems operating on incompatible timescales. Education curriculum and accreditation cycles take years to change — ACARA's national curriculum reviews, ASQA Training Package developments, TEQSA course accreditations all operate on multi-year horizons. AI capability is advancing on a six-month cycle.

No institutional governance structure was designed to respond at this speed. The gap is not a failure of will; it is a failure of the systems education operates within to adapt to a rate of change they were never designed to handle. Which means closing the gap requires something those systems can't do by themselves — a partner who understands both education and AI deeply enough to work within the constraints while moving faster than the formal mechanisms allow.

**4\. The problems — how the mission fails**

Framed through the missions, the AI problem in education is not primarily about academic integrity or operational disruption. It is about mission failure — the quiet, accumulating gap between what these institutions promise their students and what they are actually delivering.

| FORMATION MISSION FAILURE — CEM · WESLEY |
| :---- |

**Producing credential-holders, not capable persons**

The formation mission promises graduates who can think independently, reason ethically, and contribute to society with genuine capability. AI is creating two distinct risks to that promise — both serious, and both under-addressed.

The first risk is intellectual outsourcing. When students routinely use AI to perform the effortful cognitive work — research, drafting, analysis, synthesis — that builds underlying thinking capacity, they may complete their education with credentials intact but capability underdeveloped. The analogy with GPS and spatial navigation is useful here: people who use GPS constantly become measurably worse at navigation without it. The same dynamic applies to AI and reasoning. A formation education that allows AI to do the forming is not achieving its mission.

The second risk is the omission of AI as a domain of formation. These institutions form students in ethics, in civic responsibility, in the capacity for relationship and contribution. They have not yet formed students in the ethics of AI use, in the civic implications of AI governance, or in what it means to be a responsible AI user across a professional life. That is a gap in the formation that the mission demands.

| *A Wesley or CEM graduate who cannot think critically about AI — who cannot evaluate an AI output, who has no ethical framework for when and how to use it, who has never had to make a judgment call about AI in a high-stakes context — is not the capable, ethical person these institutions' missions describe.* |
| :---- |

| MOBILITY MISSION FAILURE — OPEN COLLEGES · ACKNOWLEDGE EDUCATION |
| :---- |

**Credentials degrading in employment value — silently**

The mobility mission fails when the credential stops reliably translating to employment at the level of capability employers need. This failure is insidious because it is slow and largely invisible — to the institution, to students at enrolment, and to the broader market — until it isn't.

What is already happening: employers in AI-transformed fields are asking different questions in interviews, discovering capability gaps in early performance reviews, and — in some cases — quietly adjusting the weight they place on credentials from institutions whose curriculum they know hasn't kept pace. The credential still gets awarded; the job door is opening less reliably. Students don't know this at enrolment. They discover it afterwards.

The students most harmed by this failure are those who invested the most and can least afford to discover that the investment underperformed. An international student who has borrowed significantly against family resources to study in Australia, and who graduates into a competitive job market without the AI literacy that their peers from more AI-engaged institutions have developed, is not served by the mission that brought them here.

| *The mobility mission does not fail loudly. It fails in the quiet accumulation of job applications that don't advance, interviews where unexpressed capability gaps surface, and first-year performance reviews where expectations are not met. By the time it becomes visible at the institutional level, cohorts of students have already borne the cost.* |
| :---- |

| PROFESSIONAL COMPETENCE MISSION FAILURE — KAPLAN |
| :---- |

**Licensed but not contemporary-competent**

Kaplan's mission fails when graduates pass licensing assessments and enter their professions without the AI competence those professions now require. The failure is particularly acute because it has a downstream human cost that the formation and mobility mission failures do not: a client receives advice from a practitioner who is licensed but not equipped to recognise when the AI-generated analysis they are presenting is wrong.

The professional licensing frameworks haven't caught up. FASEA's competency standards for financial advice, the Real Estate Institute's training requirements, the MFAA's mortgage broking competencies — none of them yet mandate AI competence explicitly. But the workplaces these practitioners enter are using AI tools now. The gap between the regulatory standard and the actual standard of professional competence is already open, even if it is not yet officially acknowledged.

This creates a specific liability structure: practitioners who rely on AI tools they were never trained to critically evaluate, in professions where errors create real harm to real clients, in a regulatory environment that will eventually catch up and hold someone accountable for the gap. Kaplan's graduates are operating in that gap right now.

**5\. Root causes — why the gap exists**

Understanding root causes is essential for designing effective interventions. Most current responses in the education sector address symptoms. The root causes run deeper.

* **Curriculum was designed for a world that no longer exists.** Every curriculum, competency standard, and accreditation framework currently in use was designed before generative AI. The knowledge and skills they require students to demonstrate were chosen because they mattered in a pre-AI world. Many still matter. Some matter less. New ones matter greatly and are not present. The curriculum has not been redesigned because the cycles that govern redesign are too slow.

* **Institutions are measuring the wrong things.** Assessment is the most powerful signal in any education system — what gets assessed is what gets developed. If assessment measures pre-AI capabilities using formats AI can now complete without the student doing the cognitive work, the institution is not measuring what it thinks it is measuring. The credential is being awarded for the wrong performance. Redesigning assessment is the hardest and most resource-intensive part of the curriculum challenge, which is why it is the most avoided.

* **No trusted, education-specific source of AI guidance exists.** Technology vendors understand AI but not education. Education consultants understand pedagogy but not AI. Government frameworks are years behind the current state of AI capability. The gap between what institutions need and what is available is not a gap in the market — it is the defining gap in the market. Institutions are making decisions without the expertise to make them well, and they know it.

* **Economic incentives misalign with the harder path.** For RTOs and private providers especially, the commercial pressure to maintain enrolments and completion rates creates a structural disincentive to acknowledge that the curriculum is insufficient. Redesigning curriculum and assessment costs time and money. Failing students who are not developing the required capabilities costs revenue. The easier path — award the credential, hope the market doesn't notice — is structurally incentivised even when leaders know it is wrong.

* **The people who design curriculum don't have AI expertise.** Curriculum designers, academic directors and training managers in education bring deep expertise in learning design and their subject domains. They do not, in most cases, have deep expertise in AI — what it can do, how it is changing their fields, or how to design learning experiences that develop AI-era capabilities. The expertise that is needed does not currently reside inside these institutions.

* **Professional identity resistance.** For teachers, trainers and academics, AI creates a professional identity challenge that is rarely named. If AI can produce competent outputs in the domain they teach, what is their role? The instinctive response — to protect existing practice from AI rather than evolve it — is understandable but counterproductive. It means the people who are closest to students and most able to influence the curriculum are often the least motivated to change it.

**7\. Who fails — the stakeholder map**

The mission failure is not experienced by institutions. It is experienced by people. Understanding who bears the cost — and how — is the foundation for empathic, effective messaging and programme design.

**Students — bearing the cost they weren't warned about**

| Formation students (CEM · Wesley) Graduate without a mature, capable relationship with AI — lacking the discernment to use it well, the ethics to use it responsibly, and the judgment to know when not to use it at all. Enter workplaces, further education and civic life underprepared for the most significant technological shift of their generation. The students from disadvantaged backgrounds within these systems — scholarship students, first-generation families who chose Catholic education for its values — are most exposed. They have fewer alternative pathways to AI literacy. | Mobility and professional students (Open Colleges · Acknowledge · Kaplan) The credential investment underperforms. The job door opens less reliably. The wage increase doesn't materialise at the expected rate. For Kaplan graduates specifically, the professional practice is less safe — they are operating in AI-augmented environments without the training to do so responsibly. International students and adult career-changers who have made the largest personal sacrifices for the credential bear the greatest cost of its underperformance. They are also the least able to absorb that cost. |
| :---- | :---- |

**Employers — discovering the gap at their own cost**

Employers in AI-transformed industries are encountering graduates who are credentialled for roles but not equipped for how those roles now work. The discovery happens in onboarding (the tools are unfamiliar), in early performance (the judgment isn't there), and in client outcomes (the quality isn't what was expected from a credentialled professional). Employers absorb the training cost that the credentialling institution should have included — and adjust their future hiring behaviour accordingly, quietly disadvantaging subsequent cohorts from the same institution.

**Clients and consumers — the downstream human cost**

In fields where Kaplan graduates practice, the ultimate stakeholder is the client. A retail investor whose financial adviser presents AI-generated portfolio recommendations without understanding their limitations. A first-home buyer whose mortgage broker didn't adequately evaluate the AI credit assessment that shaped their loan structure. A vendor whose agent used AI-generated marketing copy that was factually incorrect. These are not hypothetical harms — they are the foreseeable consequences of professional practice without AI competence, in professions where AI tools are already embedded in the workflow.

**The wider community — the equity and civic stakes**

| The equity dimension Education has historically been the mechanism by which disadvantaged students close the gap with advantaged ones. AI literacy is becoming a significant dimension of that gap. Students from advantaged backgrounds are developing AI capabilities through family networks, technology access, and peer communities that take this seriously. Students who depend on a formal institution to develop those capabilities — because they have no alternative pathway — face a new form of structural disadvantage if the institution doesn't deliver. The civic dimension A generation of graduates who have not been helped to think critically and ethically about AI — who have not developed the judgment to evaluate AI outputs, the ethics to use AI responsibly, or the civic understanding to participate in AI governance — will be less equipped to make the collective decisions that AI's social consequences will require. Education institutions that are supposed to develop civic capacity are, inadvertently, failing to develop AI civic capacity. |

**8\. Impacts — the stakes across time**

The impacts of institutions failing to close the capability gap compound over time. What is currently a quiet professional shortfall will become a visible, measurable, and ultimately very public failure if it is not addressed. The timeline matters because it shapes the urgency of the intervention.

| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Graduate employment outcomes beginning to diverge between AI-literate and non-AI-literate cohorts — not yet statistically visible at the institutional level but experienced individually by graduates. Employer interview and onboarding processes beginning to surface AI literacy as an implicit requirement — with graduates who lack it performing less well. First professional misconduct cases involving AI misuse by practitioners from institutions whose training did not address AI competence. Early-adopter institutions beginning to differentiate on AI curriculum — creating reputational and enrolment pressure on institutions that have not moved. Institutional enrolment decline for RTOs and private providers whose employment outcome data is public and unfavourable. The equity gap widening measurably — AI literacy a significant predictor of graduate employment outcomes, and access to AI-competent education correlated with socioeconomic advantage. | If institutions fail: workforce in key sectors systematically underprepared for AI-augmented professional practice; professional quality and consumer safety consequences in licensed fields; social mobility through education degrading as credential value declines; civic AI literacy deficit as a generation of graduates lacks the formation to engage well with AI governance. | The long-term community stake is not that AI is bad for graduates. It is that the transition from a pre-AI to an AI-augmented education system is being navigated without adequate guidance — and the cost of getting it wrong is borne disproportionately by the students who most needed the institution to get it right. |

**9\. Current responses — and why they miss the point**

The responses education institutions have deployed to the AI challenge are largely focused on managing the disruption to existing practice — preventing cheating, updating policies, controlling tool use. Almost none of them address the capability gap that is the real mission-level problem.

**What institutions are doing — and what it doesn't address**

| Blanket bans Prohibiting AI tool use in assessment and sometimes in school environments. Intent: protect assessment integrity. Why it misses the point Prevents students from developing the AI capabilities they need for employment. Graduates who have never learned to use AI responsibly enter workplaces where it is embedded in the workflow. The ban protects the credential. It damages the graduate. | Detection tools Using AI detection software to identify AI-generated content in submissions. Intent: enforce academic integrity standards. Why it misses the point Addresses the symptom (AI-assisted cheating) without addressing the cause (assessment designed for a pre-AI world). Creates adversarial dynamics, produces false positives that harm students, and does nothing to develop the capabilities employment requires. |
| :---- | :---- |

| One-off professional development Staff professional learning sessions on AI tools and their use in education. Intent: build teacher capability and awareness. Why it misses the point Builds awareness of AI. Does not change what is taught, how it is assessed, or what capabilities students develop. Teachers who attend a PD session and return to an unchanged curriculum have not moved the institution toward closing the capability gap. | Generic AI policies Publishing acceptable-use policies, AI guidelines and academic integrity frameworks. Intent: establish governance and manage risk. Why it misses the point Governance of existing practice. Not redesign of that practice to close the capability gap. A policy that governs how AI is used in assessment does nothing to ensure assessment is developing the capabilities students need. Governance of the wrong thing, governed well, is still the wrong thing. |
| :---- | :---- |

| The common failure across every current response is the framing of the problem as a disruption to manage, rather than a mission to fulfil. The question being asked is 'how do we protect what we are currently doing from AI?' The question that needs to be asked is 'what do we need to do differently so that our graduates are prepared for the world AI is creating?' |

**10\. The design opportunity — what this implies for Zero Team**

This analysis points to a specific, under-occupied space that Zero Team is positioned to fill — and to the messaging and programme design principles that should flow from it.

**The gap that no one is filling**

Education organisations need a partner who can help them answer a question that is more difficult than it sounds: 'What does good education look like in a world where AI is transforming the nature of work, and how do we build the curriculum, pedagogy and assessment to deliver it?' Answering this question requires:

* Deep expertise in education — what learning looks like, how curriculum is designed, how teachers change their practice, how assessment is redesigned

* Deep expertise in AI — what it can do, where it fails, how it is changing specific professional fields, and what responsible AI use looks like in practice

* Understanding of the specific missions of education institutions — the difference between a formation mission and a mobility mission and a professional competence mission, and how each shapes what AI adoption needs to achieve

* Change management capability — the ability to shift the practice of a large, distributed, sometimes resistant workforce in a way that produces lasting change rather than compliance theatre

No technology vendor fills this gap. No standard education consultancy fills it. No government framework fills it. It is the specific intersection of all four — and it is where Zero Team operates.

**What this means for Zero Team's messaging**

| Start with the mission, not the technology. The conversation that opens a door in this sector is not 'look what AI can do in education.' That question lands differently — it is the institution's own mission doing the work, not a vendor pitch. Name the employment stakes, not the integrity stakes.
