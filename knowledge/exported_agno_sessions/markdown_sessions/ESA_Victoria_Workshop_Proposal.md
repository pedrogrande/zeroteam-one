
# Workshop Proposal
## Agents, Trust & the Future of Work
### A half-day workshop for the Economic Society of Australia (Victoria)

---

## The Problem Worth Solving

Agentic AI — systems where autonomous agents plan, act, and coordinate without step-by-step human direction — is restructuring how work is organised, how institutions govern, and how accountability is assigned. Most senior economists have sufficient knowledge of the macroeconomic implications but lack an experiential frame for *how these systems actually behave*, which is the knowledge that matters most for governance design, policy advice, and institutional leadership.

This workshop closes that gap in three hours.

## Learning Outcomes

### Primary (achieved through direct experience and structured discussion)

**1. Understand multi-agent systems as an economic redesign of work — not just automation of tasks**
Attendees will be able to explain the comparative strengths of agents and humans using economic reasoning (comparative advantage, marginal cost, coordination costs, deliberate complementarity) and articulate why effective implementation requires redesigning workflows rather than replacing roles.

*Observable evidence: Attendee can diagnose why a vague agent instruction produced a poor output and propose a redesigned task specification.*

**2. Explain why trust cannot scale — and what structural alternatives look like**
Attendees will be able to distinguish reputational trust from structural trust, explain why near-zero marginal cost verification changes the economics of institutional accountability, and identify where their own organisation's governance is declarative vs. structural.

*Observable evidence: Attendee can map at least one institutional process in their domain onto the reputational/structural trust distinction.*

**3. Identify the governance design challenge specific to autonomous agents — and explain why policy alone fails**
Attendees will be able to explain structural vs. declarative ethics using a concrete example (MCP tool boundaries), describe the key risk categories (liability gaps, accountability failures, governance collapse, trust erosion) and their economic mechanisms, and apply a basic risk lens to their sector.

*Observable evidence: Attendee can identify one declarative governance control in their domain and articulate what a structural alternative would require.*

### Secondary (introduced through demo, discussion, and passing reference)
- Demonstrate basic agentic tool usage in a no-code environment
- Explain core AI terminology in context (agent, multi-agent system, MCP, context window, tool, orchestrator)
- Explain agentic fluency and its organisational importance for leadership teams
- Apply responsible AI case studies as diagnostic material (not standalone content)

## The Through-Line: Trust

Every segment of this workshop is organised around a single economic concept that this audience already knows and uses — trust — examined through a lens they have not yet encountered: what happens to institutional trust when autonomous agents enter the system?

The session opens with a familiar framing (trust as economic input), disrupts it (you cannot scale trust), rebuilds it (structure replaces trust), and closes with a provocation that travels home:

> *"What in your institution still runs on trust that should run on structure?"*

---

## Session Design

**Duration:** 3 hours (plus 15-minute break)
**Format:** Half experiential, half conceptual — no lecture longer than 15 minutes uninterrupted

### Segment 1 — The Trust Paradox (0:00–0:20)
*Opening provocation + framing*
- "What happens when coordination costs approach zero?"
- Facilitator shares personal discovery narrative: weeks of experimentation compressed into a three-hour session
- Economic translation table introduced (multi-agent system = market with near-zero transaction costs; agent = perfect specialist with zero coordination cost and no values)
- Learning outcomes framed as questions, not objectives

### Segment 2 — Agents as Economic Actors (0:20–0:50)
*Conceptual foundation + first live demo*
- Live demo: single agent given a complex task → context wall → failure
- Live demo: same task decomposed across a team of agents → success
- Discussion: "This isn't automation. It's a redesign of the division of labour."
- Introduce comparative advantage frame: agents for consistency, scale, execution; humans for judgment, ethics, irreversibility
- Key concept introduced: tasks as contracts — "when you give an agent a task, you are writing a contract. The agent executes it faithfully whether it's good or not."

### Segment 3 — Hands-On: Build, Break, Observe (0:50–1:20)
*Structured experimentation in Dify / Agno*
- Attendees interact with pre-configured agent workflows (no technical knowledge required)
- Guided prompts: modify the agent's instruction and observe the output change
- Facilitated debrief: "What did you observe? What surprised you? What does this tell you about specification quality?"
- Introduce: near-zero marginal cost verification — live demo of executor + reviewer agent pair catching an introduced error

### BREAK (1:20–1:35)

### Segment 4 — Trust Erosion: A Diagnostic Exercise (1:35–2:00)
*Case study + group analysis*
- Present one real institutional AI failure (automated welfare denial, biased hiring system, or financial services liability gap)
- Groups diagnose the failure using economic concepts + concepts from Segments 2–3
- Introduce: structural vs. declarative ethics — live demo of MCP tool boundary enforcement (agent attempts out-of-scope action, fails structurally)
- Key distinction: "Policy says don't. Structure can't."

### Segment 5 — Your Sector (2:00–2:35)
*Small group application exercise*
Groups of 3–4 answer three prompts about their own domain:
1. "What workflow in your organisation has the highest multi-agent disruption potential?"
2. "Where would trust erosion hit hardest if an agentic system failed in your domain?"
3. "Name one governance control that is currently declarative. What would make it structural?"
Share-outs: 2 minutes per group. Facilitator synthesises cross-cutting patterns.

### Segment 6 — Governance Design, Not Technology Decision (2:35–2:55)
*Synthesis + closing provocation*
- Key risk landscape: liability gaps, observability trilemma, cascading failures, regulatory lag
- Frame agentic fluency as organisational literacy: "Your leadership doesn't need to write code. They need to understand how these systems fail."
- Closing provocation: "What in your institution still runs on trust that should run on structure?"
- Attendee-generated takeaways (3 minutes: each person writes one thing they'll say differently on Monday)

### Segment 7 — Q&A + Close (2:55–3:10)
- Open questions
- Leave-behind distributed: Multi-Agent Risk Assessment one-pager
- Invitation to follow-up office hours and leadership workshop variants

## Key Takeaways (Designed to Survive a Hallway Conversation)

1. "You cannot scale trust. You can scale structure."
2. "An agent is a perfect specialist with zero coordination cost — and no values."
3. "The quality of the contract is the quality of the system."
4. "Declarative ethics don't scale. Structural ethics do."
5. "Near-zero marginal cost verification changes everything about accountability."
6. "This is not a technology decision. It is a governance design decision."
7. "Don't put agents in human roles — use them to reimagine what work looks like."

## Assumptions (To Be Revisited if Invited to Deliver)

1. **Attendee AI literacy**: Assumed low-to-moderate. No prior agent experience required by design. If a significant proportion have hands-on AI experience, Segment 3 may need an advanced track.
2. **Aneeq persona**: Confirmed as Aneeq Sarwar (Treasurer, ESA Victoria) from ESA Victoria's LinkedIn. Full profile not publicly available — persona built on role and context only.
3. **Group size**: Assumed 15–30 attendees. The "Your Sector" exercise and share-outs are calibrated for this range. Larger groups require modified facilitation.
4. **Tech infrastructure**: Assumed reliable WiFi and browser access. Pre-recorded fallback demos will be prepared for all live segments. Dify and Agno setups will be tested under realistic network conditions before delivery.
5. **Sector mix**: Assumed majority government economists with some industry and academic representation, based on council roster. If industry representation is heavier, labour market disruption content in Segment 2 may need expansion.
6. **Responsible AI case study selection**: Assumed one case study is sufficient for diagnostic purposes. Australian examples (e.g., Robodebt, automated financial advice failures) will be prioritised for relevance and resonance.
7. **Duration flexibility**: Designed for exactly 3 hours. If extended to 3.5 hours, the "Your Sector" exercise can expand to 45 minutes with individual share-outs — this would significantly deepen the application outcome.
8. **Pre-workshop primer**: Assumed the organiser can distribute a 10-minute pre-read 3–5 days before the session. If this isn't feasible, Segment 1 needs 10 additional minutes of scaffolding.
